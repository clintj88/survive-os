# Foundation Band (Grades K-2, Ages 5-8) — Social Skills & Community Living

## Overview
A community survives or fails based on how well its members cooperate, resolve conflicts, and care for one another. Social skills are not "soft" — they are the foundation of every functional community in human history. Children who learn to share, listen, take turns, resolve disputes with words, and understand others' feelings become the adults who hold communities together.

## Year 1: Getting Along

### Unit 1: Sharing and Taking Turns (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Community toys/tools, games requiring turn-taking
**Survival Relevance**: Resource sharing is the most fundamental community skill. Communities that share fairly survive. Communities that hoard and fight over resources collapse.

#### Learning Objectives
- Wait for a turn without grabbing or crying
- Share materials with a partner or group
- Use words to ask for a turn ("Can I use that when you're done?")
- Understand that taking turns means everyone gets a chance

#### Teacher Notes
Sharing is genuinely hard for young children — it requires impulse control, empathy, and delayed gratification, all of which are developing at this age. Don't punish children who struggle. Instead, model and practice constantly. Use a timer if needed: "You have it for 3 minutes, then it's Mia's turn." Make the fairness visible.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Why We Share**
- Activity: Give one child all the drawing supplies. Everyone else has nothing. "How does this feel? Is this fair?" Then distribute equally. "Now everyone can draw. When we share, everyone can do something."
- Key Concepts: Sharing isn't losing — it's making sure everyone has what they need
- Practice: Partner drawing — share one set of supplies between two

**Lesson 3-4: Asking Nicely**
- Activity: Teach the phrase: "Can I use that when you're done?" Practice with partner. Compare to grabbing — "Which way works better? Which way keeps friends?"
- Practice: Throughout the day, prompt children to use asking words instead of grabbing

**Lesson 5-6: The Turn-Taking Game**
- Activity: Play a game that requires strict turn-taking (board game, ball toss, building tower where each person adds one block). Practice waiting. Celebrate patience.
- Key Concepts: Turns mean everyone gets a chance. Waiting is part of the game.

**Lesson 7-8: Sharing When It's Hard**
- Activity: Discuss: "What if there's only one of something and two people want it?" Problem-solve together. Options: take turns, find a second one, do something else, share it (split the snack).
- Practice: Real scenarios throughout the day — teacher highlights good sharing when it happens

#### Assessment
- Can wait for a turn for 2+ minutes without distress
- Uses asking words instead of grabbing (most of the time)
- Can share materials in a group activity
- Can suggest a solution when two people want the same thing

### Unit 2: Kind Words and Listening (Weeks 9-16)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: None required
**Survival Relevance**: Clear, kind communication prevents misunderstandings and conflicts. Listening skills prevent dangerous errors in survival situations.

#### Learning Objectives
- Use kind words (please, thank you, excuse me, I'm sorry, great job)
- Listen when someone else is speaking (eyes on speaker, body still, wait to talk)
- Understand that words can help or hurt
- Follow verbal instructions accurately

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Words That Help**
- Activity: Brainstorm kind words. Write them on the wall. "Please" gets things done. "Thank you" makes people feel appreciated. "I'm sorry" fixes mistakes. "Great job" encourages people.
- Practice: Kindness challenge — each child tries to use 5 kind words per day. Tally together.

**Lesson 3-4: Words That Hurt**
- Activity: Crumple a piece of paper. Smooth it out. "The wrinkles are still there. Mean words are like that — you can say sorry, but the hurt doesn't fully go away." Discuss: what words hurt? Why?
- Key Concepts: Think before you speak. Kind words build community. Mean words tear it apart.

**Lesson 5-6: The Art of Listening**
- Activity: Play listening games. Teacher gives a 2-step instruction. Children follow. Did everyone get it? "Listening means: eyes on the speaker, body still, brain thinking about what they're saying."
- Practice: Partner listening — one talks for 1 minute about their favorite thing. Other listens without interrupting. Then retells what they heard.

**Lesson 7-8: Following Instructions**
- Activity: Multi-step instruction games. "Pick up the red stone, put it in the basket, then sit down." Increase complexity. Connect to survival: "When an adult tells you to do something in an emergency, listening carefully could save your life."
- Practice: Daily instruction-following exercises

#### Assessment
- Uses kind words regularly and naturally
- Can listen to a speaker for 2 minutes without interrupting
- Can follow a 3-step verbal instruction
- Can explain why kind words matter for the community

### Unit 3: Community Rules and Roles (Weeks 17-24)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Community role props, chart paper for rules
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding that every person has a role and every rule has a purpose is the foundation of functional governance

#### Learning Objectives
- Name at least 8 community roles and what each person does
- Understand that every job matters — there are no unimportant jobs
- Explain why community rules exist
- Follow class and community rules consistently
- Help create a class rule through group discussion

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Who Does What?**
- Activity: Community walk. Visit each area (garden, kitchen, medical, watch, school, workshop). "What does this person do? Why does it matter?" Back in class, draw the community roles as a poster.
- Key Concepts: A community is like a body — every part has a job. If one part stops, everything suffers.

**Lesson 3-4: Every Job Matters**
- Activity: "What would happen if nobody cooked? If nobody watched for danger? If nobody taught the children? If nobody tended the garden?" Children discover that every role is essential.
- Practice: Job rotation — children take on classroom roles (supply distributor, cleanup leader, greeting person) and rotate weekly

**Lesson 5-6: Why We Have Rules**
- Activity: Play a game with no rules. Chaos. Then add rules. Order. "Rules aren't to boss you around. Rules help everyone play fair and stay safe."
- Discussion: "What are the community rules? Why does each one exist?"

**Lesson 7-8: Making a Rule Together**
- Activity: Class discusses a problem (e.g., materials not being put away). Together, they draft a rule to solve it. Vote on the wording. Post it. Follow it.
- Key Concepts: Good rules come from everyone's input. Rules can change if they're not working.

#### Assessment
- Can name 8+ community roles and their importance
- Follows class rules consistently
- Can explain why a specific rule exists
- Participated in creating a class rule

### Unit 4: Cooperation Games (Weeks 25-32)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Balls, ropes, building materials, blindfolds
**Survival Relevance**: Survival requires teamwork. Cooperative skills are as important as individual skills.

#### Learning Objectives
- Work with a partner to accomplish a task
- Work in a small group (3-5 children) with shared responsibility
- Understand that cooperation means everyone contributes
- Celebrate group success rather than individual victory

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Partner Challenges**
- Activity: Partner tasks — carry a ball between two sticks together, build a tower together (each person can only use one hand), guide a blindfolded partner through an obstacle course with words only.
- Key Concepts: Two people working together can do things one person can't.

**Lesson 3-4: Group Building**
- Activity: Small groups build the tallest tower from sticks/blocks. But everyone must contribute. If one person does all the work, the tower doesn't count. Debrief: "How did you decide who did what?"
- Key Concepts: Good teams use everyone's strengths

**Lesson 5-6: The Human Machine**
- Activity: Each child becomes one "part" of a machine. One makes a motion, the next connects to it, and so on until the whole class is a moving, interconnected machine. Remove one part — the machine breaks.
- Key Concepts: In a community, we are all connected. When one person fails to do their part, it affects everyone.

**Lesson 7-8: Group Celebration**
- Activity: Class project — plant a garden bed together, build something together, create a mural together. When it's done, celebrate as a group. "WE did this. Not me, not you — WE."
- Key Concepts: Shared achievement builds community bonds

#### Assessment
- Can work cooperatively with a partner to complete a task
- Can participate productively in a group of 3-5
- Can describe what cooperation means
- Celebrates group success

### Unit 5: Solving Problems with Words (Weeks 33-40)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Roleplay props
**Survival Relevance**: Communities that resolve conflicts through talking survive. Communities where conflicts escalate to violence destroy themselves from within.

#### Learning Objectives
- Use words instead of hitting, grabbing, or screaming when upset
- Follow conflict resolution steps: 1) Stop. 2) Say how you feel. 3) Listen. 4) Find a solution.
- Understand that both people's feelings matter in a conflict
- Seek adult help for conflicts they can't solve

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Stop Before You React**
- Activity: Practice the STOP signal. When you feel angry: STOP. Take 3 breaths. THEN talk. Roleplay: someone takes your toy. Show the wrong way (grab it back, hit). Show the right way (STOP, breathe, talk).
- Practice: Use STOP throughout the day when real conflicts arise

**Lesson 3-4: Say How You Feel**
- Activity: Teach "I feel ___ when you ___ because ___." Example: "I feel angry when you take my pencil because I was using it." Practice with partner.
- Key Concepts: Saying how you feel is not being weak — it's being honest

**Lesson 5-6: Listen to the Other Person**
- Activity: After you say how you feel, the other person says how they feel. You listen. You might learn that they didn't mean to hurt you, or that they have a reason you didn't know about.
- Practice: Roleplay conflicts where listening changes the solution

**Lesson 7-8: Find a Solution Together**
- Activity: "What can we do so both people are okay?" Brainstorm solutions together. Take turns. Share. Apologize. Find a new one. Sometimes there's no perfect solution — but a fair compromise is always better than fighting.
- Practice: Real conflict resolution throughout the day with teacher support

#### Assessment
- Can use STOP and breathing when upset (most of the time)
- Can express feelings using "I feel" statements
- Can listen while another person speaks about a conflict
- Can suggest a fair solution to a conflict

## Year 2: Understanding Others

### Unit 6: Empathy — Walking in Someone Else's Shoes (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Story books or told stories, roleplay props
**Survival Relevance**: Empathy is the foundation of trust, cooperation, and community cohesion. Without it, communities fracture into hostile factions.

#### Learning Objectives
- Recognize emotions in others from facial expressions, body language, and tone
- Understand that other people have different feelings than you about the same situation
- Respond to others' distress with kindness (comfort, offer help, get adult)
- Understand that everyone has hard days

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Reading Faces**
- Activity: Show face cards with different emotions. "How is this person feeling? How do you know?" Practice reading real classmates' expressions.
- Practice: Daily check-in now includes "How is your neighbor feeling?"

**Lesson 3-4: Different Feelings, Same Situation**
- Activity: Tell a story. Ask: "How does Character A feel? How does Character B feel? They feel differently about the same thing. Can both feelings be real?" Yes — empathy means understanding that others feel differently than you.

**Lesson 5-6: When Someone Is Sad**
- Activity: "What do you do when you see someone crying?" Brainstorm: sit with them, ask if they're okay, get a teacher, offer a hug (if they want one), just be there.
- Key Concepts: You don't have to fix the problem. Sometimes just being there helps.

**Lesson 7-10: Perspective Taking**
- Activity: Roleplay scenarios from two characters' perspectives. "You're the one who broke the cup. How do you feel? Now you're the one whose cup was broken. How do you feel?" Switch roles.
- Practice: During real conflicts, ask: "How do you think ___ feels right now?"

#### Assessment
- Can identify emotions from facial expressions and body language
- Can explain how two people might feel differently about the same event
- Demonstrates kind response when a peer is upset
- Can take another person's perspective when prompted

### Unit 7: Teamwork Projects (Weeks 11-18)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Project materials (vary by project)
**Survival Relevance**: Complex survival tasks (building, farming, defense) all require coordinated teamwork

#### Learning Objectives
- Plan a simple group project together (decide what to do, who does what, what's needed)
- Divide tasks fairly among group members
- Help a struggling team member instead of criticizing
- Complete a multi-day group project from start to finish

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Planning Together**
- Activity: Groups of 4 choose a project (build a birdhouse, plant a mini garden, create a class book). First step: plan. "What do we need? Who does what? What comes first?"

**Lesson 3-4: Dividing Work**
- Activity: "Everyone does something. No one does everything. No one does nothing." Groups assign tasks based on what each person is good at or wants to learn.

**Lesson 5-8: Building and Completing**
- Activity: Groups work on their projects over 4 weeks. Teacher facilitates problem-solving when groups get stuck or have conflicts. Focus on process, not perfection.

#### Assessment
- Participated actively in planning
- Completed assigned tasks
- Helped a teammate who was struggling
- Project completed cooperatively

### Unit 8: Community Helpers and Responsibility (Weeks 19-28)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Community member visits, task charts
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding community interdependence and taking on responsibility are prerequisites for contributing community members

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain the role of 5+ specific community members and how their work helps everyone
- Complete assigned classroom responsibilities without being reminded
- Understand that responsibility means doing your part even when it's not fun
- Demonstrate reliability — do what you say you will do

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Community Helper Interviews**
- Activity: Each week, a different community member visits (or class visits them). Cook, gardener, medic, builder, watchperson. They explain their work. Children prepare 3 questions in advance.
- Key Concepts: Every job requires learning and practice. Every job helps everyone.

**Lesson 5-6: Responsibility in Class**
- Activity: Assign classroom jobs that rotate monthly: supply manager, cleanup crew, plant waterer, welcome greeter, library organizer. Children do their jobs without being reminded.
- Key Concepts: Responsibility means doing your part whether or not someone is watching.

**Lesson 7-8: Honesty and Trust**
- Activity: "What happens when someone says they'll do something and doesn't? What happens when someone always keeps their word?"
- Story: Tell a story about trust — how it's built slowly and broken quickly. "Trust is the most important thing in a community."

**Lesson 9-10: Being a Reliable Person**
- Activity: Each child takes on a multi-week responsibility (water a specific plant, keep a section tidy, help a younger child daily). Teacher tracks follow-through. Discuss at end: "How did it feel to be relied on?"

#### Assessment
- Can explain 5+ community roles in detail
- Completes classroom jobs without reminding for 4+ weeks
- Demonstrates honesty in daily interactions
- Can explain why trust and reliability matter

### Unit 9: Friendship Skills (Weeks 29-38)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: None required
**Survival Relevance**: Friendship provides emotional support, practical help, and the social bonds that hold communities together through hardship

#### Learning Objectives
- Demonstrate how to make a friend (approach kindly, ask to join, share interests)
- Demonstrate how to be a good friend (listen, help, include, forgive)
- Handle friendship challenges (disagreements, feeling left out, jealousy)
- Include children who are alone or new

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How to Make a Friend**
- Activity: Roleplay approaching someone: "Hi, I'm ___. Can I play with you?" or "What are you building? Can I help?" Practice with different partners.
- Key Concepts: Friendship starts with approaching someone kindly

**Lesson 3-4: Being a Good Friend**
- Activity: Brainstorm what good friends do: listen, share, help, tell the truth, stand up for each other, forgive mistakes. Create a "Good Friend" poster.

**Lesson 5-6: When Friends Disagree**
- Activity: "Even good friends disagree sometimes. That doesn't mean the friendship is over." Practice using conflict resolution steps from Year 1 with a friend context.

**Lesson 7-8: Including Everyone**
- Activity: "Look around. Is anyone sitting alone? Is anyone always picked last? Including someone who is left out is one of the bravest things you can do."
- Practice: Each child's goal for one week — include someone they don't usually play with

**Lesson 9-10: Forgiveness**
- Activity: "Everyone makes mistakes. Everyone hurts someone's feelings sometimes. Forgiveness means choosing to move forward rather than stay angry. It doesn't mean what happened was okay — it means you choose not to let it keep hurting."

#### Assessment
- Can demonstrate approaching and engaging a potential friend
- Can list 5 qualities of a good friend
- Can work through a friendship disagreement constructively
- Has made an effort to include someone who was left out

## Year 3: Leading and Teaching

### Unit 10: Leading a Group (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Group activity supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Every community needs leaders. Leadership skills can be developed early through structured practice.

#### Learning Objectives
- Lead a small group activity (game, project, or discussion)
- Give clear instructions that others can follow
- Listen to group members' ideas and incorporate them
- Handle it when things don't go as planned

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Makes a Good Leader?**
- Activity: Discuss: "Who are leaders in our community? What do they do? Is a leader someone who bosses people around, or someone who helps everyone work together?"
- Key Concepts: Leaders listen, plan, help, and take responsibility. Leaders serve the group.

**Lesson 3-4: Practice Leading**
- Activity: Each child leads a 5-minute group activity (a game, a building challenge, a cleanup task). Teacher observes and provides private feedback afterward.

**Lesson 5-6: When Plans Fail**
- Activity: Set up a group activity that will encounter a surprise obstacle. The leader must adapt. "Leaders don't panic when things go wrong. They figure out a new plan."

**Lesson 7-8: Different Leadership Styles**
- Activity: Try leading the same activity three ways: 1) Leader decides everything. 2) Leader asks for votes. 3) Leader asks everyone and finds consensus. Discuss: Which felt best? Which was fastest? Tradeoffs.

#### Assessment
- Successfully led a group activity
- Gave clear instructions that others could follow
- Listened to group members' input
- Handled an unexpected challenge while leading

### Unit 11: Peer Tutoring — Teaching Others (Weeks 9-18)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Teaching materials from other subjects
**Survival Relevance**: Teaching is how knowledge survives. A child who can teach what they know ensures that knowledge is preserved.

#### Learning Objectives
- Teach a younger child or struggling peer a skill they have mastered
- Adjust their teaching when the learner doesn't understand
- Show patience with a learner who is struggling
- Experience how teaching deepens their own understanding

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How to Teach**
- Activity: Teacher models teaching a simple skill slowly, step by step. "Watch how I break it into small steps. Watch how I check that you understand before moving on. Watch how I'm patient when you make a mistake."

**Lesson 3-6: Tutoring Practice**
- Activity: Pair each Year 3 child with a Year 1 child. The older child helps with a specific skill (letter recognition, counting, drawing). Weekly 15-minute sessions.
- Teacher observes and coaches tutors privately

**Lesson 7-10: Reflection**
- Activity: Tutors discuss: "What was hard about teaching? What worked? What did you try when they didn't understand the first time? Did you learn anything new yourself?"
- Key Concepts: The best way to learn something deeply is to teach it to someone else

#### Assessment
- Successfully tutored a younger child for 4+ sessions
- Demonstrated patience with a struggling learner
- Adjusted approach when initial teaching didn't work
- Can reflect on what they learned from teaching

### Unit 12: Community Meetings and Fairness (Weeks 19-28)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Meeting space, agenda chart
**Survival Relevance**: Participatory governance requires citizens who know how to listen respectfully, speak clearly, and disagree constructively

#### Learning Objectives
- Participate in a structured class meeting (raise hand, wait turn, speak clearly)
- Express an opinion with a reason ("I think ___ because ___")
- Disagree respectfully ("I see it differently because ___")
- Understand that fairness sometimes means different things for different people

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How Meetings Work**
- Activity: Hold a class meeting with structure: agenda, speaking turns, decisions. "Meetings are how communities make decisions together. Everyone gets a voice."

**Lesson 3-4: Speaking with Reasons**
- Activity: Practice: "I think we should ___ because ___." Not just opinions — opinions with reasons. "Why do you think that?" becomes a regular question.

**Lesson 5-6: Respectful Disagreement**
- Activity: Practice: "I hear what you're saying, but I think ___ because ___." Disagreement is not fighting. It's thinking together.

**Lesson 7-8: What Is Fair?**
- Activity: Scenario: Three children did different amounts of work. Should they get equal reward? Or should the hardest worker get more? Both sides have good arguments. Discuss.
- Key Concepts: Fair doesn't always mean equal. Fair means everyone gets what they need.

**Lesson 9-10: Cultural Awareness**
- Activity: "Not all families are the same. Not all backgrounds are the same. In our community, people come from different places and have different traditions. All are welcome. We learn from each other."
- Practice: Share something unique about your family or background

#### Assessment
- Participates in class meetings following the structure
- Expresses opinions with reasons
- Disagrees respectfully at least once
- Can explain that fairness might look different for different people

### Unit 13: Conflict Mediation (Weeks 29-36)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Mediation steps poster
**Survival Relevance**: A community member who can help others resolve conflicts is invaluable — they prevent small disputes from becoming community-splitting crises

#### Learning Objectives
- Help two other children resolve a dispute by mediating
- Follow mediation steps: 1) Ask each person to tell their side. 2) Repeat back what each person said. 3) Ask both what they want. 4) Help find a solution both accept.
- Stay neutral — don't take sides
- Know when a conflict is too big for peer mediation (get an adult)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Is a Mediator?**
- Activity: "A mediator is like a referee — they help both sides be heard and find a solution. They don't take sides. They help people talk to each other."

**Lesson 3-4: The Steps**
- Activity: Learn the 4 steps. Practice with teacher-created scenarios. Teacher plays mediator first, children observe. Then children practice with roleplay conflicts.

**Lesson 5-6: Staying Neutral**
- Activity: "The hardest part is not taking sides, especially if one person is your friend. A mediator treats both people fairly."
- Practice: Mediate a conflict where one "side" is your friend

**Lesson 7-8: Real Mediation**
- Activity: Volunteer mediators help resolve real (minor) classroom conflicts over the remaining weeks. Teacher supervises and coaches afterward.

#### Assessment
- Can follow the 4 mediation steps
- Demonstrates neutrality during mediation
- Successfully helps two peers reach a resolution
- Knows when to refer a conflict to an adult
