# Foundation Band (Grades K-2, Ages 5-8) — Health & Safety

## Overview
Health and safety education at this age is about building habits that prevent injury and illness. In a post-infrastructure community, children face hazards that modern children rarely encounter: open fires, untreated water, unfamiliar plants, livestock, and tools. Every lesson is practical and habit-forming. Children who internalize these rules save lives — starting with their own.

## Year 1: Keeping Myself Safe and Healthy

### Unit 1: Clean Hands, Clean Body (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Soap (or ash-and-fat soap), water basin, toothbrush or chew stick, towels/rags
**Survival Relevance**: Hygiene prevents the diseases that kill the most people in communities without modern sanitation — diarrhea, skin infections, respiratory illness

#### Learning Objectives
- Demonstrate proper handwashing technique (20+ seconds, all surfaces)
- List the 5 times you must wash hands (before eating, after toilet, after touching animals, after touching wounds, before cooking)
- Demonstrate basic dental care (brushing or chew stick, twice daily)
- Explain why bathing regularly prevents sickness

#### Teacher Notes
Handwashing alone prevents up to 50% of diarrheal disease. This is not an exaggeration — it is the single most impactful health intervention available. Make handwashing a ritual: before every meal, every day, no exceptions. If soap is scarce, ash and water work. The goal is automatic habit, not just knowledge.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Handwashing Song**
- Activity: Teach a handwashing song that lasts 20 seconds. Practice at the wash station. Wet hands, apply soap/ash, scrub palms, backs, between fingers, under nails, rinse, dry. Every child does it while singing.
- Key Concepts: Germs are too small to see but they cause sickness. Soap breaks germs apart.
- Practice: Handwashing before every meal for the rest of the year (and life)

**Lesson 3-4: Why We Bathe**
- Activity: Look at hands under magnifying glass (if available) before and after washing. Even if no magnifying glass, rub dirty hands on a white cloth — see the dirt. "Germs hide in dirt."
- Key Concepts: Skin infections come from dirty skin. Bathing removes germs and prevents rashes, boils, and infections.
- Practice: Establish bathing routine (frequency depends on water availability)

**Lesson 5-6: Teeth Matter**
- Activity: Demonstrate brushing with toothbrush or chew stick. Show proper technique — all surfaces, gums too. Discuss what happens without dental care (cavities, pain, infection, tooth loss).
- Key Concepts: Tooth decay is permanent. A lost tooth doesn't grow back (after adult teeth come in). Prevention is the only option.
- Practice: Morning and evening brushing routine

#### Assessment
- Demonstrates proper handwashing without prompting before meals
- Can name 5 times to wash hands
- Demonstrates tooth brushing technique
- Can explain why hygiene prevents sickness in simple terms

### Unit 2: My Body, My Safety (Weeks 7-12)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: None required
**Survival Relevance**: Personal safety awareness prevents abuse, injury, and dangerous situations

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify trusted adults in the community they can go to for help
- Understand body boundaries (private parts, appropriate vs inappropriate touch)
- Know what to do if they feel unsafe (tell a trusted adult, don't keep bad secrets)
- Demonstrate saying "no" firmly and getting help

#### Teacher Notes
This is sensitive content. Present it matter-of-factly and age-appropriately. Use correct body part names. The goal is empowerment, not fear. Children who know their body belongs to them and who have language to report problems are safer. In a community setting, children interact with many adults — this education is essential. If a child discloses abuse during this unit, follow your community's reporting protocol immediately. Never promise a child you won't tell — say "I will help you, and that might mean telling someone who can help more."

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: My Body Belongs to Me**
- Activity: "Your body is yours. Nobody has the right to touch you in a way that makes you uncomfortable." Practice saying "Stop, I don't like that" in a firm voice. Roleplay scenarios (someone grabs a toy, someone touches your hair without asking).
- Key Concepts: Consent, boundaries, body autonomy
- Practice: Partner practice saying "no" firmly

**Lesson 3-4: Safe Touch, Unsafe Touch**
- Activity: Discuss the difference between safe touches (high five, pat on back, hug from someone you trust) and touches that feel wrong. "If any touch makes you feel scared, confused, or uncomfortable, tell a trusted adult."
- Key Concepts: Private body parts, secrets vs. surprises (surprises are fun and temporary, bad secrets make you feel bad and someone tells you never to tell)

**Lesson 5-6: My Safety Team**
- Activity: Each child identifies 3 trusted adults they can go to if they feel unsafe. Draw them. Practice: "If something happens and I'm scared, I go to ___."
- Key Concepts: You are never in trouble for telling. It is always the right thing to tell.

#### Assessment
- Can name 3 trusted adults
- Can demonstrate saying "no" firmly
- Understands the difference between safe and unsafe touch
- Knows to tell a trusted adult if they feel unsafe

### Unit 3: Fire, Water, and Danger (Weeks 13-20)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Fire safety demonstration materials, water safety props
**Survival Relevance**: Fire and water are the two most common causes of accidental death in children in communities without modern safety infrastructure

#### Learning Objectives
- State fire safety rules (stay away from open fires, never play with fire, stop-drop-roll)
- State water safety rules (never swim alone, don't drink untreated water, stay away from fast-moving water)
- Identify common poisons (unknown plants, berries, mushrooms, cleaning chemicals)
- Demonstrate what to do in an emergency (call for help, go to a safe place, find an adult)

#### Teacher Notes
Children in survival communities are around open cooking fires, wood stoves, candles, and untreated water sources daily. These are not theoretical dangers — they are constant risks. Repeat these rules frequently. Post visual reminders. Make the rules into songs or chants.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Fire Rules**
- Activity: Visit the cooking area. Show the safe distance. "This line is the safe zone. Children stay behind this line." Demonstrate stop-drop-roll on the ground. Everyone practices.
- Key Concepts: Fire is a tool, not a toy. If your clothes catch fire: stop, drop, roll. Never run.
- Practice: Weekly fire safety drill for the rest of the year

**Lesson 3-4: Water Rules**
- Activity: Visit the community water source. "This is where our water comes from. Water from here must be treated before drinking. NEVER drink water from a stream, puddle, or pond."
- Key Concepts: Dirty water makes you very sick. Always drink treated water. Never swim alone.
- Practice: "Where do I get safe drinking water?" quiz

**Lesson 5-6: Don't Eat That!**
- Activity: Walk through the area with an agriculture team member. Show plants that look tempting but are dangerous. "NEVER eat any plant, berry, or mushroom unless an adult who knows plants says it is safe."
- Key Concepts: Many beautiful plants are poisonous. Even adults sometimes can't tell. When in doubt, don't eat it.
- Rule: "If you didn't grow it and you don't know it, don't put it in your mouth."

**Lesson 7-8: What to Do in an Emergency**
- Activity: Practice the emergency plan. "If something bad happens: 1) Stay calm. 2) Call for help (yell 'HELP!'). 3) Go to a safe place. 4) Find an adult." Practice each step.
- Key Concepts: Panicking makes things worse. Having a plan makes things better.
- Practice: Emergency drills monthly

#### Assessment
- Can state 3 fire safety rules
- Can demonstrate stop-drop-roll
- Can state the water safety rule about drinking water
- Knows not to eat unknown plants
- Can demonstrate emergency response steps

### Unit 4: Food and Sleep (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Examples of community foods, simple drawings of food groups
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding nutrition prevents malnutrition and deficiency diseases. Understanding sleep prevents fatigue-related injuries and supports immune health.

#### Learning Objectives
- Name basic food groups using available community foods (energy foods, body-building foods, protective foods)
- Understand that different foods do different things for the body
- Explain why sleep is important (body repairs, brain grows, energy restores)
- Demonstrate good sleep habits (consistent bedtime, calming routine)

#### Teacher Notes
Don't use the standard food pyramid — it assumes grocery store access. Instead use three groups: Energy foods (grains, roots, fats), Body-building foods (meat, eggs, beans, dairy), and Protective foods (fruits, vegetables). Use whatever foods your community actually has. If food is scarce, be sensitive — don't make children feel bad about what they eat. Focus on "when we have choices, here's how to choose well."

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Energy Foods**
- Activity: Show examples of community energy foods (bread, rice, potatoes, corn, oil). "These give your body energy to run, play, and work."
- Practice: Children identify energy foods from their meals

**Lesson 3-4: Body-Building and Protective Foods**
- Activity: Show protein foods and fruits/vegetables. "Body-building foods help you grow and get strong. Protective foods keep you from getting sick."
- Practice: Sort food items into three groups

**Lesson 5-6: Building a Meal**
- Activity: "A good meal has something from each group." Practice building balanced meals from available foods.
- Key Concepts: Variety is important. Eating only one food makes you sick over time.

**Lesson 7-8: Sleep is Medicine**
- Activity: Discuss how everyone feels after a bad night's sleep vs. a good one. "Sleep is when your body fixes itself. Children need lots of sleep because you're still growing."
- Key Concepts: Consistent bedtime, dark and quiet sleeping area, calming routine before bed
- Practice: Each child draws their bedtime routine

#### Assessment
- Can sort community foods into three groups
- Can explain what each food group does
- Can explain why sleep is important
- Can describe a good bedtime routine

## Year 2: Helping Myself and Others

### Unit 5: Boo-Boos and Owies — Basic First Aid (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Clean cloth strips for bandages, clean water, soap
**Survival Relevance**: In a community without a hospital nearby, basic first aid knowledge in every person — even children — saves lives and prevents minor injuries from becoming major ones

#### Learning Objectives
- Clean a small cut or scrape (wash with clean water and soap, cover with clean cloth)
- Know when to get adult help (bleeding that won't stop, burns, broken bones, bites, head injuries)
- Apply a simple bandage to a small wound
- Identify and respond to bee stings (remove stinger, cold cloth, tell adult, watch for allergic reaction signs)
- Know the difference between "I can handle this" and "I need an adult"

#### Teacher Notes
Children at this age can absolutely learn basic wound care. The key rule: CLEAN IT AND COVER IT. Every wound that gets infected started as a wound that wasn't cleaned. Teach children to treat small injuries themselves (scrapes, minor cuts) and to GET HELP for anything serious. The triage concept at this age is simple: "Is it small and I know what to do? → Handle it. Is it big, scary, or I'm not sure? → Get an adult NOW."

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Clean It and Cover It**
- Activity: Demonstrate wound cleaning on a practice "wound" (draw a scrape on an arm with marker). Wash with clean water and soap. Pat dry. Cover with clean cloth.
- Key Concepts: Dirt in a wound causes infection. Clean water and soap are the best medicine for small wounds.
- Practice: Partners practice on each other's "pretend wounds"

**Lesson 3-4: When to Get Help**
- Activity: Show pictures/descriptions of injuries. For each one: "Handle it yourself, or get an adult?" Small scrape → handle it. Deep cut with lots of blood → GET HELP. Burn → GET HELP. Splinter → try, but get help if it won't come out.
- Practice: Sorting game — cards with injuries, sort into "I can handle" vs "Get an adult"

**Lesson 5-6: Bandaging**
- Activity: Practice wrapping a cloth bandage around a hand, finger, knee. Not too tight (check: can you still wiggle fingers?). Secure with a knot or tuck.
- Practice: Bandage relay race (teams race to properly bandage a "wound")

**Lesson 7-8: Burns and Stings**
- Activity: Burns: "Cool water for 10 minutes. Do NOT use butter, oil, or toothpaste. Cover loosely. Tell an adult." Bee stings: "Scrape out the stinger (don't squeeze it). Cold cloth. Tell an adult. If the person can't breathe or swells up a lot → EMERGENCY."
- Key Concepts: Burns and stings are common. Knowing what to do prevents panic.

**Lesson 9-10: First Aid Review**
- Activity: Set up stations. Children rotate through and demonstrate: wound cleaning, bandaging, burn response, when-to-get-help decisions.
- Assessment activity: "First Aid Badge" — children who demonstrate all skills earn recognition

#### Assessment
- Can clean and bandage a small wound properly
- Can correctly identify when to get adult help
- Can describe burn first aid steps
- Can describe bee sting response

### Unit 6: Don't Spread It — Preventing Illness (Weeks 11-18)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: None required
**Survival Relevance**: In communities without antibiotics and hospitals, preventing disease spread is more important than treating disease. Children are primary vectors for contagious illness.

#### Learning Objectives
- Demonstrate covering coughs and sneezes (into elbow, not hands)
- Explain why sick people should stay home
- Identify signs of common illness (fever = hot forehead, cough, runny nose, vomiting, diarrhea)
- Understand that germs spread through touch, coughs, and dirty water

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Cover Your Cough**
- Activity: Spray water from a spray bottle to show how far a cough travels. "Germs fly through the air when you cough. Cover your cough in your elbow — not your hand, because then your germs go everywhere you touch."
- Practice: Cough-in-elbow practice until it's automatic

**Lesson 3-4: When to Stay Home**
- Activity: "If you feel hot, have a bad cough, are throwing up, or have diarrhea — tell an adult and stay home. You are not in trouble. You are protecting your friends."
- Key Concepts: Sick people who come to school make others sick. Resting helps you get better faster.

**Lesson 5-6: How Germs Travel**
- Activity: Glitter germ game — put glitter on one child's hands. They shake hands with another, who shakes with another. Check everyone's hands. "See how germs spread through touching?"
- Key Concepts: Germs are invisible but real. They spread through contact.

**Lesson 7-8: Clean Water Keeps Us Healthy**
- Activity: Review water safety from Year 1. Add: diarrhea is usually caused by dirty water. "Always drink from the clean water supply. If you're not sure the water is clean, don't drink it — ask an adult."
- Practice: Trace the community water system again

#### Assessment
- Demonstrates cough/sneeze covering automatically
- Can list 4 signs of illness
- Understands why sick people should stay isolated
- Can explain how germs spread

### Unit 7: Feelings Are Real (Weeks 19-26)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Drawings of facial expressions, feeling words cards
**Survival Relevance**: Emotional regulation prevents conflicts, supports mental health, and builds the resilience children need to thrive in a challenging environment

#### Learning Objectives
- Name at least 8 feelings (happy, sad, angry, scared, frustrated, worried, proud, calm)
- Recognize feelings in themselves and others
- Use at least 2 calming strategies (deep breathing, counting to 10, taking a break, talking to someone)
- Ask for help when feelings are too big to handle alone

#### Teacher Notes
Children in post-crisis environments may carry significant trauma. This unit is not therapy, but it gives children language for their inner experience. A child who can say "I'm scared" is less likely to lash out. A child who knows how to calm down is more resilient. Never dismiss feelings: "You shouldn't feel that way" is always wrong. Instead: "I can see you're feeling angry. That makes sense. Let's figure out what to do about it."

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Naming Feelings**
- Activity: Show face drawings with different expressions. Name each feeling. "Everyone has these feelings. All of them are normal."
- Practice: Daily check-in — "How are you feeling today?" Children point to or name their feeling.

**Lesson 3-4: Feelings in My Body**
- Activity: "Where do you feel angry? (Tight fists, hot face, fast heartbeat.) Where do you feel scared? (Tummy ache, shaky.) Where do you feel happy? (Smile, warm chest.)"
- Key Concepts: Feelings live in your body. Noticing them helps you manage them.

**Lesson 5-6: Calming Strategies**
- Activity: Teach and practice: 1) Belly breathing (hand on tummy, breathe in — tummy goes out, breathe out — tummy goes in, 5 times). 2) Count to 10 slowly. 3) Take a walk. 4) Talk to someone.
- Practice: Use calming strategies during real moments throughout the day

**Lesson 7-8: Asking for Help**
- Activity: "Sometimes feelings are too big to handle alone. That's when you ask for help. Asking for help is brave, not weak." Practice: "I'm feeling ___ and I need help."
- Key Concepts: Everyone needs help sometimes. Strong people ask for help.

#### Assessment
- Can name 8+ feelings
- Can identify their own feeling during daily check-in
- Can demonstrate 2 calming strategies
- Has practiced asking for help at least once

### Unit 8: Sun, Animals, and Outdoor Safety (Weeks 27-34)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Hats, water bottles
**Survival Relevance**: Children in survival communities spend significant time outdoors. Sun exposure, animal encounters, and outdoor hazards are daily realities.

#### Learning Objectives
- Know sun safety rules (hat, shade during midday, drink water, recognize sunburn)
- Know animal safety rules (approach livestock calmly, never corner an animal, leave wildlife alone, dog bite prevention)
- Recognize and avoid common outdoor hazards (unstable structures, sharp objects, deep holes)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Sun Is Powerful**
- Activity: Demonstrate how the sun heats things — dark cloth gets hot fast, light cloth stays cooler. "Your skin is like that cloth. Too much sun burns your skin."
- Rules: Wear a hat. Stay in shade during the hottest part of the day. Drink water often. If skin turns red and hurts, that's sunburn — get into shade.

**Lesson 3-4: Animals Are Not Toys**
- Activity: Visit livestock areas with an agriculture team member. "These animals help us, but they can hurt you if scared. Always approach slowly and calmly. Never run at animals, scream near them, or corner them."
- Rules: Never touch a wild animal. If a dog growls, stand still like a tree — don't run. Tell an adult about animal bites immediately.

**Lesson 5-6: Watching Where You Go**
- Activity: Walk through the community identifying hazards — old buildings, sharp metal, deep holes, unstable fences. "These are real dangers. How do we stay safe around them?"
- Practice: Children create a hazard map of the community

#### Assessment
- Can state 3 sun safety rules
- Can demonstrate how to approach livestock
- Knows what to do if a dog growls
- Can identify 3 hazards in the community

## Year 3: Growing Up Safe

### Unit 9: Recognizing Infection (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: None required (use descriptions and drawings)
**Survival Relevance**: Untreated infections kill. A child who recognizes the signs of infection and reports them promptly can prevent a minor wound from becoming life-threatening.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify the 4 signs of infection: redness, swelling, heat, and increasing pain around a wound
- Know to tell an adult immediately if these signs appear
- Understand that infection means germs got into the wound and are making it worse
- Know basic wound care review (clean, cover, check daily)

#### Teacher Notes
In a post-infrastructure setting, even minor infections can become serious without antibiotics. The most important skill is RECOGNITION AND REPORTING. A child who says "my cut is red and hot" gives the medical team hours of lead time. Emphasize: checking wounds daily is as important as brushing teeth.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Four Warning Signs**
- Activity: Draw a wound. Around it, draw the 4 signs: red spreading color, puffy swelling, wavy heat lines, lightning bolts for pain. "If your cut looks like this, tell an adult RIGHT NOW."
- Practice: Daily wound check — children with any bandage check it and report to teacher

**Lesson 3-4: Clean It, Cover It, Check It**
- Activity: Full wound care review with the added step: check it every day. "Day 1: clean and cover. Day 2: look at it. Is it getting better or worse? Day 3: still check."
- Key Concepts: Wounds should get a little better each day. If they get worse, something is wrong.

**Lesson 5-6: Getting Help Fast**
- Activity: Roleplay scenarios — "Your brother has a cut on his leg. It's been 2 days. The skin around it is red and puffy and hot. What do you do?" Practice going to the medical team or a trusted adult.
- Key Concept: You might save someone's life by noticing infection early.

#### Assessment
- Can name the 4 signs of infection
- Can explain the clean-cover-check routine
- Demonstrates appropriate urgency in reporting infection signs

### Unit 10: CPR and Choking Awareness (Weeks 7-12)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: A cushion or stuffed object for practice
**Survival Relevance**: Children won't perform CPR at this age, but knowing what it is and how to get help fast makes them effective responders

#### Learning Objectives
- Know what CPR is (pushing on the chest to keep blood moving when someone's heart stops)
- Know the universal choking sign (hands on throat)
- Know to get help IMMEDIATELY if someone is not breathing, not responding, or choking
- Know NOT to slap a choking person on the back unless trained (get adult help instead)

#### Teacher Notes
We are NOT teaching children to perform CPR — they are too small and lack the training. We ARE teaching them to RECOGNIZE an emergency and GET HELP FAST. Seconds matter in these situations. A child who runs for help immediately instead of standing frozen in fear is invaluable.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Is CPR?**
- Activity: "Sometimes a person's heart stops beating. CPR means pushing on their chest in a rhythm to keep blood moving until help arrives. Adults and older kids learn to do this. Your job right now is to GET HELP FAST."
- Practice: "If you see someone who isn't moving and isn't breathing: YELL FOR HELP. Run to the nearest adult."

**Lesson 3-4: Choking — What to Watch For**
- Activity: Demonstrate the universal choking sign. "If someone grabs their throat, can't talk, can't cough, and their face turns red or blue — they are choking. GET AN ADULT IMMEDIATELY."
- Key Concepts: Choking is when food or an object blocks the airway. It is very dangerous. Speed matters.

**Lesson 5-6: Emergency Speed Drills**
- Activity: Practice running for help. Time each child. "If someone is not breathing, how fast can you get to the medical area?" Make it a practiced response, not a theoretical concept.
- Practice: Monthly emergency drills for the rest of the year

#### Assessment
- Can explain what CPR is in simple terms
- Can demonstrate the choking sign
- Can demonstrate emergency response: yell for help, run to nearest adult
- Understands urgency — these situations require immediate action

### Unit 11: Eating Well for Our Community (Weeks 13-20)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Community food examples, simple meal planning cards
**Survival Relevance**: Nutrition planning is a practical life skill that directly impacts community health outcomes

#### Learning Objectives
- Help plan a balanced meal using available foods
- Understand that food needs change (growing children need more, sick people need different food, workers need more energy food)
- Recognize signs of not eating enough (tiredness, dizziness, getting sick often, not growing)
- Understand seasonal eating (different foods available at different times)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Meal Planning Helper**
- Activity: Review the three food groups from Year 1. Now apply: "Help me plan dinner. We have [list available foods]. How do we make sure we have energy food, body-building food, and protective food?"
- Practice: Each child plans a meal and explains their choices

**Lesson 3-4: Different People, Different Needs**
- Activity: "A person working in the fields all day needs more energy food. A sick person needs lots of water and easy-to-digest food. A growing child needs lots of body-building food."
- Key Concepts: Nutrition isn't one-size-fits-all

**Lesson 5-6: Seasonal Eating**
- Activity: What foods are available now? What will be available in 3 months? "Our food changes with the seasons. We eat what grows. That's how humans have always eaten."
- Practice: Create a simple seasonal food calendar

**Lesson 7-8: Signs of Not Enough**
- Activity: "If someone is always tired, gets sick a lot, isn't growing, or feels dizzy — they might not be getting enough food or the right kind of food. Tell an adult."
- Key Concepts: Nutritional deficiency is preventable if caught early

#### Assessment
- Can plan a balanced meal from available foods
- Can explain why different people need different amounts
- Can identify 3 signs of poor nutrition
- Can explain seasonal eating

### Unit 12: Growing Up — My Body Is Changing (Weeks 21-26)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Simple anatomical drawings (age-appropriate)
**Survival Relevance**: Early, accurate information about puberty prevents fear and misinformation, and prepares children for the more comprehensive health education in Building Band

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that bodies change as people grow up — this is normal and healthy
- Know the basic changes that will happen (getting taller, body shape changes, hair growth, skin changes)
- Understand that changes happen at different times for different people — all timelines are normal
- Know who to talk to with questions about body changes

#### Teacher Notes
This is a PREVIEW, not a comprehensive puberty unit (that comes in Building Band Grade 4). The goal is to normalize body changes before they happen, so children are not frightened. Keep it simple and factual. Avoid: giggling, embarrassment, making it weird. Model calm, matter-of-fact tone. Both boys and girls learn about all changes — this is not separated by gender.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Bodies Grow and Change**
- Activity: "Look at a baby, a child your age, a teenager, and an adult. Bodies change as you grow. This is called growing up, and it happens to everyone."
- Key Concepts: Growth is normal. Everyone goes through it. It takes years.

**Lesson 3-4: What Changes?**
- Activity: Simple overview — "As you get older: you get taller, your body shape changes, hair grows in new places, your skin might get oily, and you might sweat more."
- Key Concepts: These changes are signs that your body is working correctly. Nothing is wrong.

**Lesson 5-6: Everyone's Timeline Is Different**
- Activity: "Some kids start changing at 8 or 9, some not until 13 or 14. Both are normal. There is no 'right' time. Never tease someone about their body."
- Key Concepts: Comparison is not helpful. Everyone's body has its own schedule.
- Resource: "If you have questions about your body, talk to [trusted adult, medical team member, teacher]."

#### Assessment
- Can state that body changes during growing up are normal
- Can name 3 body changes that happen during puberty
- Knows that different timelines are normal
- Knows who to ask with questions about body changes
