# Foundation Band (Grades K-2, Ages 5-8) — Arts & Music

## Overview
Arts are not a luxury — they are a survival skill. Communities without art, music, and storytelling lose morale, cultural identity, and the creative thinking that solves novel problems. Arts also produce tangible trade goods: pottery, textiles, instruments, and decorative items. This curriculum develops creativity, fine motor skills, cultural literacy, and the ability to express ideas beyond words. Every child sings, draws, builds, and performs.

## Year 1: Making and Moving

### Unit 1: Rhythm and Singing (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Hands (for clapping), voices, sticks, stones, any containers that can serve as drums
**Survival Relevance**: Music builds community bonds, preserves cultural knowledge through songs, maintains morale in hard times, and develops mathematical pattern recognition

#### Learning Objectives
- Keep a steady beat by clapping, tapping, or stepping
- Echo simple rhythm patterns (clap back what the teacher claps)
- Sing 5+ community songs from memory
- Understand that music is made of patterns (rhythm = patterns in time)

#### Teacher Notes
You do not need to be a musician. You need to be willing to sing, clap, and move. Every culture in human history has had music. If you can keep a beat and carry a tune even roughly, you can teach this unit. Use songs your community already knows. If you don't know songs, make them up — work songs, counting songs, alphabet songs, safety rule songs. Music is how humans remember things. A rule set to a song sticks forever.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Heartbeat**
- Activity: "Put your hand on your chest. Feel that? That's a beat. Your body is already making music." Clap along with the heartbeat. Speed it up. Slow it down. "Music starts with a beat."
- Practice: Clap a steady beat together. Try walking to the beat. March to the beat.

**Lesson 3-4: Echo Clapping**
- Activity: Teacher claps a simple 4-beat pattern. Children echo it back. Start simple (clap-clap-clap-clap). Get more complex (clap-clap-pause-clap, clap-tap knees-clap-tap knees).
- Key Concepts: Rhythm is a pattern that repeats. Music is made of patterns.

**Lesson 5-6: Our Community Songs**
- Activity: Learn 3-5 songs the community values — work songs, gathering songs, celebration songs, bedtime songs. If the community doesn't have songs yet, create them together.
- Practice: Sing daily. Before meals, at the end of the school day, during transitions.

**Lesson 7-8: Echo Singing**
- Activity: Teacher sings a phrase, children sing it back. Start with simple melodies. This builds pitch matching and musical memory.
- Key Concepts: Everyone can sing. Some voices are high, some low — all are needed.

#### Assessment
- Can keep a steady beat for 30+ seconds
- Can echo back a 4-beat rhythm pattern
- Can sing 5 community songs from memory
- Participates willingly in group singing

### Unit 2: Drawing from Observation (Weeks 9-16)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Any drawing implement (charcoal, pencil, chalk, burnt stick), any drawing surface (paper, bark, smooth stone, dirt)
**Survival Relevance**: Observational drawing builds the careful seeing needed for plant identification, animal tracking, map-making, and scientific observation. It is also the foundation of technical drawing for construction and engineering.

#### Learning Objectives
- Draw a recognizable representation of a real object (plant, animal, tool, person)
- Use observation rather than imagination — draw what you SEE, not what you think you see
- Include details that distinguish one object from another
- Begin a nature sketchbook

#### Teacher Notes
This is not "art class" in the decorative sense — this is training the eye and hand to record what is observed. Scientists, engineers, and medics all need this skill. "I can't draw" is not an acceptable excuse — drawing is a trainable skill, not a talent. If you can write letters, you can draw. Start with simple objects and build complexity. Never tell a child their drawing is wrong — ask "What did you see?" and help them look more carefully.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Really Looking**
- Activity: Place a simple object (a leaf, a stone, a cup) in front of each child. "Look at it for one full minute. What do you notice? Shape? Color? Texture? Shadows?" Then draw it. Compare drawing to object. "What did you miss?"
- Key Concepts: Drawing is seeing. The better you look, the better you draw.

**Lesson 3-4: Shapes in Everything**
- Activity: "Everything is made of simple shapes. A tree trunk is a rectangle. The leaf crown is a circle. A house is a square with a triangle on top." Practice drawing complex objects by breaking them into simple shapes first.
- Practice: Draw 3 objects using the shape-first method

**Lesson 5-6: Nature Sketching**
- Activity: Go outside. Each child picks one plant and draws it as carefully as possible. Count the petals. Notice the leaf shape. How many branches?
- Begin nature sketchbook: one drawing per week for the rest of the year
- Connection: This directly supports science (plant identification) and agriculture

**Lesson 7-8: Drawing People and Animals**
- Activity: Draw a partner. Focus on proportions — head, body, arms, legs. Draw a community animal. Focus on what makes it recognizable.
- Key Concepts: Drawing people and animals requires practice. Start simple, add detail.

#### Assessment
- Can draw a recognizable representation of a real object
- Drawing includes specific observed details (not generic)
- Has maintained a nature sketchbook with 4+ entries
- Uses shapes to construct complex drawings

### Unit 3: Color and Natural Pigments (Weeks 17-22)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Berries, charcoal, clay, turmeric, onion skins, beet juice, walnut husks — whatever natural colorants are available locally. Grinding stones, water, containers.
**Survival Relevance**: Natural dye and pigment knowledge is essential for textile work, pottery decoration, and producing trade goods. Color chemistry connects to later chemistry education.

#### Learning Objectives
- Create pigments from at least 3 natural materials
- Mix colors (primary combinations) to make new colors
- Apply pigments to surfaces using fingers, sticks, brushes made from grass/hair
- Understand that different materials produce different colors

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Making Colors from Nature**
- Activity: Collect natural materials. Crush berries for red/purple. Grind charcoal for black. Mix clay with water for brown/tan. Boil onion skins for yellow. Each child makes at least 2 pigments.
- Key Concepts: Color comes from nature. Humans have been making paint from nature for 40,000+ years (cave paintings).

**Lesson 3-4: Mixing Colors**
- Activity: "Red + blue = ?" Mix pigments to discover new colors. Yellow + blue = green. Red + yellow = orange. Document results.
- Practice: Create a color chart showing all the colors they can make

**Lesson 5-6: Painting with Nature**
- Activity: Using homemade pigments and brushes (grass bundles, feathers, sticks with frayed ends, fingers), paint a picture on bark, paper, or stone.
- Final project: Each child creates a painted piece for display

#### Assessment
- Can create pigments from 3+ natural materials
- Can mix primary colors to create secondary colors
- Can apply pigments to a surface to create a recognizable image
- Can explain where each color comes from

### Unit 4: Storytelling through Pictures and Movement (Weeks 23-32)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Drawing supplies, open space for movement
**Survival Relevance**: Visual storytelling preserves information where literacy hasn't yet developed. Dance and movement build body awareness, coordination, and group synchronization.

#### Learning Objectives
- Tell a story through a sequence of drawings (comic strip format)
- Retell a community story through movement and dance
- Participate in group movement activities that require coordination
- Understand that stories can be told without words

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Picture Stories**
- Activity: Draw a 4-panel story (like a comic strip). Beginning, something happens, what they do about it, how it ends. Tell the story to a partner using only the pictures.
- Key Concepts: Before writing, humans told stories through pictures. Cave paintings are picture stories.

**Lesson 3-4: Story Murals**
- Activity: Small groups create a large picture story on a big surface. Each person draws one part of the story. Together, it tells a complete tale.

**Lesson 5-6: Movement and Expression**
- Activity: "Show me how a tree moves in the wind. Show me how a rabbit moves. Show me how rain falls." Use body movement to express concepts.
- Practice: Freeze dance, mirror games (copy partner's movements), follow-the-leader with creative movements

**Lesson 7-8: Group Dance**
- Activity: Learn a simple community dance (or create one). Practice until everyone knows the steps. Perform for another class or for families.
- Key Concepts: Dance is organized movement. It requires listening, watching, and moving together.

**Lesson 9-10: Putting It Together**
- Activity: Combine picture story, movement, and narration into a simple performance. Groups tell a story using pictures as backdrops, movement as action, and words as narration.

#### Assessment
- Can tell a story through a sequence of 4+ pictures
- Participates in group movement activities
- Can perform a simple dance with the group
- Can combine visual and movement storytelling

## Year 2: Creating and Performing

### Unit 5: Making Instruments (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Containers with lids, dried beans/rice/stones, sticks, string/twine, reeds/bamboo, boxes, rubber bands, clay
**Survival Relevance**: Instrument-building develops engineering skills, fine motor control, and understanding of acoustics. Instruments are valuable trade goods and essential for community cultural life.

#### Learning Objectives
- Build at least 3 different types of simple instruments (percussion, wind, string)
- Understand how each instrument produces sound (vibration)
- Play their instruments in a group rhythm
- Understand that sound is vibration and different materials vibrate differently

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Drums and Shakers**
- Activity: Build drums from containers with stretched material over the top (leather, fabric, thick leaf). Build shakers from sealed containers with small items inside. "Hit the drum — what vibrates?"
- Key Concepts: Sound = vibration. Bigger drums = lower sound. Smaller drums = higher sound.

**Lesson 3-4: Wind Instruments**
- Activity: Build simple flutes from reeds or bamboo (cut a tube, make finger holes). Build panpipes from tubes of different lengths. Blow across the top. "The air inside vibrates."
- Key Concepts: Longer tube = lower pitch. Shorter tube = higher pitch.

**Lesson 5-6: String Instruments**
- Activity: Build a simple string instrument from a box and rubber bands (or string stretched over a container). Pluck the strings. "The string vibrates."
- Key Concepts: Tight string = higher pitch. Loose string = lower pitch. Thicker string = lower pitch.

**Lesson 7-8: Playing Together**
- Activity: Everyone brings their instruments. Teacher leads a group rhythm. Drums keep the beat. Shakers add texture. Flutes and strings add melody.
- Practice: Daily group music time using student-built instruments

**Lesson 9-10: Instrument Exhibition**
- Activity: Each child presents their best instrument. Explain how they built it and how it makes sound. Demonstrate playing it.

#### Assessment
- Built at least 3 working instruments of different types
- Can explain how each instrument produces sound
- Can play in time with a group rhythm
- Can explain the relationship between instrument features and pitch

### Unit 6: Painting and Printmaking (Weeks 11-18)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Natural pigments from Year 1, carved potatoes/clay stamps, leaves, textured objects, flat surfaces
**Survival Relevance**: Printmaking is the predecessor of printing — understanding how to reproduce images is connected to knowledge preservation. Textile printing is a trade skill.

#### Learning Objectives
- Create a stamp from a carved potato, clay, or found object
- Produce multiple identical prints from a single stamp
- Create patterns using repeated printed shapes
- Understand that printmaking allows reproducing images without redrawing each one

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Found Object Printing**
- Activity: Dip leaves, bark, textured fabrics in pigment. Press onto paper or cloth. Observe the patterns that emerge.

**Lesson 3-4: Carved Stamps**
- Activity: Carve a simple shape into a potato half or clay block. Apply pigment. Press. Repeat. "You carved it once, but you can print it many times."
- Connection: "This is how printing was invented — carve once, print many times."

**Lesson 5-6: Pattern Design**
- Activity: Create a repeating pattern using stamps. Rows of shapes. Alternating designs. "Patterns make cloth, pottery, and art beautiful."
- Connection: Textile design — these patterns could decorate cloth or pottery

**Lesson 7-8: Printmaking Project**
- Activity: Each child designs and prints a multi-color composition using stamps and natural pigments.

#### Assessment
- Can create a working stamp
- Can produce clean, repeated prints
- Can create a repeating pattern
- Understands the connection between printmaking and printing

### Unit 7: Group Performance (Weeks 19-26)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Costumes (whatever is available), props, instruments
**Survival Relevance**: Performance builds confidence, public speaking skills, memorization, and community bonding. Community theater is one of the oldest human traditions.

#### Learning Objectives
- Memorize and perform a role in a group song, play, or skit
- Speak clearly and loudly enough for an audience to hear
- Cooperate with others to create a performance
- Experience performing for community members

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Choosing Our Show**
- Activity: Class decides on a performance — a song collection, a short play, a retelling of a community story, or a combination. Plan together.

**Lesson 3-6: Rehearsal**
- Activity: Assign roles. Learn lines/songs. Practice staging. Teacher directs but children contribute ideas. Run through multiple times.
- Key Concepts: Performance takes practice. Even professionals rehearse.

**Lesson 7-8: Performance!**
- Activity: Perform for families, other classes, or the community. Celebrate afterward.
- Debrief: "How did it feel? What went well? What would you do differently?"

#### Assessment
- Memorized and performed their part
- Spoke/sang clearly for the audience
- Cooperated with the group throughout rehearsal and performance
- Participated in the performance

### Unit 8: Pattern Design and Weaving Introduction (Weeks 27-34)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Strips of paper or fabric, simple frame loom (sticks and string), natural materials for weaving
**Survival Relevance**: Textile production is essential for clothing, blankets, and trade goods. Pattern design is mathematical thinking applied to art.

#### Learning Objectives
- Create a repeating pattern using 2-3 colors or materials
- Weave a simple mat or fabric square on a frame loom
- Understand over-under weaving pattern
- Connect pattern work to mathematics (repeating sequences)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Paper Weaving**
- Activity: Cut strips of paper. Weave them over-under through a slotted base. Two colors create a checkerboard pattern.
- Key Concepts: Weaving = systematic over-under pattern. This is how all cloth is made.

**Lesson 3-4: Frame Loom Setup**
- Activity: Build a simple frame loom from sticks and string. Stretch warp threads (vertical). Begin weaving weft threads (horizontal) over-under.

**Lesson 5-6: Color Patterns**
- Activity: Choose 2-3 colors of material. Weave in a pattern (2 red, 1 blue, 2 red, 1 blue). "You are designing fabric. This is how cloth patterns are made."

**Lesson 7-8: Finished Piece**
- Activity: Complete a woven square. Remove from loom. Display or give as a gift.
- Connection: "People have been weaving for at least 10,000 years. You are doing what your ancestors did."

#### Assessment
- Can describe and create a repeating color pattern
- Can weave using the over-under technique
- Completed a woven piece on a frame loom
- Can explain the connection between weaving and cloth production

### Unit 9: Folk Tales and Oral Tradition (Weeks 35-40)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: None required
**Survival Relevance**: Oral tradition is how knowledge was preserved for 200,000 years before writing. Storytelling preserves cultural identity, teaches moral lessons, and entertains.

#### Learning Objectives
- Retell a community story with correct plot and details
- Tell an original story with beginning, middle, and end
- Use voice (volume, speed, tone) and gesture to make stories engaging
- Understand that oral tradition was humanity's first knowledge preservation system

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Listening to Stories**
- Activity: Community elders or adults tell traditional stories. Children listen and then retell. "How close did you get? What details did you remember?"

**Lesson 3-4: Story Craft**
- Activity: "A good story has: a character, a problem, attempts to solve it, and a resolution." Each child creates an original story using this structure.

**Lesson 5-6: Performance Storytelling**
- Activity: Practice telling stories with expression. Vary your voice: quiet for scary parts, loud for exciting parts, slow for sad parts, fast for action.
- Final performance: Each child tells a story to the class.

#### Assessment
- Can retell a heard story with key details intact
- Can tell an original story with clear structure
- Uses voice variation to engage listeners
- Understands that oral tradition predates writing

## Year 3: Creating and Documenting

### Unit 10: Songwriting and Composition (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Instruments (built in Year 2), writing supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Creating new songs preserves community experiences and builds creative problem-solving skills

#### Learning Objectives
- Write original song lyrics on a given topic
- Create a simple melody for their lyrics
- Perform their song for others
- Understand song structure (verse, chorus)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How Songs Work**
- Activity: Analyze a known song. "Where does it repeat? That's the chorus. The parts that change are verses. Most songs: verse-chorus-verse-chorus."

**Lesson 3-4: Writing Lyrics**
- Activity: Choose a topic (our community, a season, a feeling, a story). Write lyrics that tell about it. Make them rhyme if possible, but meaning matters more than rhyme.

**Lesson 5-6: Adding a Melody**
- Activity: Hum different melodies over your words. Find one that feels right. Teach it to a partner.

**Lesson 7-8: Performance**
- Activity: Each child or pair performs their original song. Class learns 1-2 of the best for the community songbook.

### Unit 11: Sculpture and Community Mural (Weeks 9-18)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Clay, found materials, large flat surface for mural, natural pigments
**Survival Relevance**: Pottery is a critical survival skill (storage vessels, cooking pots, water containers). Sculpture develops 3D spatial thinking needed for construction and engineering.

#### Learning Objectives
- Create a 3D sculpture from clay or found materials
- Collaborate on a community mural
- Understand functional pottery (bowls, cups, containers)
- Work with clay: wedge, shape, smooth, dry

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Working with Clay**
- Activity: Dig or collect clay. Learn to wedge it (remove air bubbles). Shape basic forms: pinch pot, coil pot, slab construction. Let dry. If kiln available, fire. If not, air-dry.
- Connection: "Pottery is one of humanity's oldest technologies. It lets us store water, cook food, and preserve grain."

**Lesson 5-6: Found Material Sculpture**
- Activity: Create sculptures from sticks, stones, wire, fabric, and other found materials. Express an idea in 3D form.

**Lesson 7-10: Community Mural**
- Activity: Class designs and paints a mural on a community wall, board, or fabric. Theme: something about the community — its values, its story, its hopes. This is a permanent contribution to community life.

### Unit 12: Dramatic Performance (Weeks 19-26)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Costumes, props, staging area
**Survival Relevance**: Theater develops confidence, memorization, public speaking, empathy (playing a character), and community entertainment

#### Learning Objectives
- Perform a role in a full short play (10-15 minutes)
- Memorize lines and blocking (stage positions)
- Support fellow performers and handle mistakes gracefully
- Contribute to production elements (costumes, props, staging)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Choosing and Adapting a Play**
- Activity: Class selects or writes a short play. Can adapt a folk tale, community story, or original idea. Assign roles.

**Lesson 3-6: Rehearsal Process**
- Activity: Read-through. Blocking (where to stand, when to move). Run-throughs. Dress rehearsal. Build costumes and props.

**Lesson 7-8: Performance**
- Activity: Perform for community. Full production with costumes, props, and staging.

### Unit 13: Instrument Building Workshop (Weeks 27-32)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Wood, string, metal, animal skin, bamboo/reed, gourds, tools
**Survival Relevance**: Instrument building combines woodworking, acoustics, and artistry. Quality instruments are valuable trade goods.

#### Learning Objectives
- Design and build one quality instrument from available materials
- Tune the instrument (adjust string tension, tube length, membrane tightness)
- Play their instrument well enough to perform a simple piece
- Understand the acoustic principles behind their instrument

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Design Phase**
- Activity: Review instrument types from Year 2. Choose one to build at higher quality. Plan materials and construction steps.

**Lesson 3-4: Construction**
- Activity: Build the instrument with teacher/trades team guidance. Focus on quality — this should be a usable instrument, not a toy.

**Lesson 5-6: Tuning and Playing**
- Activity: Tune the instrument. Learn to play a simple piece. Perform for the class.
- Connection: "A skilled instrument builder can produce valuable trade goods."

### Unit 14: Art as Historical Record (Weeks 33-40)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Drawing/painting supplies, writing supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Before photography, art was how communities documented their lives. This skill directly supports community record-keeping.

#### Learning Objectives
- Create an artistic documentation of a community event, place, or person
- Understand that art preserves history (cave paintings, historical paintings, portraits)
- Create a contribution to the community record using visual art
- Combine image and text to tell a story about the community

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Art as Memory**
- Activity: "Before cameras, if you wanted to remember what something looked like, someone had to draw it. Cave paintings from 40,000 years ago tell us about animals that no longer exist. Art is memory."

**Lesson 3-4: Documenting Our Community**
- Activity: Each child selects a subject to document: a community building, a person at work, a garden, an animal, a meal being prepared. Draw it carefully and accurately.

**Lesson 5-6: Adding Words**
- Activity: Write a description to accompany the drawing. When was this? Who is in it? What was happening? Why does it matter?

**Lesson 7-8: Community Record Contribution**
- Activity: Compile all documentation into a class book or wall display. Present to the community. "This is our record of life right now. In 50 years, someone will look at this and learn about us."
- Connection: This directly supports the community history project started in the History curriculum.

#### Assessment (Year 3 Overall)
- Created an original song with lyrics and melody
- Built a functional instrument and can play a simple piece
- Contributed to the community mural
- Performed in the class play
- Created an art-based community record entry
- Can explain why arts matter for community survival
