# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Language Arts

## Overview
At the intermediate level, students transition from learning to read to reading to learn, and from basic composition to purposeful writing. They develop critical reading skills, formal and persuasive writing, public speaking, and the ability to create teaching materials for others. By Grade 8, students should be capable of drafting community documents and preparing instructional content.

## Grade 6: Analytical Reading and Formal Writing

### Unit 1: Literary Genres (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Available books across genres (fiction, nonfiction, poetry, drama, biography, technical manuals)
**Survival Relevance**: Recognizing different text types helps readers find and use information efficiently — a critical skill when resources are limited and time matters

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify and describe major literary genres (fiction, nonfiction, poetry, drama, biography, technical)
- Analyze the purpose and audience for each genre
- Read and respond to at least one work from each genre
- Explain which genres are most useful for community knowledge preservation

#### Teacher Notes
Use whatever texts are available. A survival manual is as valid a text as a novel. Prioritize reading that builds both literary understanding and practical knowledge.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Kind of Text Is This?**
- Activity: Present 6 short excerpts — one from each genre. Students sort and identify them. Discuss: What clues tell you what kind of text this is? Who is the intended audience?
- Key Concepts: Genre is a category of writing, each genre has conventions and purposes
- Practice: Find examples of 3 genres in available community materials

**Lesson 3-4: Reading Fiction Critically**
- Activity: Read a short story or novel chapter together. Analyze: Who is the protagonist? What is the conflict? What is the author's perspective? How does the story reflect the world the author lived in?
- Key Concepts: Fiction reveals truths about human experience, critical reading means asking questions of the text
- Practice: Write a 1-page response connecting the story to community experience

**Lesson 5-6: Reading Nonfiction Critically**
- Activity: Read a nonfiction text (historical account, scientific description, how-to manual). Evaluate: Is this source reliable? What evidence supports its claims? What is its bias?
- Key Concepts: Nonfiction must be evaluated for accuracy and bias, critical reading applies to all texts
- Practice: Compare two nonfiction texts on the same topic — how do they differ? Why?

#### Assessment
- Correctly identify and describe 6 literary genres
- Write a critical response to one fiction and one nonfiction text
- Explain the purpose and audience of at least 3 genres

### Unit 2: Research Papers (Weeks 7-14)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Reference materials (books, manuals, community records), paper, writing tools
**Survival Relevance**: Research and clear reporting are essential for community problem-solving — investigating a water quality issue, summarizing medical protocols, or compiling agricultural data

#### Learning Objectives
- Formulate a research question
- Locate and evaluate sources (books, records, interviews, observation)
- Organize information with an outline
- Write a 3-5 page research paper with introduction, body, and conclusion
- Cite sources properly

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Choosing a Topic and Forming Questions**
- Activity: Brainstorm topics relevant to the community. Select one. Transform it from a topic into a question. "Water" → "What are the most common contaminants in our water supply and how can we test for them?"
- Key Concepts: Good research starts with a focused question, questions are better than topics
- Practice: Develop 3 research questions from community topics

**Lesson 3-4: Finding and Evaluating Sources**
- Activity: For your question, find at least 3 sources (books, manuals, knowledgeable community members, personal observation). Evaluate each: Is this reliable? How do you know? What is this source's limitation?
- Key Concepts: Not all sources are equal, primary sources (firsthand) vs secondary (reported), cross-checking improves reliability
- Practice: Create a source evaluation chart for your 3 sources

**Lesson 5-6: Outlining and Drafting**
- Activity: Organize your findings into an outline: introduction (question + why it matters), body paragraphs (one main point each with evidence), conclusion (answer to your question + recommendations).
- Key Concepts: Organization makes complex information clear, each paragraph has one main idea supported by evidence
- Practice: Write a complete first draft following the outline

**Lesson 7-8: Revision, Editing, and Final Paper**
- Activity: Peer review — exchange papers and provide written feedback on clarity, evidence, and organization. Revise based on feedback. Edit for grammar and mechanics. Produce final version.
- Key Concepts: First drafts are never final, peer review improves quality, revision is re-seeing your argument
- Practice: Submit a polished final paper. Present findings to class in a 5-minute talk.

#### Assessment
- Research paper with clear question, 3+ sources, organized argument, and proper citations
- 5-minute oral presentation of findings
- Peer review of another student's paper demonstrating constructive feedback

### Unit 3: Formal Writing and Public Speaking (Weeks 15-22)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Writing materials, community meeting space
**Survival Relevance**: The ability to communicate formally — in writing and speech — is essential for governance, trade negotiation, inter-community diplomacy, and leadership

#### Learning Objectives
- Write formal letters, proposals, and reports
- Distinguish formal from informal register
- Deliver a 5-minute prepared speech with clear structure
- Respond to audience questions with composure

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Formal vs Informal Writing**
- Activity: Write about the same topic in informal style (letter to a friend) and formal style (report to community council). Compare. What changed? Why?
- Key Concepts: Register is the level of formality, audience determines register, formal writing is precise and impersonal
- Practice: Convert 3 informal communications into formal ones

**Lesson 3-4: Letters and Proposals**
- Activity: Write a formal letter to community leadership proposing a project or change. Include: problem statement, proposed solution, expected benefits, resources needed, timeline.
- Key Concepts: Proposals persuade through evidence and logic, structure makes complex ideas clear
- Practice: Peer review proposals for clarity and persuasiveness

**Lesson 5-6: Prepared Speech**
- Activity: Choose a topic of community relevance. Write and rehearse a 5-minute speech with clear introduction, 3 main points, and conclusion. Practice delivery — posture, eye contact, pacing, volume.
- Key Concepts: Speaking and writing are different skills, delivery matters as much as content, practice builds confidence
- Practice: Deliver speech to class, receive feedback, revise and re-deliver

**Lesson 7-8: Question and Response**
- Activity: After each speech, audience asks questions. Speaker responds. Practice: staying calm, admitting when you don't know, redirecting to your main points.
- Key Concepts: Handling questions demonstrates understanding, "I don't know but I'll find out" is a strong answer
- Practice: Mock community council presentation with Q&A

#### Assessment
- Formal letter or proposal that meets all structural requirements
- 5-minute speech delivered with confidence and clear structure
- Demonstrate ability to handle audience questions composedly

### Unit 4: Vocabulary Building and Critical Reading (Weeks 23-30)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Available texts of increasing complexity (technical manuals, historical accounts, scientific texts)
**Survival Relevance**: An expanded vocabulary enables access to more complex texts — medical manuals, engineering guides, legal documents — that the community depends on

#### Learning Objectives
- Learn 10+ new vocabulary words per week from context
- Use context clues to determine word meaning
- Identify and analyze author's purpose, tone, and bias
- Summarize complex texts accurately

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Words from Context**
- Activity: Read a challenging text. Circle unfamiliar words. Before looking them up, use context clues — surrounding words, sentence structure, root words — to guess meanings. Then verify.
- Key Concepts: Context clues (definition, example, contrast, inference), root words from Latin and Greek
- Practice: Keep a vocabulary journal — word, context, guessed meaning, actual meaning

**Lesson 3-4: Technical Vocabulary**
- Activity: Read a technical text (medical manual, engineering guide, agricultural reference). Identify specialized vocabulary. Create a glossary for the text that a non-specialist could use.
- Key Concepts: Every field has specialized vocabulary, understanding these words unlocks the knowledge
- Practice: Create a 20-word glossary for a technical text

**Lesson 5-6: Author's Purpose and Bias**
- Activity: Read two accounts of the same event or topic written from different perspectives. Identify: What is each author's purpose? What is their bias? How do the accounts differ?
- Key Concepts: All writing has a perspective, recognizing bias is essential for critical thinking
- Practice: Write an analysis comparing two texts and identifying their biases

**Lesson 7-8: Summarizing Complex Material**
- Activity: Read a 5-page technical or historical text. Write a 1-page summary that captures all key points. Then write a 1-paragraph summary. Then a 1-sentence summary.
- Key Concepts: Summarizing requires identifying what's essential, different contexts need different levels of detail
- Practice: Summarize 3 complex texts at different levels of compression

#### Assessment
- Vocabulary journal with 60+ entries showing context-clue reasoning
- Technical glossary for a community-relevant text
- Bias analysis comparing two texts
- Accurate summaries at 3 levels of compression

## Grade 7: Persuasion and Analysis

### Unit 5: Persuasive and Argumentative Writing (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Writing materials, examples of persuasive writing
**Survival Relevance**: The ability to argue persuasively — in writing and speech — is how community members influence decisions, advocate for resources, and resolve disputes

#### Learning Objectives
- Distinguish between persuasion (emotion + logic) and argumentation (logic + evidence)
- Construct a thesis-driven argument with supporting evidence
- Address counterarguments honestly
- Write a persuasive essay and an argumentative essay

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Persuasion vs Argumentation**
- Activity: Read a persuasive text (speech, editorial) and an argumentative text (research-based analysis). How do they differ? Where does each use emotion? Logic? Evidence?
- Key Concepts: Persuasion appeals to emotion and logic, argumentation relies primarily on evidence and reasoning
- Practice: Identify 3 persuasive techniques and 3 argumentative techniques in sample texts

**Lesson 3-4: Building an Argument**
- Activity: Choose a community issue. State a clear thesis. Find 3 pieces of evidence that support it. Organize into an outline: thesis → evidence 1 → evidence 2 → evidence 3 → counterargument → rebuttal → conclusion.
- Key Concepts: A thesis is a debatable claim, evidence must be specific, addressing counterarguments strengthens your position
- Practice: Write an outline for an argumentative essay

**Lesson 5-6: The Persuasive Essay**
- Activity: Write a persuasive essay on a community topic. Use both logical argument and emotional appeal (vivid examples, personal stories, appeals to community values).
- Key Concepts: Effective persuasion combines logic and emotion, know your audience, structure matters
- Practice: Peer review and revise

**Lesson 7-8: The Argumentative Essay**
- Activity: Write a formal argumentative essay — evidence-based, acknowledging counterarguments, arriving at a reasoned conclusion. No emotional appeals — only logic and evidence.
- Key Concepts: Argumentation is the foundation of governance documents, policies, and formal proposals
- Practice: Submit final essay. Participate in a class critique — defend your argument against questions.

#### Assessment
- Persuasive essay demonstrating emotional appeal and logical reasoning
- Argumentative essay with thesis, evidence, counterargument, and conclusion
- Ability to defend an argument under questioning

### Unit 6: Literary Analysis (Weeks 9-14)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: A substantial text available to all students (novel, play, collection of stories)
**Survival Relevance**: Literary analysis develops the critical thinking skills needed to evaluate any complex information — proposals, reports, propaganda, agreements

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze theme, symbolism, and character development in a literary text
- Support interpretations with textual evidence (quotations, specific references)
- Write a literary analysis essay
- Understand that texts have multiple valid interpretations

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Theme and Symbolism**
- Activity: Read together. Identify themes — what is this text really about beneath the surface? Identify symbols — what objects, events, or characters represent larger ideas?
- Key Concepts: Theme is the underlying message, symbolism uses concrete things to represent abstract ideas
- Practice: Identify 2 themes and 3 symbols with textual evidence

**Lesson 3-4: Character Analysis**
- Activity: Choose a character. Track their development — how do they change? Why? What motivates them? How does the author reveal character (dialogue, action, description, other characters' reactions)?
- Key Concepts: Dynamic characters change, static characters don't, character is revealed through choices
- Practice: Write a character analysis with specific textual evidence

**Lesson 5-6: The Analysis Essay**
- Activity: Write a 3-4 page literary analysis essay. Choose a thesis about theme, character, or symbolism. Support with quotations from the text. Analyze — don't summarize.
- Key Concepts: Analysis asks "what does this mean?" not "what happened?", quotations are evidence
- Practice: Draft, peer review, revise, submit

#### Assessment
- Literary analysis essay with clear thesis and textual evidence
- Demonstrate understanding that multiple interpretations can be valid
- Distinguish between analysis and summary

### Unit 7: Rhetoric, Technical Documentation, and Journalism (Weeks 15-24)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Examples of rhetoric (speeches), technical documents (manuals, procedures), community newsletter materials
**Survival Relevance**: Rhetoric builds leadership communication, technical writing creates usable community documents, and journalism keeps the community informed and accountable

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify and use rhetorical devices (repetition, parallelism, rhetorical questions, analogy, anecdote)
- Write clear technical documentation (procedures, manuals, how-to guides)
- Produce journalistic writing (news reports, interviews, opinion pieces)
- Understand the role of free information flow in healthy communities

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Rhetoric — The Art of Persuasion**
- Activity: Read great speeches from diverse cultures. Identify what makes them effective. Practice: write a 3-minute speech using at least 3 rhetorical devices.
- Key Concepts: Rhetoric is the craft of effective communication, used in governance, teaching, and leadership
- Practice: Deliver the speech and identify devices used

**Lesson 3-4: Technical Documentation**
- Activity: Choose a community process (water purification, first aid for burns, starting a fire, planting a garden bed). Write step-by-step instructions that a complete beginner could follow. Test on a partner who doesn't know the process.
- Key Concepts: Technical writing is clear, sequential, and assumes no prior knowledge, test your instructions
- Practice: Write and test 2 technical documents

**Lesson 5-6: Creative Writing Workshop**
- Activity: Workshop format — students bring creative writing (fiction, poetry, personal narrative), read aloud, and receive structured feedback. Focus on craft elements: voice, imagery, pacing, dialogue.
- Key Concepts: Writing improves through practice and feedback, creative writing develops voice and empathy
- Practice: Write, workshop, and revise 3 pieces

**Lesson 7-8: Journalism**
- Activity: Start a class or community newsletter. Assign roles — editor, reporters, opinion writers, illustrator. Cover community events, interview leaders, investigate issues.
- Key Concepts: Journalism informs and holds leadership accountable, facts vs opinion, balanced reporting
- Practice: Produce at least 2 editions of a community newsletter

**Lesson 9-10: Information and Power**
- Activity: Discuss: When communities control information flow, what happens? When information flows freely? Historical examples of censorship and free press. What does our community need?
- Key Concepts: Information access is a governance issue, transparency builds trust, journalism is a public service
- Practice: Write an opinion piece on information policy for the community

#### Assessment
- Speech using rhetorical devices, delivered effectively
- Technical document tested and confirmed usable by a non-expert
- At least 2 published pieces in the community newsletter
- Opinion piece on information policy

## Grade 8: Synthesis and Leadership

### Unit 8: Logic, Logical Fallacies, and Advanced Composition (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Writing materials, examples of logical and illogical arguments
**Survival Relevance**: The ability to identify faulty reasoning protects communities from manipulation, poor decisions, and internal conflict based on false premises

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify 12+ logical fallacies (ad hominem, straw man, false dilemma, appeal to authority, slippery slope, bandwagon, red herring, circular reasoning, hasty generalization, appeal to emotion, tu quoque, false cause)
- Construct logically valid arguments
- Evaluate real-world arguments for logical validity
- Write advanced compositions integrating research, argumentation, and rhetorical skill

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Is Logic?**
- Activity: Present simple syllogisms. All humans need water. We are human. Therefore we need water. — Valid. Some plants are poisonous. This is a plant. Therefore this is poisonous. — Invalid. Why?
- Key Concepts: Premises, conclusions, validity, soundness
- Practice: Evaluate 10 simple arguments as valid or invalid

**Lesson 3-4: Logical Fallacies**
- Activity: Learn 4 fallacies per lesson. For each: definition, example from real life, why it's fallacious, how to counter it. Create a fallacy reference card.
- Key Concepts: Fallacies are errors in reasoning that feel convincing but aren't, recognizing them is a defense skill
- Practice: Find examples of fallacies in available texts or remembered arguments

**Lesson 5-6: Fallacy Detection in Practice**
- Activity: Hold a debate on a community issue. Assign one person as fallacy judge — they stop the debate whenever a fallacy is used and identify it.
- Key Concepts: Real-world arguments are full of fallacies, catching them improves decision-making
- Practice: Write an analysis of a public argument identifying all fallacies

**Lesson 7-8: Advanced Composition**
- Activity: Write a 5-7 page composition on a community issue that integrates research, logical argumentation, rhetorical craft, and acknowledges complexity. This is the highest level of writing expected in the intermediate band.
- Key Concepts: Advanced writing synthesizes multiple skills — research, logic, rhetoric, empathy, structure
- Practice: Draft, peer review, revise, submit. Best papers presented to community leadership.

**Lesson 9-10: Document Drafting**
- Activity: Draft a community document — a policy proposal, charter amendment, trade agreement, or code of conduct. Use proper formal structure, logical reasoning, and clear language.
- Key Concepts: Document drafting is a civic skill, clarity and precision matter when documents govern behavior
- Practice: Complete document, review with peers and teacher, revise

#### Assessment
- Identify 12 logical fallacies by name and example
- Write an advanced composition integrating research, logic, and rhetoric
- Draft a formal community document meeting all structural requirements

### Unit 9: Primary Source Analysis and Teaching Materials (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Available primary and secondary sources, materials for creating teaching resources
**Survival Relevance**: Primary source analysis is critical for evaluating information in a world without internet — you must assess whether a document, testimony, or text is trustworthy. Creating teaching materials ensures knowledge transfer to the next generation.

#### Learning Objectives
- Distinguish primary from secondary sources
- Apply a source analysis framework (author, audience, purpose, context, bias, corroboration)
- Read and analyze primary sources from multiple periods
- Create teaching materials for younger students (lesson plans, worksheets, reading materials)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Primary vs Secondary Sources**
- Activity: Present pairs — an original letter AND a textbook description of the same event. A medical case record AND a health guide summary. Which is primary? Which is secondary? Why does the distinction matter?
- Key Concepts: Primary sources are created at the time by participants/witnesses, secondary sources interpret primary sources
- Practice: Classify 10 sources as primary or secondary

**Lesson 3-4: Source Analysis Framework**
- Activity: Apply the framework to 3 primary sources: Who created this? For whom? Why? When and where? What biases might be present? Can other sources confirm it?
- Key Concepts: Every source has a perspective, analysis requires systematic questioning, corroboration strengthens claims
- Practice: Complete a written source analysis for each text

**Lesson 5-6: Reading Challenging Primary Sources**
- Activity: Read a primary source with archaic or technical language. Work through it together — paraphrase difficult passages, identify the author's main argument, evaluate its credibility.
- Key Concepts: Historical texts require patience, vocabulary changes over time, context is essential
- Practice: Independently analyze a challenging primary source and present findings

**Lesson 7-8: Creating Teaching Materials**
- Activity: Design a lesson plan, worksheet, or reading material on a topic you know well — aimed at Foundation or Building band students. Consider their reading level, attention span, and learning needs.
- Key Concepts: Teaching deepens your own understanding, materials must match the audience's level, clear instruction is a skill
- Practice: Create a complete lesson with objectives, activities, and assessment

**Lesson 9-10: Teaching Practicum**
- Activity: Deliver your lesson to a younger class. Observe what works and what doesn't. Revise the materials based on experience.
- Key Concepts: Plans change on contact with learners, flexibility and observation are teaching skills, helping others learn is a community responsibility
- Practice: Teach, reflect, and revise materials for the community teaching library

#### Assessment
- Written source analysis using the full framework
- Independent analysis of a challenging primary source
- Teaching materials created, delivered to younger students, and revised based on experience
