# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Health & First Aid

## Overview
At the intermediate level, health education transitions from personal health habits to community health skills. Students progress from comprehensive first aid through wilderness medicine to emergency medical response. By Grade 8, students should be capable first responders and community health planners.

## Grade 6: Comprehensive First Aid

### Unit 1: Wound Care and Bleeding Control (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: First aid kit, clean cloth for bandaging practice, splinting materials (sticks, cloth), practice mannequin or pillows
**Survival Relevance**: Wound care is the most common medical intervention in a post-infrastructure community. Proper technique prevents infection and saves lives.

#### Learning Objectives
- Assess a wound (type, severity, contamination)
- Clean, dress, and bandage wounds correctly
- Control bleeding using direct pressure, elevation, and pressure points
- Recognize signs of serious bleeding requiring advanced intervention

#### Teacher Notes
All practice should be supervised. Use realistic but safe simulation. Emphasize: first aid is not "playing doctor" — it is a critical community skill. Every person who can provide first aid makes the community safer.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Wound Assessment**
- Activity: Present wound scenarios (descriptions or simulated on volunteers using makeup/paint). Classify: abrasion (scrape), laceration (cut), puncture (stab), avulsion (skin torn away), incision (clean cut). Assess severity: minor (surface), moderate (deeper, bleeding), severe (deep, heavy bleeding, contamination).
- Key Concepts: Accurate assessment determines treatment, severity guides urgency, contamination increases infection risk
- Practice: Assess and classify 10 wound scenarios

**Lesson 3-4: Wound Cleaning and Dressing**
- Activity: Practice wound cleaning — irrigate with clean water (flush contaminants out), remove visible debris, apply antiseptic if available. Apply sterile dressing and secure with bandage. Practice on simulated wounds.
- Key Concepts: Clean wounds heal, dirty wounds infect, irrigation is more important than antiseptic, dressing must be clean and changed regularly
- Practice: Clean and dress 5 simulated wounds of different types

**Lesson 5-6: Bleeding Control**
- Activity: Practice direct pressure (firm, sustained pressure on the wound), elevation (raise the limb above the heart), and pressure points (brachial artery for arm, femoral artery for leg). Tourniquet use as last resort for life-threatening extremity bleeding.
- Key Concepts: Most bleeding stops with direct pressure, don't remove a soaked dressing (add more on top), tourniquets save lives but are a last resort
- Practice: Demonstrate bleeding control for arm and leg wounds

#### Assessment
- Correctly assess and classify wound types and severity
- Demonstrate proper wound cleaning and dressing technique
- Demonstrate bleeding control using direct pressure and pressure points

### Unit 2: Fractures, Burns, and Shock (Weeks 7-14)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Splinting materials (padded boards, SAM splints if available, cloth triangular bandages), cool water for burn treatment
**Survival Relevance**: Fractures from falls, burns from fire, and shock from any serious injury are among the most common emergencies in a working community

#### Learning Objectives
- Recognize signs of fracture (deformity, swelling, pain, inability to use limb)
- Immobilize fractures using available materials
- Classify burns by degree and treat appropriately
- Recognize and treat shock

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Fracture Recognition and Splinting**
- Activity: Signs of fracture: deformity, swelling, bruising, pain on movement, crepitus (grinding), inability to bear weight. Splinting principle: immobilize the joint above AND below the break. Practice splinting forearm, lower leg, finger.
- Key Concepts: Splint in the position found (don't straighten), pad the splint, check circulation below the splint (pulse, sensation, color)
- Practice: Splint 3 different fracture simulations

**Lesson 3-4: Special Fractures**
- Activity: Suspected spinal injury — DO NOT MOVE unless in immediate danger. Stabilize the head and neck. Skull fracture — watch for clear fluid from ears/nose. Rib fracture — have the patient support with a pillow, watch for breathing difficulty. Pelvic fracture — immobilize, treat for shock.
- Key Concepts: Some fractures are life-threatening, the spine is the most critical, when in doubt, immobilize and get help
- Practice: Demonstrate management for suspected spinal, skull, and rib injuries

**Lesson 5-6: Burn Treatment**
- Activity: Classify: first degree (red, painful — sunburn), second degree (blisters — partial thickness), third degree (white/charred, painless — full thickness). Treatment: cool with clean water (not ice) for 10-20 minutes, cover loosely with clean cloth, do NOT break blisters, do NOT apply butter/grease.
- Key Concepts: Burns damage in three zones (coagulation, stasis, hyperemia), cooling prevents the stasis zone from progressing, infection is the primary risk after initial treatment
- Practice: Classify and treat 5 burn scenarios

**Lesson 7-8: Shock Recognition and Treatment**
- Activity: Shock = the body's organs aren't getting enough blood. Signs: pale/cool/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, rapid shallow breathing, confusion, thirst. Treatment: lay flat, elevate legs 12 inches, keep warm, treat the cause, reassure.
- Key Concepts: Shock can kill even when the injury seems survivable, early recognition is key, warming and positioning are the primary treatments available
- Practice: Demonstrate shock recognition and management

#### Assessment
- Splint a fracture correctly with appropriate checking
- Classify burns by degree and demonstrate proper treatment
- Recognize shock signs and demonstrate management protocol

### Unit 3: Choking, CPR, and Unconscious Patient (Weeks 15-22)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: CPR practice mannequin (or firm pillow), AED trainer (if available)
**Survival Relevance**: Choking, cardiac arrest, and unconsciousness are time-critical emergencies — knowing what to do in the first 5 minutes determines outcome

#### Learning Objectives
- Perform the Heimlich maneuver on conscious choking victims (adult, child, infant, self)
- Perform CPR (adult, child, infant) with correct compression depth, rate, and ratio
- Place an unconscious breathing patient in the recovery position
- Manage an unconscious non-breathing patient until help arrives

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Choking Response**
- Activity: Signs: universal choking sign (hands to throat), inability to speak/cough/breathe, blue color. Conscious adult: stand behind, fist above navel, quick upward thrusts. Conscious infant: 5 back blows, 5 chest thrusts. Self: use chair back or own fist.
- Key Concepts: Choking is common and fixable, act immediately, if the person can cough — let them cough
- Practice: Demonstrate Heimlich on a mannequin or willing partner (gentle)

**Lesson 3-4: CPR — Adult**
- Activity: Check scene safety. Check responsiveness. Call for help. Check breathing (10 seconds). If not breathing: 30 compressions (center of chest, 2 inches deep, 100-120/min) then 2 rescue breaths. Continue until help arrives or patient recovers.
- Key Concepts: CPR keeps blood circulating until the heart restarts, compression quality matters more than anything, push hard and fast in the center of the chest
- Practice: Full CPR practice on mannequin — timed 2-minute sets with feedback

**Lesson 5-6: CPR — Child and Infant**
- Activity: Child (1-puberty): same as adult but use one or two hands, compress 1/3 chest depth. Infant (under 1): two fingers on sternum, compress 1/3 depth, puffs instead of full breaths.
- Key Concepts: Techniques adjust for size, the principle is the same — circulate blood and deliver oxygen
- Practice: Demonstrate child and infant CPR on appropriate practice devices

**Lesson 7-8: Recovery Position and Ongoing Care**
- Activity: Unconscious but breathing: place in recovery position (on side, upper leg bent, lower arm extended, head tilted back to keep airway open). Monitor breathing. Do NOT leave unconscious patients on their back (choking risk).
- Key Concepts: The recovery position protects the airway, unconscious patients need constant monitoring, recovery position is one of the simplest and most important skills
- Practice: Place a volunteer in recovery position and explain why each element matters

#### Assessment
- Demonstrate Heimlich maneuver on an adult and infant mannequin
- Perform 2 minutes of quality CPR with correct rate and depth
- Demonstrate recovery position with explanation

### Unit 4: Mental Health and Hygiene Science (Weeks 23-30)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Discussion space, handwashing demonstration supplies, hygiene materials
**Survival Relevance**: Mental health crises are common in post-infrastructure communities, and hygiene science prevents the infections that kill more people than injuries do

#### Learning Objectives
- Recognize signs of common mental health conditions (depression, anxiety, PTSD, grief)
- Provide basic psychological first aid (listen, assess, refer)
- Explain the science behind hygiene practices (germ theory, infection routes)
- Implement community hygiene protocols

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Mental Health Awareness**
- Activity: Discuss common mental health experiences — sadness, worry, anger, grief, flashbacks. When are these normal? When do they become concerning? Warning signs: withdrawal from community, sleep changes, eating changes, loss of interest, talk of hopelessness or self-harm.
- Key Concepts: Mental health is health, everyone struggles sometimes, recognizing when someone needs help is a skill, stigma prevents people from seeking help
- Practice: Identify warning signs in 5 scenarios and discuss appropriate responses

**Lesson 3-4: Psychological First Aid**
- Activity: Practice the RAPID model: Reflective listening, Assessment, Prioritization, Intervention, Disposition. Role-play: a peer is upset after a difficult event. Listen. Don't try to fix — just be present and assess safety.
- Key Concepts: Psychological first aid is not therapy — it is stabilization, the most important thing is being a calm, caring presence, always assess for safety (risk to self or others)
- Practice: Role-play 3 psychological first aid scenarios

**Lesson 5-6: Hygiene Science**
- Activity: Germ theory — diseases are caused by microorganisms too small to see. Demonstration: put glitter on hands, touch surfaces, observe spread. Handwashing technique: 20 seconds with soap, friction on all surfaces. When to wash: before eating, after toilet, after handling animals, after treating wounds.
- Key Concepts: Handwashing is the single most effective disease prevention measure, hygiene is science not just habit, contamination pathways are predictable
- Practice: Demonstrate proper handwashing technique, identify contamination pathways in a scenario

**Lesson 7-8: Community Hygiene Protocols**
- Activity: Design community hygiene protocols: latrine placement and maintenance (downstream, downhill from water), food handling safety (clean surfaces, cook thoroughly, separate raw/cooked), water treatment standards, waste management, isolation procedures for infectious illness.
- Key Concepts: Community-level hygiene prevents epidemic disease, protocols must be practical and consistently followed
- Practice: Draft community hygiene protocols and present for review

#### Assessment
- Recognize mental health warning signs and provide basic psychological first aid
- Explain germ theory and infection routes
- Design practical community hygiene protocols

### Unit 5: Nutrition for Growth (Weeks 31-36)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Food samples, nutrition reference materials, growth charts
**Survival Relevance**: Adolescents have specific nutritional needs — inadequate nutrition during growth years causes permanent health consequences

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand adolescent nutritional requirements (increased calories, protein, calcium, iron)
- Recognize signs of malnutrition (stunting, wasting, deficiency symptoms)
- Plan nutritionally adequate meals for adolescents using available foods
- Understand the relationship between nutrition, growth, and cognitive function

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Adolescent Nutrition Needs**
- Activity: Adolescents need MORE calories than adults during growth spurts. Need extra calcium (bones growing), iron (especially menstruating individuals), protein (muscle development). Compare adolescent needs to adult needs.
- Key Concepts: Growth requires extra nutrition, inadequate nutrition during adolescence has permanent consequences (stunting, weakened bones), teens are not small adults nutritionally
- Practice: Calculate approximate daily caloric and nutrient needs for adolescents of different activity levels

**Lesson 3-4: Recognizing Malnutrition**
- Activity: Signs of malnutrition: stunting (short for age), wasting (thin for height), specific deficiency signs (bleeding gums = vitamin C, night blindness = vitamin A, pale skin/fatigue = iron, bone pain = vitamin D). Early recognition enables early intervention.
- Key Concepts: Malnutrition is common in resource-limited settings, it affects cognition and learning, it is preventable with planning
- Practice: Assess nutritional status using height/weight and physical signs

**Lesson 5-6: Meal Planning for Adolescents**
- Activity: Design a week of meals specifically addressing adolescent needs from available community foods. Ensure adequate calories, protein, calcium (dairy, leafy greens, bone broth), iron (meat, beans, leafy greens), and vitamins.
- Key Concepts: Meal planning is a practical application of nutrition science, trade-offs are necessary when resources are limited, prioritize the most critical nutrients
- Practice: Complete a meal plan and analyze it for nutritional adequacy

#### Assessment
- Describe adolescent nutritional requirements and why they differ from adults
- Recognize 5 signs of malnutrition and identify the specific deficiency
- Design a nutritionally adequate meal plan for adolescents from available foods

## Grade 7: Wilderness First Aid and Natural Medicine

### Unit 6: Wilderness First Aid (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: First aid kit, splinting materials, litter-building materials (poles, blankets), outdoor training area
**Survival Relevance**: When help is hours or days away, extended care skills keep patients alive until definitive treatment is possible

#### Learning Objectives
- Apply first aid when evacuation is delayed (hours to days)
- Manage environmental emergencies (hypothermia, heat stroke, dehydration, drowning)
- Build and use improvised stretchers
- Make evacuation vs stay decisions

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Wilderness First Aid Principles**
- Activity: "In town first aid, help arrives in minutes. In wilderness first aid, you might be the only help for hours or days." Key differences: extended monitoring, improvised supplies, evacuation planning, patient comfort over extended time.
- Key Concepts: Scene safety, primary survey (ABCs — airway, breathing, circulation), secondary survey (head-to-toe exam), documentation, ongoing monitoring
- Practice: Conduct a full patient assessment on a simulated patient

**Lesson 3-4: Hypothermia and Heat Emergencies**
- Activity: Hypothermia stages: shivering → confusion → loss of shivering → unconsciousness. Treatment: remove from cold, remove wet clothing, warm gradually (warm drinks if conscious, body heat, insulation). Heat exhaustion/stroke: cool rapidly, hydrate, shade.
- Key Concepts: Temperature emergencies are progressive — early intervention prevents escalation, the body can't regulate temperature when exhausted or impaired
- Practice: Recognize and treat hypothermia and heat stroke scenarios

**Lesson 5-6: Dehydration and Drowning**
- Activity: Dehydration: signs (dark urine, dry mouth, headache, dizziness, confusion). Treatment: oral rehydration (water + salt + sugar). Prevention: drink before thirsty. Drowning: remove from water safely, clear airway, CPR if not breathing, recovery position if breathing.
- Key Concepts: Dehydration kills slowly but reliably, most drowning deaths are preventable with water safety rules
- Practice: Mix oral rehydration solution, demonstrate water rescue from shore

**Lesson 7-8: Bites, Stings, and Poisoning**
- Activity: Snake bites: keep calm, immobilize limb, get to medical care (do NOT cut, suck, or tourniquet). Insect stings: remove stinger, cold compress, watch for anaphylaxis (severe allergic reaction — throat swelling, breathing difficulty). Plant poisoning: identify if possible, do NOT induce vomiting unless directed.
- Key Concepts: Most bites/stings are not life-threatening, anaphylaxis is the primary danger from stings, identification helps treatment
- Practice: Manage 3 bite/sting/poisoning scenarios

**Lesson 9-10: Patient Evacuation**
- Activity: Build improvised stretchers — two poles + blanket, two poles + shirts, door/plank. Practice carrying: 4-person carry, 2-person carry, drag. Decide: can the patient walk? Do they need to be carried? Is evacuation safe?
- Key Concepts: Evacuation decisions balance patient condition against distance and terrain, moving a spinal injury patient is dangerous, improvised stretchers work
- Practice: Build a stretcher, load a patient, carry 50 meters

#### Assessment
- Conduct a complete patient assessment
- Manage 3 environmental emergencies correctly
- Build a stretcher, load a patient, and carry safely

### Unit 7: Natural Medicines and Their Limits (Weeks 11-16)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Medicinal plants (local specimens), reference materials, tea-making supplies
**Survival Relevance**: When pharmaceutical supplies are limited, understanding which natural remedies have evidence and which are dangerous is essential

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify 10+ medicinal plants available locally
- Understand the evidence basis for herbal remedies (what works, what doesn't, what's dangerous)
- Prepare basic herbal remedies (teas, poultices, tinctures)
- Know the limits of herbal medicine (when to seek trained medical care)

#### Teacher Notes
Be honest about evidence. Some herbal remedies have strong evidence (willow bark/aspirin, aloe for burns). Some have tradition but limited evidence. Some are dangerous. Teach students to think critically, not to either dismiss or uncritically accept herbal medicine.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Evidence-Based Herbal Medicine**
- Activity: Discuss: many pharmaceuticals come from plants (aspirin from willow bark, morphine from poppies, quinine from cinchona bark, digitalis from foxglove). Some herbal remedies have strong evidence. Others don't. How do we tell the difference?
- Key Concepts: Evidence matters, traditional use is a starting point not proof, dose matters (the same plant can be medicine or poison), "natural" doesn't mean safe
- Practice: Research the evidence basis for 5 common herbal remedies

**Lesson 3-4: Local Medicinal Plants**
- Activity: Field walk to identify medicinal plants in the community's area. Learn identification, preparation, uses, and cautions for each. Document in a community medicinal plant guide.
- Key Concepts: Know your local plants, multiple identifying features prevent misidentification, preparation method matters
- Practice: Identify, document, and prepare remedies from 5 local medicinal plants

**Lesson 5-6: Limits and Dangers**
- Activity: Discuss cases where herbal medicine is NOT sufficient: infection requiring antibiotics, surgical emergencies, broken bones requiring setting, severe allergic reactions, mental health crises, childbirth complications.
- Key Concepts: Herbal medicine is supplementary not replacement, recognizing limits saves lives, referring to trained medical care when needed is the responsible choice
- Practice: For 10 medical scenarios, decide: herbal remedy sufficient, herbal + conventional, or conventional only

#### Assessment
- Identify 10 medicinal plants with their uses and preparation methods
- Evaluate the evidence basis for 5 herbal remedies
- Correctly identify when herbal medicine is insufficient and referral is needed

### Unit 8: Reproductive Health and Substance Education (Weeks 17-24)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Anatomical diagrams, age-appropriate reference materials
**Survival Relevance**: Comprehensive reproductive health education prevents teen pregnancy, STI transmission, and dangerous misinformation. Substance education prevents poisoning and addiction.

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand reproductive anatomy and physiology in detail
- Understand conception, pregnancy, and childbirth at a biological level
- Identify common substances of abuse and their effects
- Apply harm reduction principles

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Reproductive Anatomy and Physiology**
- Activity: Detailed study of male and female reproductive systems — anatomy, physiology, hormonal regulation, menstrual cycle, spermatogenesis. Comprehensive, accurate, and matter-of-fact.
- Key Concepts: Understanding reproductive biology is health literacy, accurate knowledge replaces dangerous myths
- Practice: Label diagrams, describe physiological processes in own words

**Lesson 3-4: Conception, Pregnancy, and Childbirth**
- Activity: Fertilization → implantation → embryonic development → fetal development → labor and delivery. Pregnancy complications that require medical attention. Prenatal nutrition and health.
- Key Concepts: Pregnancy is a normal biological process with real medical risks, informed knowledge enables good decisions, complications are common enough that everyone should recognize warning signs
- Practice: Describe the stages of pregnancy and identify 5 warning signs requiring medical attention

**Lesson 5-6: Contraception and STI Prevention**
- Activity: Present available contraceptive methods factually with effectiveness rates. Discuss STI transmission, prevention, and treatment. Barrier methods, natural methods, their reliability.
- Key Concepts: Knowledge enables informed choices, no method is 100% effective except abstinence, STIs are preventable with knowledge
- Practice: Compare contraceptive methods by effectiveness, availability, and appropriateness for different situations

**Lesson 7-8: Substance Education**
- Activity: Common substances: alcohol (effects, addiction, withdrawal dangers), unknown plant ingestion (identification, poison response), fermented/distilled products, tobacco, and any other substances likely encountered. Focus on: what it does to the body, addiction mechanisms, harm reduction.
- Key Concepts: Substance education is harm reduction not moralizing, understanding pharmacology prevents dangerous experimentation, addiction is a medical condition not a moral failing
- Practice: Describe the physiological effects of alcohol and identify signs of dangerous intoxication

#### Assessment
- Demonstrate comprehensive understanding of reproductive biology
- Identify pregnancy warning signs requiring medical attention
- Describe effects and risks of common substances

### Unit 9: Dental Care Advanced (Weeks 25-30)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Dental hygiene supplies, diagrams, dental instruments (if available — even basic ones)
**Survival Relevance**: Dental disease is one of the most common health problems and, untreated, can become life-threatening through infection

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand dental anatomy and common pathology (cavities, gum disease, abscess)
- Perform thorough oral hygiene with available materials
- Recognize dental emergencies (abscess, broken tooth, avulsion)
- Provide basic dental first aid

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Dental Anatomy and Disease**
- Activity: Study tooth structure (enamel, dentin, pulp, root). How cavities form: bacteria + sugar → acid → enamel erosion → dentin decay → pulp infection → abscess. Gum disease: plaque buildup → gingivitis → periodontitis → bone loss → tooth loss.
- Key Concepts: Dental disease is preventable, progression is predictable, early intervention is always better
- Practice: Identify stages of dental disease from descriptions

**Lesson 3-4: Oral Hygiene Without Commercial Products**
- Activity: Brushing alternatives: chew sticks (certain species), cloth wrapped on finger with salt or baking soda, charcoal powder. Flossing alternatives: thread, fiber. Rinses: salt water, herbal rinses. The goal is mechanical removal of plaque.
- Key Concepts: Mechanical cleaning matters more than the specific product, consistency prevents disease, salt water rinse reduces bacterial load
- Practice: Demonstrate effective cleaning using improvised materials

**Lesson 5-6: Dental First Aid**
- Activity: Broken tooth: save fragments, rinse gently, cold compress. Avulsed (knocked out) tooth: hold by crown, rinse, try to reimplant, or store in milk/saliva. Abscess: warm salt water rinses, antibiotics if available, DO NOT ignore (can become systemic infection). Toothache: clove oil if available, salt water rinse, pain management.
- Key Concepts: Dental emergencies require prompt action, abscess is potentially life-threatening if untreated, preservation of knocked-out teeth has a time window
- Practice: Manage 3 dental emergency scenarios

#### Assessment
- Explain dental anatomy and disease progression
- Demonstrate effective oral hygiene with improvised materials
- Manage dental emergency scenarios correctly

## Grade 8: Emergency Response and Community Health

### Unit 10: Emergency Medical Response (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Full first aid kit, triage tags (colored cloths), stretcher materials, training mannequins
**Survival Relevance**: Multiple-casualty events (building collapse, weather disaster, fire, conflict) require organized emergency response — triage, team coordination, and prioritized care

#### Learning Objectives
- Conduct triage using the START system (Simple Triage And Rapid Treatment)
- Coordinate a multi-person emergency response
- Perform stop-the-bleed interventions (tourniquet, wound packing)
- Understand emergency childbirth basics (recognition that birth is imminent, preparation, when to call for help)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Triage Principles**
- Activity: Multiple casualty scenario (simulated). Walk through, assess each patient in 30 seconds. RED: immediate (life-threatening but savable), YELLOW: delayed (serious but stable), GREEN: minor (walking wounded), BLACK: expectant (unlikely to survive with available resources). Hard decisions made honestly.
- Key Concepts: Triage saves the most lives with limited resources, it means treating the MOST SAVABLE first (not the sickest), emotional difficulty is part of the skill
- Practice: Triage 10 simulated patients and justify each classification

**Lesson 3-4: Team Response**
- Activity: Organize the class into an emergency response team. Assign roles: incident commander (directs overall response), triage officer (classifies patients), treatment teams (provide care), transport team (moves patients). Run a full scenario.
- Key Concepts: Organization prevents chaos, clear roles prevent duplication and gaps, communication must be standardized
- Practice: Run 3 multi-casualty scenarios with rotating roles

**Lesson 5-6: Stop the Bleed**
- Activity: Practice tourniquet application (upper arm, upper leg), wound packing (pack cloth tightly into wound, apply pressure), and pressure dressing. These are the interventions most likely to save a life in a trauma scenario.
- Key Concepts: Hemorrhage is the leading preventable cause of death in trauma, these skills are simple and highly effective
- Practice: Demonstrate tourniquet, wound packing, and pressure dressing under timed conditions

**Lesson 7-8: Emergency Childbirth Awareness**
- Activity: Signs that birth is imminent: contractions close together, urge to push, visible crowning. If birth happens before medical help arrives: keep calm, support the baby's head, do not pull, keep baby warm, clamp/cut cord only if trained, deliver placenta. WHEN TO CALL FOR HELP: always, but especially if bleeding won't stop, cord around neck, baby not breathing, placenta not delivering.
- Key Concepts: Most births proceed normally, the primary role of a first responder is to support a natural process, complications require trained medical attention, warmth and cleanliness are the top priorities
- Practice: Walk through the emergency birth protocol (verbal/diagram — no physical simulation)

**Lesson 9-10: Scenario Integration**
- Activity: Large-scale emergency exercise — earthquake, fire, or storm scenario. Full response: scene safety, triage, treatment, transport, documentation. Community members participate as patients. Debrief thoroughly.
- Key Concepts: Skills must be practiced under realistic stress, debrief is where the most learning happens
- Practice: Complete a full emergency response exercise

#### Assessment
- Correctly triage patients using the START system
- Function effectively in an assigned role during a multi-casualty response
- Demonstrate stop-the-bleed interventions under time pressure

### Unit 11: Community Health Planning (Weeks 11-18)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Community health data, maps, survey materials
**Survival Relevance**: Community health is not just individual care — it requires planning, surveillance, and prevention at the population level

#### Learning Objectives
- Conduct a community health assessment (identify top health risks, resources, gaps)
- Design a disease surveillance system (how to detect outbreaks early)
- Create health education materials for the community
- Develop a community health improvement plan

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Community Health Assessment**
- Activity: Survey community health status: What are the most common illnesses/injuries? What health resources exist (medical personnel, supplies, equipment)? What are the gaps? Interview medical staff and community members.
- Key Concepts: Community health starts with assessment, data drives decisions, gaps must be identified before they can be addressed
- Practice: Complete a community health assessment report

**Lesson 3-4: Disease Surveillance**
- Activity: Design a system to detect outbreaks early: standardized reporting (all illness reports to medical team), pattern detection (3+ cases of same symptoms = investigate), response triggers (when to quarantine, treat, alert neighbors).
- Key Concepts: Early detection prevents epidemics, surveillance requires standardized reporting, response plans must be in place BEFORE they're needed
- Practice: Create a surveillance protocol with reporting forms and response triggers

**Lesson 5-6: Health Education Materials**
- Activity: Create health education materials for the community — handwashing posters, food safety guides, first aid quick-reference cards, hygiene protocol summaries. Make them clear, visual, and practical.
- Key Concepts: Health education is prevention, materials must be accessible to all literacy levels, visual aids are powerful
- Practice: Create and distribute 3 health education materials

**Lesson 7-8: Community Health Plan**
- Activity: Synthesize all work into a comprehensive community health improvement plan: top 5 health priorities, intervention strategies for each, resource requirements, timeline, metrics for success, responsible parties.
- Key Concepts: Planning without implementation is useless — plans must be actionable, measurable, and have accountability
- Practice: Present community health plan to medical team and community leadership

#### Assessment
- Complete a community health assessment
- Design a disease surveillance system
- Present a community health improvement plan

### Unit 12: Water, Sanitation, and Psychological First Aid (Weeks 19-28)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Water treatment supplies, sanitation planning materials, role-play space
**Survival Relevance**: WASH (Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene) prevents the diseases that historically kill more people than violence in every crisis. Psychological first aid addresses the invisible injuries that affect everyone.

#### Learning Objectives
- Design and implement a community water treatment system
- Plan sanitation facilities (latrines, waste management, greywater)
- Provide psychological first aid to individuals in crisis
- Support community resilience through structured mental health programs

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Water Treatment Methods**
- Activity: Practice multiple treatment methods: boiling (rolling boil for 1 minute), solar disinfection (SODIS — clear bottles in sunlight for 6 hours), filtration (sand/gravel/charcoal filter), chemical treatment (chlorine tablets, iodine). Each has advantages and limitations.
- Key Concepts: No single method works for all contaminants, multi-stage treatment is most reliable, testing confirms effectiveness
- Practice: Build a multi-stage water treatment system and test output quality

**Lesson 3-4: Sanitation Planning**
- Activity: Design latrine systems: location (30+ meters from water, downhill), design (ventilated improved pit latrine), maintenance schedule, handwashing station nearby. Plan waste management: compostable vs non-compostable, ash disposal, medical waste segregation.
- Key Concepts: Sanitation is the foundation of public health, proper latrine design prevents groundwater contamination, waste management prevents disease vectors
- Practice: Design a complete sanitation system for the community

**Lesson 5-6: Psychological First Aid — Structured Approach**
- Activity: Learn the 5 elements of psychological first aid: Safety (ensure physical safety), Calming (help regulate emotions), Self-efficacy (support coping abilities), Connection (link to social support), Hope (support realistic optimism).
- Key Concepts: Psychological first aid is a structured skill, not just "being nice," it can be taught and practiced, most people recover from traumatic stress with adequate support
- Practice: Role-play 5 PFA scenarios with structured debriefing

**Lesson 7-8: Supporting Others in Crisis**
- Activity: Grief support, stress management techniques (breathing, grounding, physical activity), when to refer to trained mental health support, peer support programs, community resilience activities.
- Key Concepts: Community mental health is everyone's responsibility, structured support prevents chronic problems, peer support is powerful
- Practice: Facilitate a stress management activity for a small group

**Lesson 9-10: Community Mental Health Program**
- Activity: Design a community mental health program: regular check-ins, peer support groups, crisis response protocol, stress management activities, referral pathways, special attention groups (children, isolated individuals, caregivers).
- Key Concepts: Mental health programs are preventive infrastructure, they reduce crisis frequency and severity, they must be sustainable and accessible
- Practice: Present community mental health program to leadership

#### Assessment
- Demonstrate water treatment using at least 2 methods
- Design a complete sanitation system
- Provide psychological first aid using the structured 5-element model
- Present a community mental health program
