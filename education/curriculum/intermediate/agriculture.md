# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Agriculture

## Overview
Intermediate agriculture moves from gardening to farm management. Students learn the science behind soil, water, and crop management, then apply it to livestock care, food preservation, and eventually to designing and managing a complete farm system for community food security.

## Grade 6: Soil, Water, and Crop Science

### Unit 1: Soil Chemistry and Amendment (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Soil samples, pH test strips, compost materials, measuring tools, garden access
**Survival Relevance**: Soil is the foundation of food production. Understanding soil chemistry enables improving and maintaining fertility without industrial inputs.

#### Learning Objectives
- Test soil pH and interpret results for crop selection
- Understand macronutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium) and their plant roles
- Create and apply soil amendments (compost, lime, wood ash, green manure)
- Develop a soil improvement plan for community agricultural areas

#### Teacher Notes
This is applied chemistry. Connect every concept to visible results in the garden. Students should test, amend, and observe real soil throughout the unit.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Soil Testing**
- Activity: Collect soil from 5 community agricultural areas. Test pH (target 6.0-7.0 for most crops). Test texture (jar settling method). Observe color (dark = organic matter, red = iron, gray = waterlogged). Record results.
- Key Concepts: Soil testing is the foundation of soil management, you can't fix what you don't measure, test before adding anything
- Practice: Create soil profile reports for 5 locations

**Lesson 3-4: Plant Nutrients (N-P-K)**
- Activity: Nitrogen (N): leaf growth, dark green color. Deficiency: yellow leaves. Sources: manure, legume cover crops, compost. Phosphorus (P): roots and flowers. Deficiency: purple leaves, poor fruiting. Sources: bone meal, rock dust. Potassium (K): overall vigor and disease resistance. Deficiency: brown leaf edges. Sources: wood ash, compost.
- Key Concepts: Plants need specific nutrients in specific amounts, deficiency shows visible symptoms, each nutrient has natural sources
- Practice: Diagnose nutrient deficiencies from plant symptoms in 5 scenarios

**Lesson 5-6: Soil Amendment**
- Activity: Create amendments: compost (balanced nutrients from decomposed organic matter), lime (raises pH if too acid), wood ash (raises pH + adds potassium), green manure (legume cover crop for nitrogen). Apply to test plots and observe results over weeks.
- Key Concepts: Amendments change soil chemistry, effects take time, over-amending is as harmful as under-amending
- Practice: Apply appropriate amendments to 3 test plots based on soil test results

**Lesson 7-8: Soil Improvement Plan**
- Activity: For each community agricultural area, create a multi-year soil improvement plan: current condition, target condition, amendment schedule, cover crop rotation, composting inputs, monitoring plan.
- Key Concepts: Soil building is a multi-year process, consistent management yields compounding benefits, the plan should grow with the soil
- Practice: Present soil improvement plans to community agriculture team

#### Assessment
- Conduct and interpret soil tests
- Diagnose nutrient deficiencies from plant symptoms
- Create a multi-year soil improvement plan for a real agricultural area

### Unit 2: Irrigation Systems (Weeks 9-14)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Containers, tubing/pipes (or bamboo), string/wire, gravity-feed demonstration area, measuring tools
**Survival Relevance**: Reliable water delivery to crops is the difference between harvest and famine. Understanding irrigation lets communities design water-efficient systems from available materials.

#### Learning Objectives
- Calculate crop water needs for different plants and seasons
- Design gravity-fed irrigation systems
- Build and maintain drip irrigation from available materials
- Manage water use efficiency (minimize waste, maximize crop benefit)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Crop Water Needs**
- Activity: Different crops need different amounts of water. Tomatoes: heavy water. Corn: moderate. Beans: less. Root vegetables: moderate but consistent. Calculate: garden area × water depth needed per week = liters required.
- Key Concepts: Water needs vary by crop, growth stage, temperature, and soil type, over-watering wastes water and can harm crops
- Practice: Calculate weekly water needs for the community garden

**Lesson 3-4: Gravity-Fed Systems**
- Activity: Design a gravity-fed irrigation system. Water source (tank, pond, spring) must be higher than the garden. Calculate head (height difference) and flow rate. Layout distribution channels or pipes.
- Key Concepts: Gravity is free — use it for water delivery, head determines pressure, even small height differences can move water usefully
- Practice: Design a gravity-fed system for a section of community garden

**Lesson 5-6: Drip Irrigation**
- Activity: Build drip irrigation from plastic bottles (punctured caps), tubing, or hollow bamboo. Drip delivers water directly to roots — reduces evaporation by 30-50% compared to overhead watering.
- Key Concepts: Drip irrigation is the most water-efficient method, it reduces weed growth and foliar disease, it can be built from salvaged materials
- Practice: Build and install a drip system for a garden bed and measure water savings

#### Assessment
- Calculate crop water needs for a garden plan
- Design a gravity-fed irrigation system with correct calculations
- Build a functional drip irrigation system from available materials

### Unit 3: Crop Rotation Science (Weeks 15-20)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Garden planning materials, seed inventory, planting calendar
**Survival Relevance**: Crop rotation prevents soil depletion, breaks pest cycles, and maintains long-term productivity — without it, yields decline year after year

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain why crop rotation works (nutrient management, pest disruption, disease prevention)
- Design a 3-4 year rotation plan using crop families
- Integrate cover crops into the rotation
- Monitor and adjust rotation based on results

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Why Rotate?**
- Activity: "If you plant tomatoes in the same spot every year: the soil loses the nutrients tomatoes need, tomato-specific pests build up, tomato diseases persist in the soil. After 3 years, yield crashes." Rotation breaks all three cycles.
- Key Concepts: Monoculture depletes soil and breeds pests, different crops have different nutrient needs, rotation is free insurance
- Practice: Identify 3 problems caused by not rotating and explain how rotation addresses each

**Lesson 3-4: Crop Families and Rotation Groups**
- Activity: Group crops by family: Legumes (beans, peas — FIX nitrogen), Nightshades (tomatoes, peppers, potatoes — heavy feeders), Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli, kale), Cucurbits (squash, cucumber, melon), Roots (carrots, beets, onions), Grains (corn, wheat).
- Key Concepts: Rotate by family — don't follow one family with another from the same family, legumes before heavy feeders (they add nitrogen)
- Practice: Design a 4-year rotation plan for 4 garden beds

**Lesson 5-6: Cover Crops and Green Manure**
- Activity: Plant cover crops on fallow beds — clover or vetch (nitrogen fixation), rye or oats (organic matter), buckwheat (weed suppression). Chop and incorporate before planting next crop.
- Key Concepts: Cover crops protect and improve soil during off-season, they prevent erosion, suppress weeds, and add nutrients, they are "free fertilizer"
- Practice: Plan cover crop integration into the rotation calendar

#### Assessment
- Explain the science behind crop rotation
- Design a multi-year rotation plan for community garden beds
- Integrate cover crops into a rotation calendar

### Unit 4: Integrated Pest Management (Weeks 21-26)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Hand lenses, field guides for pest identification, companion planting references, garden access
**Survival Relevance**: Pest damage can destroy a harvest. IPM controls pests sustainably without relying on chemical inputs that may not be available.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify common garden pests and their damage patterns
- Identify beneficial organisms (predators, parasitoids, pollinators)
- Implement IPM strategies (cultural, biological, physical, chemical as last resort)
- Monitor pest populations and make management decisions

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Pest Identification**
- Activity: Survey community gardens for pests. Identify common ones: aphids, caterpillars, beetles, slugs, mites. Document damage patterns — chewed leaves, stippled leaves, wilting, tunneling.
- Key Concepts: Correct identification is essential — don't kill beneficials thinking they're pests, damage patterns help identify the cause
- Practice: Identify and document 5 pest species and their damage

**Lesson 3-4: Beneficial Organisms**
- Activity: Survey for beneficials: ladybugs (eat aphids), lacewings (eat aphids, mites), praying mantis (eat many pests), ground beetles (eat slugs), parasitic wasps (control caterpillars), bees (pollination), earthworms (soil health).
- Key Concepts: Most insects are beneficial or neutral, protecting beneficials is often better than attacking pests, biodiversity is natural pest control
- Practice: Identify and document 5 beneficial species and their roles

**Lesson 5-6: IPM Strategies**
- Activity: Implement a tiered approach: Cultural (crop rotation, resistant varieties, proper spacing, good sanitation). Biological (encourage predators, companion planting, beneficial insect habitat). Physical (handpicking, barriers, traps, row covers). Chemical only as last resort (soap spray, neem, pyrethrin — all plant-derived).
- Key Concepts: Start with the least invasive method, escalate only if needed, prevention is better than reaction, monitoring tells you when action is needed
- Practice: Design an IPM plan for the community garden

#### Assessment
- Identify 5 common pests and 5 beneficial organisms
- Design an IPM plan using all four strategy tiers
- Monitor a garden area and make evidence-based pest management decisions

### Unit 5: Greenhouse Basics (Weeks 27-34)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Clear plastic or glass, frame materials (wood, pipe), thermometer, ventilation design, seeds for starting
**Survival Relevance**: Greenhouses extend the growing season, protect tender crops, and enable year-round food production in cold climates

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain how a greenhouse works (solar heating, trapped warm air, wind protection)
- Design a simple greenhouse appropriate for the community's climate
- Manage greenhouse conditions (temperature, humidity, ventilation)
- Start seedlings and grow season-extending crops

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Greenhouse Science**
- Activity: Demonstrate the greenhouse effect — place a thermometer inside a clear container in sunlight and one outside. The container is warmer. "Sunlight enters, heats the ground and plants inside, heat is trapped by the clear cover."
- Key Concepts: Greenhouses work by trapping solar heat, they extend growing season both earlier and later, they protect from frost, wind, and heavy rain
- Practice: Measure temperature differences between inside and outside a demonstration enclosure

**Lesson 3-4: Greenhouse Design**
- Activity: Design a greenhouse for the community: size, orientation (long axis east-west for maximum sun in cold climates), materials (salvaged windows, clear plastic, glass), foundation, ventilation (overheating is the biggest problem).
- Key Concepts: Orientation and ventilation are the critical design decisions, thermal mass (water barrels, stone floor) moderates temperature swings
- Practice: Create a greenhouse design with materials list and estimated dimensions

**Lesson 5-6: Managing Growing Conditions**
- Activity: Practice daily greenhouse management: open vents when too hot (above 30°C), close at night, water based on soil moisture not schedule, monitor for pest buildup (greenhouses are warm and moist — pests love them).
- Key Concepts: Greenhouses require daily management, temperature and humidity swings damage crops, pest monitoring is critical in enclosed spaces
- Practice: Manage a greenhouse or cold frame for 2 weeks with daily records

**Lesson 7-8: Season Extension**
- Activity: Start seedlings 6-8 weeks before last frost date. Grow cold-hardy crops (lettuce, spinach, kale, peas) through cold months. Calculate: how many additional growing weeks does the greenhouse provide?
- Key Concepts: Season extension can double the usable growing period, cold-hardy crops are the best greenhouse investments, timing seedlings to transplant date matters
- Practice: Start a full tray of seedlings and manage through to transplant-ready stage

#### Assessment
- Explain greenhouse science and design principles
- Design a greenhouse appropriate for the community's climate
- Manage greenhouse conditions and start seedlings successfully

## Grade 7: Livestock and Food Preservation

### Unit 6: Livestock Management (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Access to community livestock (chickens, rabbits, goats), feed supplies, housing materials
**Survival Relevance**: Livestock provide eggs, meat, milk, fiber, leather, manure, and pest control. Proper management maximizes output and prevents disease.

#### Learning Objectives
- Manage daily care for chickens, rabbits, and goats (or available livestock)
- Understand nutrition requirements for each species
- Recognize signs of illness and provide basic veterinary first aid
- Design appropriate housing for livestock

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Chicken Management**
- Activity: Daily routine: collect eggs, fill water, provide feed (grain + kitchen scraps + grit), clean coop weekly, check for predator damage, observe bird behavior for health signs.
- Key Concepts: Chickens are the easiest livestock — they provide eggs daily, eat scraps, and produce manure for gardens, healthy chickens are active and vocal
- Practice: Manage the chicken flock for 1 week independently

**Lesson 3-4: Rabbit Management**
- Activity: Daily: fresh water, hay (primary diet), greens, pellets if available. Weekly: clean hutch, check teeth (overgrowth is common), observe behavior. Rabbits reproduce quickly — breeding management is essential.
- Key Concepts: Rabbits convert plant matter to protein efficiently, they reproduce rapidly, housing must protect from predators and weather
- Practice: Design an optimal rabbit housing system and manage rabbits for 1 week

**Lesson 5-6: Goat Management**
- Activity: Daily: water, browse/hay, grain supplement for milking does. Monitor: body condition, hoof health, parasite load (check eyelid color — FAMACHA method). Milking technique if applicable.
- Key Concepts: Goats are versatile (milk, meat, brush clearing, fiber) but need more management than chickens, parasites are the primary health challenge, fencing must be very secure
- Practice: Participate in daily goat care for 2 weeks

**Lesson 7-8: Livestock Health**
- Activity: Recognize illness signs: lethargy, loss of appetite, abnormal droppings, discharge, limping, isolation from flock/herd. Basic interventions: wound care, parasite treatment (if medications available), isolation of sick animals, nutrition support.
- Key Concepts: Early illness detection prevents losses, quarantine prevents spread, prevention (good nutrition, clean housing, parasite management) is better than treatment
- Practice: Conduct a health assessment on 5 animals and create a health record for each

**Lesson 9-10: Breeding Basics**
- Activity: Understand breeding cycles for available livestock. Selection criteria: choose the healthiest, most productive animals. Record keeping: track lineage to prevent inbreeding. Timing: plan breeding to match food availability.
- Key Concepts: Selective breeding improves livestock over generations, inbreeding causes problems, records are essential for long-term management
- Practice: Create a breeding plan for one livestock species with selection criteria and timeline

#### Assessment
- Manage daily care for at least one livestock species independently
- Recognize 5 illness signs and describe appropriate responses
- Create a breeding plan with selection criteria

### Unit 7: Veterinary First Aid (Weeks 11-16)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Basic veterinary supplies (wound care, anti-parasitic treatments if available), restraint equipment
**Survival Relevance**: Losing livestock to preventable illness or treatable injury wastes resources that took months to build. Basic veterinary care preserves the community's animal investment.

#### Learning Objectives
- Restrain animals safely for examination
- Treat common livestock injuries (cuts, abscesses, foot problems)
- Administer basic treatments (deworming, wound cleaning, hoof trimming)
- Know when veterinary expertise is needed vs what can be managed

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Animal Restraint and Examination**
- Activity: Safe handling: approach calmly, use low voice, move slowly. Restrain chickens (wrap in towel, hold wings), rabbits (scruff + support hind legs), goats (halter, horn-hold if horned, stanchion if available). Conduct a head-to-tail physical exam.
- Key Concepts: Safe restraint protects both the animal and handler, stressed animals are harder to examine, routine handling makes animals easier to manage
- Practice: Safely restrain and examine 3 different animals

**Lesson 3-4: Wound Care and Common Problems**
- Activity: Clean wounds with saline or dilute antiseptic. Remove debris. For abscesses: lance (if trained), drain, flush, keep open to drain. Hoof care: trim overgrown hooves, treat foot rot (trim, disinfect, dry environment).
- Key Concepts: Wound care principles are the same for animals as humans (clean, protect, monitor), foot problems are the most common livestock issue
- Practice: Demonstrate wound care and hoof trimming techniques

**Lesson 5-6: Parasite Management and Limitations**
- Activity: Internal parasites are the #1 livestock health problem. FAMACHA scoring for goats (eyelid color indicates anemia from blood-sucking parasites). Rotational grazing breaks parasite cycles. Deworming medication if available.
- Key Concepts: Parasite management is ongoing not one-time, rotational grazing is the best prevention, know when you're out of your depth — some conditions need trained veterinary care
- Practice: Score 5 animals for parasite load and create a management plan

#### Assessment
- Safely restrain and examine livestock
- Demonstrate wound care technique
- Create a parasite management plan for community livestock

### Unit 8: Food Preservation (Weeks 17-26)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Preservation supplies (salt, sugar, vinegar, smoke setup, drying racks, fermentation vessels, canning jars if available)
**Survival Relevance**: Without preservation, harvest surplus rots within days. Preservation converts seasonal abundance into year-round food security.

#### Learning Objectives
- Preserve food using 5+ methods (drying, smoking, salting, fermenting, pickling, canning)
- Understand the science behind each method (what prevents spoilage)
- Assess preservation quality and safety
- Plan a preservation calendar matching harvest to preservation method

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Drying**
- Activity: Dry herbs (hang in bundles), fruits (slice thin, lay on racks in sun or near heat), vegetables (slice thin, dry completely). Test for dryness — should snap, not bend.
- Key Concepts: Drying removes water that bacteria need, properly dried food lasts months to years, rehydrate before cooking
- Practice: Dry 3 different food items and test for quality

**Lesson 3-4: Smoking**
- Activity: Build or use a simple smoker (fire pit + frame + cloth cover). Cold smoking (for preservation — days at low temperature) vs hot smoking (for cooking — hours at higher temperature). Smoke fish, meat, or vegetables.
- Key Concepts: Smoke contains antimicrobial compounds, combined with salt curing it preserves for months, wood type affects flavor
- Practice: Smoke-preserve one batch of meat or fish

**Lesson 5-6: Salt Curing and Fermentation**
- Activity: Salt curing: pack meat or fish in heavy salt — draws out water and inhibits bacteria. Fermentation: sauerkraut (cabbage + salt → lactobacillus), kimchi, yogurt, sourdough. "Fermentation uses GOOD bacteria to prevent BAD bacteria."
- Key Concepts: Salt creates an environment hostile to spoilage organisms, fermentation creates acid that preserves, fermented foods have nutritional benefits (probiotics, vitamin production)
- Practice: Create at least one salt-cured and one fermented product

**Lesson 7-8: Pickling and Vinegar Preservation**
- Activity: Vinegar pickling: submerge vegetables in vinegar brine (vinegar + water + salt + optional spices). Quick pickles (refrigerate, eat within weeks) vs shelf-stable pickles (proper acidity required).
- Key Concepts: Acidity below pH 4.6 prevents botulism, vinegar provides the acid, correct ratios are not optional — they're safety requirements
- Practice: Create pickled vegetables and test pH

**Lesson 9-10: Preservation Calendar**
- Activity: Match each harvest crop to the best preservation method. Create a calendar: "June — dry herbs, pickle early cucumbers. August — smoke excess fish, ferment cabbage. September — dry beans, salt-cure meat for winter." Calculate: how much preserved food is needed for the lean months?
- Key Concepts: Preservation must be planned in advance, seasonal gluts are the opportunity, running out in lean months is the risk
- Practice: Create a complete preservation calendar for the community

#### Assessment
- Preserve food using at least 3 different methods with safe technique
- Explain the science behind each preservation method
- Create a preservation calendar matching harvest to method

## Grade 8: Farm Design and Agricultural Economics

### Unit 9: Farm Planning and Design (Weeks 1-12)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Graph paper, measuring tools, community agricultural land access, seed inventory, climate data
**Survival Relevance**: This is the capstone — designing a complete farm system to feed a community. This is the skill that determines whether the community eats or goes hungry.

#### Learning Objectives
- Design a farm to feed 50 people using available land
- Integrate crops, livestock, water management, and soil building
- Apply permaculture design principles (zones, sectors, stacking functions)
- Create a complete annual farm plan with planting, harvest, and preservation calendars

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Needs Assessment**
- Activity: 50 people need approximately 2,000 calories per person per day = 100,000 calories per day. How much land is needed? What crops produce the most calories per acre? (Potatoes, corn, beans, squash are among the highest.) What livestock contributes?
- Key Concepts: Start with caloric needs, work backward to land requirements, diversity prevents single-point failure
- Practice: Calculate caloric needs and required production for 50 people

**Lesson 3-4: Site Analysis**
- Activity: Survey available agricultural land: soil quality (test it), water access, sun exposure, slope, existing vegetation, microclimates, proximity to community, wildlife pressure. Map everything.
- Key Concepts: Design follows the site — don't fight the landscape, work with it, a thorough site analysis prevents expensive mistakes
- Practice: Complete a site analysis map with all factors documented

**Lesson 5-6: Permaculture Zones**
- Activity: Zone 0: home/buildings. Zone 1: daily-visit areas (kitchen garden, herb spiral, chicken coop). Zone 2: regular maintenance (main garden, orchard, staple crops). Zone 3: occasional management (grain fields, grazing). Zone 4: minimal management (managed forest, wild harvest). Zone 5: wilderness (observation only).
- Key Concepts: Place things based on how often you visit them, energy efficiency drives design, every element should serve multiple functions
- Practice: Design a zone map for the community's agricultural areas

**Lesson 7-8: Integration Design**
- Activity: Connect all elements: chicken manure → compost → garden → food scraps → chickens. Fruit trees provide shade for heat-sensitive crops. Goats clear brush in Zone 4. Bees pollinate orchard and provide honey. Every output becomes an input.
- Key Concepts: Integration reduces waste and inputs, ecosystem thinking applies to farm design, the whole is greater than the sum of parts
- Practice: Create an integration diagram showing resource flows between all farm elements

**Lesson 9-10: Annual Farm Calendar**
- Activity: Create a complete annual plan: when to start seeds, when to transplant, when each crop is ready, when to harvest, when to preserve, when to plant cover crops, when to rotate livestock, when to maintain equipment.
- Key Concepts: Timing is everything in farming, a calendar prevents missed windows, flexibility accounts for weather variation
- Practice: Create a week-by-week farm calendar for one full year

**Lesson 11-12: Farm Plan Presentation**
- Activity: Compile all elements — needs assessment, site analysis, zone design, integration plan, annual calendar, contingency plans — into a complete farm plan. Present to community agriculture leadership.
- Key Concepts: A complete plan is the foundation of successful farming, plans must be communicated and accepted to be implemented
- Practice: Present and defend the farm plan

#### Assessment
- Calculate food production requirements for a community
- Design a complete farm layout using permaculture principles
- Create an annual farm calendar with planting, harvest, and preservation schedules
- Present and defend the complete farm plan

### Unit 10: Seed Science, Aquaculture, and Agricultural Economics (Weeks 13-24)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Seeds, microscope or magnification, pond or tank access (for aquaculture), economic planning materials
**Survival Relevance**: Seed sovereignty, protein from fish, and economic viability determine long-term food security

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Seed Science**
- Activity: Seed anatomy (embryo, endosperm, seed coat). Germination requirements (water, temperature, sometimes light or cold period). Seed viability testing — plant 10 seeds, count germination. Seed saving: open-pollinated vs hybrid (only open-pollinated breeds true).
- Key Concepts: Seeds are the foundation of agriculture, seed sovereignty means the community controls its own seed supply, hybrid seeds don't breed true

**Lesson 3-4: Seed Genetics**
- Activity: Apply Punnett squares to plant breeding. Understand cross-pollination and isolation distances. Practice seed selection for desired traits (disease resistance, yield, early maturity, flavor).
- Key Concepts: Intentional seed selection IS genetic improvement, isolation prevents unwanted crosses, long-term seed programs build varieties adapted to local conditions
- Practice: Design a seed selection and saving program for 5 key crops

**Lesson 5-6: Aquaculture Basics**
- Activity: Fish farming in ponds or tanks. Species selection (tilapia, catfish, trout depending on climate). Feeding (commercial feed, kitchen waste, insect larvae, duckweed). Water quality management (aeration, waste removal, temperature).
- Key Concepts: Aquaculture produces protein efficiently, fish convert feed to meat better than any land animal, water quality is the critical management factor
- Practice: Design an aquaculture system appropriate for the community

**Lesson 7-8: Agricultural Economics**
- Activity: Calculate yield per acre, labor per crop, caloric value per labor hour. Compare crops by efficiency. Which crops produce the most food per unit of land? Per unit of labor? Per unit of water?
- Key Concepts: Not all crops are equal — efficiency metrics guide planting decisions, labor is usually the limiting factor, economic analysis optimizes resource allocation
- Practice: Create an efficiency comparison for 10 community crops

**Lesson 9-10: Farm Financial Planning**
- Activity: Create a farm budget: inputs (seeds, amendments, labor, water, tools), outputs (food produced, trade surplus). Calculate: is the farm operation sustainable? What investments would improve efficiency?
- Key Concepts: Even without money, farms have economics — resource in, product out, sustainability means not depleting inputs
- Practice: Create a farm budget and identify the top 3 efficiency improvements

**Lesson 11-12: Agricultural Capstone**
- Activity: Integrate all agricultural knowledge into a comprehensive food security plan for the community: current production, current consumption, gap analysis, improvement priorities, implementation timeline, contingency for crop failure.
- Key Concepts: Food security is the community's top priority, a comprehensive plan addresses production, preservation, distribution, and contingency
- Practice: Present food security plan to community leadership

#### Assessment
- Demonstrate seed saving and viability testing
- Design an aquaculture system with management plan
- Create a farm budget with efficiency analysis
- Present a comprehensive food security plan
