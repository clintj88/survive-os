# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Earth Science

## Overview
Earth science connects students to the physical world they depend on — geology reveals where to find useful materials, meteorology enables weather prediction, hydrology manages water resources, and astronomy enables navigation. Every unit has direct survival application.

## Grade 6: Geology and Earth's Structure

### Unit 1: Plate Tectonics (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Maps showing tectonic plates, clay or cardboard for modeling, sandpaper blocks for friction demonstration
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding plate tectonics explains earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, mountain formation, and mineral distribution — essential knowledge for site selection and hazard awareness

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the structure of Earth (crust, mantle, outer core, inner core)
- Explain plate tectonic theory and the three types of plate boundaries
- Connect plate tectonics to earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain formation
- Assess geological hazards in the community's region

#### Teacher Notes
Plate tectonics is one of science's most powerful unifying theories — it explains why mountains exist, why earthquakes happen, where minerals concentrate, and why continents have their current shapes. Make it concrete with local examples.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Earth's Layers**
- Activity: Model Earth's layers with clay — thin crust (5-70 km), thick mantle (2,900 km), liquid outer core (2,200 km), solid inner core (1,200 km radius). The crust is proportionally thinner than an eggshell on an egg.
- Key Concepts: Earth is layered by density, the crust is the only part we interact with, heat from the core drives geological processes
- Practice: Draw and label Earth's layers with correct proportions

**Lesson 3-4: Plate Boundaries**
- Activity: Model three boundary types with cardboard pieces. Divergent (pulling apart — new crust forms, like Mid-Atlantic Ridge). Convergent (pushing together — mountains form or one plate subducts). Transform (sliding past — earthquakes, like San Andreas Fault).
- Key Concepts: Earth's crust is broken into plates that move, boundary interactions cause geological events
- Practice: Identify plate boundary types on a world map and predict geological activity at each

**Lesson 5-6: Hazard Assessment**
- Activity: Research the geological setting of your community's region. What plate boundary is nearest? What historical earthquakes or volcanic events have occurred? What is the local earthquake and volcano risk?
- Key Concepts: Geological hazard assessment informs community safety planning, proximity to plate boundaries indicates risk level
- Practice: Create a geological hazard report for the community's region

#### Assessment
- Describe Earth's layers and their characteristics
- Explain three types of plate boundaries with examples
- Complete a geological hazard assessment for the local area

### Unit 2: Rock Cycle and Mineralogy (Weeks 7-14)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Rock and mineral samples (collected locally), streak plates (unglazed tile), hardness testing materials (fingernail, copper coin, steel nail, glass), dilute acid (vinegar) for carbonate testing
**Survival Relevance**: Identifying rocks and minerals is essential for finding building stone, clay for pottery, flint for tools, limestone for mortar, iron ore, and other resources

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the three rock types (igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic) and how they form
- Explain the rock cycle
- Identify 10+ minerals using physical properties (hardness, streak, luster, cleavage)
- Identify useful rocks and minerals in the local area

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Three Rock Types**
- Activity: Examine samples. Igneous (formed from cooled magma — granite, basalt, obsidian). Sedimentary (formed from compressed layers — sandstone, limestone, shale). Metamorphic (transformed by heat and pressure — marble from limestone, slate from shale, quartzite from sandstone).
- Key Concepts: All rocks belong to one of three types, formation process determines properties
- Practice: Classify 10 local rock samples by type

**Lesson 3-4: The Rock Cycle**
- Activity: Diagram the rock cycle — magma → igneous (cooling) → sediment (weathering/erosion) → sedimentary (compaction/cementation) → metamorphic (heat/pressure) → magma (melting). Any rock can become any other type given enough time and the right conditions.
- Key Concepts: Rocks are continuously recycled over geological time, the cycle connects all three types
- Practice: Draw and label the rock cycle, trace a rock through multiple transformations

**Lesson 5-6: Mineral Identification**
- Activity: Test minerals using physical properties. Hardness (Mohs scale — fingernail ~2.5, copper coin ~3.5, steel nail ~5.5, glass ~5.5). Streak (color of powder on unglazed tile). Luster (metallic vs nonmetallic). Cleavage (how it breaks). Acid test (fizzes = calcite/limestone).
- Key Concepts: Systematic testing identifies minerals reliably, multiple tests are needed, identification skills locate resources
- Practice: Identify 10 minerals using the testing procedure

**Lesson 7-8: Useful Local Resources**
- Activity: Survey the community's area for useful geological resources. Clay (pottery, bricks). Sand (construction, glassmaking). Limestone (mortar, soil amendment). Flint/chert (tools, fire-starting). Iron-bearing rocks. Building stone. Coal or peat.
- Key Concepts: Geological resources are essential for community self-sufficiency, knowing your local geology helps find them
- Practice: Create a local geological resource map with identified materials and potential uses

#### Assessment
- Classify rocks by type and explain their formation
- Identify 10 minerals using physical properties
- Map local geological resources with documented potential uses

### Unit 3: Fossil Record and Geological Time (Weeks 15-20)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Fossil samples (if available), diagrams, timeline materials
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding geological time provides perspective on environmental change, climate variation, and the long-term consequences of resource decisions

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain how fossils form and what they tell us
- Describe the major eras of geological time and their key organisms
- Understand relative dating (older layers are deeper) and radioactive dating concept
- Connect geological history to current conditions (why resources are where they are)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How Fossils Form**
- Activity: Simulate fossilization — press shells or leaves into clay (mold), fill with plaster (cast). "Fossils are natural records of ancient life — they form when organisms are buried in sediment before they decompose."
- Key Concepts: Fossils require specific conditions (quick burial, hard parts), they are rare, each one is evidence of past life
- Practice: Create mold and cast fossils, examine any real fossils available

**Lesson 3-4: Geological Time Scale**
- Activity: Create a geological timeline on a long paper strip or rope. Scale: 1 cm = 10 million years. Earth is 4.6 billion years old (460 cm = 4.6 m). First life: 3.5 billion years ago. First animals: 600 million years ago. Dinosaurs: 230-66 million years ago. Humans: 0.3 cm at the very end.
- Key Concepts: Earth is ancient, life has existed for most of Earth's history, humans are very recent, geological processes are slow but powerful
- Practice: Label major events on the timeline with correct proportional positioning

**Lesson 5-6: What the Past Tells Us**
- Activity: "Why is there coal in some places? Because 300 million years ago, those areas were tropical swamps. Why is there limestone here? Because this was once an ocean floor. Why are there fossils of marine animals on mountaintops? Because the mountains were pushed up from the sea floor."
- Key Concepts: Current geology reflects ancient conditions, understanding this helps locate resources and understand landscapes
- Practice: Research the geological history of the local area and connect it to current conditions

#### Assessment
- Explain fossil formation and significance
- Place major events on a geological timeline
- Connect local geological conditions to their historical origin

## Grade 7: Meteorology, Climate, and Hydrology

### Unit 4: Weather Systems and Forecasting (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Thermometer, barometer (or improvised: jar with balloon stretched over top), rain gauge (any graduated container), wind vane (handmade), weather journal
**Survival Relevance**: Weather forecasting without technology requires understanding weather patterns — essential for agriculture, travel planning, construction, and emergency preparation

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain how air pressure, temperature, and humidity create weather
- Describe weather fronts (warm, cold, stationary, occluded) and the weather they bring
- Forecast weather using observations and instruments
- Maintain a weather journal and identify patterns

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Creates Weather**
- Activity: Heat air in a jar, cool air in another. Hot air rises (low pressure), cold air sinks (high pressure). Air moves from high to low pressure = wind. Add water (evaporation, condensation) = clouds and precipitation.
- Key Concepts: Weather is driven by solar heating, air pressure differences, and water cycling through the atmosphere
- Practice: Explain the basic weather system in your own words with a diagram

**Lesson 3-4: Weather Fronts**
- Activity: Model fronts using warm and cold water with food coloring in a tank. Cold front — cold air pushes under warm air (fast, sharp storms). Warm front — warm air rides over cold air (slow, long precipitation). Observe how they interact.
- Key Concepts: Fronts are boundaries between air masses, fronts cause most significant weather, cold fronts move faster than warm fronts
- Practice: Identify front types from weather descriptions and predict resulting weather

**Lesson 5-6: Weather Instruments and Observation**
- Activity: Build or use: thermometer (temperature), barometer (air pressure — rising = fair weather, falling = storms coming), rain gauge (precipitation), wind vane (wind direction), anemometer or Beaufort scale (wind speed estimation).
- Key Concepts: Instruments extend our senses, trends matter more than single readings, falling pressure is the most reliable storm predictor
- Practice: Take and record weather measurements twice daily for 2 weeks

**Lesson 7-8: Cloud Reading**
- Activity: Learn cloud types: cirrus (high, wispy — fair weather but change coming), cumulus (puffy — fair weather), stratus (flat, gray — drizzle or overcast), cumulonimbus (tall, dark — thunderstorms). Observe and identify clouds daily.
- Key Concepts: Cloud types predict weather 6-24 hours ahead, cloud progression tells a story (cirrus → stratus → rain often means a warm front approaching)
- Practice: Daily cloud observation and weather prediction based on clouds

**Lesson 9-10: Forecasting Without Technology**
- Activity: Combine all observations — pressure trends, cloud changes, wind shifts, temperature changes, animal behavior — to make 24-hour forecasts. Compare predictions to actual weather. Refine technique.
- Key Concepts: Pattern recognition is the foundation of forecasting, local knowledge improves accuracy over time, forecast confidence varies
- Practice: Make and verify daily forecasts for 2 weeks, track accuracy rate

#### Assessment
- Explain the basic mechanisms that create weather
- Identify weather fronts and predict their associated weather
- Forecast weather with 60%+ accuracy over a 2-week period using observation only

### Unit 5: Climate Science (Weeks 11-16)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Weather journal data, historical climate information, globe or world map
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding long-term climate patterns enables agricultural planning, water management, and adaptation to changing conditions

#### Learning Objectives
- Distinguish weather (short-term) from climate (long-term patterns)
- Explain what determines a region's climate (latitude, altitude, proximity to water, prevailing winds)
- Understand climate variability and its effects on communities
- Analyze the community's climate patterns for planning purposes

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Weather vs Climate**
- Activity: "Weather is what's happening outside right now. Climate is what you expect over many years." Use weather journal data — individual days vary, but patterns emerge over weeks and months.
- Key Concepts: Climate is the average of weather over 30+ years, climate determines what's possible (crops, building, water), weather determines daily decisions
- Practice: Calculate average temperature and rainfall from weather journal data

**Lesson 3-4: Climate Factors**
- Activity: Why is the equator hot and the poles cold? (Sun angle.) Why are coastal areas milder than inland? (Water moderates temperature.) Why is it colder at higher altitude? (Air pressure decreases, air expands and cools.) Why do some sides of mountains get more rain? (Rain shadow effect.)
- Key Concepts: Climate is determined by geographic factors, understanding these factors explains local climate
- Practice: Analyze the community's climate using these factors

**Lesson 5-6: Climate Variability**
- Activity: Discuss natural climate variation — El Niño/La Niña, volcanic eruptions affecting climate (Tambora 1815 → "Year Without a Summer"), ice ages, and ongoing climate change. What does variation mean for agricultural planning?
- Key Concepts: Climate is not static, communities must plan for variation and change, diversification and flexibility are adaptive strategies
- Practice: Research historical climate events that affected agriculture and draw lessons for community planning

#### Assessment
- Explain the difference between weather and climate with examples
- Analyze local climate using geographic factors
- Discuss climate variability and its implications for community planning

### Unit 6: Hydrology (Weeks 17-24)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Maps of local water features, water testing supplies (if available), soil samples for infiltration tests, rain gauge
**Survival Relevance**: Water is the most critical resource. Understanding where it comes from, where it goes, and how to maintain its quality is essential for community survival.

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the components of a watershed
- Explain groundwater (water table, aquifers, wells, springs)
- Understand water quality factors and basic testing
- Assess the community's water resources and recommend improvements

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Watersheds**
- Activity: Pour water on a landscape model (sand, clay). Watch where it flows — it follows gravity to the lowest point. Every stream, river, and pond is part of a watershed — all the land that drains to a common point.
- Key Concepts: Watersheds define water supply boundaries, what happens upstream affects downstream, protecting the watershed protects the water supply
- Practice: Map the community's watershed — where does water enter and exit?

**Lesson 3-4: Groundwater**
- Activity: Layer gravel, sand, and clay in a clear container. Pour water in — it flows through gravel and sand but stops at clay. The top of the saturated zone is the water table. Wells tap below the water table. Springs occur where the water table meets the surface.
- Key Concepts: Groundwater is stored in aquifers (permeable rock/soil), the water table rises and falls with rainfall, wells must reach below the water table, contamination can reach groundwater
- Practice: Determine the approximate water table depth in the community's area

**Lesson 5-6: Water Quality**
- Activity: Test water from multiple sources (well, stream, rain barrel) for: clarity (turbidity), pH (acid/base), temperature, dissolved solids (taste/evaporation residue). If test kits available: coliform bacteria, nitrates, hardness.
- Key Concepts: Water quality determines safety, different contaminants require different treatments, regular testing catches problems early
- Practice: Test water from 3 sources and compare results

**Lesson 7-8: Community Water Assessment**
- Activity: Comprehensive assessment: water sources (type, volume, reliability), quality (testing results), treatment methods (filtration, boiling, chemical, solar), distribution system, wastewater management, drought vulnerability.
- Key Concepts: Water security requires multiple sources, treatment before use, protecting supply from contamination, and planning for drought
- Practice: Complete a community water assessment with findings and recommendations

#### Assessment
- Describe watershed components and map the local watershed
- Explain groundwater concepts and water quality factors
- Complete a community water assessment with actionable recommendations

### Unit 7: Soil Science Advanced (Weeks 25-30)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Soil samples from multiple locations, jars for settling tests, pH test strips, composting area access
**Survival Relevance**: Soil is the foundation of food production. Understanding soil science enables maintaining and improving soil fertility without industrial inputs.

#### Learning Objectives
- Classify soil types by texture (sand, silt, clay proportions)
- Test and interpret soil pH
- Understand soil nutrient cycling and the role of organic matter
- Develop a soil management plan for community agriculture

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Soil Texture**
- Activity: Jar settling test — fill a jar 1/3 with soil, add water, shake, let settle. Sand settles first (bottom), then silt (middle), then clay (top). Measure layers to determine soil composition.
- Key Concepts: Soil texture affects water retention, drainage, and workability, ideal garden soil is loam (balanced sand, silt, clay)
- Practice: Test soil from 3 locations and classify by texture

**Lesson 3-4: Soil pH and Nutrients**
- Activity: Test soil pH with strips or indicator solution. Most crops prefer pH 6.0-7.0. Too acidic: add lime. Too alkaline: add sulfur or organic matter. Discuss macro-nutrients (N, P, K) and their roles.
- Key Concepts: pH affects nutrient availability, most nutrient problems are actually pH problems, organic matter improves both acid and alkaline soils
- Practice: Test pH at 5 locations and recommend amendments

**Lesson 5-6: Soil Management Plan**
- Activity: For the community's agricultural areas, develop a comprehensive soil management plan: composting program, cover cropping, crop rotation for soil health, mulching, erosion prevention, soil testing schedule.
- Key Concepts: Soil is a living ecosystem, it can be improved or destroyed by management choices, building soil is building food security
- Practice: Present soil management plan to community agriculture team

#### Assessment
- Classify soil types using the jar settling method
- Test and interpret soil pH with amendment recommendations
- Develop a comprehensive soil management plan

## Grade 8: Astronomy, Natural Hazards, and Resources

### Unit 8: Astronomy (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Clear night sky access, star charts (drawn), protractor for angle measurement, sticks for shadow tracking
**Survival Relevance**: Astronomy enables navigation without technology, seasonal planning without calendars, and time-keeping without clocks

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the solar system (planets, orbits, scale)
- Explain seasons, moon phases, eclipses, and tides
- Use stars for navigation (building on Building Band skills)
- Use astronomical observations for calendar-keeping and time-telling

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Solar System**
- Activity: Scale model — if the sun is a basketball, Earth is a peppercorn 26 meters away. Jupiter is a walnut 135 meters away. The solar system is mostly empty space. Discuss each planet briefly.
- Key Concepts: Scale and distance, planets orbit the sun, inner rocky planets vs outer gas giants
- Practice: Create a scale model and memorize planet order

**Lesson 3-4: Seasons and Earth's Tilt**
- Activity: Use a ball (Earth) and a light (sun). Tilt the ball 23.5°. Orbit around the light. When tilted toward: summer (more direct sunlight, longer days). Tilted away: winter (less direct, shorter days). This explains seasons.
- Key Concepts: Seasons are caused by Earth's axial tilt, NOT distance from the sun, opposite hemispheres have opposite seasons
- Practice: Track shadow length at noon over several weeks — changing length demonstrates seasonal change

**Lesson 5-6: Moon Phases and Eclipses**
- Activity: Use a ball (moon) and light (sun). Walk around a central point (Earth). Observe how the illuminated portion changes — new moon, crescent, quarter, gibbous, full, then back. Takes 29.5 days. Eclipses: solar (moon between Earth and sun) and lunar (Earth between sun and moon).
- Key Concepts: Moon phases are caused by viewing angle, the lunar cycle is a natural calendar, eclipses are predictable alignment events
- Practice: Track moon phases for one full cycle (29 days)

**Lesson 7-8: Navigation by Stars (Advanced)**
- Activity: Build on Building Band skills. Determine latitude from Polaris altitude (angle above horizon = latitude). Tell time by star rotation (Big Dipper rotates 15° per hour around Polaris). Identify seasonal constellations.
- Key Concepts: Stars provide latitude, time, season, and direction, these skills require no technology
- Practice: Determine latitude using Polaris, estimate time using stellar rotation

**Lesson 9-10: Astronomical Calendar**
- Activity: Build a simple sundial for time. Track solstice and equinox dates using shadow length. "For most of human history, astronomy WAS the calendar. No electronics needed."
- Key Concepts: Astronomical observations track time and seasons reliably, every agricultural civilization developed astronomical calendars
- Practice: Construct a sundial and track shadow length at noon for seasonal change

#### Assessment
- Describe the solar system at correct scale
- Explain seasons, moon phases, and eclipses with demonstrations
- Determine latitude and estimate time using astronomical observations

### Unit 9: Natural Hazards (Weeks 11-18)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Maps, community emergency plans, historical hazard data
**Survival Relevance**: Identifying, preparing for, and responding to natural hazards is essential community safety planning

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify natural hazards relevant to the community's region
- Explain the science behind each hazard (earthquakes, floods, drought, storms)
- Develop warning signs and response protocols for each hazard
- Create or improve the community's natural hazard preparedness plan

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Earthquakes**
- Activity: Review plate tectonics (from Unit 1). Earthquakes occur at faults. Waves radiate from the epicenter. Building damage depends on construction type, distance, and soil type. Soft soil amplifies shaking (liquefaction).
- Key Concepts: Earthquakes are sudden but not random — they occur on known faults, building construction is the primary controllable factor in earthquake safety
- Practice: Assess community buildings for earthquake vulnerability

**Lesson 3-4: Floods and Drought**
- Activity: Study the local watershed. Where would flooding occur? (Low-lying areas, floodplains, areas downstream of impoundments.) What causes drought? (Prolonged precipitation deficit, groundwater depletion, deforestation reducing water retention.)
- Key Concepts: Floods and drought are opposites but both are water management problems, land use decisions affect both risks
- Practice: Map flood risk zones and drought vulnerability factors for the community

**Lesson 5-6: Severe Weather**
- Activity: Based on regional climate — what severe weather threatens? Thunderstorms, tornadoes, hurricanes, blizzards, ice storms, heat waves. For each relevant hazard: warning signs, safe locations, response protocol.
- Key Concepts: Different regions face different weather hazards, early warning depends on observation skills, shelter selection saves lives
- Practice: Create a severe weather response card for each relevant hazard

**Lesson 7-8: Community Hazard Plan**
- Activity: Comprehensive assessment — identify all natural hazards for the area, rate their probability and potential impact, map safe zones and evacuation routes, stockpile requirements, communication plan, special needs considerations.
- Key Concepts: Preparedness reduces casualties, plans must be specific and practiced, every community member should know the plan
- Practice: Present hazard preparedness plan to community leadership

#### Assessment
- Explain the science behind at least 3 natural hazards
- Identify hazards relevant to the community's region
- Complete a hazard preparedness plan with specific protocols

### Unit 10: Resource Geology (Weeks 19-26)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Rock and mineral samples, maps, digging tools for exploration
**Survival Relevance**: Locating and utilizing geological resources — clay, stone, metal ore, sand, gravel, coal, salt, flint — is essential for community self-sufficiency

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify geological resources present in the local area
- Understand how geological processes concentrate useful materials
- Prospect for resources using geological clues
- Assess resource quality and quantity for community use

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What We Need from the Ground**
- Activity: Inventory community needs that come from geological sources: building stone, clay for pottery/bricks, sand/gravel for construction, iron/steel (from ore or salvage), flint/chert for tools, limestone for mortar/soil amendment, salt, coal/peat for fuel.
- Key Concepts: Communities depend on geological resources, knowing what's available locally reduces trade dependence
- Practice: List all geological resources currently used by the community and their sources

**Lesson 3-4: How Resources Concentrate**
- Activity: Why are resources found in specific places? Stream deposits concentrate heavy minerals (gold, iron). Evaporation concentrates salt. Heat and pressure create metamorphic minerals. Volcanic activity brings metals near the surface.
- Key Concepts: Geological processes concentrate resources in predictable patterns, understanding these patterns guides exploration
- Practice: Predict where to find clay, sand, and metallic minerals based on local geology

**Lesson 5-6: Prospecting Skills**
- Activity: Field prospecting exercise — survey an area for useful materials. Look for: clay deposits (stream banks, exposed soil), usable stone (outcrops, river beds), sand and gravel (stream deposits, glacial deposits), mineral indicators.
- Key Concepts: Prospecting is systematic observation, geological maps help but field observation is essential
- Practice: Survey a defined area and map all identified resources

**Lesson 7-8: Resource Assessment**
- Activity: For each identified resource: estimate quantity available, assess quality (is it usable?), determine extraction difficulty, evaluate environmental impact of extraction, recommend sustainable use levels.
- Key Concepts: Resource assessment balances availability with sustainability, extraction always has environmental costs, planning prevents depletion
- Practice: Complete a resource assessment for at least 3 geological materials

#### Assessment
- Identify 5+ useful geological resources in the local area
- Explain how geological processes concentrate resources
- Complete a resource assessment with sustainable use recommendations
