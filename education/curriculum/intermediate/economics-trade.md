# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Economics & Trade

## Overview
Economics at the intermediate level focuses on practical skills — accounting, negotiation, market design, and community economic planning. Historical analysis of economic systems is covered in the History curriculum; this course teaches students to DO economics, not just study it. By Grade 8, students should be capable of managing community trade relationships and designing economic systems.

## Grade 6: Foundations of Exchange

### Unit 1: Barter and Exchange (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Trade simulation items (dried beans, stones, sticks representing different resources), ledger paper
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding exchange is fundamental to community survival — no community is fully self-sufficient, and trade is how gaps are filled

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain why exchange exists (specialization, resource distribution, comparative advantage)
- Conduct fair exchanges using barter
- Identify problems with pure barter (double coincidence of wants)
- Understand the concept of value (use value vs exchange value)

#### Teacher Notes
Cross-reference with History curriculum Theme 3 (Trade & Economics). This course covers practical skills; history covers historical analysis. Avoid duplication — focus on what students need to DO.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Why We Trade**
- Activity: Assign each student a "resource" (farmer produces grain, potter produces bowls, woodworker produces tools). No one can produce everything they need. "How do you get what you need?" Trade.
- Key Concepts: Specialization increases total production, trade distributes surplus, no community can produce everything
- Practice: Simulate a trade session — each student starts with one resource and must acquire what they need

**Lesson 3-4: The Barter Problem**
- Activity: "You have grain but need a bowl. The potter wants tools, not grain. The toolmaker wants grain. You need a three-way trade." Demonstrate the double coincidence of wants problem — both parties must want what the other has.
- Key Concepts: Barter works but is inefficient, complex trades require intermediaries, this problem led to the invention of money
- Practice: Solve 5 multi-party trade scenarios

**Lesson 5-6: What Is Value?**
- Activity: "Is water more valuable than gold? Depends on context. In the desert, water. At a trade fair, gold." Discuss use value (how useful something is) vs exchange value (what you can trade it for). What determines value? (Scarcity, need, labor to produce, alternatives available.)
- Key Concepts: Value is subjective and contextual, understanding value is the foundation of fair trade, both parties should benefit from trade
- Practice: Rank 10 items by use value and exchange value — discuss where they differ and why

#### Assessment
- Explain why communities trade with examples
- Conduct a multi-party barter exchange
- Analyze the value of 5 goods in different contexts

### Unit 2: Value, Scarcity, and Supply-Demand (Weeks 7-14)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Simulation supplies, graph paper, market simulation area
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding supply and demand helps communities price goods fairly, prevent hoarding, and manage scarce resources during crises

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain scarcity as the fundamental economic problem
- Graph and interpret supply and demand curves
- Predict price changes from supply or demand shifts
- Apply supply-demand thinking to community resource management

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Scarcity**
- Activity: "We have 100 liters of fuel this month. We need fuel for cooking, heating, generator, and transport. Total needs: 150 liters. We have a scarcity problem." Discuss: How do we decide who gets fuel? (Rationing, priority, price, lottery, need-based.)
- Key Concepts: Scarcity means wants exceed resources, every community faces scarcity, how you handle scarcity is an economic AND governance choice
- Practice: Propose and evaluate 3 different approaches to allocating a scarce community resource

**Lesson 3-4: Demand**
- Activity: "How many eggs would you buy at 1 trade-unit each? At 5? At 10?" Survey the class. Plot price vs quantity demanded — the curve slopes downward (higher price = less demand). Discuss: what shifts demand? (More people, changing tastes, income changes, availability of substitutes.)
- Key Concepts: Demand is the relationship between price and quantity people want to buy, the law of demand (higher price = lower quantity demanded), demand can shift
- Practice: Create demand curves for 3 community goods

**Lesson 5-6: Supply**
- Activity: "If you're a chicken farmer, how many eggs would you sell at 1 trade-unit? At 5? At 10?" Higher price = more incentive to produce. Plot supply curve — slopes upward. What shifts supply? (Better technology, more producers, input costs, weather.)
- Key Concepts: Supply is the relationship between price and quantity producers offer, the law of supply (higher price = higher quantity supplied), supply can shift
- Practice: Create supply curves for 3 community goods

**Lesson 7-8: Market Equilibrium**
- Activity: Overlay supply and demand curves. Where they cross is the equilibrium — the price where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. What happens at prices above equilibrium? (Surplus.) Below? (Shortage.) Simulate a market and watch prices converge.
- Key Concepts: Markets tend toward equilibrium, surpluses push prices down, shortages push prices up, disruptions (hoarding, price fixing) distort markets
- Practice: Find equilibrium for 3 scenarios and predict effects of supply/demand shifts

#### Assessment
- Graph supply and demand curves
- Find and interpret market equilibrium
- Predict the effect of supply or demand changes on price and quantity

### Unit 3: Community Resource Management (Weeks 15-22)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Community inventory data, planning templates, calculator
**Survival Relevance**: Managing community resources is applied economics — tracking what we have, projecting what we need, and making allocation decisions

#### Learning Objectives
- Conduct a resource inventory (what do we have, how much, how fast do we use it)
- Project future resource needs using consumption rates
- Design a resource allocation system (who gets what, how much, by what criteria)
- Identify resource surpluses and deficits for trade planning

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Resource Inventory**
- Activity: Create a complete inventory of a community resource category (food stores, medical supplies, tools, fuel). Count, measure, categorize, record. Calculate: how long will current supplies last at current consumption rate?
- Key Concepts: You can't manage what you don't measure, inventory is the foundation of planning, consumption rate determines urgency
- Practice: Complete an inventory with projected duration

**Lesson 3-4: Consumption Projection**
- Activity: Using consumption rate data, project resource levels over the next 3, 6, and 12 months. What runs out first? What do we have surplus of? When do we need to trade or produce more?
- Key Concepts: Projection reveals future problems before they're emergencies, seasonal variation affects consumption, margin for error is essential
- Practice: Create 12-month projections for 3 resource categories

**Lesson 5-6: Allocation Systems**
- Activity: Design a resource allocation system for a scarce item. Options: equal shares, need-based (more for workers, sick, children), contribution-based (more for producers), market-based (trade for it). Evaluate each for fairness, efficiency, and community impact.
- Key Concepts: There is no perfect allocation system, transparency builds trust, perceived fairness matters as much as actual fairness
- Practice: Propose and defend an allocation system for a real community resource

**Lesson 7-8: Trade Planning**
- Activity: Identify surpluses (what we produce more than we need) and deficits (what we need but don't produce enough). These determine what we should trade and what we need to acquire. Create a trade wish-list prioritized by urgency.
- Key Concepts: Trade planning starts from surplus/deficit analysis, knowing your trading position before negotiation is essential
- Practice: Create a community trade plan with surpluses, deficits, and priorities

#### Assessment
- Complete a resource inventory with consumption projections
- Design and justify a resource allocation system
- Create a trade plan based on surplus/deficit analysis

## Grade 7: Practical Economic Skills

### Unit 4: Accounting Basics (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Ledger books or paper, pencils, ruler
**Survival Relevance**: Accounting tracks resources accurately — without it, communities lose track of supplies, trades, and obligations, leading to conflict and waste

#### Learning Objectives
- Maintain a single-entry ledger (income, expense, balance)
- Understand double-entry bookkeeping concept (every transaction has two sides)
- Track inventory using a stock register
- Reconcile accounts (verify records match reality)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Single-Entry Ledger**
- Activity: Create a ledger for a small operation (garden, kitchen, workshop). Record all inputs (received) and outputs (used/traded). Calculate running balance. "At any point you should know exactly how much you have."
- Key Concepts: Record every transaction, date everything, balance tells you current status, a ledger is a memory aid for the community
- Practice: Maintain a ledger for 2 weeks with at least 20 entries

**Lesson 3-4: Double-Entry Concept**
- Activity: "When you trade 10 kg of grain for 2 kg of salt, two things happen: grain goes DOWN by 10, salt goes UP by 2. Every transaction affects at least two accounts." Demonstrate with T-accounts.
- Key Concepts: Double-entry ensures accuracy (debits must equal credits), it catches errors, it provides a complete picture of transactions
- Practice: Record 10 transactions using double-entry T-accounts

**Lesson 5-6: Inventory Tracking**
- Activity: Set up a stock register for a storage area. Record: item, date received, quantity, source, date issued, quantity, destination, running balance. Conduct a physical count and compare to records.
- Key Concepts: Stock registers prevent theft and waste, discrepancies require investigation, physical counts verify records
- Practice: Manage a stock register for 2 weeks and reconcile with physical count

**Lesson 7-8: Financial Reporting**
- Activity: Compile ledger data into a summary report: total inputs, total outputs, balance, trends, anomalies, projections. Present to class as if reporting to community leadership.
- Key Concepts: Reports make data useful for decision-makers, trends matter more than individual numbers, clarity is essential
- Practice: Create and present a financial summary report

#### Assessment
- Maintain an accurate ledger with proper entries
- Record transactions using double-entry concept
- Reconcile an inventory register with physical count

### Unit 5: Trade Negotiation (Weeks 9-16)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Trade simulation supplies, negotiation scenario cards
**Survival Relevance**: Trade negotiation is how communities acquire what they need — good negotiators get better terms, maintain relationships, and avoid exploitation

#### Learning Objectives
- Prepare for a negotiation (know your position, alternatives, and limits)
- Conduct a negotiation using principled techniques (interests over positions)
- Recognize and resist manipulative tactics
- Document trade agreements clearly

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Preparation**
- Activity: "You're trading community grain for a neighboring community's medical supplies. Before you meet: What do you need? How much? What can you offer? What's your best alternative if this trade fails (BATNA)? What's your walk-away point?"
- Key Concepts: Preparation determines outcome more than charisma, know your BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement), never negotiate unprepared
- Practice: Prepare negotiation briefs for 3 trade scenarios

**Lesson 3-4: Principled Negotiation**
- Activity: From "Getting to Yes" framework: Focus on interests (WHY you want something) not positions (WHAT you demand). Generate multiple options. Use objective criteria (fair market value, production cost, precedent).
- Key Concepts: Win-win outcomes build long-term relationships, positions create conflict — interests create solutions, creative options expand the pie
- Practice: Negotiate 3 scenarios using principled techniques

**Lesson 5-6: Recognizing Manipulation**
- Activity: Learn to recognize: anchoring (extreme first offer), time pressure ("decide now or lose the deal"), good cop/bad cop, false scarcity, emotional manipulation, take-it-or-leave-it.
- Key Concepts: Manipulative tactics work only if you don't recognize them, the best defense is preparation and willingness to walk away
- Practice: Identify manipulation tactics in 5 scenarios and practice countering them

**Lesson 7-8: Trade Agreements**
- Activity: Negotiate a complete trade deal. Document it: parties, items exchanged, quantities, quality standards, delivery terms, dispute resolution, duration. Both parties sign.
- Key Concepts: Written agreements prevent misunderstandings, clear terms prevent disputes, good agreements benefit both parties
- Practice: Negotiate and document 2 complete trade agreements

#### Assessment
- Prepare a thorough negotiation brief
- Conduct a negotiation using principled techniques
- Identify manipulation tactics and demonstrate resistance
- Document a clear, complete trade agreement

### Unit 6: Market Design and Labor Economics (Weeks 17-24)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Space for market simulation, role cards, trade goods
**Survival Relevance**: Designing fair markets and distributing labor equitably prevents exploitation, conflict, and waste — these are governance skills as much as economic ones

#### Learning Objectives
- Design a functioning community market (rules, schedules, dispute resolution)
- Understand labor economics (how work is valued, distributed, and compensated)
- Address common economic problems (hoarding, price gouging, free-riding)
- Create a labor distribution plan for a community

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Market Design**
- Activity: "We're going to create a weekly community market. What rules do we need?" Consider: who can trade, what can be traded, how are disputes resolved, is there a market manager, what about quality standards, what about people who can't trade (sick, elderly)?
- Key Concepts: Markets need rules to function fairly, unregulated markets create winners and losers, rules should promote fairness without stifling trade
- Practice: Design a complete market system with rules, roles, and dispute resolution

**Lesson 3-4: Labor Value**
- Activity: "Is an hour of farming worth the same as an hour of medical care? Why or why not?" Discuss: skill, training time, risk, demand, unpleasantness, physical difficulty. How should a community value different kinds of work?
- Key Concepts: Labor valuation is one of the hardest economic problems, there is no objectively correct answer, the system must be perceived as fair to work
- Practice: Propose and debate 3 different labor valuation systems

**Lesson 5-6: Economic Problems**
- Activity: Simulate hoarding (one person stockpiles and creates artificial scarcity), price gouging (charging extreme prices during emergencies), and free-riding (benefiting from community work without contributing). Discuss: how do we prevent these without excessive control?
- Key Concepts: These problems are as old as civilization, every community must design systems to address them, prevention is better than punishment
- Practice: Design anti-hoarding, anti-gouging, and anti-free-riding policies

**Lesson 7-8: Labor Distribution Plan**
- Activity: Design a labor allocation system for a community of 200 people. How many hours of work per adult per week? How are tasks assigned (volunteer, rotation, skill-based, elected)? How are unpleasant but necessary tasks handled? What about people who can't work?
- Key Concepts: Labor allocation is a governance decision, fairness and flexibility matter, everyone must see the system as legitimate
- Practice: Create and present a complete labor distribution plan

#### Assessment
- Design a community market with comprehensive rules
- Propose a labor valuation system with justification
- Create a labor distribution plan for a community

## Grade 8: Economic Systems and Planning

### Unit 7: Economic Systems Comparison (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Case study materials, simulation supplies, planning templates
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding different economic systems helps communities choose and adapt the system that best serves their survival needs

#### Learning Objectives
- Compare major economic systems by their mechanisms and outcomes (not ideology)
- Analyze the economic system of the community
- Identify strengths and weaknesses of the current system
- Propose evidence-based improvements

#### Teacher Notes
Present systems as tools with tradeoffs, not as ideologies to support or oppose. Cross-reference History curriculum Unit 2 (Economics) for historical examples.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Market Systems**
- Activity: Simulate a free market — prices set by supply and demand, individuals make decisions, profits motivate production. Observe outcomes: some people thrive, some struggle, resources flow to highest bidder, innovation is high.
- Key Concepts: Market systems are efficient at allocating resources, they reward innovation but create inequality, they require regulation to prevent exploitation
- Practice: Identify 3 strengths and 3 weaknesses of market systems with evidence

**Lesson 3-4: Command Systems**
- Activity: Simulate a command economy — central authority allocates resources, sets prices, assigns work. Observe outcomes: equality of distribution but potential inefficiency, reduced incentive for innovation, corruption risk.
- Key Concepts: Command systems can ensure basic needs but reduce individual choice, information problems make central planning difficult at scale
- Practice: Identify 3 strengths and 3 weaknesses of command systems with evidence

**Lesson 5-6: Mixed and Commons-Based Systems**
- Activity: Most real economies are mixed — some market elements, some central planning, some commons management. Study Elinor Ostrom's commons governance principles. Apply to community shared resources.
- Key Concepts: No pure system works perfectly, mixing elements from different systems is practical, commons governance works for shared resources
- Practice: Analyze the community's current economic system and identify its elements

**Lesson 7-8: System Analysis Project**
- Activity: Evaluate the community's economic system: What's working? What's not? What elements from different systems could improve it? Present analysis and recommendations with evidence.
- Key Concepts: Economic systems should serve the community, systems can and should be adapted as conditions change
- Practice: Present a comprehensive economic system analysis with recommendations

#### Assessment
- Compare 3+ economic systems by mechanisms and outcomes
- Analyze the community's current economic system
- Propose evidence-based improvements

### Unit 8: Community Economic Planning (Weeks 9-16)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Community economic data, planning templates, calculator
**Survival Relevance**: Long-term economic planning prevents resource depletion, ensures fair distribution, and builds community resilience

#### Learning Objectives
- Create a comprehensive community economic plan
- Model resource flows (production, consumption, trade, waste)
- Plan for seasonal variation and contingencies
- Present economic plans to community leadership

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Economic Assessment**
- Activity: Comprehensive assessment: What does the community produce? What does it consume? What is traded? What is the current balance (surplus or deficit)? What are the trends?
- Key Concepts: Planning starts with assessment, data quality determines plan quality
- Practice: Complete an economic assessment using available data

**Lesson 3-4: Resource Flow Modeling**
- Activity: Create a flow diagram showing resources moving through the community: raw materials → production → distribution → consumption → waste/recycling. Identify bottlenecks, waste points, and improvement opportunities.
- Key Concepts: Resource flows reveal system health, bottlenecks limit the whole system, waste is an opportunity
- Practice: Create a resource flow diagram for one major community resource

**Lesson 5-6: Seasonal and Contingency Planning**
- Activity: Map seasonal variation: growing season, harvest, lean period. Plan: how much must be preserved for lean months? What if harvest is 30% below expected? What if trade routes are disrupted? Build contingency reserves.
- Key Concepts: Plans must account for variation and disruption, contingency planning (what if?) is not pessimism — it's prudence
- Practice: Create a seasonal resource calendar with contingency scenarios

**Lesson 7-8: Community Economic Plan Capstone**
- Activity: Integrate all work into a comprehensive community economic plan: current assessment, 12-month projection, trade strategy, labor allocation, contingency plans, investment priorities (what should we build/improve this year?).
- Key Concepts: A good economic plan is specific, realistic, and adaptable, it must be understood and accepted by the community to work
- Practice: Present the economic plan to community leadership for feedback

#### Assessment
- Complete a community economic assessment
- Create resource flow models with improvement recommendations
- Present a comprehensive economic plan

### Unit 9: Inter-Community Trade and Currency (Weeks 17-24)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Negotiation materials, agreement templates, sample currency systems
**Survival Relevance**: Inter-community trade is how communities acquire critical resources they can't produce themselves — medicine, seeds, tools, expertise

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze trade relationships between communities (what flows, what's fair, what's risky)
- Design trade agreements with appropriate protections
- Understand currency and alternative currency systems
- Manage financial records for inter-community trade

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Trade Relationship Analysis**
- Activity: Map the community's trade relationships. For each trading partner: What do we send? What do we receive? Is the trade balanced? What happens if this relationship ends?
- Key Concepts: Trade dependency creates vulnerability, diversification reduces risk, relationships must be maintained
- Practice: Create a trade relationship map with dependency analysis

**Lesson 3-4: Trade Agreement Design**
- Activity: Draft a model inter-community trade agreement covering: goods exchanged, quantities, quality standards, delivery schedule, transport responsibility, dispute resolution, duration, renewal terms, force majeure (what if something goes wrong?).
- Key Concepts: Written agreements protect both parties, good agreements anticipate problems, relationships outlast any single trade
- Practice: Draft a complete model trade agreement

**Lesson 5-6: Currency Systems**
- Activity: Explore currency options: commodity money (salt, grain, metal), representative money (tokens backed by stored goods), fiat money (declared valuable by authority), time-based currency (1 hour of work = 1 credit regardless of task), mixed systems.
- Key Concepts: Currency solves the double coincidence of wants problem, any currency requires trust, different systems suit different situations
- Practice: Design a currency system for inter-community trade and evaluate its strengths and weaknesses

**Lesson 7-8: Financial Record Keeping for Trade**
- Activity: Set up a trade ledger for inter-community transactions. Record: date, counterparty, items exchanged, agreed values, delivery status, balance of trade. Generate monthly trade balance reports.
- Key Concepts: Trade records prevent disputes, balance of trade shows whether relationships are equitable, records inform future negotiations
- Practice: Maintain a trade ledger for a simulated month of inter-community trade

#### Assessment
- Analyze trade relationships with dependency assessment
- Draft a complete trade agreement
- Design a currency system with justification
- Maintain accurate trade records and produce a balance report
