# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Thematic History

## Overview
Students transition from case-study learning to analytical investigation. They work with primary sources, engage in structured debate, examine multiple perspectives on the same events, and begin to understand historiography — that history itself is an interpretation shaped by who tells it. Every unit connects directly to practical community governance, economics, and planning.

**Thematic Pillars**: All 9 pillars investigated analytically
**Core Commitments**: No national framing. Multiple perspectives always. Honest about uncertainty. No ideology as truth. Atrocities acknowledged, not weaponized. No hero worship. Religion is cultural history, not theology.

## Year 1 (Grade 6): Systems and Power

### Unit 1: Governance Systems Compared (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Primary source excerpts, comparison frameworks, debate materials, governance proposal templates
**Survival Relevance**: Students are preparing to participate in (and eventually lead) community governance
**Theme**: 2 (Governance)

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze 6+ governance systems by structure, decision-making, power distribution, and succession
- Evaluate tradeoffs of each system with evidence from historical outcomes
- Understand that governance evolves — most societies have tried multiple systems
- Draft a governance proposal for a hypothetical community

#### Teacher Notes
This is one of the most important units in the entire curriculum. Students will use this analysis for the rest of their education and civic life. Present ALL systems neutrally — as documented experiments in human organization with measurable outcomes. Let students draw their own conclusions from evidence. If students ask "which system is best?", redirect: "Best for what situation? Under what conditions? Let's look at the evidence."

#### Comparative Analysis Framework
For EACH system studied, students analyze:
1. How are decisions made? (One person, council, vote, consensus)
2. Who has power? (One, few, many, all)
3. How does power transfer? (Inheritance, election, force, appointment)
4. What checks exist on power abuse?
5. What happens when it fails?
6. Historical examples of it working and failing

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Tribal/Clan Council and Direct Democracy**
- Activity: Tribal/Clan Council — Indigenous governance worldwide. Elders, consensus, oral law. Iroquois Confederacy (influenced the U.S. Constitution — documented historical connection). San Bushmen consensus model. Direct Democracy — Athenian democracy. All citizens vote on all decisions. Who counted as a "citizen"? (Excluded: women, enslaved people, foreigners — about 10-15% of population.)
- Multiple perspectives: Athens was revolutionary in expanding power AND exclusionary by modern standards.
- Key concept: Every system has strengths and limitations.
- Practice: Complete the analysis framework for both systems.

**Lesson 3-4: Republic and Monarchy**
- Activity: Republic/Representative Democracy — Roman Republic, modern examples. Elected representatives make decisions. Monarchy/Hereditary Rule — Egyptian pharaohs, medieval European kingdoms, Japanese emperors. Power through birth.
- Key concept: Scale matters. What works for 1,000 people may not work for 1,000,000.
- Practice: Complete the analysis framework for both systems. Compare with previous systems.

**Lesson 5-6: Constitutional and Authoritarian Systems**
- Activity: Constitutional Systems — documents that limit power. Magna Carta (1215), Iroquois Great Law of Peace, modern constitutions. Key concept: written rules that even leaders must follow. Authoritarian Systems — what conditions produce them, how they maintain power, how they end. Historical examples analyzed structurally, not as abstract evil.
- Key concept: The difference between constitutional and authoritarian governance is whether rules constrain the powerful.
- Practice: Complete the analysis framework for both systems.

**Lesson 7-8: Comparative Analysis**
- Activity: Lay out all 6 systems side by side. What patterns emerge? What conditions favor each system? When do systems change?
- Discussion: Is there one best system? (No — context matters. But some patterns are clear: systems with accountability tend to last longer, systems without checks on power tend to become abusive.)
- Practice: Write a comparative analysis essay.

**Lesson 9-10: Governance Design Debate**
- Activity: Class divides into groups, each representing a governance system. Each group presents its system's case to a "founding community" (the class) who then votes on which system to adopt.
- Debrief: What arguments were most persuasive? What information was missing?
- Practice: Draft a governance proposal for a hypothetical community of 300 people.

#### Assessment
- Complete comparative analysis of 6 governance systems using the framework
- Participate in governance debate with evidence-based arguments
- Draft a governance proposal with historical justification

### Unit 2: Economics — Who Gets What and Why (Weeks 11-18)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Trade simulation supplies, economic data from community, planning templates
**Survival Relevance**: Direct application to community resource management and inter-community trade
**Theme**: 3 (Trade & Economics)

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain supply and demand with historical examples
- Analyze why some communities became wealthy and others didn't
- Understand multiple economic systems with historical evidence
- Design a resource management plan for a community of 200 people

#### Teacher Notes
Present economic systems as documented experiments, not ideologies. Students should evaluate systems by outcomes, not rhetoric. Use the community's own economic situation as a constant reference point. Cross-reference with the economics-trade.md practical skills — this unit covers the historical analysis.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Barter, Gift, and Money**
- Activity: Barter and gift economies — pre-currency exchange. Potlatch systems of Pacific Northwest (gift-giving as economics). Then money emerged: shells, salt, cattle, metal coins, paper. Why? (Solves the "double coincidence of wants" problem.)
- Key concept: Money is a technology. It was invented to solve a specific problem.
- Practice: Run a barter exchange. Then introduce "currency." Compare the experience.

**Lesson 3-4: Trade Empires and Economic Power**
- Activity: Venice (controlled Mediterranean trade), Hanseatic League (northern European trade network), Zimbabwe (gold trade), Majapahit (Southeast Asian spice trade). How controlling trade creates power.
- Key concept: Economic power and political power are deeply connected.
- Practice: Analyze how trade position gave each example political influence.

**Lesson 5-6: Economic Collapse Case Studies**
- Activity: Hyperinflation in Weimar Germany (wheelbarrows of cash to buy bread), Great Depression (how bank failures cascaded), Zimbabwe hyperinflation (2008). What causes economic systems to fail?
- Key concept: Economic systems fail when trust in the system is lost.
- Practice: Identify what factors these collapses have in common.

**Lesson 7-8: Economic Systems Compared**
- Activity: Present as systems with documented outcomes, not ideology. Market economy, command economy, mixed systems, commons-based (Elinor Ostrom's research on successful commons management). Each has situations where it works and situations where it fails.
- Applied project: Design a complete economic system for the community. How are resources allocated? How is labor valued? How is trade managed? What prevents hoarding?
- Practice: Present economic system design for peer critique.

#### Assessment
- Explain supply and demand with historical examples
- Analyze 3 economic collapses and identify common factors
- Compare economic systems by documented outcomes
- Design a resource management plan for 200 people

### Unit 3: Religion, Belief, and Culture as Historical Forces (Weeks 19-26)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Comparative religion reference materials, discussion circle, art supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding how belief systems shape societies helps navigate the diverse backgrounds within any community
**Theme**: 9 (Meaning & Culture)

#### Teacher Notes — CRITICAL
This is the most sensitive unit in the history curriculum. Present ALL traditions with equal respect and analytical distance. You are teaching about what people believed and how those beliefs shaped history — NEVER whether any belief is correct. If a student asks "which religion is right?", the appropriate answer is "that is a deeply personal question each person answers for themselves. Our job here is to understand how these traditions shaped human history."

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe 6+ major religious and philosophical traditions and their historical impact
- Analyze how belief systems influenced governance, law, art, science, and daily life
- Identify common elements across traditions
- Discuss the relationship between religion and power throughout history
- Demonstrate respectful engagement with beliefs different from their own

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Eastern Traditions**
- Activity: Hinduism/Vedic Tradition — one of the oldest continuous traditions. Caste system and its social effects. Rich philosophical tradition. Buddhism — emerged as a reform movement. Spread across Asia through trade routes. Monastic communities as knowledge preservers. Chinese Philosophy — Confucianism (social order, duty, education), Daoism (harmony with nature, balance), Legalism (strict laws and punishment).
- Key concept: These traditions shaped billions of people's lives for thousands of years.
- Practice: For each tradition, identify one way it shaped governance and one way it shaped daily life.

**Lesson 3-4: Abrahamic Traditions**
- Activity: Judaism — monotheism and covenant. Diaspora as a model of cultural preservation without a homeland. Christianity — from Jewish sect to Roman state religion. Monastic preservation of knowledge through the Dark Ages. Diverse traditions (Orthodox, Catholic, Protestant). Islam — rapid expansion. Islamic Golden Age (800-1400): mathematics, astronomy, medicine, architecture, philosophy.
- Key concept: These three traditions share roots and influenced each other for centuries.
- Practice: Map the historical connections and divergences between the three traditions.

**Lesson 5-6: Indigenous and Secular Traditions**
- Activity: Indigenous spiritual traditions — animism, ancestor veneration, land-based spirituality across continents. Not one system but thousands, each tied to specific environments. Often dismissed historically but containing deep ecological knowledge. Secular philosophy — Greek philosophy (Socrates, Plato, Aristotle), Enlightenment rationalism, scientific method.
- Key concept: Every human culture independently developed frameworks for understanding meaning.
- Practice: Identify common elements across ALL traditions studied (golden rule, care for vulnerable, community obligation).

**Lesson 7-8: Religion, Power, and Our Community**
- Discussion: How have religious institutions both preserved and suppressed knowledge? (Monasteries saved ancient texts AND the Inquisition banned books.) What role should belief play in governance? Compare theocracy, state religion, secular governance.
- Key concept: The relationship between belief and power is complex and consequential.
- Practice: Write a position paper on the role of belief in community governance, supported by historical evidence.

#### Assessment
- Describe 6+ traditions and their historical impact
- Identify common elements across traditions
- Analyze the relationship between religion and power with historical evidence
- Demonstrate respectful analytical engagement with diverse beliefs

## Year 2 (Grade 7): Crisis and Adaptation

### Unit 4: Epidemics That Changed History (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Primary source excerpts, mapping materials, epidemic response planning templates
**Survival Relevance**: Epidemic preparedness is directly relevant. This is applied epidemiology through history.
**Theme**: 5 (Disease)

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze 5 major epidemics using a consistent analytical framework
- Work with primary sources to understand historical experience of disease
- Identify social conditions that amplify epidemics
- Create an epidemic response plan using historical lessons

#### Teacher Notes
This unit uses primary sources — real accounts from people who lived through epidemics. These sources are powerful and sometimes disturbing. Prepare students that they will read difficult material. The analytical framework keeps the focus on learning: for each epidemic, what was the pathogen, how did it spread, what social conditions amplified it, how did communities respond, what was the lasting impact, and what could have been done differently?

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Plague of Athens and Black Death**
- Activity: Plague of Athens (430 BCE) — read excerpts from Thucydides' account, one of the first detailed epidemic descriptions. Killed 25% including Pericles. Black Death (1347-1353) — 30-50% of Europe died. Read from Boccaccio's description. Flagellant movements, persecution of Jewish communities (pattern of scapegoating during epidemics). Aftermath: labor shortage → higher wages → weakened feudalism.
- Key concept: Epidemics reshape societies. The Black Death actually improved conditions for surviving workers.
- Practice: Apply the analytical framework to both epidemics.

**Lesson 3-4: Smallpox in the Americas and 1918 Influenza**
- Activity: Smallpox — 90% mortality in some Indigenous populations. Cortez conquered the Aztec Empire partly because smallpox arrived before he did. Read Indigenous perspectives on the devastation. 1918 Influenza — killed 50-100 million worldwide. Censored during wartime (named "Spanish Flu" because Spain, being neutral, was the only country honestly reporting).
- Key concept: Information suppression during epidemics costs lives. Honest reporting saves them.
- Practice: Apply the analytical framework. Compare information handling in 1918 to other epidemics.

**Lesson 5-6: HIV/AIDS and Patterns**
- Activity: Modern epidemic shaped by stigma, politics, and science. Shows how social factors amplify or mitigate disease. Compare all 5 epidemics: what patterns emerge across centuries?
- Key concept: The same factors amplify every epidemic: density, poor sanitation, contaminated water, stigma, information suppression, delayed response.
- Practice: Create a pattern analysis chart across all 5 epidemics.

**Lesson 7-8: Community Epidemic Response Plan**
- Applied project: Students create an epidemic response plan for their community using historical lessons. Address: detection, isolation, communication, treatment, resource management, morale. Present to community health team for review.
- Practice: Present and defend the plan, incorporating feedback.

#### Assessment
- Analyze 5 epidemics using the consistent framework
- Work with primary sources and extract relevant information
- Identify common amplifying factors across epidemics
- Create a community epidemic response plan

### Unit 5: Revolution and Change — Why Societies Transform (Weeks 9-18)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Primary source excerpts, debate materials, multiple-perspective worksheets
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding what drives social upheaval helps communities manage internal tensions before they become crises
**Theme**: 2 (Governance), 7 (Conflict)

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze 6 major historical transformations from multiple perspectives
- Identify conditions that produce revolutionary change
- Evaluate whether revolutions achieve their stated goals
- Apply lessons to community tension management

#### Teacher Notes
Every revolution is presented from multiple perspectives. The French Revolution looks different from the perspective of an aristocrat, a bourgeois merchant, a peasant, and a woman. The Industrial Revolution looks different from the perspective of a factory owner and a child laborer. Insist that students engage with perspectives they find uncomfortable.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Agricultural to Urban (Neolithic) and French Revolution**
- Activity: Neolithic — the original revolution. Voluntary? Forced by population pressure? Gradual or sudden? Debate the evidence. French Revolution (1789) — inequality → hunger → revolution → terror → dictatorship → democracy (eventually). Perspectives: aristocracy, bourgeoisie, peasants, women.
- Key concept: Revolution often produces worse before better.
- Practice: Write a diary entry from two different perspectives of the French Revolution.

**Lesson 3-4: Haitian Revolution and Industrial Revolution**
- Activity: Haitian Revolution (1791-1804) — enslaved people defeated European armies and established the first free Black republic. Largely omitted from European-centered histories. What does that omission tell us about who writes history? Industrial Revolution (1760-1840) — technological, not political. Child labor, urbanization, pollution, wealth creation, eventually improved living standards.
- Multiple perspectives: Haiti — enslaved person, French plantation owner, British observer. Industrial — factory owner, factory worker, displaced farmer, child laborer.
- Key concept: Who tells the story determines what gets remembered.
- Practice: Write about the Industrial Revolution from two different perspectives.

**Lesson 5-6: Indian Independence and Green Revolution**
- Activity: Indian Independence (1947) — non-violent resistance as a strategy. Not just Gandhi but thousands of unnamed activists. Partition and its consequences. Green Revolution (1960s) — new crop varieties, fertilizers, irrigation vastly increased food production. Saved billions from famine. Also created dependency on chemicals, reduced crop diversity, displaced small farmers.
- Key concept: Independence doesn't solve all problems. Technology has tradeoffs.
- Practice: Analyze the Green Revolution's tradeoffs in a structured cost-benefit format.

**Lesson 7-8: Patterns of Revolution**
- Discussion: What conditions produce revolution? (Usually: inequality + food insecurity + delegitimized leadership + triggering event.) Do revolutions achieve their goals? Is gradual change or revolution more effective?
- Practice: Write an analytical essay on the conditions that produce revolutionary change.

**Lesson 9-10: Application to Our Community**
- Activity: What tensions exist in our community? What historical patterns do they match? What can we learn about managing tensions before they become crises?
- Practice: Prepare a community tensions analysis with historical parallels and recommendations.

#### Assessment
- Analyze 6 historical transformations from multiple perspectives
- Identify common conditions that produce revolution
- Write analytical essays using historical evidence
- Apply revolutionary patterns to community tension management

### Unit 6: Knowledge Lost and Found (Weeks 19-24)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Case study materials, knowledge preservation planning templates
**Survival Relevance**: This is the historical argument for SURVIVE OS itself
**Theme**: 4 (Communication & Knowledge), 8 (Collapse)

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze 5 cases of knowledge loss and preservation
- Understand that literacy and technical knowledge are not permanent
- Evaluate the role of institutions in preserving knowledge
- Design a knowledge preservation strategy for the community

#### Teacher Notes
This unit is deeply personal for a post-collapse community. Students may be living in a world where knowledge has already been lost. Frame this constructively: "Understanding how knowledge was lost in the past helps us prevent further loss." The SURVIVE OS platform itself is a knowledge preservation tool — make this connection explicit.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Knowledge Lost**
- Activity: Bronze Age Collapse — Mycenaean Greece had writing (Linear B). After the collapse (~1177 BCE), writing was lost for 400 years. An entire civilization forgot how to write. Post-Roman knowledge loss — concrete formula lost for 1,000 years, indoor plumbing disappeared from Europe, medical knowledge regressed. Why? The institutions that preserved knowledge collapsed.
- Key concept: Knowledge is fragile. Without institutions to preserve it, even basic skills can be lost in a generation.
- Practice: Identify which community knowledge is most at risk of being lost and why.

**Lesson 3-4: Knowledge Preserved**
- Activity: Islamic Golden Age — while Europe lost knowledge, Islamic scholars preserved, translated, and extended Greek, Roman, Persian, and Indian texts. Al-Khwarizmi (algebra), Ibn al-Haytham (optics), Ibn Sina (medicine). Without Islamic preservation, much classical knowledge would be permanently lost.
- Key concept: Knowledge preservation requires deliberate effort and dedicated institutions.
- Practice: Design a knowledge preservation institution for the community.

**Lesson 5-6: Knowledge Destroyed and Our Responsibility**
- Activity: Colonial powers systematically destroyed Indigenous knowledge systems — burning Maya codices, banning Aboriginal languages, disrupting oral traditions. Knowledge accumulated over millennia was lost in decades. Modern preservation efforts: Kiwix, Internet Archive, Svalbard Seed Vault. Our project is in this tradition.
- Capstone discussion: "We are living in a post-knowledge-loss scenario. What knowledge has already been lost? What are we doing to prevent further loss? What should we be doing?"
- Practice: Create a knowledge preservation priority list for the community with action plan.

#### Assessment
- Analyze 5 cases of knowledge loss or preservation
- Explain why institutions matter for knowledge preservation
- Design a knowledge preservation strategy for the community
- Identify the community's most at-risk knowledge

## Year 3 (Grade 8): Synthesis and Application

### Unit 7: The Collapse Seminar (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: All available reference materials, research templates, presentation materials
**Survival Relevance**: Capstone application of all historical learning to community survival
**Theme**: 8 (Collapse & Resilience)

#### Learning Objectives
- Conduct independent historical research
- Apply a multi-factor analysis framework to civilizational collapse
- Compare collapse cases with resilience cases
- Generate specific, actionable recommendations for the community

#### Teacher Notes
Students become historians in this unit. They select a collapsed civilization, research it independently, and present a "forensic analysis." This is their first experience with independent research at depth. Provide the framework but let students drive the investigation. Pair each collapse study with a resilience counter-study — this prevents the unit from being purely negative.

#### Collapse Analysis Framework
1. **Environmental factors**: Climate change, resource depletion, soil degradation, water problems
2. **Governance factors**: Leadership failure, corruption, loss of legitimacy, inability to adapt
3. **Economic factors**: Trade disruption, inequality, debt, inflation, resource misallocation
4. **Social factors**: Loss of social cohesion, internal conflict, erosion of trust, knowledge loss
5. **External factors**: Invasion, disease, trade partner collapse, natural disaster
6. **The combination**: How did multiple factors interact? (Collapse is almost never monocausal)
7. **What survived**: What knowledge, institutions, or people continued after the collapse?
8. **Lessons for us**: Specific, actionable recommendations for our community

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Framework Introduction and Case Selection**
- Activity: Review the analysis framework. Present available collapse studies: Bronze Age Collapse, Western Roman Empire, Maya Classic Period, Khmer Empire (Angkor), Easter Island (Rapa Nui), Greenland Norse, Cahokia (Mississippi), Great Zimbabwe, Soviet Union. Each student or pair selects one.
- Practice: Begin research with framework as guide.

**Lesson 3-6: Independent Research**
- Activity: Students research their selected civilization using all available sources. Apply the 8-point analysis framework systematically. Teacher provides guidance and checks progress weekly.
- Practice: Maintain a research journal with evidence and analysis.

**Lesson 7-8: Resilience Counter-Studies**
- Activity: Each student also studies one civilization that survived a comparable crisis: Byzantine Empire (survived the fall of Rome for 1,000 years), Japan (maintained continuity through radical transformation), Aboriginal Australian cultures (65,000 years), Chinese civilization (survived multiple collapses), Jewish diaspora communities (maintained identity without homeland for 2,000 years).
- Key concept: What did the survivors do differently?
- Practice: Compare collapse case to resilience case.

**Lesson 9-10: Presentations**
- Activity: Each student presents their forensic analysis to the class. Format: 15-minute presentation followed by 10-minute Q&A. Class evaluates each presentation using a shared rubric.
- Practice: Present and defend analysis under questioning.

#### Assessment
- Complete 8-point analysis of a collapsed civilization
- Complete resilience counter-study with comparison
- Deliver a 15-minute presentation with Q&A
- Generate specific, actionable recommendations for the community

### Unit 8: Historiography — Who Tells the Story Matters (Weeks 11-16)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Multiple accounts of the same events, source analysis worksheets, Community History Book from Foundation Band
**Survival Relevance**: Critical thinking about information sources is essential for governance and decision-making
**Theme**: All themes

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that all historical accounts are interpretations, not objective truth
- Identify bias in historical sources (who wrote it, for what audience, with what purpose)
- Compare accounts of the same event from different perspectives
- Apply source analysis skills to current community information

#### Teacher Notes
This unit teaches the most important skill in the entire history curriculum: the ability to analyze information critically. Every source has a perspective. Every account omits something. Students who can identify bias in historical sources can also identify bias in current information — a critical skill for community governance.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Same Event, Different Stories**
- Activity: Read 2-3 accounts of the same event from different perspectives. How do they differ? Why? Examples: accounts of the Columbian Exchange from European and Indigenous perspectives, accounts of the Industrial Revolution from factory owners and workers, accounts of any war from both sides.
- Key concept: There is no "view from nowhere." Every account has a perspective.
- Practice: Analyze 3 pairs of conflicting accounts and identify the source of disagreement.

**Lesson 3-4: Who's Missing?**
- Activity: Examine a historical narrative and identify whose voices are absent. Women's experiences in accounts of war. Workers' experiences in accounts of economic growth. Children's experiences in accounts of migration. What would the story look like from their perspective?
- Key concept: What is left out of a story is as important as what is included.
- Practice: Rewrite a historical account to include a missing perspective.

**Lesson 5-6: Our History, Our Bias**
- Activity: Examine the Community History Book started in Foundation Band. What biases might it contain? Whose perspectives are underrepresented? Revise entries to be more complete.
- Discussion: How has history been used as a political tool? Examples of history being rewritten to serve power.
- Practice: Revise a Community History Book entry to be more complete and less biased.

#### Assessment
- Analyze accounts for bias and identify source perspectives
- Identify missing voices in historical narratives
- Revise a historical account to include underrepresented perspectives
- Apply source analysis skills to current community information

### Unit 9: Applied Governance Design (Weeks 17-24)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: All previous history materials, governance design templates, presentation materials
**Survival Relevance**: This is a practicum — students design real governance solutions
**Theme**: 2 (Governance), 3 (Economics), 7 (Conflict)

#### Learning Objectives
- Synthesize historical governance, economic, and conflict resolution knowledge
- Design a complete governance system drawing on historical models
- Create practical policy documents based on historical precedents
- Present and defend governance proposals to community leaders

#### Teacher Notes
This is NOT a simulation. It is preparation for real civic participation. The governance designs students produce should be taken seriously — selected elements may actually be adopted by the community. Invite community leaders to the final presentations and ensure they engage meaningfully with the students' work.

#### The Scenario
Students are presented with a scenario: "A community of 300 people needs to establish a governance system. They have diverse backgrounds, limited resources, and intermittent contact with 3 neighboring communities."

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Charter/Constitution Design**
- Activity: Teams draft a community charter/constitution drawing on all historical governance models studied. Must include: purpose statement, decision-making structure, rights and responsibilities, amendment process, power limitations.
- Practice: Draft and revise charter through peer review.

**Lesson 3-4: Justice System Design**
- Activity: Design a justice system incorporating conflict resolution methods from multiple cultures. Address: disputes between individuals, disputes between groups, violations of community rules, appeals process, restorative vs punitive approaches.
- Practice: Draft justice system procedures and test with mock disputes.

**Lesson 5-6: Economic Plan and Knowledge Strategy**
- Activity: Create an economic plan (resource allocation, trade policy, labor organization). Develop a knowledge preservation strategy (what to prioritize, how to teach, how to record).
- Practice: Draft both plans with historical justification for each decision.

**Lesson 7-8: Diplomacy Framework and Final Presentation**
- Activity: Write an inter-community diplomacy framework (trade agreements, mutual defense, cultural exchange). Compile all components into a complete governance package.
- Presentation: Teams present their complete governance design to the class and community leaders. Community leaders provide feedback on practicality. Selected elements may be adopted by the actual community.
- Practice: Present, defend under questioning, and revise based on feedback.

#### Assessment
- Complete governance package (charter, justice system, economic plan, knowledge strategy, diplomacy framework)
- Historical justification for all design decisions
- Successful presentation to community leaders
- Incorporation of feedback into revised design
- Peer evaluation of team contribution
