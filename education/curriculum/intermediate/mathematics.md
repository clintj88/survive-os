# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Mathematics

## Overview
Mathematics at the intermediate level bridges arithmetic and algebra. Students develop proportional reasoning, work with variables and equations, and apply math to real-world survival problems — calculating crop yields, designing structures, managing inventories, and understanding probability. By Grade 8, students should have a solid algebra foundation and practical financial math skills.

## Grade 6: Ratios, Proportions, and Data

### Unit 1: Ratios and Proportions (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Measuring tools, recipe cards, trade goods for simulation, paper and pencil
**Survival Relevance**: Ratios govern recipe scaling, mixing solutions (water purification, medicines), fuel mixtures, and fair distribution of resources

#### Learning Objectives
- Write and simplify ratios
- Solve proportion problems using cross-multiplication
- Apply proportional reasoning to real-world contexts (recipes, mixtures, maps)
- Understand rate as a special kind of ratio (speed, density, yield)

#### Teacher Notes
Every ratio problem should have a survival context. Don't teach abstract proportion problems — teach "we have a recipe for 4 people and need to feed 15" or "the map scale is 1:50,000, how far is the water source?"

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Is a Ratio?**
- Activity: Count boys and girls in class. "The ratio of boys to girls is 5:8." Mix paint colors. "2 parts blue to 1 part yellow." Inventory supplies. "3 bags of grain per family."
- Key Concepts: A ratio compares two quantities, ratios can be written as fractions, simplifying ratios
- Practice: Write 10 ratios from the classroom and community

**Lesson 3-4: Proportions**
- Activity: "If 2 cups of flour make 1 loaf of bread, how many cups make 7 loaves?" Set up as 2/1 = x/7. Cross-multiply: x = 14.
- Key Concepts: Proportions are equal ratios, cross-multiplication solves for unknowns
- Practice: 15 proportion problems with survival contexts (recipe scaling, supply calculations)

**Lesson 5-6: Rate Problems**
- Activity: Walking speed: if you walk 5 km in 1 hour, how far in 3.5 hours? Crop yield: if 1 acre produces 200 kg of grain, how many acres for 1500 kg? Water flow: if the spring produces 10 liters per minute, how long to fill a 500-liter tank?
- Key Concepts: Rate = quantity per unit (speed, yield, flow, cost per item), unit rates simplify comparison
- Practice: 15 rate problems using community-relevant scenarios

**Lesson 7-8: Map Scale and Proportional Reasoning**
- Activity: Using maps — convert distances, calculate travel times, determine area. "The community garden is 3 cm × 4 cm on the map at 1:500 scale. What is the real area?"
- Key Concepts: Map scale is a proportion, area scales by the square of the linear scale
- Practice: 10 map-based problems, checked by actual measurement where possible

#### Assessment
- Solve 20 proportion problems with 90%+ accuracy
- Apply proportional reasoning to solve 3 community-relevant problems
- Explain the concept of rate and give 3 real-world examples

### Unit 2: Percentage Applications (Weeks 9-14)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Calculator (if available), paper and pencil, inventory records
**Survival Relevance**: Percentages are used in yield calculations, loss rates, spoilage, survival probabilities, medical dosing (by body weight), and resource allocation

#### Learning Objectives
- Convert between fractions, decimals, and percentages
- Calculate percentage of a number, percentage increase/decrease, and find the whole given a percentage
- Apply percentages to real-world problems (spoilage, yield, allocation)
- Understand percentage as a standardized way to compare different quantities

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Percent Means "Per Hundred"**
- Activity: Shade a 10×10 grid. "37 squares shaded = 37%." Convert familiar fractions: 1/2 = 50%, 1/4 = 25%, 1/5 = 20%, 3/4 = 75%.
- Key Concepts: Percent means per hundred, converting between fractions, decimals, and percentages
- Practice: Convert 20 values between all three forms

**Lesson 3-4: Finding Percentages**
- Activity: "We harvested 800 kg of potatoes. 15% were damaged. How many kg were damaged? How many are usable?" Solve: 0.15 × 800 = 120 damaged, 680 usable.
- Key Concepts: Multiply by the decimal form to find a percentage of a number
- Practice: 15 problems using community inventory and production data

**Lesson 5-6: Percentage Change**
- Activity: "Last year we grew 500 kg of corn. This year we grew 600 kg. What's the percentage increase?" (600-500)/500 × 100 = 20% increase.
- Key Concepts: Percentage change = (difference / original) × 100, important for tracking growth, loss, and trends
- Practice: Calculate percentage changes for crop yields, population, water usage, and supply levels

#### Assessment
- Fluently convert between fractions, decimals, and percentages
- Solve percentage problems in survival contexts with 85%+ accuracy
- Calculate and interpret percentage changes

### Unit 3: Integers and the Coordinate Plane (Weeks 15-20)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Graph paper, rulers, thermometer (for negative number context)
**Survival Relevance**: Negative numbers describe temperature, elevation below sea level, debt, and losses. The coordinate plane is the foundation for maps, graphs, and data visualization.

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand, compare, and order positive and negative integers
- Add, subtract, multiply, and divide integers
- Plot points on a four-quadrant coordinate plane
- Read and create graphs on the coordinate plane

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Negative Numbers**
- Activity: Use a thermometer. "What does -5°C mean? What's colder, -5 or -10?" Discuss elevation: sea level is 0, below is negative, above is positive. Discuss debt: if you owe 3 units and have 1, your balance is -2.
- Key Concepts: Negative numbers represent below zero, left of zero, or deficits, zero is neither positive nor negative
- Practice: Order 15 integers from least to greatest, including negatives

**Lesson 3-4: Operations with Integers**
- Activity: Number line addition and subtraction. Adding a negative is like subtracting. Subtracting a negative is like adding. Multiplication: positive × negative = negative, negative × negative = positive.
- Key Concepts: Integer operation rules, number line as a visual model
- Practice: 30 integer operation problems

**Lesson 5-6: The Coordinate Plane**
- Activity: Draw axes on graph paper. Plot points in all four quadrants. Connect points to make shapes. "Your community is at (0,0). The water source is at (3, 5). The nearest neighbor is at (-4, 2). Plot them."
- Key Concepts: X-axis (horizontal), Y-axis (vertical), ordered pairs (x, y), four quadrants
- Practice: Plot 15 points and read coordinates of 15 plotted points

#### Assessment
- Perform all four operations with integers accurately
- Plot and read points in all four quadrants
- Apply integers and coordinates to a real-world scenario

### Unit 4: Basic Statistics (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Data from the community (weather records, harvest data, patrol logs), graph paper
**Survival Relevance**: Statistics turn raw data into decisions — analyzing crop yields, tracking disease patterns, planning resource allocation, and evaluating the effectiveness of community programs

#### Learning Objectives
- Calculate mean, median, mode, and range for a data set
- Create and interpret bar graphs, line graphs, and circle graphs (pie charts)
- Understand what each measure tells you (and when each is most useful)
- Collect, organize, and analyze real community data

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Measures of Center**
- Activity: Collect data — heights of students, daily water usage per household, weekly egg production. Calculate mean (average), median (middle), and mode (most common) for each set.
- Key Concepts: Mean is affected by extremes, median resists outliers, mode shows most common value
- Practice: Calculate all three measures for 5 data sets. Discuss which is most useful for each.

**Lesson 3-4: Range and Data Spread**
- Activity: Two gardens produce the same average yield but one is consistent (40, 42, 39, 41) and one is volatile (10, 70, 35, 55). Range shows the difference. Which is better for food planning?
- Key Concepts: Range = highest - lowest, consistency matters for planning, averages can hide important variation
- Practice: Calculate range for data sets and discuss implications

**Lesson 5-6: Graphing**
- Activity: Create bar graphs (comparing categories — yield by crop type), line graphs (showing change over time — daily temperature), and circle graphs (showing parts of a whole — water usage by category).
- Key Concepts: Different graphs suit different data types, good graphs communicate clearly, labels and titles are essential
- Practice: Create one of each graph type using community data

**Lesson 7-8: Community Data Project**
- Activity: Each student or pair selects a community question. Collect data through measurement, survey, or records. Analyze using statistical measures. Present findings with appropriate graphs. Recommend action.
- Key Concepts: Statistics answer real questions, data-driven decisions are better decisions
- Practice: Complete data project and present to class or community group

#### Assessment
- Calculate mean, median, mode, and range accurately
- Create 3 types of graphs with proper labels and titles
- Complete a data analysis project with findings and recommendations

### Unit 5: Area of Complex Shapes (Weeks 29-34)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Measuring tools, graph paper, community area that needs planning (garden, building)
**Survival Relevance**: Calculating area is essential for garden planning, construction, roofing, flooring, land allocation, and material estimation

#### Learning Objectives
- Calculate area of triangles, parallelograms, and trapezoids
- Break complex shapes into simpler shapes to find total area
- Estimate area of irregular shapes using grid overlay
- Apply area calculations to community planning problems

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Triangle and Parallelogram Area**
- Activity: Demonstrate: a parallelogram can be cut and rearranged into a rectangle (A = base × height). A triangle is half a parallelogram (A = ½ × base × height). Practice with community measurements.
- Key Concepts: Area formulas are derived from rectangles, height must be perpendicular to base
- Practice: Calculate areas of 10 triangles and parallelograms

**Lesson 3-4: Trapezoids and Complex Shapes**
- Activity: Trapezoid area = ½ × (base₁ + base₂) × height. For complex shapes — divide into rectangles, triangles, and trapezoids, find each area, add together.
- Key Concepts: Any shape can be divided into simpler shapes, accuracy in measurement determines accuracy of result
- Practice: Calculate areas of 5 complex shapes by decomposition

**Lesson 5-6: Irregular Shapes and Grid Estimation**
- Activity: Place a grid over an irregularly shaped area (garden plot, pond, building footprint). Count full squares, estimate partial squares. Multiply by area per square.
- Key Concepts: Grid estimation is practical for real-world irregular shapes, precision depends on grid size
- Practice: Estimate the area of 3 real community spaces using grid overlay

#### Assessment
- Calculate area of triangles, parallelograms, and trapezoids accurately
- Find the area of a complex shape by decomposition
- Estimate the area of a real irregular space using the grid method

## Grade 7: Pre-Algebra and Geometry

### Unit 6: Expressions and Equations (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Paper, pencil
**Survival Relevance**: Algebra is the language of relationships — it lets you generalize from specific cases, predict outcomes, and solve problems with unknowns (how many acres to plant, how long supplies will last, what trade is fair)

#### Learning Objectives
- Write and evaluate algebraic expressions
- Solve one-step and two-step equations
- Solve one-step inequalities
- Translate word problems into equations

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Variables and Expressions**
- Activity: "We have x bags of grain. Each bag feeds 4 people for a week. The expression 4x tells us how many person-weeks of food we have." Write expressions for community scenarios.
- Key Concepts: Variables represent unknown or changing quantities, expressions combine variables, numbers, and operations
- Practice: Write expressions for 10 scenarios, evaluate each for given values of the variable

**Lesson 3-4: One-Step Equations**
- Activity: "4x = 200. We need to feed 200 person-weeks. How many bags?" Divide both sides by 4: x = 50. Practice with addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.
- Key Concepts: Equations have an equals sign, solve by doing the same thing to both sides, check by substituting
- Practice: Solve 20 one-step equations

**Lesson 5-6: Two-Step Equations**
- Activity: "2x + 5 = 17. We have 5 jars in storage plus 2 jars per day of production. After how many days do we have 17?" Subtract 5, divide by 2: x = 6 days.
- Key Concepts: Undo operations in reverse order (PEMDAS backward), two steps = two inverse operations
- Practice: Solve 20 two-step equations

**Lesson 7-8: Inequalities**
- Activity: "We need at least 100 liters of water per day. The well produces 8 liters per hour. How many hours minimum? 8h ≥ 100, so h ≥ 12.5 hours."
- Key Concepts: Inequalities show "at least," "at most," "more than," "less than," flip the sign when multiplying/dividing by negative
- Practice: Solve 15 inequality problems

**Lesson 9-10: Word Problems to Equations**
- Activity: Given a word problem, identify the unknown, define the variable, write the equation, solve, and check. Focus on translating language into math.
- Key Concepts: Translation is the bridge between real problems and mathematical solutions
- Practice: Translate and solve 10 community-relevant word problems

#### Assessment
- Solve one-step and two-step equations with 90%+ accuracy
- Solve inequalities and graph solutions on a number line
- Translate 5 word problems into equations and solve

### Unit 7: Proportional Relationships and Geometry (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Compass, protractor (if available), ruler, graph paper, string
**Survival Relevance**: Proportional relationships underlie scaling (blueprints, recipes, maps), and geometry underlies all construction and land measurement

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify and work with proportional relationships (tables, graphs, equations)
- Calculate angle relationships (complementary, supplementary, vertical, angles in triangles)
- Understand properties of triangles and circles
- Apply geometric principles to construction problems

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Proportional Relationships**
- Activity: "A chicken produces 5 eggs per week. Graph the relationship between chickens and weekly eggs." Plot points, connect — it's a straight line through the origin. The equation is y = 5x.
- Key Concepts: Proportional relationships are linear through the origin, the constant of proportionality is the unit rate
- Practice: Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and equations

**Lesson 3-4: Angle Relationships**
- Activity: Measure angles with a protractor. Complementary (sum to 90°), supplementary (sum to 180°), vertical angles (equal). "If two boards meet at 65°, what's the supplementary angle on the other side?"
- Key Concepts: Angle relationships are predictable, they determine how structures fit together
- Practice: Find missing angles in 15 geometric figures

**Lesson 5-6: Triangles**
- Activity: Triangle angle sum = 180° (demonstrate by tearing corners and arranging). Types: equilateral, isosceles, scalene, right. The triangle is the strongest structural shape.
- Key Concepts: Triangle angle sum, triangle inequality (sum of any two sides > third side), triangles in construction
- Practice: Classify triangles, find missing angles, apply to structural problems

**Lesson 7-8: Circles**
- Activity: Measure diameter and circumference of round objects. Calculate the ratio — it's always approximately 3.14 (π). Circumference = πd. Area = πr².
- Key Concepts: Pi is a constant ratio, circle formulas, applications to wells, tanks, wheels, pipes
- Practice: Calculate circumference and area for 10 circles with practical contexts

**Lesson 9-10: Applied Geometry Project**
- Activity: Design a small structure (storage shed, chicken coop, garden frame). Calculate all angles, lengths, and areas. Create a scaled technical drawing.
- Key Concepts: Geometry is the math of building, accurate calculations prevent wasted materials
- Practice: Complete design with full calculations and present

#### Assessment
- Solve angle relationship problems accurately
- Calculate triangle and circle properties
- Complete an applied geometry design project with correct calculations

### Unit 8: Probability and Data Analysis (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Dice, coins, colored objects for drawing, data from community records
**Survival Relevance**: Probability informs risk assessment (weather, crop failure, disease), while data analysis supports evidence-based community decision-making

#### Learning Objectives
- Calculate theoretical and experimental probability
- Understand independent and dependent events
- Create and interpret two-way tables, scatter plots, and histograms
- Use data analysis to support community recommendations

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Probability Basics**
- Activity: Flip a coin 50 times. Record results. "The theoretical probability of heads is 1/2. What was your experimental probability?" Roll dice — theoretical probability of a 6 is 1/6.
- Key Concepts: Probability is the likelihood of an event (0 to 1), theoretical vs experimental, more trials → closer to theoretical
- Practice: Calculate probabilities for 15 scenarios

**Lesson 3-4: Compound Events**
- Activity: "What's the probability of flipping heads AND rolling a 6?" Independent events: multiply probabilities. 1/2 × 1/6 = 1/12. "What's the probability of drawing 2 red marbles without replacement?" Dependent events: probability changes.
- Key Concepts: Independent vs dependent events, multiplication rule
- Practice: Calculate compound probabilities for 10 scenarios

**Lesson 5-6: Data Visualization**
- Activity: Create histograms (distribution of ages, harvest weights), scatter plots (temperature vs yield), and two-way tables (crop type vs success rate by field).
- Key Concepts: Different visualizations reveal different patterns, scatter plots show relationships, histograms show distributions
- Practice: Create appropriate visualizations for 3 community data sets

**Lesson 7-8: Data-Driven Decisions**
- Activity: Analyze a real community dataset. What story does the data tell? What recommendations follow? What additional data would be helpful? Present findings.
- Key Concepts: Data supports decisions but doesn't make them, correlation vs causation, sample size matters
- Practice: Complete analysis and present recommendations to class

#### Assessment
- Calculate simple and compound probabilities accurately
- Create appropriate data visualizations for different data types
- Complete a data analysis project with evidence-based recommendations

## Grade 8: Algebra and Financial Math

### Unit 9: Algebra I — Linear Equations and Systems (Weeks 1-12)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Graph paper, ruler, pencil
**Survival Relevance**: Linear equations model countless real situations — supply consumption over time, trade relationships, growth rates. Systems of equations solve problems with multiple unknowns — critical for resource planning.

#### Learning Objectives
- Graph linear equations on the coordinate plane
- Find slope and y-intercept from equations and graphs
- Solve systems of two linear equations (graphing, substitution, elimination)
- Model real-world situations with linear equations and systems

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Linear Equations and Slope**
- Activity: "We use 5 liters of fuel per day. We have 200 liters. Graph fuel remaining over time." The equation is y = 200 - 5x. Slope = -5 (decreasing 5 per day). Y-intercept = 200 (starting amount).
- Key Concepts: Slope is rate of change, y-intercept is starting value, y = mx + b
- Practice: Graph 10 linear equations and identify slope and intercept

**Lesson 3-4: Graphing Systems**
- Activity: "We use 5 liters/day and have 200 liters. Our neighbor uses 3 liters/day and has 100 liters. When do we have the same amount?" Graph both lines — the intersection is the answer.
- Key Concepts: Systems of equations, the solution is where lines intersect, no intersection = no solution
- Practice: Solve 5 systems by graphing

**Lesson 5-6: Substitution Method**
- Activity: y = 200 - 5x and y = 100 - 3x. Set equal: 200 - 5x = 100 - 3x. Solve: 100 = 2x, x = 50. Faster than graphing, more precise.
- Key Concepts: Substitution replaces one variable with an expression, solve the resulting single-variable equation
- Practice: Solve 10 systems by substitution

**Lesson 7-8: Elimination Method**
- Activity: 2x + 3y = 12 and 4x - 3y = 6. Add the equations: 6x = 18, x = 3. Substitute back: y = 2. Works well when coefficients are opposites.
- Key Concepts: Add or subtract equations to eliminate a variable, may need to multiply first
- Practice: Solve 10 systems by elimination

**Lesson 9-10: Functions**
- Activity: A function is a rule that assigns each input exactly one output. f(x) = 3x + 2 means "triple x and add 2." Evaluate functions, create tables, graph.
- Key Concepts: Function notation, domain and range, function as a machine (input → rule → output)
- Practice: Evaluate, table, and graph 10 functions

**Lesson 11-12: Modeling with Equations**
- Activity: Real-world modeling problems — supply consumption, population growth/decline, trade break-even points. Write the equation from the situation, solve, and interpret.
- Key Concepts: Mathematics models reality, models are simplifications, interpret results in context
- Practice: Model and solve 5 community-relevant problems

#### Assessment
- Graph linear equations and identify slope/intercept
- Solve systems of equations using all three methods
- Model a real-world situation with a system of equations and interpret the solution

### Unit 10: Pythagorean Theorem and Geometry (Weeks 13-18)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Measuring tools, string, stakes, buildings/structures for measurement
**Survival Relevance**: The Pythagorean theorem is essential for construction (ensuring square corners, calculating diagonal distances, determining heights without climbing)

#### Learning Objectives
- State and apply the Pythagorean theorem (a² + b² = c²)
- Determine if a triangle is a right triangle using the theorem
- Apply the theorem to real-world measurement problems
- Use the 3-4-5 rule for squaring corners in construction

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Theorem**
- Activity: Draw right triangles on graph paper. Measure the sides. Square each: a², b², c². Verify that a² + b² = c² for each. This always works for right triangles.
- Key Concepts: The Pythagorean theorem relates the three sides of a right triangle, c (hypotenuse) is always the longest side
- Practice: Calculate the missing side in 15 right triangles

**Lesson 3-4: Real-World Applications**
- Activity: "How tall is that tree?" Measure the distance from the base (a). Measure the angle and calculate the height (b). Or use shadow length and similar triangles. "How far apart are two points?" If you know two sides of a right triangle, find the third.
- Key Concepts: The theorem solves distance and height problems without direct measurement
- Practice: Measure 3 real heights or distances using the Pythagorean theorem

**Lesson 5-6: The 3-4-5 Rule**
- Activity: In construction, you need square (90°) corners. Measure 3 units along one wall, 4 units along the other. If the diagonal is exactly 5 units, the corner is square. Demonstrate with string and stakes.
- Key Concepts: The 3-4-5 rule is the Pythagorean theorem applied to construction, any multiple works (6-8-10, 9-12-15)
- Practice: Use the 3-4-5 method to verify or create square corners on a real structure

#### Assessment
- Solve Pythagorean theorem problems with 90%+ accuracy
- Apply the theorem to measure a real distance or height
- Demonstrate the 3-4-5 construction method

### Unit 11: Financial Mathematics (Weeks 19-28)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Paper, pencil, ledger templates, community trade records
**Survival Relevance**: Financial math manages community resources — budgeting food supplies, tracking inventory, calculating fair trade values, and planning for future needs

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand and calculate simple and compound interest
- Create and manage a budget
- Perform trade calculations (unit cost, bulk discounts, exchange rates between goods)
- Understand exponential growth and its implications (population, debt, resource depletion)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Simple Interest**
- Activity: "You lend 10 units of grain to a neighbor at 10% interest per season. After 1 season they owe 11. After 2, they owe 12." Simple interest: I = P × r × t.
- Key Concepts: Interest is the cost of borrowing, principal, rate, time, simple interest grows linearly
- Practice: Calculate simple interest for 10 scenarios

**Lesson 3-4: Compound Interest and Exponential Growth**
- Activity: "Same loan but interest compounds — 10% of the NEW balance each season." After 1: 11. After 2: 12.1. After 3: 13.31. Growth accelerates. A = P(1 + r)^t.
- Key Concepts: Compound interest grows exponentially, exponential growth starts slow and then accelerates dramatically
- Practice: Calculate compound amounts for 10 scenarios. Compare to simple interest.

**Lesson 5-6: Budgeting**
- Activity: Create a monthly budget for a family of 4. Resources: food rations, water allocation, fuel, trade goods, labor credits. Expenses: food, fuel, clothing, medical, tools. Can you balance the budget?
- Key Concepts: Income must meet or exceed expenses, prioritization, savings/surplus
- Practice: Create and balance 3 different household budgets under different resource constraints

**Lesson 7-8: Trade Math**
- Activity: "We trade 5 kg of potatoes for 1 kg of salt with Community A. Community B offers 3 kg of potatoes for 1 kg of salt. Which is better? What's the unit price?" Calculate unit costs, compare deals, factor in transportation.
- Key Concepts: Unit cost enables comparison, opportunity cost, transaction costs (transportation, time)
- Practice: Solve 10 trade calculation problems

**Lesson 9-10: Community Economic Modeling**
- Activity: Model the community's resource consumption over 12 months. If we consume at rate X and produce at rate Y, when do we run out? When do we have surplus? What happens if production drops by 20%?
- Key Concepts: Mathematical modeling for planning, sensitivity analysis (what if things change?), sustainability
- Practice: Build a 12-month resource model for one community resource and present findings

#### Assessment
- Calculate simple and compound interest accurately
- Create a balanced budget under resource constraints
- Complete a community economic model with sensitivity analysis
