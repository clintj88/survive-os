# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Arts

## Overview
Arts at the intermediate level shift from skill development to applied creation. Students create functional art (pottery, textiles, bookbinding), lead community cultural events, document community life, and begin teaching younger students. Arts serve practical community needs while maintaining the cultural life that keeps communities cohesive.

## Grade 6: Advanced Performance and Applied Visual Arts

### Unit 1: Advanced Music (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Available instruments (handmade and salvaged), music notation materials, performance space
**Survival Relevance**: Music maintains morale, preserves cultural identity, marks community events, and creates a shared emotional experience that strengthens social bonds

#### Learning Objectives
- Read and write standard music notation (treble clef, note values, rests, time signatures)
- Play an instrument with intermediate proficiency (scales, simple pieces, sight-reading)
- Compose a piece for ensemble performance
- Perform for the community as part of an ensemble

#### Teacher Notes
Adapt to available instruments. A strong ensemble can be built from drums, simple flutes, voices, and anything that makes sound. Musical quality comes from practice and listening, not expensive instruments.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Music Theory — Notation**
- Activity: Staff, clef, notes (whole, half, quarter, eighth), rests, time signatures (4/4, 3/4, 6/8). Read simple melodies from notation. Write melodies from hearing (dictation).
- Key Concepts: Notation is the writing system of music, it allows music to be preserved and shared across time and distance, reading notation opens access to any written music
- Practice: Read 5 melodies from notation and write 3 melodies from dictation

**Lesson 3-4: Instrument Proficiency**
- Activity: Each student practices their chosen instrument: major and minor scales, 5+ pieces of increasing difficulty, ensemble parts. Daily practice minimum 15 minutes.
- Key Concepts: Proficiency requires consistent practice, musical skill transfers between instruments, listening is as important as playing
- Practice: Demonstrate scales and perform 3 pieces

**Lesson 5-6: Composition**
- Activity: Compose a piece for the available ensemble — melody, harmony (if instruments allow), rhythm section. Write the score with parts for each instrument. Rehearse with ensemble.
- Key Concepts: Composition is organized creativity, write for the instruments you have, rehearsal reveals what works on paper vs in practice
- Practice: Complete a composition, notate all parts, rehearse with ensemble

**Lesson 7-8: Ensemble Performance**
- Activity: Prepare 5-6 pieces for a community performance. Rehearse: balance (all parts heard equally), dynamics (loud and soft), tempo (together), expression (emotional communication).
- Key Concepts: Ensemble playing is a team skill, listening and adjusting to each other, performance serves the community
- Practice: Perform for the community at a gathering or event

**Lesson 9-10: Music Documentation**
- Activity: Expand the community songbook. Transcribe songs from community members into notation. Add new compositions. Create arrangements for available instruments.
- Key Concepts: Musical documentation preserves community culture, notation ensures songs survive even if all current musicians leave
- Practice: Add at least 5 songs to the community songbook with full notation

#### Assessment
- Read music notation and play from a written score
- Compose and notate a piece for ensemble
- Perform as part of an ensemble for the community
- Contribute to the community songbook

### Unit 2: Visual Arts — Drawing, Painting, and Sculpture (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Drawing materials, natural pigments/paints, clay, found materials for sculpture, carving tools
**Survival Relevance**: Visual arts skills produce botanical illustrations, medical diagrams, technical drawings, signage, maps, decorative items for trade, and the visual documentation that preserves community history

#### Learning Objectives
- Draw from observation with accuracy and detail (botanical illustration quality)
- Create paintings using natural and available pigments
- Sculpt functional and artistic objects from clay and found materials
- Apply printmaking to produce multiple copies of useful images

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Botanical and Technical Illustration**
- Activity: Draw a plant with scientific accuracy — every leaf, vein, petal, stem detail visible and correct. Label parts. "This drawing should allow someone to identify this plant from your illustration alone."
- Key Concepts: Illustration is a tool for documentation and communication, accuracy is more important than beauty in technical drawing, every detail matters for identification
- Practice: Create 3 botanical illustrations of local plants with labels

**Lesson 3-4: Painting with Natural Pigments**
- Activity: Expand the pigment palette: charcoal (black), clay (red/yellow/brown), berries (purple/blue), copper compounds (green — verdigris from vinegar + copper), egg tempera (egg yolk as binder for any pigment), walnut oil as medium. Practice mixing and applying.
- Key Concepts: Natural pigments are available everywhere, binders (egg, oil, gum arabic) make pigments stick, lightfastness varies (some fade, some don't)
- Practice: Create a landscape or community scene painting using only natural pigments

**Lesson 5-6: Sculpture**
- Activity: Functional sculpture — create something useful AND beautiful: a carved handle for a tool, a clay vessel with decorative elements, a garden marker in carved stone, a relief panel for a community building.
- Key Concepts: The best art serves a purpose, craftsmanship combines function and beauty, working in three dimensions develops spatial thinking
- Practice: Complete a functional sculpture piece

**Lesson 7-8: Printmaking for Community Use**
- Activity: Produce useful printed items — signs for community areas (garden, medical, kitchen, storage), illustrated educational posters for younger students, decorative elements for community spaces, labels for preserved food containers.
- Key Concepts: Printmaking is a production technology, one carved block produces many identical copies, quality prints require careful inking and consistent pressure
- Practice: Create and distribute at least 20 printed items for community use

**Lesson 9-10: Survival Applications Portfolio**
- Activity: Compile all work demonstrating art skills with survival applications: botanical illustrations, technical diagrams, map illustrations, printed signs, functional sculptures. Write a statement for each piece explaining its practical value.
- Key Concepts: Art is not a luxury — it communicates, documents, teaches, and produces useful objects
- Practice: Present portfolio demonstrating art's practical value

#### Assessment
- Create accurate botanical illustrations
- Paint using natural pigments
- Complete a functional sculpture
- Produce printed materials for community use

### Unit 3: Drama and Community Theater (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Performance space, simple costume and prop materials, writing materials
**Survival Relevance**: Theater builds communication skills, processes community experiences, preserves stories, and provides entertainment that maintains morale during difficult times

#### Learning Objectives
- Write scripts collaboratively (dialogue, stage directions, scene structure)
- Direct a short play (blocking, pacing, character development)
- Perform in character (voice, movement, emotional truth)
- Produce a community theater event (organize all aspects)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Scriptwriting**
- Activity: Choose a theme relevant to the community — a challenge overcome, a lesson from history, an imagined future, a moral dilemma. Develop characters, outline plot, write scenes with dialogue and stage directions.
- Key Concepts: Scripts are blueprints for performance, dialogue reveals character, conflict drives plot, every scene should advance the story
- Practice: Write a complete short play (15-20 minutes)

**Lesson 3-4: Directing**
- Activity: Students take turns directing scenes: block the movement (where actors stand and move), give notes on delivery (pacing, emphasis, emotion), manage the rehearsal process.
- Key Concepts: Directing is leadership — clear communication, constructive feedback, vision for the whole, trusting your actors
- Practice: Each student directs at least one scene

**Lesson 5-6: Performance Skills**
- Activity: Advanced acting: physical characterization (how does your character walk, sit, gesture?), vocal characterization (pitch, pace, accent?), emotional truth (what does your character want? What are they afraid of?).
- Key Concepts: Believable performance comes from understanding your character's inner life, rehearsal builds muscle memory, audience connection requires presence
- Practice: Perform a monologue and a scene with full characterization

**Lesson 7-8: Community Theater Event**
- Activity: Produce a full community theater event: performance, music (opening act or between scenes), set design, costumes, programs (if materials available), audience seating, post-show discussion.
- Key Concepts: Producing is project management, every detail matters for the audience experience, community events strengthen social bonds
- Practice: Execute all production roles and perform for the community

#### Assessment
- Participate in writing a complete script
- Direct at least one scene effectively
- Perform a role with full characterization
- Contribute to producing a community theater event

## Grade 7: Applied and Documentary Arts

### Unit 4: Functional Arts — Pottery, Textiles, Woodcarving, Bookbinding (Weeks 1-14)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Clay, kiln/pit fire, weaving supplies, carving tools and wood, bookbinding supplies (paper, needle, thread, covers)
**Survival Relevance**: Functional arts produce essential community goods — storage vessels, clothing, tools, and books. These skills create trade goods and reduce dependence on external supplies.

#### Learning Objectives
- Create functional pottery for community use (bowls, jars, cups)
- Produce textile items using weaving and other techniques
- Carve functional wooden items (spoons, handles, containers)
- Bind books for community records and education

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-3: Advanced Pottery**
- Activity: Build larger functional pieces — storage jars with lids, cooking vessels, water storage. Explore glazing if materials available (wood ash glaze, slip glaze). Focus on function: watertightness, durability, appropriate size for intended use.
- Key Concepts: Functional pottery serves daily needs, consistent wall thickness prevents cracking, proper firing determines durability, form follows function
- Practice: Create at least 3 functional pieces for community use

**Lesson 4-6: Textile Creation**
- Activity: Build a frame loom and weave fabric. Produce cloth for bags, towels, blankets, or clothing patches. If spinning materials available (drop spindle), produce thread from fiber (wool, cotton, plant fiber).
- Key Concepts: Textile production is one of the oldest and most essential crafts, spinning creates thread, weaving creates cloth, every community needs textile skills
- Practice: Weave a piece of functional cloth at least 30cm × 30cm

**Lesson 7-9: Woodcarving**
- Activity: Carve functional items: spoons and ladles (greenwood is easier to carve), tool handles (shaped for comfortable grip), bowls (carved or burned hollow), containers with lids.
- Key Concepts: Carving is subtractive — you can only remove material, plan carefully, grain direction affects both carving and strength, sharp tools make clean cuts
- Practice: Carve at least 2 functional items

**Lesson 10-12: Bookbinding**
- Activity: Bind books using simple methods: pamphlet stitch (3-hole, single signature), coptic binding (exposed spine, lies flat), Japanese stab binding (decorative, works with single sheets). Create: community record books, student journals, educational workbooks.
- Key Concepts: Books are the primary technology for knowledge preservation, binding makes pages durable and organized, different bindings suit different purposes
- Practice: Bind at least 3 books using different methods

**Lesson 13-14: Production and Trade**
- Activity: Create a batch of items for community use or trade — matching sets of bowls, bound journals, woven bags, carved utensils. Calculate: materials cost, labor time, trade value.
- Key Concepts: Production runs enable trade, consistency and quality determine trade value, applied arts are economically valuable
- Practice: Complete a production batch and calculate its trade value

#### Assessment
- Create functional pottery for community use
- Weave functional cloth
- Carve functional wooden items
- Bind books using at least 2 methods
- Complete a production batch with trade value calculation

### Unit 5: Music for Community Events (Weeks 15-20)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Instruments, performance space, event planning materials
**Survival Relevance**: Community events mark seasons, celebrate achievements, mourn losses, and build the social fabric. Music is central to all of these.

#### Learning Objectives
- Plan music for different types of community events (celebration, memorial, seasonal, civic)
- Adapt repertoire to event context (solemn, joyful, reflective, energizing)
- Lead community singing and participation
- Organize and manage musical events

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Music and Occasion**
- Activity: Match music to events: upbeat songs for harvest celebration, solemn music for memorials, seasonal songs for solstice/equinox, work songs for community labor days. Create event-specific playlists from the community songbook.
- Key Concepts: Music sets emotional tone, appropriate selection matters, community participation is more important than performance quality
- Practice: Create music plans for 4 different event types

**Lesson 3-4: Leading Community Music**
- Activity: Practice leading a group in singing — clear starting pitch, steady tempo, visible conducting gestures, encouraging participation. Not everyone can carry a tune — that's fine, participation matters.
- Key Concepts: Song leaders are facilitators not performers, energy and enthusiasm are contagious, everyone can participate regardless of skill level
- Practice: Lead the class in 5 different songs

**Lesson 5-6: Event Organization**
- Activity: Plan and execute a musical event — choose the occasion, select the program, rehearse the ensemble, arrange the space, invite the community, run the event, clean up.
- Key Concepts: Events require planning, execution, and follow-through, the community benefits from regular cultural gatherings
- Practice: Organize and execute a community musical event

#### Assessment
- Plan appropriate music for different event types
- Lead community singing confidently
- Organize and execute a community musical event

### Unit 6: Documentary Arts (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Drawing materials, writing materials, camera (if available), interview supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Documentation is cultural preservation. Community life that isn't recorded is eventually forgotten. Documentary arts create the visual and written record.

#### Learning Objectives
- Document community life through detailed drawing and journaling
- Conduct and document interviews (oral history)
- Create a community documentary project (visual, written, or combined)
- Understand the role of documentation in cultural preservation

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Documentary Drawing**
- Activity: Draw community life as it happens — people working, buildings, gardens, animals, gatherings. Include details that show context: tools, clothing, weather, season. Date every drawing.
- Key Concepts: Documentary drawing captures what cameras once did, detail and accuracy create historical value, daily life is worth documenting
- Practice: Create a portfolio of 10+ documentary drawings of community life

**Lesson 3-4: Journaling and Written Documentation**
- Activity: Keep a detailed community journal — daily events, weather, notable occurrences, decisions made, visitors, problems solved. This is the community's day-to-day historical record.
- Key Concepts: Written records are the backbone of history, future generations will rely on what we write today, consistency matters more than literary quality
- Practice: Maintain a community journal for 4 weeks

**Lesson 5-6: Oral History**
- Activity: Interview community members about their experiences — before the community formed, how they arrived, what they brought, what they've learned. Record in writing (or audio if equipment available). Transcribe and archive.
- Key Concepts: Oral history captures perspectives that written records miss, interview skills matter (open questions, active listening, respect), older community members hold irreplaceable knowledge
- Practice: Conduct and document 3 oral history interviews

**Lesson 7-8: Community Documentary Project**
- Activity: Combine drawings, journal entries, and oral histories into a comprehensive community documentary. Organize chronologically or thematically. Include illustrations, maps, and portraits. Bind and present to community leadership.
- Key Concepts: A community documentary is a gift to the future, it becomes more valuable with time, it should be maintained and expanded continuously
- Practice: Complete and present the community documentary

#### Assessment
- Create a portfolio of documentary drawings
- Maintain a community journal consistently
- Conduct oral history interviews with documentation
- Complete a community documentary project

## Grade 8: Arts Leadership and Cultural Stewardship

### Unit 7: Arts Leadership — Community Cultural Event (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: All available art and performance materials, event planning resources
**Survival Relevance**: Planning and executing community events develops project management, leadership, and organizational skills while serving the community's cultural needs

#### Learning Objectives
- Plan a comprehensive community cultural event from concept to execution
- Coordinate multiple participants and art forms
- Manage logistics (space, materials, schedule, communication)
- Evaluate the event and plan improvements for the next one

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Event Concept and Planning**
- Activity: What kind of cultural event does the community need? Seasonal celebration? Arts festival? Talent show? Historical commemoration? Choose the concept. Define: date, location, program structure, participants needed, materials needed, timeline for preparation.
- Key Concepts: Great events start with clear purpose, planning prevents chaos, involving the community early builds ownership
- Practice: Create a complete event plan with timeline

**Lesson 3-4: Coordination and Delegation**
- Activity: Recruit and organize participants: musicians, visual artists, performers, speakers. Assign roles: stage manager, setup/cleanup crews, program announcer, hospitality. Coordinate rehearsals and preparation.
- Key Concepts: Leadership means enabling others, delegation requires trust and clear communication, check in but don't micromanage
- Practice: Lead a planning meeting and delegate responsibilities

**Lesson 5-6: Production and Preparation**
- Activity: Prepare all elements: performance rehearsals, art installations, food preparation (if applicable), space setup, signage, programs. Conduct a full rehearsal/walkthrough the day before.
- Key Concepts: Production is managing many details simultaneously, checklists prevent omissions, the walkthrough reveals problems
- Practice: Complete all preparation and conduct a walkthrough

**Lesson 7-8: Event Execution**
- Activity: Run the event. Problem-solve in real time (weather changes, no-shows, technical difficulties, timing adjustments). Support participants. Welcome attendees. Manage the flow.
- Key Concepts: Plans change — flexibility is essential, stay calm under pressure, the community's experience is the priority
- Practice: Execute the community cultural event

**Lesson 9-10: Evaluation and Documentation**
- Activity: After the event: what worked? What didn't? What would you do differently? Gather feedback from participants and attendees. Document the event with drawings, writing, and photographs (if available). Write an event report with recommendations for next time.
- Key Concepts: Evaluation improves future events, documentation creates institutional memory, feedback is a gift
- Practice: Complete an event evaluation report with specific recommendations

#### Assessment
- Plan and execute a community cultural event
- Demonstrate leadership and coordination skills
- Complete an evaluation with actionable recommendations

### Unit 8: Portfolio and Teaching Arts (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: All student work from Grades 6-8, portfolio materials, teaching preparation materials
**Survival Relevance**: Portfolios demonstrate capability for specialization and apprenticeship. Teaching arts to younger students ensures cultural continuity.

#### Learning Objectives
- Curate a comprehensive arts portfolio (visual, performing, applied, documentary)
- Write artist statements demonstrating critical thinking about creative work
- Plan and deliver arts lessons to younger students
- Present work to a public audience with confidence

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Portfolio Curation**
- Activity: Gather all work from Grades 6-8 arts. Select 15-20 pieces that demonstrate range, growth, and quality across all art forms. Organize into a coherent presentation — chronological, thematic, or by medium.
- Key Concepts: Curation is choosing what represents you best, a portfolio tells a story of growth, quality over quantity
- Practice: Select and organize portfolio pieces

**Lesson 3-4: Artist Statements and Reflection**
- Activity: Write a statement for each portfolio piece: what you were trying to do, what you learned, how it connects to community needs. Write an overall artist statement: who you are as a creator, what matters to you, where you want to grow.
- Key Concepts: Reflection deepens learning, articulating your process helps others understand and learn from you
- Practice: Complete all statements and overall reflection

**Lesson 5-6: Teaching Preparation**
- Activity: Design 3 art lessons for Foundation or Building band students. Consider: appropriate skill level, available materials, clear instructions, safety, assessment. Create lesson plans with objectives, materials lists, step-by-step instructions, and expected outcomes.
- Key Concepts: Teaching is the highest form of understanding, adapting content for younger learners requires empathy and clarity, prepared lessons prevent wasted time and materials
- Practice: Complete 3 detailed lesson plans

**Lesson 7-8: Teaching Practicum**
- Activity: Deliver the prepared lessons to younger students. Observe what works. Adapt in real time. Manage behavior and materials. Assess student learning.
- Key Concepts: Plans meet reality when you teach, flexibility and observation are essential, helping others learn is deeply rewarding
- Practice: Teach 3 lessons and document observations

**Lesson 9-10: Cultural Documentation Capstone**
- Activity: Create a comprehensive cultural documentation project: update the community songbook, expand the visual documentation archive, record new oral histories, produce functional art for community needs.
- Key Concepts: Cultural stewardship is ongoing, each generation must actively preserve and add to the cultural record, the arts program perpetuates itself through teaching
- Practice: Complete and present the cultural documentation capstone

#### Assessment
- Present a comprehensive portfolio with artist statements
- Deliver 3 arts lessons to younger students
- Complete a cultural documentation project
- Demonstrate growth across all art forms over the 3-year intermediate program
