# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Life Science

## Overview
Life science at the intermediate level moves from observation to mechanism. Students learn how cells work, how organisms are classified and related, how the human body functions in detail, and how ecosystems operate. Every unit connects to practical survival — agriculture, medicine, food production, and environmental management.

## Grade 6: Cells, Classification, and Ecosystems

### Unit 1: Cell Biology (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Magnifying glass or microscope (if available), onion skin, cheek swab, pond water, drawing materials
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding cells is the foundation of medicine and agriculture — disease, infection, fermentation, plant growth, and wound healing all operate at the cellular level

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the cell as the basic unit of life
- Identify major cell structures (membrane, nucleus, cytoplasm, mitochondria, cell wall in plants, chloroplasts in plants)
- Compare plant and animal cells
- Explain cell division (mitosis) at a basic level
- Understand single-celled organisms (bacteria, yeast) and their importance

#### Teacher Notes
If no microscope is available, use detailed diagrams and analogies. A cell is like a community — the membrane is the wall, the nucleus is the leadership, mitochondria are the power station, the cell wall is the outer fortification. Make it tangible.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Cell as Building Block**
- Activity: "Everything alive is made of cells — your skin, a leaf, bacteria in the soil. You have approximately 37 trillion cells." If a microscope is available, view onion skin cells. If not, draw from diagrams.
- Key Concepts: Cell theory — all living things are made of cells, cells come from other cells, the cell is the basic unit of life
- Practice: Draw and label a generalized cell with major organelles

**Lesson 3-4: Plant vs Animal Cells**
- Activity: Compare diagrams of plant and animal cells. Plant cells have cell walls (rigid structure) and chloroplasts (photosynthesis). Animal cells have neither but have more flexible shapes.
- Key Concepts: Plant cells are rigid (explains why wood is hard), chloroplasts capture sunlight (explains why plants are green and need light)
- Practice: Create a comparison chart and draw both cell types

**Lesson 5-6: Cell Division**
- Activity: Demonstrate mitosis using rope as chromosomes. Cells grow → chromosomes copy → cell splits into two identical cells. "This is how a seed becomes a tree. This is how a wound heals."
- Key Concepts: Mitosis creates identical copies, growth and repair depend on cell division, errors in division can cause problems
- Practice: Sequence the stages of mitosis using drawings or labeled diagrams

**Lesson 7-8: Single-Celled Life**
- Activity: Observe pond water under magnification — full of single-celled organisms. Discuss bacteria: most are harmless or helpful (gut bacteria, soil bacteria, fermentation bacteria). Some cause disease. Yeast: single-celled fungus that makes bread rise and ferments alcohol.
- Key Concepts: Single-celled life is everywhere, most of it is beneficial, understanding it helps with food production and disease prevention
- Practice: List 5 beneficial and 3 harmful single-celled organisms and their effects

#### Assessment
- Draw and label a plant cell and an animal cell with all major organelles
- Explain cell division and why it matters for growth and healing
- Describe 3 roles of single-celled organisms in community life

### Unit 2: Classification of Organisms (Weeks 9-14)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Field guides (if available), specimens (plants, insects, shells), drawing materials
**Survival Relevance**: Classification skills let you identify edible vs poisonous plants, harmful vs harmless insects, and organize biological knowledge systematically

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain why classification is useful (organization, identification, communication)
- Describe the major kingdoms (bacteria, protists, fungi, plants, animals)
- Use a simple dichotomous key to identify organisms
- Create a classification system for local organisms

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Why Classify?**
- Activity: Present 20 random objects. "Sort them." Different students sort differently. Discuss: classification depends on the criteria you choose. Scientists use evolutionary relationships.
- Key Concepts: Classification organizes knowledge, taxonomy is the science of naming and grouping, shared characteristics indicate relatedness
- Practice: Sort 15 organisms into kingdoms with justification

**Lesson 3-4: The Kingdoms of Life**
- Activity: Survey the five kingdoms with examples relevant to the community. Bacteria (gut flora, soil bacteria, pathogens). Protists (pond organisms, malaria parasite). Fungi (mushrooms, mold, yeast). Plants (crops, trees, medicinal plants). Animals (livestock, pests, wildlife).
- Key Concepts: Each kingdom has defining characteristics, kingdoms contain enormous diversity
- Practice: For each kingdom, name 3 organisms important to the community and explain why

**Lesson 5-6: Using Dichotomous Keys**
- Activity: Use or create a dichotomous key for local plants or insects. "Does it have leaves? → Yes → Are leaves opposite or alternate?" Follow the key to identify unknown specimens.
- Key Concepts: Dichotomous keys use yes/no questions to narrow identification, they are tools for systematic identification
- Practice: Use a key to identify 5 specimens, then create a key for 8 local organisms

#### Assessment
- Sort organisms into kingdoms with correct justification
- Use a dichotomous key to identify unknown organisms
- Create a functional dichotomous key for at least 8 organisms

### Unit 3: Genetics Basics (Weeks 15-20)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Coins for probability simulations, pea pods or seeds of varying traits, paper for Punnett squares
**Survival Relevance**: Genetics governs selective breeding of crops and livestock — understanding inheritance lets you improve yields, disease resistance, and animal productivity over generations

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that traits are inherited from parents via genes
- Distinguish dominant and recessive traits
- Use Punnett squares to predict offspring traits
- Explain how selective breeding applies genetics to agriculture

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Inherited Traits**
- Activity: Survey class traits — eye color, hair texture, attached/free earlobes, tongue rolling ability. "Where did you get these traits?" From parents. Traits are passed through genes.
- Key Concepts: Genes are instructions inherited from both parents, each parent contributes one copy, some traits are dominant over others
- Practice: List 10 inherited traits and identify which parent contributed each (if known)

**Lesson 3-4: Punnett Squares**
- Activity: If tall (T) is dominant over short (t), and both parents are Tt, what are the possible offspring? Draw the 2×2 grid: TT, Tt, Tt, tt. 3 out of 4 will be tall.
- Key Concepts: Punnett squares predict probability of traits, genotype (gene combination) vs phenotype (visible trait)
- Practice: Complete Punnett squares for 10 trait crosses

**Lesson 5-6: Selective Breeding**
- Activity: "If you want bigger tomatoes, what do you do? Plant seeds from the biggest tomatoes. Over generations, you get bigger tomatoes. This is selective breeding — humans have done it for 10,000 years."
- Key Concepts: Selective breeding applies genetics practically, it produced every domestic crop and animal, it takes generations but works reliably
- Practice: Design a 5-generation selective breeding plan for a desired crop trait

#### Assessment
- Complete Punnett squares accurately and interpret results
- Explain the difference between genotype and phenotype
- Design a selective breeding plan for a community crop or animal

### Unit 4: Ecosystems and Biomes (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Outdoor observation area, drawing materials, thermometer, rain gauge
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding ecosystems helps manage resources sustainably — knowing food webs prevents overharvesting, understanding nutrient cycles improves agriculture, recognizing biome characteristics guides land use decisions

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the components of an ecosystem (producers, consumers, decomposers, abiotic factors)
- Trace energy flow through food webs
- Explain nutrient cycling (carbon, nitrogen, water)
- Identify major biomes and their characteristics
- Analyze human impact on ecosystems

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Ecosystem Components**
- Activity: Survey a local area (garden, field, forest edge, pond). List everything living (biotic) and nonliving (abiotic). Categorize living things: producers (plants), consumers (animals), decomposers (fungi, bacteria).
- Key Concepts: Ecosystems are communities of organisms interacting with their physical environment, every component plays a role
- Practice: Create a complete inventory of a local ecosystem area

**Lesson 3-4: Food Webs and Energy Flow**
- Activity: Build a food web for the local ecosystem. Start with producers (grass, trees, algae). Connect to primary consumers (rabbits, insects). Connect to secondary consumers (hawks, snakes). Add decomposers.
- Key Concepts: Energy flows from sun → producers → consumers → decomposers, energy decreases at each level (10% rule), removing one species affects the whole web
- Practice: Draw a food web with at least 15 organisms and predict what happens if one species is removed

**Lesson 5-6: Nutrient Cycles**
- Activity: Trace the water cycle (evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration). Trace the carbon cycle (photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, fossil fuels). Explain the nitrogen cycle (nitrogen fixation, plant uptake, decomposition).
- Key Concepts: Matter cycles but energy flows, nutrients are recycled — nothing is wasted in nature, human activity disrupts cycles
- Practice: Diagram all three cycles with labels and explanations

**Lesson 7-8: Biomes and Human Impact**
- Activity: Survey major biomes (tropical forest, temperate forest, grassland, desert, tundra, freshwater, marine). Characteristics of each: climate, vegetation, animal life. Discuss human impact: deforestation, overfishing, soil depletion, water pollution.
- Key Concepts: Biomes are large-scale ecosystems defined by climate and vegetation, human activity can damage or sustain ecosystems
- Practice: Assess the community's impact on local ecosystems and propose improvements

#### Assessment
- Create a detailed food web for a local ecosystem
- Diagram and explain the water, carbon, and nitrogen cycles
- Analyze human impact on a local ecosystem with specific improvement recommendations

### Unit 5: Human Impact on Environment (Weeks 29-34)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Community resource data, observation access to local environment
**Survival Relevance**: In a post-infrastructure world, environmental stewardship is not abstract ethics — it is the difference between sustainable survival and resource depletion

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify specific ways the community affects its environment
- Analyze the sustainability of current practices
- Propose evidence-based environmental management strategies
- Understand the concept of carrying capacity

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Community Environmental Audit**
- Activity: Survey community impacts — water use and discharge, waste management, soil use, wood harvesting, hunting/foraging pressure, air quality from fires.
- Key Concepts: Every community has an environmental footprint, measuring it is the first step to managing it
- Practice: Complete an environmental audit with data

**Lesson 3-4: Carrying Capacity**
- Activity: "How many people can this area sustainably support?" Consider: water supply, arable land, firewood regeneration rate, game populations, soil fertility. This is carrying capacity.
- Key Concepts: Carrying capacity is the maximum population an area can sustain indefinitely, exceeding it leads to degradation and eventual collapse
- Practice: Estimate carrying capacity for the community's area and compare to current population

**Lesson 5-6: Environmental Management Plan**
- Activity: Based on the audit and carrying capacity analysis, propose an environmental management plan. Address: sustainable harvest levels, waste management, soil conservation, water protection, biodiversity preservation.
- Key Concepts: Sustainability means meeting needs without compromising future capacity, planning prevents depletion
- Practice: Present environmental management plan to class and community leaders

#### Assessment
- Complete an environmental audit of the community
- Calculate or estimate carrying capacity with justification
- Present an environmental management plan with specific, evidence-based recommendations

## Grade 7: Human Biology and Microbiology

### Unit 6: Human Anatomy and Physiology (Weeks 1-14)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Anatomical diagrams, stethoscope (if available), blood pressure cuff (if available), mirrors, first aid supplies for demonstration
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding how the body works is the foundation of all medical care — from recognizing illness to treating injuries to maintaining health

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the structure and function of all major body systems
- Explain how systems interact and depend on each other
- Identify signs of system malfunction (symptoms)
- Connect body system knowledge to first aid and health maintenance

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Skeletal and Muscular Systems**
- Activity: Identify major bones by touch (skull, clavicle, sternum, ribs, humerus, radius, ulna, pelvis, femur, tibia, fibula, spine). Demonstrate muscle groups and joint types (hinge, ball-and-socket, pivot).
- Key Concepts: 206 bones in adults, muscles move bones via tendons, joints allow movement, fractures and sprains affect these systems
- Practice: Label a skeleton diagram, identify 10 major muscles, describe 3 joint types

**Lesson 3-4: Circulatory System**
- Activity: Listen to heartbeat with stethoscope or by placing ear on chest. Take pulse at wrist and neck. Trace blood flow: heart → arteries → capillaries → veins → heart. Discuss: what happens when you exercise?
- Key Concepts: Heart pumps blood in a loop, arteries carry blood away, veins carry blood back, blood delivers oxygen and nutrients, removes waste
- Practice: Measure resting and active heart rate, explain the pathway of blood through the body

**Lesson 5-6: Respiratory System**
- Activity: Breathe in — diaphragm contracts, lungs expand, air rushes in. Breathe out — diaphragm relaxes, lungs contract, air pushed out. Oxygen in, carbon dioxide out. What happens to breathing during exercise?
- Key Concepts: Gas exchange in the lungs, respiratory rate increases with activity, respiratory illness affects oxygen delivery
- Practice: Measure breathing rate at rest and after exercise, diagram the respiratory system

**Lesson 7-8: Digestive System**
- Activity: Trace food from mouth to elimination. Mouth (chewing, saliva), esophagus (swallowing), stomach (acid, churning), small intestine (nutrient absorption), large intestine (water absorption), elimination. Takes 24-72 hours.
- Key Concepts: Digestion breaks food into usable nutrients, problems at any stage affect nutrition, hydration is critical
- Practice: Diagram the digestive tract, describe what happens to a meal at each stage

**Lesson 9-10: Nervous System**
- Activity: Test reflexes (knee reflex, pupil response to light). Discuss reaction time. "Your brain processes information and sends commands through nerves at up to 120 m/s."
- Key Concepts: Brain is command center, spinal cord is the highway, peripheral nerves connect everywhere, reflexes bypass the brain for speed
- Practice: Map the nervous system, test and record 3 reflex responses

**Lesson 11-12: Immune System**
- Activity: "Why don't minor cuts kill you? Your immune system fights invaders." White blood cells, antibodies, inflammation (redness and swelling = immune response, not just damage). Fever = immune system raising body temperature to fight infection.
- Key Concepts: Innate immunity (general defense), adaptive immunity (specific defense, memory), vaccination concept, immune disorders
- Practice: Explain the immune response to a cut in sequence

**Lesson 13-14: Endocrine and Reproductive Systems**
- Activity: Hormones are chemical messengers — growth, metabolism, stress response, reproduction. Age-appropriate, comprehensive coverage of reproductive anatomy, puberty, menstruation, conception, and development.
- Key Concepts: Hormones regulate body functions, reproductive health is health, comprehensive knowledge prevents harm
- Practice: Diagram endocrine glands and their functions, age-appropriate assessment on reproductive health

#### Assessment
- Label and describe all major body systems
- Explain how at least 3 systems interact
- Connect body system knowledge to common medical scenarios (injury, illness, health maintenance)

### Unit 7: Disease and Immunity (Weeks 15-20)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Diagrams, case studies, community health data
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding how disease works and spreads is essential for community health management, epidemic prevention, and informed medical decision-making

#### Learning Objectives
- Classify disease types (infectious, nutritional, genetic, environmental)
- Explain how infectious diseases spread (contact, droplet, airborne, vector, waterborne, foodborne)
- Describe the immune response to infection
- Apply disease prevention principles to community health planning

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Types of Disease**
- Activity: Categorize diseases: infectious (caused by pathogens — bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites), nutritional (caused by deficiency or excess), genetic (inherited), environmental (caused by toxins, radiation). Most community health issues will be infectious or nutritional.
- Key Concepts: Different causes require different responses, accurate diagnosis starts with correct classification
- Practice: Classify 15 common diseases by type

**Lesson 3-4: Disease Transmission**
- Activity: Glitter demonstration — put glitter on one student's hands. After 10 minutes of normal activity, see where it's spread. "This is how contact transmission works." Discuss other routes: droplets (coughing), airborne, vectors (mosquitoes, fleas), waterborne, foodborne.
- Key Concepts: Understanding transmission routes is the key to prevention, different routes require different interventions
- Practice: For 10 diseases, identify the transmission route and appropriate prevention measure

**Lesson 5-6: Prevention and Response**
- Activity: Design a community disease prevention plan. Address: water sanitation, food safety, personal hygiene, quarantine procedures, waste management, vector control (mosquito nets, fly screens).
- Key Concepts: Prevention is far more effective than treatment, most epidemic diseases are preventable with basic sanitation and hygiene
- Practice: Complete prevention plan and present to community health team

#### Assessment
- Classify diseases by type and transmission route
- Explain the immune response to infection
- Present a community disease prevention plan

### Unit 8: Nutrition Science Advanced (Weeks 21-26)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Food samples, nutrition data, community meal records
**Survival Relevance**: Advanced nutrition knowledge prevents deficiency diseases, optimizes crop planning, and ensures the community gets adequate nutrition from available resources

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand macronutrients (protein, carbohydrates, fats) and their roles
- Identify key micronutrients (vitamins A, B, C, D, iron, calcium, zinc) and deficiency symptoms
- Calculate caloric needs for different activity levels
- Design nutritionally complete meal plans from available foods

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Macronutrients**
- Activity: Sort foods by primary macronutrient. Proteins (meat, beans, eggs, nuts): build and repair tissue. Carbohydrates (grains, potatoes, fruit): primary energy source. Fats (oils, nuts, animal fat): concentrated energy, vitamin absorption, brain function.
- Key Concepts: All three macronutrients are essential, balance matters, extreme restriction of any causes problems
- Practice: Analyze a day's meals for macronutrient balance

**Lesson 3-4: Micronutrients and Deficiency**
- Activity: Learn the signs of common deficiencies: Vitamin C deficiency (scurvy — bleeding gums, fatigue), Vitamin A (night blindness), Iron (anemia — fatigue, pale skin), Vitamin D (weak bones), Vitamin B (various neurological symptoms).
- Key Concepts: Micronutrient deficiencies can be severe, they are preventable with dietary diversity, knowing symptoms enables early intervention
- Practice: Identify which available community foods address each deficiency

**Lesson 5-6: Meal Planning**
- Activity: Design a week of meals for a family of 4 using only locally available foods. Ensure all macro and micronutrient needs are met. Calculate approximate calories per person per day. Adjust for workers (higher calorie needs) and children (different nutrient ratios).
- Key Concepts: Meal planning is a survival skill, nutritional adequacy requires variety, seasonal availability affects planning
- Practice: Complete a 7-day meal plan with nutritional analysis

#### Assessment
- Identify all major macronutrients and key micronutrients with their functions
- Recognize 5 deficiency conditions and their dietary solutions
- Design a nutritionally complete meal plan from available community foods

### Unit 9: Microbiology (Weeks 27-34)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Magnification equipment (if available), petri dishes or clear containers, bread for mold experiments, fermentation supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Microbiology is directly applicable to food preservation (fermentation), water treatment, composting, disease prevention, and understanding infection

#### Learning Objectives
- Distinguish bacteria, viruses, and fungi
- Understand beneficial vs harmful microorganisms
- Explain fermentation as a microbial process
- Apply microbiology to food safety and water treatment

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Bacteria**
- Activity: Bacteria are everywhere — skin, soil, water, food. Most are harmless or helpful. Gut bacteria help digest food. Soil bacteria break down organic matter. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria help plants grow. Pathogenic bacteria cause illness.
- Key Concepts: Bacteria are single-celled organisms, they reproduce rapidly by division, antibiotics target bacteria (not viruses)
- Practice: List 5 beneficial and 5 harmful bacteria and their effects

**Lesson 3-4: Viruses**
- Activity: Viruses are not technically alive — they need a host cell to reproduce. Compare to bacteria: smaller, can't reproduce independently, antibiotics don't work. Examples: common cold, influenza, rabies.
- Key Concepts: Viruses hijack cells, immunity comes from exposure or vaccination, prevention (hygiene, quarantine) is the primary defense
- Practice: Compare bacteria and viruses in a detailed chart

**Lesson 5-6: Fungi**
- Activity: Grow mold on bread (leave bread in warm, moist container for several days). Observe growth. Discuss: fungi decompose organic matter (essential for soil), some are edible (mushrooms), some cause disease (athlete's foot, crop blight), yeast is a fungus used in bread and fermentation.
- Key Concepts: Fungi are decomposers, they play essential ecosystem roles, identification matters (edible vs poisonous mushrooms)
- Practice: Document mold growth with drawings and observations over 7 days

**Lesson 7-8: Applied Microbiology**
- Activity: Fermentation lab — make sauerkraut (cabbage + salt → lactobacillus fermentation), or bread (flour + yeast → CO₂ leavening), or yogurt (milk + bacterial culture). "You are using microbiology to preserve food."
- Key Concepts: Fermentation is controlled microbial activity, it preserves food, creates useful products, and has been used for thousands of years
- Practice: Complete a fermentation project and document the process, observations, and results

#### Assessment
- Distinguish bacteria, viruses, and fungi by characteristics
- Explain 3 beneficial applications of microorganisms
- Complete a fermentation project with documented results

## Grade 8: Ecology, Evolution, and Applied Biology

### Unit 10: Ecology and Conservation (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Field observation area, data collection tools, community land use maps
**Survival Relevance**: Ecological management determines whether the community's resource base grows, sustains, or depletes over time

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze population dynamics (growth, decline, limiting factors, carrying capacity)
- Understand succession (how ecosystems change over time)
- Evaluate conservation strategies for community resources
- Conduct a field ecology study

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Population Dynamics**
- Activity: Model population growth with beans in a jar. Start with 2. Double each generation (exponential: 2, 4, 8, 16...). But the jar has limited space and food. What happens? Population crashes or stabilizes.
- Key Concepts: Populations grow exponentially when unchecked, resources limit growth (carrying capacity), boom-bust cycles
- Practice: Graph population growth curves — exponential and logistic. Apply to a community resource (fish, deer, firewood).

**Lesson 3-4: Ecological Succession**
- Activity: Find a disturbed area (cleared land, old burn). Observe what's growing. Pioneer species (grasses, weeds) come first, then shrubs, then trees. This is succession — ecosystems rebuild in predictable stages.
- Key Concepts: Succession is predictable, climax communities are stable, human activity resets succession
- Practice: Document the succession stage of 3 local areas and predict future changes

**Lesson 5-6: Conservation Strategies**
- Activity: For key community resources (water, soil, timber, game, fish), design sustainable harvest levels. How much can you take without depleting the resource? What monitoring is needed?
- Key Concepts: Sustainable yield is the amount you can harvest while allowing regeneration, monitoring detects problems early
- Practice: Create a conservation plan for one community resource with harvest limits and monitoring schedule

**Lesson 7-8: Field Ecology Study**
- Activity: Design and conduct a field study — species survey, population estimate (quadrat sampling), habitat assessment, or water quality testing. Collect data, analyze, report findings.
- Key Concepts: Ecological science is observation-based, good data drives good decisions, field skills are essential
- Practice: Complete field study with data, analysis, and written report

#### Assessment
- Explain population dynamics including carrying capacity
- Describe ecological succession with local examples
- Complete a field ecology study with data, analysis, and management recommendations

### Unit 11: Evolution and Adaptation (Weeks 9-14)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Specimens showing variation (seeds, shells, leaves), historical examples
**Survival Relevance**: Evolution explains why organisms have the characteristics they do, why pests develop resistance, why crop diversity matters, and how populations change over time

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain natural selection as the mechanism of evolution
- Provide evidence for evolution (fossil record, comparative anatomy, selective breeding, observed speciation)
- Understand adaptation as a population-level response to environmental pressure
- Apply evolutionary thinking to agricultural and medical challenges

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Variation and Natural Selection**
- Activity: Collect 20 seeds of the same species. Measure variation (size, weight, color). "In nature, some of these will survive and reproduce better than others based on their traits. Over many generations, the population changes."
- Key Concepts: Individuals vary, some variations improve survival, traits are inherited, populations change over time — this is natural selection
- Practice: Simulate natural selection with colored paper "moths" on different backgrounds

**Lesson 3-4: Evidence for Evolution**
- Activity: Compare forelimbs of different vertebrates (human arm, bird wing, whale flipper, bat wing) — same bones, different functions (homology). Discuss fossil record, vestigial structures, selective breeding as accelerated evolution.
- Key Concepts: Multiple lines of evidence support evolution, homology shows common ancestry, fossil record shows change over time
- Practice: Identify 3 lines of evidence for evolution and explain each

**Lesson 5-6: Applied Evolutionary Thinking**
- Activity: "Why do pests develop resistance to pesticides? Why do bacteria develop antibiotic resistance? Because we're applying selective pressure — the resistant ones survive and reproduce." What does this mean for agriculture and medicine?
- Key Concepts: Resistance evolution is natural selection in action, crop diversity reduces pest adaptation, antibiotic stewardship prevents resistance
- Practice: Analyze a pest resistance scenario and propose management strategies using evolutionary principles

#### Assessment
- Explain natural selection with a concrete example
- Present 3 types of evidence for evolution
- Apply evolutionary thinking to an agricultural or medical problem

### Unit 12: Agricultural Science and Ethnobotany (Weeks 15-24)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Garden access, plant specimens, field guides, community elder knowledge
**Survival Relevance**: This is where biology becomes directly applicable to community food production, medicine, and material sourcing

#### Learning Objectives
- Apply selective breeding principles to crop improvement
- Understand soil biology (microbiome, mycorrhizae, nitrogen fixation)
- Identify at least 20 local plants by species with their uses
- Document ethnobotanical knowledge from community members

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Selective Breeding in Practice**
- Activity: Examine crops grown in the community. How were they selected? Save seeds from the best plants — largest, healthiest, earliest to produce. This IS selective breeding.
- Key Concepts: Every crop we eat was created by selective breeding, the process continues, it requires saving and selecting seeds intentionally
- Practice: Select and save seeds from 3 crops based on desired traits

**Lesson 3-4: Soil Biology**
- Activity: Dig a soil sample. Examine with magnification — worms, insects, fungal threads, root networks. "One teaspoon of healthy soil contains more microorganisms than people on Earth." Discuss mycorrhizae (fungi that extend root networks) and nitrogen-fixing bacteria (in legume root nodules).
- Key Concepts: Healthy soil is alive, soil organisms drive fertility, practices that kill soil biology reduce productivity
- Practice: Assess soil health at 3 locations and recommend improvements

**Lesson 5-6: Pest Management Using Biology**
- Activity: Identify pests in community gardens. Identify their natural predators. Design an integrated pest management plan using biological controls (predators, companion planting, crop rotation) rather than chemicals.
- Key Concepts: Ecosystems have built-in pest control, biological controls are sustainable, monoculture creates pest problems
- Practice: Create an IPM plan for the community garden

**Lesson 7-8: Botanical Identification**
- Activity: Field walks to identify local plants. Learn 5 plants per session — identification features, habitat, uses (food, medicine, material, toxic). Create a community plant guide with detailed drawings.
- Key Concepts: Plant identification is a survival skill, look-alikes can be dangerous, multiple identifying features are needed
- Practice: Identify 20 local plants by sight with their uses

**Lesson 9-10: Ethnobotany Project**
- Activity: Interview community members about plant knowledge — traditional uses, preparation methods, seasonal availability, cultivation techniques. Document systematically.
- Key Concepts: Ethnobotanical knowledge is accumulated over generations, it is at risk of being lost, documenting it preserves it
- Practice: Complete an ethnobotany report on at least 10 plants with community-sourced knowledge

#### Assessment
- Identify 20 local plants and describe their uses
- Create a soil health assessment with recommendations
- Complete an ethnobotany report documenting community plant knowledge
