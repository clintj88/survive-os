# SURVIVE OS Education Module — Teaching Guide

## For Community Members Who Are Not Professional Teachers

You don't need a teaching degree. You need to care about children, be willing to prepare, and follow this guide. Humans have been teaching their children for 200,000 years. Schools are only 200 years old. You can do this.

## Core Teaching Principles

### 1. Every Child Can Learn
Some learn fast, some learn slow. Some learn by hearing, some by doing, some by seeing. Your job is to find what works for each child, not to decide which children are "smart." The child who struggles with reading may be your best builder. The child who can't sit still may be your best scout.

### 2. Teach by Doing
Children under 12 learn primarily through physical activity, not lectures. If you're talking for more than 5-10 minutes at a stretch, you're losing them. Get them moving, building, measuring, growing, drawing, counting real objects, reading real books, writing real letters.

### 3. Routine Creates Safety
Children who have experienced disruption need predictability. Same schedule every day. Same signals for transitions. Same expectations. Routine is not boring to children — it is stabilizing.

### 4. Never Shame
A child who is shamed stops trying. A child who is encouraged keeps trying even when it's hard. Correct errors matter-of-factly: "Not quite — try it this way" rather than "No, that's wrong." Celebrate effort and improvement, not just perfection.

### 5. Mixed-Age Teaching Works
In a small community, you may have children of different ages in one room. This is actually an advantage. Older children teaching younger children is the most powerful learning strategy known. A 10-year-old who teaches a 6-year-old to read understands reading more deeply than they did before.

## Daily Schedule Template

Adapt to your community's rhythm, but maintain consistency:

| Time | Activity | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Morning (1 hr) | Language Arts | Reading, writing, speaking |
| Morning (1 hr) | Mathematics | Hands-on math with real materials |
| Mid-morning (30 min) | Break / Snack | Outdoor free play |
| Late morning (1 hr) | Science or Health | Observation, experiments, outdoor when possible |
| Midday (1 hr) | Lunch + Free time | |
| Early afternoon (1 hr) | Applied subject | Agriculture, trades, navigation (rotates by day) |
| Afternoon (45 min) | History/Civics/Arts | Rotate by day of week |
| Late afternoon (15 min) | Journal / Reflection | Students write or draw about what they learned |

**Total structured instruction: ~5 hours/day, 5 days/week**

Adjust for seasons — shorter days in winter, more outdoor/agricultural time in growing season.

## Preparing a Lesson

Before you teach, spend 15-20 minutes reviewing the lesson in the curriculum:

1. **Read the whole unit** — understand where this lesson fits in the sequence
2. **Read the teacher notes** — these tell you common mistakes and tips
3. **Gather materials** — most lessons need only objects from the environment
4. **Try it yourself first** — if the lesson involves an experiment or activity, do it once before teaching it
5. **Plan your questions** — asking good questions is more important than giving good lectures

## Managing Mixed-Age Groups

### Strategy 1: Tiered Activities
Same topic, different levels. Example: Math day on measurement.
- Younger children: Measure objects with hand spans
- Middle children: Measure with rulers, record in centimeters
- Older children: Calculate area and perimeter of the garden

### Strategy 2: Peer Teaching
Pair an older child with a younger child. The older child teaches or helps. Both learn.

### Strategy 3: Learning Stations
Set up 3-4 activity stations around the room. Groups rotate every 20-30 minutes. You work intensively with one group while others work independently at their stations.

### Strategy 4: Whole Group + Independent
Start with 15 minutes of whole-group instruction, then split. Younger children do guided practice with you, older children work independently. Swap after 20 minutes.

## Children with Difficulties

### Can't Sit Still
This child needs movement. Let them stand, fidget, walk while listening. Give them active jobs (distribute materials, write on the board, be the class messenger). This is not a behavior problem — it's a body need.

### Struggling to Read
Extra time with phonics activities (see Foundation Language Arts Unit 2-4). Pair with a patient reading buddy. Read aloud to them daily — they need to hear fluent reading. NEVER make them read aloud in front of the class if they're embarrassed. Private, supported practice.

### Struggling with Math
Go back to physical objects. If abstract numbers aren't working, the child needs more time with real countable things. There is no shame in going back a step. A solid foundation matters more than keeping pace.

### Behavioral Challenges
First assume an unmet need: hunger, fear, pain, grief, exhaustion, feeling unseen. Address the need first. Consistent, firm, kind boundaries. Natural consequences over punishment. A child who disrupts class can take a break outside, not as punishment but as self-regulation. "It seems like you need a minute. Take a walk and come back when you're ready."

### Gifted / Advanced
Give them harder material and leadership roles. They can teach younger children, work through advanced curriculum independently, assist the teacher, or pursue independent projects. Boredom creates behavior problems in advanced children.

## Assessment Without Tests

Traditional paper tests are less valuable than these methods:

### Observation
Watch children work. Can they do the skill? Note it in your record book.

### Conversation
Ask them to explain what they know. "Tell me how you figured that out." Understanding is revealed through explanation.

### Demonstration
"Show me how to..." is the best assessment for practical skills. Can they build a fire? Identify the plant? Measure the wood? Set the snare? Navigate to the waypoint?

### Portfolio
Each child keeps a portfolio (folder, box, or bound journal) of their best work throughout the year. This shows growth over time and serves as their record.

### Mastery, Not Grades
We don't use letter grades or scores. A child either demonstrates mastery of a skill or needs more practice. There is no "failing" — there is only "not yet." Every child moves at their own pace toward mastery.

## Record Keeping

Maintain a simple record for each child:

```
Name: _______________
Date started: _______________

Language Arts:
  [ ] Letter recognition     Date mastered: ___
  [ ] CVC word reading        Date mastered: ___
  [ ] Sight words (50)        Date mastered: ___
  [ ] Independent reading     Date mastered: ___
  [ ] Paragraph writing       Date mastered: ___
  (continue for each major milestone)

Mathematics:
  [ ] Counting to 100         Date mastered: ___
  [ ] Addition within 20      Date mastered: ___
  (continue...)
```

This record transfers with the child and shows any future teacher exactly where they are.

## You Are The Most Important Person in This Community

Teachers build the future. Every farmer, medic, builder, leader, and scientist your community will ever have passes through your hands first. What you do every day with these children determines whether your community thrives in a generation or merely survives. Take care of yourself so you can take care of them.
