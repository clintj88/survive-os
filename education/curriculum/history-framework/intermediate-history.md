# Intermediate Band (Grades 6-8, Ages 11-14) — Thematic History

## Overview
Students transition from case-study learning to analytical investigation. They work with primary sources, engage in structured debate, examine multiple perspectives on the same events, and begin to understand historiography — that history itself is an interpretation shaped by who tells it. Every unit connects directly to practical community governance, economics, and planning.

## Year 1 (Grade 6): Systems and Power

### Unit 1: Governance Systems Compared (10 weeks)
**Theme**: 2 (Governance)
**Survival Relevance**: Students are preparing to participate in (and eventually lead) community governance

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze 6+ governance systems by structure, decision-making, power distribution, and succession
- Evaluate tradeoffs of each system with evidence from historical outcomes
- Understand that governance evolves — most societies have tried multiple systems
- Draft a governance proposal for a hypothetical community

#### Comparative Analysis Framework
For EACH system studied, students analyze:
1. How are decisions made? (One person, council, vote, consensus)
2. Who has power? (One, few, many, all)
3. How does power transfer? (Inheritance, election, force, appointment)
4. What checks exist on power abuse?
5. What happens when it fails?
6. Historical examples of it working and failing

#### Systems Studied
**Tribal/Clan Council**: Indigenous governance worldwide. Elders, consensus, oral law. Iroquois Confederacy (influenced the U.S. Constitution — documented historical connection). San Bushmen consensus model. Strengths: participatory, community-embedded. Weaknesses: difficult to scale, slow decisions.

**Direct Democracy**: Athenian democracy. All citizens vote on all decisions. Who counted as a "citizen"? (Exclude: women, enslaved people, foreigners — about 10-15% of population). Multiple perspectives: revolutionary expansion of power AND exclusionary by modern standards. Strengths: high participation. Weaknesses: slow, vulnerable to manipulation, doesn't scale.

**Republic/Representative Democracy**: Roman Republic, modern examples. Elected representatives make decisions. Strengths: scalable, faster decisions. Weaknesses: representatives may not represent, money corrupts.

**Monarchy/Hereditary Rule**: Egyptian pharaohs, medieval European kingdoms, Japanese emperors. Power through birth. Strengths: stability, clear succession. Weaknesses: quality varies wildly (good king → bad king), no accountability.

**Constitutional Systems**: Documents that limit power. Magna Carta (1215), Iroquois Great Law of Peace, modern constitutions. Key concept: written rules that even leaders must follow.

**Authoritarian Systems**: Historical examples presented analytically — what conditions produce them, how they maintain power, how they end. Soviet system, various military juntas, one-party states. Presented as "this is what happens when X conditions exist" not as abstract evil.

#### Debate Project
Class divides into groups, each representing a governance system. Each group presents its system's case to a "founding community" (the class) who then votes on which system to adopt. Debrief: What arguments were most persuasive? What information was missing?

### Unit 2: Economics — Who Gets What and Why (8 weeks)
**Theme**: 3 (Trade & Economics)
**Survival Relevance**: Direct application to community resource management and inter-community trade

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain supply and demand with historical examples
- Analyze why some communities became wealthy and others didn't (geography, trade access, resources, governance)
- Understand multiple economic systems (barter, market, command, mixed) with historical evidence
- Design a resource management plan for a community of 200 people

#### Units of Study
**Barter and Gift Economies**: Pre-currency exchange. Potlatch systems of Pacific Northwest (gift-giving as economics). Advantages and limitations.

**The Invention of Money**: Shells, salt, cattle, metal coins, paper. Why did money emerge? (Solves the "double coincidence of wants" problem.) Who controlled money and why it mattered.

**Trade Empires and Economic Power**: Venice (controlled Mediterranean trade), Hanseatic League (northern European trade network), Zimbabwe (gold trade), Majapahit (Southeast Asian spice trade). How controlling trade creates power.

**Economic Collapse Case Studies**: Hyperinflation in Weimar Germany (wheelbarrows of cash to buy bread), Great Depression (how bank failures cascaded), Zimbabwe hyperinflation (2008). What causes economic systems to fail?

**Economic Systems Compared**: Present as systems with documented outcomes, not ideology:
- Market economy: Prices set by supply/demand. Historical examples of success and failure.
- Command economy: Government sets prices/production. Historical examples of success and failure.
- Mixed systems: Most real economies. How different mixes produce different outcomes.
- Commons-based: Shared resources with rules. Elinor Ostrom's research on successful commons management.

#### Applied Project
Design a complete economic system for the community: How are resources allocated? How is labor valued? How is trade with other communities managed? What prevents hoarding? Present to class for critique.

### Unit 3: Religion, Belief, and Culture as Historical Forces (8 weeks)
**Theme**: 9 (Meaning & Culture)
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding how belief systems shape societies helps navigate the diverse backgrounds within any community

#### Teacher Notes — CRITICAL
This is the most sensitive unit in the history curriculum. Present ALL traditions with equal respect and analytical distance. You are teaching about what people believed and how those beliefs shaped history — NEVER whether any belief is correct. If a student asks "which religion is right?", the appropriate answer is "that is a deeply personal question each person answers for themselves. Our job here is to understand how these traditions shaped human history."

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe 6+ major religious and philosophical traditions and their historical impact
- Analyze how belief systems influenced governance, law, art, science, and daily life
- Identify common elements across traditions (ethics, community, meaning, mortality, justice)
- Discuss the relationship between religion and power throughout history
- Demonstrate respectful engagement with beliefs different from their own

#### Traditions Studied (presented comparatively, not sequentially)
**Hinduism/Vedic Tradition**: One of the oldest continuous traditions. Caste system and its social effects. Rich philosophical tradition. Shaped Indian civilization for 4,000+ years.

**Buddhism**: Emerged as a reform movement. Spread across Asia through trade routes. Monastic communities as knowledge preservers. Emphasis on suffering and compassion.

**Judaism**: Monotheism and covenant. Diaspora as a model of cultural preservation without a homeland. Torah as law, history, and identity document. Influence on Christianity and Islam.

**Christianity**: From Jewish sect to Roman state religion. Shaped European civilization. Monastic preservation of knowledge through the Dark Ages. Diverse traditions (Orthodox, Catholic, Protestant).

**Islam**: Rapid expansion. Islamic Golden Age (800-1400): mathematics, astronomy, medicine, architecture, philosophy. Preserved and extended Greek/Roman/Persian knowledge. Diverse traditions (Sunni, Shia, Sufi).

**Chinese Philosophy**: Confucianism (social order, duty, education), Daoism (harmony with nature, balance), Legalism (strict laws and punishment). These competed and coexisted, shaping Chinese civilization.

**Indigenous Spiritual Traditions**: Animism, ancestor veneration, land-based spirituality across continents. Not one system but thousands, each tied to specific environments and communities. Often dismissed historically but containing deep ecological knowledge.

**Secular Philosophy**: Greek philosophy (Socrates, Plato, Aristotle), Enlightenment rationalism, scientific method as a framework for understanding the world.

#### Key Questions (All Multiple Perspectives)
- Every human culture independently developed spiritual/philosophical traditions. What does that universality tell us?
- How have religious institutions both preserved and suppressed knowledge? (Monasteries saved ancient texts AND the Inquisition banned books)
- What role should belief play in governance? Compare theocracy, state religion, secular governance. What are the tradeoffs of each?
- What elements appear across almost all traditions? (Golden Rule/reciprocity, care for the vulnerable, community obligation, meaning beyond the material)

## Year 2 (Grade 7): Crisis and Adaptation

### Unit 4: Epidemics That Changed History (8 weeks)
**Theme**: 5 (Disease)
**Survival Relevance**: Epidemic preparedness is directly relevant. This is applied epidemiology through history.

#### Case Studies with Primary Sources
**Plague of Athens (430 BCE)**: Thucydides' account — one of the first detailed epidemic descriptions. Killed 25% of the population including Pericles. Weakened Athens in the Peloponnesian War.

**Black Death (1347-1353)**: 30-50% of Europe died. Primary sources: Boccaccio's description, Flagellant movements, Jewish persecution (blamed for plague — pattern of scapegoating during epidemics). Aftermath: labor shortage → higher wages → weakened feudalism. Positive consequences from catastrophe.

**Smallpox in the Americas (1500s)**: 90% mortality in some populations. Cortez conquered the Aztec Empire partly because smallpox arrived before he did. Completely reshaped the demographics of two continents. Indigenous perspectives on the devastation.

**1918 Influenza**: Killed 50-100 million people worldwide. Censored during wartime (named "Spanish Flu" because Spain, being neutral, was the only country honestly reporting). Lessons in pandemic communication.

**HIV/AIDS (1980s-present)**: Modern epidemic shaped by stigma, politics, and science. Shows how social factors amplify or mitigate disease.

#### Analytical Framework
For each epidemic: What was the pathogen? How did it spread? What social conditions amplified it? How did communities respond? What was the lasting impact? What could have been done differently?

#### Applied Project
Students create an epidemic response plan for their community using historical lessons. Present to community health team for review.

### Unit 5: Revolution and Change — Why Societies Transform (10 weeks)
**Theme**: 2 (Governance), 7 (Conflict)
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding what drives social upheaval helps communities manage internal tensions before they become crises

#### Case Studies (each presented from multiple perspectives)
**Agricultural to Urban (Neolithic)**: The original revolution. Voluntary? Forced by population pressure? Gradual or sudden? Debate the evidence.

**French Revolution (1789)**: Inequality → hunger → revolution → terror → dictatorship → democracy (eventually). Shows that revolution often produces worse before better. Perspectives: aristocracy, bourgeoisie, peasants, women.

**Haitian Revolution (1791-1804)**: Enslaved people defeated European armies and established the first free Black republic. Largely omitted from European-centered histories. What does that omission tell us about who writes history?

**Industrial Revolution (1760-1840)**: Technological, not political. But transformed every aspect of life. Child labor, urbanization, pollution, wealth creation, eventually improved living standards. Perspectives: factory owner, factory worker, displaced farmer, child laborer.

**Indian Independence Movement (1947)**: Non-violent resistance as a strategy. Gandhi, but also thousands of unnamed activists. Partition and its consequences. Shows that independence doesn't solve all problems — it creates new ones.

**Green Revolution (1960s)**: Agricultural technology (new crop varieties, fertilizers, irrigation) vastly increased food production. Saved billions from famine. Also created dependency on chemicals, reduced crop diversity, displaced small farmers. Tradeoffs.

#### Key Questions
- What conditions produce revolution? (Usually: inequality + food insecurity + delegitimized leadership + triggering event)
- Do revolutions achieve their goals? (Almost never immediately. Often the goals are achieved a generation later through the institutions the revolution creates.)
- Is gradual change or revolution more effective? (Depends on context. Debate with evidence.)
- What can our community learn about managing tensions before they become crises?

### Unit 6: Knowledge Lost and Found (6 weeks)
**Theme**: 4 (Communication & Knowledge), 8 (Collapse)
**Survival Relevance**: This is the historical argument for SURVIVE OS itself

#### Case Studies
**Bronze Age Collapse — Linear B Forgotten**: Mycenaean Greece had writing (Linear B). After the collapse (~1177 BCE), writing was lost for 400 years. An entire civilization forgot how to write. Shows that literacy is not permanent.

**Post-Roman Knowledge Loss**: Roman concrete formula was lost for 1,000 years. Indoor plumbing disappeared from Europe. Medical knowledge regressed. Why? The institutions that preserved knowledge (schools, libraries, professional guilds) collapsed.

**Islamic Golden Age Preservation**: While Europe lost knowledge, Islamic scholars preserved, translated, and extended Greek, Roman, Persian, and Indian texts. Al-Khwarizmi (algebra), Ibn al-Haytham (optics), Ibn Sina (medicine). Without Islamic preservation, much classical knowledge would be permanently lost.

**Destruction of Indigenous Knowledge**: Colonial powers systematically destroyed Indigenous knowledge systems — burning Maya codices, banning Aboriginal languages, disrupting oral traditions. Knowledge accumulated over millennia was lost in decades. Multiple perspectives: colonizers' justifications (framed as "civilizing") vs. the reality of knowledge destruction.

**Modern Library Projects**: Kiwix (offline Wikipedia), Internet Archive, Svalbard Global Seed Vault, GitHub Arctic Code Vault. Efforts to preserve knowledge against catastrophe. Our project is in this tradition.

#### Capstone Discussion
"We are living in a post-knowledge-loss scenario. What knowledge has already been lost in our community? What are we doing to prevent further loss? What should we be doing?"

## Year 3 (Grade 8): Synthesis and Application

### Unit 7: The Collapse Seminar (10 weeks)
**Theme**: 8 (Collapse & Resilience)
**Survival Relevance**: Capstone application of all historical learning to community survival

#### Structure
Students become historians. Each student or pair selects a collapsed civilization, conducts research using all available sources, and presents a "forensic analysis" to the class.

#### Collapse Studies Available
Bronze Age Collapse, Western Roman Empire, Maya Classic Period, Khmer Empire (Angkor), Easter Island (Rapa Nui), Greenland Norse, Cahokia (Mississippi), Great Zimbabwe, Soviet Union, multiple others

#### Analysis Framework (Applied to Each Case)
1. **Environmental factors**: Climate change, resource depletion, soil degradation, water problems
2. **Governance factors**: Leadership failure, corruption, loss of legitimacy, inability to adapt
3. **Economic factors**: Trade disruption, inequality, debt, inflation, resource misallocation
4. **Social factors**: Loss of social cohesion, internal conflict, erosion of trust, knowledge loss
5. **External factors**: Invasion, disease, trade partner collapse, natural disaster
6. **The combination**: How did multiple factors interact? (Collapse is almost never monocausal)
7. **What survived**: What knowledge, institutions, or people continued after the collapse?
8. **Lessons for us**: Specific, actionable recommendations for our community

#### Resilience Counter-Studies
Each student also studies one civilization that survived a comparable crisis:
- Byzantine Empire (survived the fall of Rome for 1,000 years)
- Japan (maintained continuity through radical transformation — feudal → modern in one generation)
- Aboriginal Australian cultures (65,000 years of continuous culture)
- Chinese civilization (survived multiple invasions and internal collapses)
- Jewish diaspora communities (maintained cultural identity without a homeland for 2,000 years)

What did the survivors do differently? Students compare their collapse case to their resilience case.

### Unit 8: Historiography — Who Tells the Story Matters (6 weeks)
**Theme**: All themes
**Survival Relevance**: Critical thinking about information sources is essential for governance and decision-making

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that all historical accounts are interpretations, not objective truth
- Identify bias in historical sources (who wrote it, for what audience, with what purpose)
- Compare accounts of the same event from different perspectives
- Understand how history has been used as a political tool
- Apply source analysis skills to current community information

#### Exercises
**Same Event, Different Stories**: Read 2-3 accounts of the same event from different perspectives. How do they differ? Why?

**Who's Missing?**: Examine a historical narrative and identify whose voices are absent. What would the story look like from their perspective?

**Our History, Our Bias**: Examine the Community History Book started in Foundation Band. What biases might it contain? Whose perspectives are underrepresented? Revise entries to be more complete.

### Unit 9: Applied Governance Design (8 weeks)
**Theme**: 2 (Governance), 3 (Economics), 7 (Conflict)
**Survival Relevance**: This is a practicum — students design real governance solutions

#### The Scenario
Students are presented with a scenario: "A community of 300 people needs to establish a governance system. They have diverse backgrounds, limited resources, and intermittent contact with 3 neighboring communities."

#### Tasks (Completed in Teams)
1. Draft a community charter/constitution (drawing on all historical governance models studied)
2. Design a justice system (incorporating conflict resolution methods from multiple cultures)
3. Create an economic plan (resource allocation, trade policy, labor organization)
4. Develop a knowledge preservation strategy (what to prioritize, how to teach, how to record)
5. Write an inter-community diplomacy framework (trade agreements, mutual defense, cultural exchange)

#### Presentation
Teams present their complete governance design to the class and community leaders. Community leaders provide feedback on practicality. Selected elements may be adopted by the actual community.

This is not a simulation — it is preparation for real civic participation.
