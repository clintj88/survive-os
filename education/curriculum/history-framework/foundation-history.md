# Foundation Band (Grades K-2, Ages 5-8) — History & Community

## Overview
At this age, history is about understanding that people live in different ways, that our community has a story, and that we can learn from how other people solved the same problems we face. No dates, no memorization — just stories, comparisons, and curiosity.

## Year 1: Our Community, Our Story

### Unit 1: Who We Are (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Drawing supplies, interview questions
**Survival Relevance**: Community identity and cohesion — knowing who we are and where we came from binds us together
**Theme**: All themes (introduction)

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe their own family and its story
- Name the roles different community members play
- Understand that every person contributes to the community
- Begin a community history book (class project)

#### Teacher Notes
This unit is about belonging. Children who feel they are part of something larger are more resilient. Be sensitive to children who have lost family members — frame "family" broadly (the people who take care of you and love you).

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: My Story**
- Activity: Each child draws their family and tells a story about them. "Where did your family come from? What do they do in the community?" Transcribe for non-writers.
- Key concept: Every person has a story. Every story matters.

**Lesson 3-4: Community Helpers**
- Activity: Walk through the community. Visit the garden, the medical area, the kitchen, the workshop, the watch post. Each person explains what they do and why it matters.
- Key concept: A community works because everyone does their part. (Theme 2: Governance)

**Lesson 5-6: Our Community Story**
- Activity: Invite an elder or founding member to tell the story of how the community formed. Children draw key events. Begin a "Community History Book" that the class adds to all year.
- Key concept: We have a history too. Recording it preserves it. (Theme 4: Knowledge)

**Lesson 7-8: Community Rules and Why We Have Them**
- Activity: Discuss community rules. Why does each one exist? What would happen without it? Children propose a class rule and discuss it democratically.
- Key concept: Rules exist to help everyone, not to punish. People agree on rules together. (Theme 2: Governance)

#### Assessment
- Can name 5+ community roles and explain what each person does
- Can tell the story of their community's founding in their own words
- Participates in creating and following a class rule

### Unit 2: How People Meet Basic Needs (Weeks 9-18)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Pictures/drawings of different shelters, foods, clothing from around the world
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding that all humans solve the same basic problems (food, shelter, water, safety) in different ways depending on their environment
**Theme**: 1 (Food), 6 (Engineering)

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify the four basic human needs: food, water, shelter, safety
- Describe how their community meets each need
- Compare how people in different environments meet the same needs differently
- Understand that environment shapes how people live

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Do We Need to Survive?**
- Activity: Brainstorm as a class. "What would happen if we didn't have water? Food? Shelter?" Rank needs in order of urgency (connects to Rule of 3s from knowledge base).
- Key concept: All humans everywhere need the same things

**Lesson 3-4: How We Get Our Food**
- Activity: Visit the garden and food storage. Where does our food come from? Who grows it? What happens at each step from seed to plate?
- Compare: "People in the desert grow different food than people in the forest. Why?"
- Key concept: Food systems depend on environment (Theme 1)

**Lesson 5-6: Shelters Around the World**
- Activity: Teacher describes (or shows pictures of) shelters from different environments: igloo (Arctic), grass hut (tropical), adobe (desert), log cabin (forest), yurt (steppe), stilt house (wetland). Why is each shape and material suited to its place?
- Build: Each child designs a shelter for a given environment using sticks, clay, grass
- Key concept: People solve the same problem differently depending on what's available (Theme 6)

**Lesson 7-8: Water — The Most Important Thing**
- Activity: Trace the community's water supply. Where does it come from? How is it cleaned? What would happen if it stopped?
- Compare: How did ancient people get water? (Wells, rivers, rain collection, aqueducts — simple descriptions)
- Key concept: Every civilization in history was built near water (Theme 6)

**Lesson 9-10: Staying Safe**
- Activity: How does our community stay safe? (Watch schedule, walls/fences, rules, teamwork)
- Compare: How did other communities stay safe? (Castles, walls, guard animals, alarm systems)
- Key concept: Safety comes from working together, not from one strong person (Theme 7)

### Unit 3: Long Ago and Today (Weeks 19-28)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Drawings, storytelling
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding that technology and knowledge build over time — and can be lost
**Theme**: 4 (Knowledge), 6 (Engineering)

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand "long ago," "recently," and "now" as time concepts
- Compare daily life now with daily life "long ago" (pre-electricity, pre-industrial)
- Understand that people invented things over time to solve problems
- Understand that knowledge can be lost if not preserved

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How Did People Live Without Electricity?**
- Activity: Spend a lesson doing things "the old way." Grind grain by hand. Carry water. Use a candle for light. Draw instead of photograph.
- Discussion: "What was harder? What was actually nice about it?"
- Key concept: People lived successfully for thousands of years without electricity. We can too.

**Lesson 3-4: The Invention Chain**
- Activity: Tell the story of inventions as a chain. Someone figured out fire → cooking → pottery → metalworking → tools → farming → writing → printing. Each invention made the next one possible.
- Practice: Children draw the invention chain as a mural
- Key concept: Knowledge builds on previous knowledge (Theme 4)

**Lesson 5-6: What Happens When Knowledge Is Lost?**
- Activity: Play "telephone" with a complex instruction (like a recipe). By the end, it's garbled. "This is what happens when knowledge isn't written down."
- Story: Tell about the Library of Alexandria — a great collection of knowledge that was destroyed. "What was lost? Could we lose knowledge today?"
- Key concept: Writing things down and teaching others preserves knowledge. This is why we go to school. (Theme 4)

**Lesson 7-8: People Who Figured Things Out**
- Activity: Tell stories of discoveries from different cultures — NOT as hero stories but as "humans solving problems": Chinese invention of paper, West African iron smelting, Polynesian ocean navigation, Indigenous Australian fire management, Inuit snow shelter design
- Key concept: Every culture contributed to human knowledge. No single group invented everything. (Theme 6)

**Lesson 9-10: Our Job**
- Activity: "What knowledge does our community have that we need to preserve?" Brainstorm as a class. Each child picks one skill they want to learn from an adult and draws/writes about it.
- Key concept: We are part of the chain. What we learn and teach keeps knowledge alive. (Theme 4)

### Unit 4: Getting Along (Weeks 29-36)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Roleplay props, discussion circle
**Survival Relevance**: Conflict resolution and cooperation are the foundation of community survival
**Theme**: 2 (Governance), 7 (Conflict)

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that disagreements are normal and can be resolved without violence
- Practice basic conflict resolution (use words, listen, find a compromise)
- Understand that fair rules help everyone
- Understand the concept of trade and fairness in exchange

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: When People Disagree**
- Activity: Roleplay common conflicts (two children want the same tool, someone feels a chore assignment is unfair). Practice steps: 1) Say how you feel, 2) Listen to the other person, 3) Find a solution you both accept.
- Key concept: Conflicts are solved by talking, not fighting. This has always been true.

**Lesson 3-4: Fair and Unfair**
- Activity: Give children a scenario with an unfair distribution (one child gets all the snacks, others get none). How does it feel? How would you make it fair?
- Historical connection: "Communities throughout history have struggled with fairness. Let's hear about some ways they tried to solve it."
- Key concept: Fairness doesn't always mean equal — sometimes people need more (sick people need more medicine, workers need more food). Fair means everyone gets what they need. (Theme 2)

**Lesson 5-6: Trading**
- Activity: Give each child different "resources" (colored stones, sticks, leaves). Each needs a set of all three types. They must trade to get what they need. Debrief: Was it easy? Was it fair? What made trading work well?
- Key concept: Trade is how communities help each other get what they need. It requires trust and fairness. (Theme 3)

**Lesson 7-8: Making Decisions Together**
- Activity: The class must decide something real (what to name the class garden plot, what game to play, what mural to paint). Practice different methods: voting, consensus, leader decides. Discuss pros and cons of each.
- Key concept: There are many ways to make group decisions. Each has tradeoffs. (Theme 2)

## Year 2: People and Places

### Unit 5: Different Places, Different Lives (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Theme**: 1 (Food), 6 (Engineering), 9 (Culture)

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe how people in at least 4 different environments live (arctic, desert, tropical, temperate)
- Explain why their lives are different (environment determines resources)
- Identify similarities across all human communities (all need food, shelter, water, community, stories)
- Show respect for different ways of living

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Arctic Peoples**
- How Inuit and Sami people survive in extreme cold
- Igloos, hunting seals and caribou, clothing from animal skins, dog sleds
- What can we learn? Insulation principles, fat-rich diet in cold, community cooperation for hunts

**Lesson 3-4: Desert Peoples**
- How Bedouin, Aboriginal Australian, and Pueblo peoples thrive in dry environments
- Water conservation, shade architecture, night travel, drought-resistant crops
- What can we learn? Water management, building for heat, navigating by stars

**Lesson 5-6: Tropical Peoples**
- How peoples of the Amazon, Southeast Asia, and Congo Basin live in dense forest/heat
- Stilt houses, slash-and-burn agriculture, medicinal plants, river transportation
- What can we learn? Plant knowledge, building for humidity, living with abundant biodiversity

**Lesson 7-8: Island Peoples**
- How Polynesian navigators crossed the Pacific, how island communities manage limited resources
- Navigation by stars and waves, fishing, coconut/breadfruit cultivation, reef management
- What can we learn? Navigation without instruments, sustainable fishing, managing limited resources

**Lesson 9-10: Our Place**
- How does our environment compare? What are our advantages and challenges?
- What ideas from other cultures could help us?
- Project: Draw a poster showing "What we can learn from people around the world"

### Unit 6: How Food Changed Everything (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Theme**: 1 (Food), 8 (Collapse)

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that humans were hunters and gatherers for most of history
- Understand that farming was invented independently in many places
- Explain how farming changed how people lived (settled in one place, built towns, specialized jobs)
- Understand what happens when food systems fail (famine, migration, conflict)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Life Before Farming**
- Story: Imagine life as a hunter-gatherer. Move with the seasons, follow animal herds, know hundreds of plants. Small groups of 20-50 people. Everyone knows everyone.
- Activity: Foraging walk — how much food can you find in one hour? (Supervised, identification only)
- Key concept: Hunter-gatherers were not primitive — they had deep knowledge of their environment

**Lesson 3-4: Someone Figured Out Farming**
- Story: In at least 7 different places, people independently discovered that they could plant seeds and grow food. Mesopotamia (wheat), China (rice), Mexico (corn), Africa (sorghum, millet), New Guinea (taro), Andes (potatoes)
- Activity: Plant seeds in the classroom. "You are doing what people discovered 10,000 years ago."
- Key concept: This is the most important invention in human history. It changed everything.

**Lesson 5-6: What Farming Changed**
- Discussion: Once you can grow food in one place, you don't have to move. What happens next? (Build permanent homes → villages → towns → need for rules → leaders → jobs → trade → writing to keep track of things)
- Activity: Draw a "farming changed everything" flowchart
- Key concept: Agriculture made civilization possible. Everything we have traces back to farming.

**Lesson 7-8: Animals Join the Farm**
- Story: People domesticated animals for food, work, and companionship. Different animals in different places: sheep, goats, cattle, horses, camels, llamas, chickens, dogs
- Activity: Visit community livestock. What does each animal provide?
- Key concept: Animals multiply human capability (Theme 6)

**Lesson 9-10: When Food Runs Out**
- Discussion (age-appropriate): What happens when crops fail? People go hungry, move, or fight over what's left. This has happened many times throughout history.
- Story: Tell an age-appropriate version of a famine (Irish Potato Famine is accessible — relied on one crop, it failed, millions suffered). What's the lesson?
- Key concept: Never depend on just one food source. Diversity is survival. (Theme 8)

### Unit 7: How People Talk to Each Other (Weeks 21-30)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Theme**: 4 (Communication & Knowledge)

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that language is humanity's most important invention
- Know that people communicate in many ways (speech, writing, pictures, signals, music)
- Understand why writing was invented and why it matters
- Value the preservation of knowledge in their own community

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Before Writing**
- How did people remember things without writing? Oral tradition, memory palaces, songs, art
- Activity: Learn a piece of community knowledge as a song or rhyme. See how well it sticks after a week.
- Story: Aboriginal Australians preserved knowledge through songlines for 60,000+ years — the longest continuous cultural tradition on Earth

**Lesson 3-4: The Invention of Writing**
- Writing was invented independently at least 4 times: Sumerians (cuneiform), Egyptians (hieroglyphics), Chinese (oracle bones), Mesoamericans (Maya glyphs)
- Activity: Invent your own writing system. Create symbols for 10 common words. Trade messages with a partner.
- Key concept: Writing allows knowledge to outlive the person who learned it

**Lesson 5-6: Sending Messages Far Away**
- How have people communicated across distance? Drums, smoke signals, messenger runners, carrier pigeons, signal fires, semaphore, telegraph, radio
- Activity: Send a message across the school yard using only visual signals (flags, arm positions)
- Key concept: Long-distance communication connects communities (connects to Meshtastic/radio modules)

**Lesson 7-8: Printing Changed Everything**
- Story: For most of history, every book was hand-copied. One book could take a year to make. Then printing was invented (China: woodblock printing ~700 CE, Europe: movable type ~1440 CE). Suddenly books were everywhere. Knowledge exploded.
- Activity: Make a simple stamp print (carved potato or clay). Print multiple copies. Compare to hand-drawing multiple copies. Which is faster?
- Key concept: Making knowledge easy to copy is how learning spreads

**Lesson 9-10: Our Responsibility**
- Discussion: "We have a knowledge base on our computers. We have books in our library. We have elders who know things. What happens if we don't teach what we know to younger children?"
- Project: Each child learns one thing from an elder and writes/draws it for the community record
- Key concept: YOU are part of the chain of knowledge. What you learn and pass on matters.

## Year 3: How Communities Work

### Unit 8: Rules, Leaders, and Fairness (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Theme**: 2 (Governance), 7 (Conflict)

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand why communities need rules and leaders
- Compare different ways communities have been led (council of elders, elected leader, hereditary chief, consensus)
- Understand basic concepts of justice (fairness, hearing both sides, consequences matching actions)
- Participate in a classroom governance simulation

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Why Rules Exist**
- Activity: Play a game with no rules. Chaos ensues. Then add rules. Discuss: "Were rules limiting or freeing?"
- Historical examples: Earliest known laws (Code of Ur-Nammu ~2100 BCE, even before Hammurabi). What were they about? Mostly fairness: wages, property, injury, family disputes.
- Key concept: The first thing every community in history does is make rules

**Lesson 3-4: Different Kinds of Leaders**
- Present different systems (age-appropriate, neutral tone):
  - Council of elders (many Indigenous societies worldwide — decisions by experienced members)
  - Elected leader (Athens, many modern communities — people vote)
  - Hereditary chief/king (medieval Europe, many kingdoms — leadership passes in a family)
  - Consensus (Quaker meetings, Iroquois Confederacy — discussion until everyone agrees)
- Activity: Try each method for a class decision. Discuss: which was fastest? Which felt fairest? Which was hardest?
- Key concept: There is no perfect system. Every method has tradeoffs.

**Lesson 5-6: When Leaders Go Wrong**
- Discussion (age-appropriate): What happens when a leader stops listening? When rules only help some people? When there's no way to change bad rules?
- Stories: Age-appropriate examples of communities that changed their leadership when it wasn't working (simplified: American Revolution, Indian independence movement, community-level examples)
- Key concept: The ability to change leadership peacefully is the most important feature of any system

**Lesson 7-8: Justice — Hearing Both Sides**
- Activity: Mock trial or dispute resolution. Two students roleplay a conflict. A "judge" (rotating role) hears both sides, class discusses fair outcome.
- Key concept: Justice means hearing both sides before deciding. Even in our community, we do this.

**Lesson 9-10: Our Community Governance**
- Activity: Interview community leaders. How are decisions made here? How are disputes resolved? What would they change?
- Project: Create a "How Our Community Works" poster for the community board

### Unit 9: Trade — How Communities Help Each Other (Weeks 11-18)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Theme**: 3 (Trade), 7 (Conflict)

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that no community can make everything it needs
- Understand trade as mutual benefit (not winners and losers)
- Describe how trade routes connected ancient communities
- Understand that trade creates peace and relationships between communities

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What We Can't Make Ourselves**
- Activity: Inventory something the community uses that it can't produce itself (salt? metal? medicine? certain seeds?). "How do we get these things?"
- Key concept: Self-sufficiency is a myth. Every community in history traded.

**Lesson 3-4: Ancient Trade Routes (Stories)**
- Silk Road: Connected China to Rome. Ideas, inventions, food, and disease all traveled along it.
- Trans-Saharan trade: Gold from West Africa traded for salt from the Sahara. Salt was as valuable as gold.
- Polynesian trade: Canoe networks across thousands of miles of ocean.
- Key concept: Trade routes are also knowledge routes. Ideas travel with goods.

**Lesson 5-6: What Makes Trade Fair?**
- Activity: Simulate a trade market. Different groups have different resources. Trade to get what you need. Debrief: Was it fair? Did anyone feel cheated? What makes a trade feel fair?
- Key concept: Trust is the foundation of trade. Cheating ends trade relationships.

**Lesson 7-8: Trade and Peace**
- Discussion: "If your community trades with another community every month, are you more or less likely to fight with them?"
- Historical examples: Trade between communities almost always reduces conflict. Cutting off trade often leads to war.
- Key concept: Trading partners rarely become enemies. This is one of the most reliable patterns in all of human history. (Theme 7)

### Unit 10: What Lasts and What Doesn't (Weeks 19-28)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Theme**: 8 (Collapse & Resilience)

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that communities and civilizations can grow, change, shrink, and end
- Identify factors that help communities last (adaptability, fairness, knowledge preservation, trade)
- Identify factors that cause communities to struggle (environmental damage, unfair leadership, isolation, losing knowledge)
- Apply these lessons to their own community

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Things That Last**
- Activity: What's the oldest thing in our community? The oldest building? The oldest tree? The oldest knowledge? What helped it survive?
- Story: Communities that have lasted a very long time — Aboriginal Australians (65,000+ years), Chinese civilization (continuous culture for 5,000 years). What did they do right?
- Key concept: Some things last because people take care of them

**Lesson 3-4: The Easter Island Story**
- Story (age-appropriate): People settled a beautiful island. They cut down all the trees to move big statues. Without trees: no boats for fishing, no soil for farming (erosion), no wood for shelter. The community collapsed.
- Discussion: "Could this happen to us? How do we prevent it?"
- Key concept: If you use up your resources without replacing them, you can't survive. (Theme 8)

**Lesson 5-6: Why Some Communities Struggled**
- Stories (simplified, age-appropriate, non-nationalistic):
  - A community that stopped sharing fairly — people fought and some left
  - A community that cut down its forest — ran out of fuel and building material
  - A community that stopped teaching its children — forgot how to do essential things in one generation
- Key concept: Communities don't fail because of bad luck. They fail because of choices that compound over time.

**Lesson 7-8: What Makes a Community Strong?**
- Class brainstorm: Based on everything we've learned, what makes a community strong? (Expect: fairness, sharing, teaching children, taking care of the environment, trading with neighbors, having good rules, preserving knowledge)
- Activity: Create a "Recipe for a Strong Community" mural

**Lesson 9-10: Our Pledge**
- Activity: Class writes a pledge based on what they've learned. "We will preserve knowledge. We will share fairly. We will take care of our environment. We will solve conflicts with words."
- Add to the Community History Book started in Unit 1
- This closes the loop — from "who are we" to "what we've learned from all of history" to "what we commit to do"

#### Assessment Across All History Units
- Can explain why communities need rules and leadership
- Can describe how people in different environments meet their basic needs
- Can tell the story of at least 3 historical discoveries/inventions from different cultures
- Can explain why trade is important for communities
- Can name 3 factors that make communities succeed and 3 that cause them to struggle
- Can participate respectfully in group decision-making and conflict resolution
- Has contributed to the Community History Book
