# Building Band (Grades 3-5, Ages 8-11) — Health

## Overview
Building Band health education transitions students from basic safety awareness to genuine medical knowledge. Students learn nutrition science, disease transmission, puberty, mental health, and first aid skills that could save lives. By Grade 5, students can clean and dress a wound, perform CPR steps, recognize medical emergencies, and plan nutritious meals. This band feeds directly into the Intermediate health-first-aid track.

## Grade 3: Nutrition Science, Disease, and First Aid Foundations

### Unit 1: Nutrition Science (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Prerequisites**: Foundation health — three food groups, basic hygiene
**Materials**: Community food examples, simple nutrition charts
**Survival Relevance**: Malnutrition and deficiency diseases are the most common health problems in post-infrastructure communities. Understanding nutrition prevents them.

#### Learning Objectives
- Name the three macronutrients (carbohydrates, proteins, fats) and their functions
- Identify key vitamins and what happens without them (vitamin C → scurvy, vitamin D → rickets)
- Understand caloric needs vary by age, size, and activity
- Recognize signs of common deficiency diseases
- Plan a balanced meal using community-available foods

#### Teacher Notes
Nutrition education must be sensitive to food scarcity. Never frame nutrition as "eat this, not that" when children may have limited choices. Instead: "When we have choices, here's how to choose wisely. When food is scarce, here's how to get the most from what's available." Emphasize nutrient-dense foods that the community can produce.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Macronutrients**
- Activity: Sort community foods into three groups: carbohydrates (grains, potatoes, bread — energy), proteins (meat, eggs, beans, nuts — building/repair), fats (oil, butter, nuts, animal fat — energy storage, brain function).
- Key Concepts: Your body needs all three. No single food has everything.

**Lesson 3-4: Vitamins and Deficiency**
- Activity: Tell the story of scurvy — sailors who ate only salt pork and hardtack got bleeding gums, lost teeth, died. Solution: citrus fruits (vitamin C). "Vitamins are in fresh foods. Without them, specific diseases develop."
- Key vitamins: C (citrus, peppers → scurvy without), D (sunlight → rickets without), A (orange vegetables, liver → night blindness without), B (whole grains, meat → fatigue without)

**Lesson 5-6: Caloric Needs**
- Activity: "A child needs about 1500-2000 calories per day. A working adult needs 2500-3000. A pregnant woman needs extra. An elderly person may need less."
- Practice: Estimate calories in common meals using simple charts

**Lesson 7-8: Deficiency Recognition**
- Activity: Learn the signs: constant tiredness = possible iron or B deficiency. Frequent illness = possible vitamin C or protein deficiency. Poor night vision = possible vitamin A. Bone pain = possible vitamin D.
- Key Concept: Recognizing deficiency early allows treatment before serious damage

**Lesson 9-10: Meal Planning**
- Activity: Plan 3 days of meals using available community foods that cover all macronutrients and major vitamins. Present plans to class.

#### Assessment
- Can name the 3 macronutrients and their functions
- Can name 4 vitamins and their deficiency symptoms
- Can plan a nutritionally balanced meal
- Can recognize basic signs of nutritional deficiency

### Unit 2: Disease Transmission (Weeks 11-18)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Glitter (for germ demonstration), water samples
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding HOW disease spreads is the foundation of prevention — the most important medical skill in a post-infrastructure community

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain 4 ways diseases spread: direct contact, airborne droplets, contaminated water/food, insect vectors
- Identify which common diseases spread by which method
- Explain how handwashing, clean water, food safety, and insect control prevent disease
- Understand that germs are living organisms too small to see

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Direct Contact**
- Activity: Glitter on one student's hands. They shake hands with 3 people, who shake with 3 more. Check everyone's hands. "Some diseases spread like this — through touching."
- Examples: skin infections, colds (hand to face)

**Lesson 3-4: Airborne Droplets**
- Activity: Spray water from a spray bottle in sunlight — see the droplet cloud. "When you cough or sneeze, droplets fly 6 feet. Germs ride on those droplets."
- Examples: flu, tuberculosis, common cold

**Lesson 5-6: Contaminated Water and Food**
- Activity: "Add one drop of dye to a glass of water — the whole glass is contaminated. That's what a single germ source does to a water supply."
- Examples: cholera, dysentery, typhoid. "These diseases are why we ALWAYS treat water."

**Lesson 7-8: Insect Vectors**
- Activity: "Mosquitoes carry malaria. Fleas carry plague. Ticks carry Lyme disease. The insect bites an infected person, then bites you, transferring the germ."
- Prevention: Mosquito nets, standing water elimination, tick checks, flea control on animals

### Unit 3: Dental Health (Weeks 19-24)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Toothbrush or chew stick, dental model or diagram
**Survival Relevance**: Without dentists, prevention is the ONLY dental care. A tooth infection can become life-threatening without antibiotics.

#### Learning Objectives
- Demonstrate proper brushing and flossing technique
- Explain how cavities form (bacteria + sugar → acid → enamel decay)
- Identify foods that protect teeth vs. harm teeth
- Understand that dental infections can spread to the bloodstream (sepsis)
- Commit to twice-daily brushing routine

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How Cavities Form**
- Activity: "Your mouth is full of bacteria. When you eat sugar, bacteria eat it too and produce acid. Acid dissolves tooth enamel. A hole forms — that's a cavity. If it gets deep enough, bacteria enter the tooth and cause infection."

**Lesson 3-4: Prevention**
- Activity: Demonstrate brushing technique — all surfaces, gums, tongue. Flossing or using a thin thread between teeth. "Brushing removes bacteria and food. Without brushing, acid builds up."
- Foods that help: crunchy vegetables, cheese, water. Foods that harm: sticky sweets, dried fruit stuck on teeth, sugary drinks.

**Lesson 5-6: When Teeth Go Wrong**
- Activity: "A toothache that won't stop means the decay has reached the nerve. Swelling, fever, or pus around a tooth is an emergency — tell the medical team immediately. Tooth infections can spread to your brain or heart."
- Key Concept: Dental health is not cosmetic — it is survival medicine.

### Unit 4: First Aid Certification Basics (Weeks 25-34)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Clean cloth strips, adhesive tape, splinting materials, practice patients (peers)
**Survival Relevance**: Expanding on Foundation first aid toward genuine competency. Every community member should be able to handle common injuries.

#### Learning Objectives
- Clean, dress, and bandage wounds of various types (cuts, scrapes, punctures)
- Apply a sling for arm injuries
- Recognize when NOT to move an injured person (spinal injury risk)
- Apply direct pressure to control bleeding
- Recognize and respond to shock (pale, cold, confused → lay flat, elevate legs, keep warm)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Wound Classification**
- Activity: Types of wounds — abrasion (scrape), laceration (cut), puncture (nail, thorn), avulsion (skin torn away). Each type gets similar treatment: clean, assess depth, dress.

**Lesson 3-4: Wound Care Protocol**
- Activity: Full protocol: 1) Wash hands. 2) Apply gloves if available. 3) Rinse wound with clean water. 4) Remove visible debris. 5) Apply pressure if bleeding. 6) Apply clean dressing. 7) Secure with bandage. 8) Check daily.
- Practice: Partners practice on each other using non-wound areas

**Lesson 5-6: Bandaging Techniques**
- Activity: Spiral bandage (limbs), figure-8 (joints), head bandage, finger bandage. Sling for arm support. Practice until confident.

**Lesson 7-8: Bleeding and Shock**
- Activity: "Heavy bleeding: apply DIRECT PRESSURE with clean cloth. Do not remove the cloth — add more on top. Elevate the wound above the heart if possible. Get help."
- Shock recognition: pale skin, rapid breathing, confusion, cold/clammy. Response: lay flat, elevate legs, keep warm, get help.

**Lesson 9-10: When NOT to Move Someone**
- Activity: "If someone falls from height, is hit hard, or complains of neck/back pain: DO NOT MOVE THEM. Moving a person with a spinal injury can cause permanent paralysis. Keep them still and get help."
- Exceptions: Move them only if they are in immediate danger (fire, flood)

## Grade 4: Puberty, Hygiene, Mental Health, and Substance Awareness

### Unit 5: Puberty and Body Changes (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Anatomical diagrams, Q&A box for anonymous questions
**Survival Relevance**: Informed adolescents make better health decisions. Misinformation causes fear, shame, and poor choices.

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the physical changes of puberty in all bodies (not separated by gender)
- Understand the biological purpose of puberty (reproductive maturation)
- Develop healthy hygiene routines for changing bodies
- Know that puberty timing varies widely and all timelines are normal
- Feel comfortable asking questions about body changes

#### Teacher Notes
This is taught to ALL students together — not separated by gender. Everyone learns about everyone's changes. Use correct anatomical terms. Set ground rules: no laughing at questions, all questions are valid, anonymous question box for shy students. If you are uncomfortable teaching this, practice beforehand. Your discomfort will transfer to students. Be matter-of-fact and clinical.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Is Puberty?**
- Activity: "Puberty is the process of your body changing from a child's body to an adult's body. It's driven by hormones — chemical messengers in your blood. It takes several years."
- Key Concepts: Normal, healthy, happens to everyone, different timing for different people

**Lesson 3-4: Changes Everyone Experiences**
- Activity: Growth spurts, body odor, oily skin/acne, emotional changes (mood swings), body hair growth (underarms, legs, pubic area).
- Practice: "How do we manage these changes?" (Bathing, deodorant, clean clothes, skin care)

**Lesson 5-6: Female-Specific Changes**
- Activity: Breast development, widening hips, menstruation. "Menstruation is the monthly shedding of the uterine lining. It means the body is capable of pregnancy."
- Practical: Menstrual hygiene — how to use and clean menstrual supplies (cloth pads, etc.). Tracking cycles. When cramps are normal vs. when to seek help.

**Lesson 7-8: Male-Specific Changes**
- Activity: Voice deepening, facial hair, muscle development, testicular growth, erections (involuntary and normal), wet dreams (normal, not something to be ashamed of).

**Lesson 9-10: Emotional Changes and Questions**
- Activity: "Hormones affect emotions too. Feeling more intense emotions, having crushes, wanting more independence — these are all normal." Open Q&A using the anonymous question box.

### Unit 6: Hygiene in Depth (Weeks 11-16)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Hygiene supplies, cleaning supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Advanced hygiene prevents the infections and diseases that are most common in communities without plumbing

#### Learning Objectives
- Establish comprehensive personal hygiene routine appropriate for puberty
- Understand menstrual hygiene management thoroughly
- Understand community hygiene (latrine use, waste management, food handling)
- Understand how skin infections develop and how to prevent them

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Personal Hygiene Routine**
- Activity: Create a daily checklist: wash face, brush teeth (morning + evening), bathe, clean clothes, deodorant, hand washing before meals and after toilet.

**Lesson 3-4: Community Hygiene**
- Activity: Visit latrine/toilet facilities. "Proper waste management prevents disease for EVERYONE. Using the latrine, covering waste, and handwashing afterward protect the whole community."

**Lesson 5-6: Skin Health**
- Activity: "Skin infections start small — a cut, a pimple, a rash. Keeping skin clean and dry prevents most infections. If you see spreading redness, pus, or increasing pain, tell the medical team."

### Unit 7: Mental Health (Weeks 17-26)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: None required (discussion-based)
**Survival Relevance**: Mental health is physical health. Anxiety, depression, and trauma are common in post-crisis communities. Children who recognize and manage their mental health become more resilient adults.

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that mental health is as real and important as physical health
- Recognize signs of stress, anxiety, and depression in themselves and others
- Practice 3+ coping strategies (deep breathing, physical activity, talking to someone, journaling)
- Know when and how to ask for help with mental health
- Reduce stigma around mental health struggles

#### Teacher Notes
In a survival community, trauma is common. Children may have experienced loss, violence, displacement, or chronic fear. This unit is not therapy — it's education. Give children vocabulary for their experience, normalize seeking help, and connect them with community mental health resources. Watch for signs of crisis in individual students and refer to appropriate adults.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Mental Health Is Real Health**
- Activity: "If you break your arm, you go to the medic. If your mind is hurting, you need help too. Feelings of sadness, worry, anger, or fear that won't go away are mental health problems — and they can be treated."

**Lesson 3-4: Recognizing Stress and Anxiety**
- Activity: "Stress: your body's response to pressure. Some stress is helpful (motivates you). Too much is harmful (can't sleep, stomach aches, can't concentrate, irritability)." Identify personal stress signs.

**Lesson 5-6: Coping Strategies**
- Activity: Practice: deep breathing (4 counts in, hold 4, out 4), progressive muscle relaxation, physical exercise, talking to a trusted person, journaling, doing something creative.
- Each student creates a personal "coping toolkit" — their top 3 strategies

**Lesson 7-8: Depression Awareness**
- Activity: "Depression is more than sadness. It's a persistent feeling of hopelessness, loss of interest in things you used to enjoy, changes in sleep/appetite, difficulty concentrating, withdrawing from people."
- Key Concept: "Depression is not weakness. It is a medical condition. It is treatable."

**Lesson 9-10: Helping Others**
- Activity: "If you notice a friend withdrawing, sleeping all the time, not eating, or talking about wanting to die — this is serious. Tell a trusted adult immediately. You might save their life."

### Unit 8: Substance Awareness (Weeks 27-32)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: None required
**Survival Relevance**: In a survival context, substance misuse wastes resources, impairs judgment, and endangers the community

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand what alcohol does to the body and brain
- Understand the danger of consuming unknown plants, fungi, or substances
- Understand that some medications are helpful but only in correct doses
- Make informed decisions about substances

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Alcohol**
- Activity: "Alcohol affects the brain — it slows thinking, impairs coordination, and removes inhibitions. In small amounts, some adults choose to drink it. In large amounts, it damages the liver, brain, and can kill."
- Key Concept: Factual, not moralistic. "You will make your own choices as an adult. Understanding the facts helps you choose wisely."

**Lesson 3-4: Unknown Substances**
- Activity: Review plant safety from Foundation. Extend: "Some plants contain drugs — they alter how you feel or think. Some are poisonous. NEVER consume anything without knowing exactly what it is."

**Lesson 5-6: Medicine Safety**
- Activity: "Medicine helps when used correctly. The wrong amount of medicine is poison. The wrong medicine for the wrong condition can make things worse."
- Rule: Never take medicine without an adult's guidance.

## Grade 5: Advanced First Aid, CPR, and Community Health

### Unit 9: Advanced First Aid (Weeks 1-14)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: First aid supplies, practice patients (peers), cushions for CPR practice
**Survival Relevance**: Grade 5 students should be capable first responders — able to assess a situation, stabilize a patient, and get appropriate help

#### Learning Objectives
- Assess a scene for safety before approaching
- Perform a primary survey (ABC: Airway, Breathing, Circulation)
- Apply direct pressure to control bleeding
- Position an unconscious breathing person in recovery position
- Recognize and respond to shock
- Know when to move and when NOT to move a patient
- Learn the CPR sequence (practice on cushion)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Scene Assessment**
- Activity: "Before you help anyone, make sure YOU are safe. Is there fire? Unstable structure? Danger? If the scene is unsafe, you becoming another victim helps no one."

**Lesson 3-4: Primary Survey (ABCs)**
- Activity: Approach → "Are you okay?" → Check Airway (head tilt, chin lift), Breathing (look, listen, feel for 10 seconds), Circulation (check for pulse at neck).
- Practice: Partners practice full primary survey

**Lesson 5-6: Bleeding Control**
- Activity: Direct pressure protocol. "Press HARD with clean cloth. Do not let go. If blood soaks through, add more cloth on top. Elevate above heart. If bleeding won't stop, apply tourniquet above the wound (this is advanced — practice with instructor supervision)."

**Lesson 7-8: Recovery Position**
- Activity: "If someone is unconscious but breathing, they could choke on vomit. Roll them onto their side (recovery position) to keep the airway clear."
- Practice: Partners practice rolling each other into recovery position

**Lesson 9-10: Shock Management**
- Activity: Review shock recognition. Treatment: lay flat, elevate legs 12 inches, keep warm, monitor breathing, get advanced help.

**Lesson 11-12: CPR Sequence**
- Activity: Learn the sequence: check scene → check responsiveness → call for help → 30 chest compressions (center of chest, push hard and fast) → 2 rescue breaths → repeat.
- Practice: Use cushion/stuffed bag for compression practice. "You probably won't need to do this at your age, but knowing the sequence means you can help if no adult is available."

**Lesson 13-14: Practical Assessment**
- Activity: Scenario stations — each student works through a simulated emergency. Teacher evaluates performance. "First Aid Level 1 Certificate" for students who demonstrate competency.

### Unit 10: Disease Prevention in Community Settings (Weeks 15-22)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: None required
**Survival Relevance**: Community-level health management prevents epidemics, which are the single greatest threat to a post-infrastructure community

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain how epidemics start and spread in communities
- Identify community health measures (clean water, sanitation, isolation of sick, food safety)
- Understand quarantine and when it's necessary
- Participate in community health monitoring

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How Epidemics Spread**
- Activity: Model epidemic spread. One "infected" student spreads to 2, who spread to 4, etc. "Epidemics grow exponentially. By the time you notice, it may be too late to contain."

**Lesson 3-4: Community Prevention**
- Activity: Map community health infrastructure — water treatment, latrine management, food storage, medical area. "Each of these is a defense against disease. If any fails, the community is vulnerable."

**Lesson 5-6: Quarantine**
- Activity: "When a contagious disease appears, the sick person must be separated from the community until they're no longer contagious. This is quarantine — it saves lives."
- Historical connection: Links to history curriculum on epidemics

**Lesson 7-8: Health Monitoring**
- Activity: "How do we detect disease early? Daily health checks, reporting symptoms, tracking illness patterns. YOU are part of this system — if you feel sick, tell someone immediately."

### Unit 11: Nutrition Planning (Weeks 23-30)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Community food inventory, meal planning templates
**Survival Relevance**: Practical meal planning is a direct community contribution — students who can plan nutritious meals serve the community immediately

#### Learning Objectives
- Plan a nutritionally balanced week of meals for a family
- Calculate approximate nutritional content of meals
- Account for special needs (pregnant women, sick people, heavy laborers, children)
- Understand food preservation and how it affects nutritional value
- Create a seasonal meal plan based on available foods

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Weekly Meal Planning**
- Activity: Using community food availability, plan 7 days of meals that cover all macronutrients and major vitamins. Three meals + snack per day.

**Lesson 3-4: Special Nutritional Needs**
- Activity: Adjust meal plans for different people. "A pregnant woman needs extra iron, calcium, and protein. A sick person needs fluids and easy-to-digest food. A child needs more protein per body weight."

**Lesson 5-6: Seasonal Planning**
- Activity: "What foods are available in spring? Summer? Fall? Winter?" Create seasonal meal plans that maintain nutrition year-round.
- Connection to agriculture module: Preserving food extends seasonal availability

**Lesson 7-8: Presentation**
- Activity: Present seasonal meal plans to the community kitchen team. Receive feedback. Revise. Best plans may be adopted for actual use.

#### Assessment (Grade 5 Overall)
- Can perform primary survey, bleeding control, recovery position
- Can demonstrate CPR sequence on a practice cushion
- Can recognize shock and initiate treatment
- Understands epidemic prevention measures
- Can plan a nutritionally balanced week of meals
- Earned First Aid Level 1 Certificate
