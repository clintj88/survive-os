# Building Band (Grades 3-5, Ages 8-11) — Introduction to Trades

## Overview
Trades skills are the backbone of community infrastructure. A community that can build, repair, and create with their hands is self-sufficient. This curriculum introduces children to tool use, measurement, knot tying, sewing, woodworking, fire building, and basic construction — progressing from safety and fundamentals in Grade 3 to building a complete project in Grade 5.

---

## Grade 3: Safety, Measurement, Knots, and Sewing

### Unit 1: Tool Safety and Identification (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Prerequisites**: Foundation — basic measurement concepts
**Materials**: Basic hand tools (hammer, saw, screwdriver, pliers, measuring tape, level, square), safety equipment
**Survival Relevance**: Every survival task requires tools. Safe, confident tool use prevents injury and enables productivity.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify and name 10+ common hand tools and their purposes
- Demonstrate safe handling, carrying, and storage of each tool
- Understand the concept of "right tool for the job"
- Demonstrate workspace safety (clean, organized, proper footwear, eye protection when needed)

#### Teacher Notes
Tool safety is non-negotiable. One serious injury can cost the community a worker and medical resources. Establish rules on day one and enforce them consistently. Never let students use a tool unsupervised until they've demonstrated safe handling three times. The goal is confident competence, not fearful avoidance.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Tool Identification**
- Activity: Display 15+ tools. Name each one. What does it do? "A hammer drives nails. A saw cuts wood. A screwdriver turns screws. A level checks if something is flat."
- Practice: Students match tools to their tasks

**Lesson 3-4: Safe Handling**
- Activity: For each tool, demonstrate safe handling. "Carry a saw by the handle with the blade pointing down. Pass a knife handle-first. Never walk with a tool pointed at someone."
- Rule: "A tool that's not being used is either in your hand or put away. Never on the ground, never on the edge of a table."

**Lesson 5-6: Workspace Setup**
- Activity: Set up a safe workspace. Clear area, stable surface, materials organized, tools laid out. Clean up completely when done. "A messy workspace is a dangerous workspace."

#### Assessment
- Can name and describe the purpose of 10+ tools
- Demonstrates safe handling for 5+ tools
- Sets up and cleans a workspace properly

### Unit 2: Measuring and Marking (Weeks 7-12)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Rulers, measuring tape, pencils, straight edge, square
**Survival Relevance**: "Measure twice, cut once" — accurate measurement prevents wasted materials and failed projects

#### Learning Objectives
- Measure length accurately to the nearest millimeter
- Mark a straight line using a straight edge
- Mark a right angle using a square
- Transfer measurements from a plan to materials
- Understand the critical importance of precision in building

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Measuring Practice**
- Activity: Measure 20 different objects. Record measurements. Partner checks. "Did you read from the zero mark? Did you look straight at the ruler, not from an angle?"

**Lesson 3-4: Marking and Lines**
- Activity: Mark a line on wood or cardboard using a ruler and pencil. "The pencil mark is where you cut. If the mark is wrong, the cut is wrong. Always double-check."
- Practice: Mark and check, mark and check.

**Lesson 5-6: Right Angles**
- Activity: Use a square to mark 90-degree angles. Check corners of objects — are they actually square? "If a building's corners aren't square, the walls won't be straight."
- Practice: Mark right angles on 10 surfaces

### Unit 3: Essential Knots (Weeks 13-22)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Rope (various thicknesses), posts or dowels
**Survival Relevance**: Knots are used in construction, agriculture, sailing, rescue, camping, and daily life. Knowing the right knot for the situation is a fundamental survival skill.

#### Learning Objectives
- Tie 8 essential knots from memory: square knot, bowline, clove hitch, two half-hitches, taut-line hitch, sheet bend, figure-eight, timber hitch
- Explain when to use each knot
- Tie each knot under time pressure and in low-light conditions
- Understand that the wrong knot can fail catastrophically

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Square Knot and Figure-Eight**
- Activity: Square knot — for joining two ropes of equal thickness. "Right over left, left over right." Figure-eight — a stopper knot that prevents rope from sliding through a hole.
- Practice: 50 repetitions of each until automatic

**Lesson 3-4: Bowline and Sheet Bend**
- Activity: Bowline — creates a loop that won't slip. "The most useful knot in the world. Use it for rescue, tie-downs, and any time you need a fixed loop." Sheet bend — joins two ropes of different thicknesses.
- Practice: Tie bowline one-handed (rescue scenario)

**Lesson 5-6: Clove Hitch and Two Half-Hitches**
- Activity: Clove hitch — quick attachment to a post. Two half-hitches — secure attachment that's easy to untie. Used for tying to posts, trees, and anchors.

**Lesson 7-8: Taut-Line Hitch and Timber Hitch**
- Activity: Taut-line hitch — adjustable tension knot for tent lines and guy wires. Timber hitch — for dragging logs.

**Lesson 9-10: Knot Olympics**
- Activity: Timed knot-tying challenges. Scenario stations: "A tent needs to be staked — which knot?" "You need to rescue someone — which knot?" "You need to join two different ropes — which knot?"

#### Assessment
- Can tie all 8 knots from memory within 30 seconds each
- Can select the correct knot for a given scenario
- Can tie knots behind their back or in the dark (bowline and square knot minimum)

### Unit 4: Basic Hand Sewing (Weeks 23-30)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Needles, thread, fabric scraps, scissors, thimble
**Survival Relevance**: Clothing and textile repair extends the life of irreplaceable materials. Sewing is a trade skill producing essential goods.

#### Learning Objectives
- Thread a needle and tie a knot
- Demonstrate running stitch (for seams)
- Demonstrate backstitch (for strong seams)
- Demonstrate whip stitch (for edges and repairs)
- Repair a tear in fabric
- Sew a simple useful item (bag, pouch, patch)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Threading and Knots**
- Activity: Thread needle, pull double, knot the end. "Take your time — this gets easier with practice." Practice with different thread thicknesses.

**Lesson 3-4: Running Stitch**
- Activity: "In and out, in and out, evenly spaced." Sew a straight line on practice fabric. Then sew two pieces together.

**Lesson 5-6: Backstitch**
- Activity: "Go forward one stitch, go back half a stitch, go forward one stitch. This creates a continuous line of thread — much stronger than running stitch."

**Lesson 7-8: Repair and Build**
- Activity: Practice repairing a tear: align edges, backstitch closed. Then sew a simple project: drawstring bag, small pouch, or patch for clothing.

## Grade 4: Woodworking, Cordage, Leatherwork, and Fire

### Unit 5: Woodworking Basics (Weeks 1-12)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Scrap wood, hand saw, hammer, nails, screws, hand drill, sandpaper, clamp, square
**Survival Relevance**: Woodworking skills build and repair shelters, furniture, tools, fences, and infrastructure

#### Learning Objectives
- Measure, mark, and cut wood to specified dimensions
- Drive nails and screws properly (pilot holes for screws)
- Join wood using nails, screws, and simple joints (butt joint, lap joint)
- Sand and finish a wooden surface
- Build a simple project following a plan

#### Teacher Notes
Supervision is critical during all saw and hammer work. Demonstrate each tool operation. Watch each student use each tool at least once before allowing independent work. Clamps are your friend — they hold work steady so students keep hands clear of cutting paths.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Sawing**
- Activity: Clamp wood securely. Mark cut line. "Start the saw with short, gentle strokes. Let the saw do the work — don't force it. Keep the saw on the waste side of the line."
- Practice: Cut 10 pieces to marked lengths. Measure results.

**Lesson 3-4: Hammering**
- Activity: "Hold the nail near the top to start it. Tap gently. Once the nail is set, move your fingers and drive it in with fuller swings. Hit the nail, not your thumb."
- Practice: Drive 20 nails into scrap wood

**Lesson 5-6: Screws and Drilling**
- Activity: "Drill a pilot hole first — this prevents wood from splitting. Then drive the screw. Screws hold stronger than nails and can be removed."

**Lesson 7-8: Joints**
- Activity: Butt joint — two pieces at a right angle, nailed or screwed. Lap joint — one piece overlaps the other, creating a stronger connection.

**Lesson 9-12: First Project**
- Activity: Build a simple shelf or storage box from a plan. Measure, mark, cut, join, sand, finish. "This is your first real woodworking project. Take pride in it."

### Unit 6: Rope and Cordage (Weeks 13-18)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Natural fibers (plant stems, bark strips, grass), twine, paracord
**Survival Relevance**: Rope is needed for everything — construction, farming, rescue, shelter building. Making cordage from natural materials means you're never without rope.

#### Learning Objectives
- Make simple cordage from natural plant fibers
- Understand fiber properties (strength, flexibility, water resistance)
- Splice and repair damaged rope
- Coil and store rope properly (prevents tangles and damage)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Fiber Identification**
- Activity: Collect natural fibers — inner bark, long grass, yucca, nettle stems. Test for strength. "Good cordage fiber is long, flexible, and doesn't break easily when twisted."

**Lesson 3-4: Making Cordage**
- Activity: Reverse twist method — take two groups of fibers, twist each group clockwise, then wrap them around each other counter-clockwise. This creates strong, balanced cord.
- Practice: Make 3 feet of usable cordage

**Lesson 5-6: Rope Care**
- Activity: Proper coiling (figure-8 or circular), storage (dry, off the ground), inspection (look for fraying, sun damage, cuts). "A rope that fails at the wrong moment can kill someone."

### Unit 7: Basic Leatherwork (Weeks 19-24)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Leather scraps, hole punch, leather needles, waxed thread, rivets
**Survival Relevance**: Leather provides durable goods — belts, sheaths, bags, sandals, protective gear. Leatherworking is a valuable trade skill.

#### Learning Objectives
- Punch holes in leather evenly spaced
- Stitch leather using saddle stitch (two needles, each passing through the same hole from opposite sides)
- Cut leather cleanly with a sharp blade
- Complete a leather project (belt, sheath, pouch, or sandal strap)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Cutting and Punching**
- Activity: Mark leather. Cut along the line with a sharp blade on a cutting surface. Punch holes at even intervals along the edge.

**Lesson 3-4: Saddle Stitch**
- Activity: "Two needles, one thread. Pass both needles through the same hole from opposite sides. Pull tight. This creates the strongest hand-stitching method for leather."

**Lesson 5-6: Project**
- Activity: Complete a useful leather item. Belt, knife sheath, tool pouch, or sandal strap repair.

### Unit 8: Fire Building and Safety (Weeks 25-34)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Tinder, kindling, firewood, matches or ferro rod, fire pit
**Survival Relevance**: Fire is fundamental — cooking, warmth, water purification, light, tool-making, signaling. Every person must be able to build and manage a fire safely.

#### Learning Objectives
- Build a fire from preparation through sustained burn
- Identify and prepare tinder, kindling, and fuel wood
- Demonstrate 2 fire-starting methods (matches and friction or ferro rod)
- Manage a fire safely (containment, wind awareness, extinguishing)
- Demonstrate complete fire extinguishing procedure
- Understand fire safety and prevention

#### Teacher Notes
Fire education requires a designated fire pit and close adult supervision. Every student must demonstrate safety awareness BEFORE being allowed to build a fire. This is serious — fire is both essential and dangerous. Treat it with appropriate respect.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Fire Triangle**
- Activity: "Fire needs three things: heat, fuel, and oxygen. Remove any one and the fire dies." Demonstrate: smother a flame (remove oxygen), let a candle burn out (fuel consumed), blow out a match (remove heat).

**Lesson 3-4: Fuel Preparation**
- Activity: Collect and prepare: tinder (dry leaves, bark shavings, dry grass — catches spark), kindling (pencil-thickness sticks — catches from tinder), fuel wood (wrist-thickness and larger — sustains the fire).
- Key Concept: "If your tinder isn't bone dry, you won't start a fire."

**Lesson 5-6: Fire Lay Structures**
- Activity: Tipi lay (kindling leaned against each other in a cone with tinder inside). Log cabin lay (kindling stacked in alternating layers). "Different structures for different purposes."

**Lesson 7-8: Starting the Fire**
- Activity: Each student builds and lights a fire (supervised). Match method: light tinder, gently blow, add kindling as it catches, add fuel as kindling catches. "Patience. Don't smother it. Let the fire breathe."

**Lesson 9-10: Fire Management and Extinguishing**
- Activity: Manage a fire for 30 minutes. Add fuel. Control size. Extinguish completely: spread coals, pour water, stir, pour more water, feel with back of hand (6 inches away). "If it's hot, it's not out."
- Rule: "NEVER leave a fire unattended. EVER."

## Grade 5: Building Projects, Metalwork, Weaving, and Circuits

### Unit 9: Build a Project (Weeks 1-14)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Wood, nails/screws, hand tools, plan drawings
**Survival Relevance**: Planning and executing a complete construction project is the practical capstone of trades introduction

#### Learning Objectives
- Read and follow a construction plan/drawing
- Create a materials list and gather materials
- Execute a multi-step construction project
- Problem-solve when things don't go as planned
- Complete, finish, and present a useful item

#### Lessons
**Weeks 1-2: Project Selection and Planning**
- Activity: Choose a project: birdhouse, tool rack, planter box, shelf, stool, or small table. Study the plan. Create a materials list. Calculate measurements.

**Weeks 3-4: Materials Preparation**
- Activity: Gather and prepare all materials. Measure and mark all cuts. "Measure twice, cut once."

**Weeks 5-10: Construction**
- Activity: Execute the plan step by step. Teacher assists but does not do the work. "When you get stuck, figure it out. Look at the plan again. Ask a question. Try a different approach."

**Weeks 11-12: Finishing**
- Activity: Sand smooth. Apply finish if available (oil, wax). Clean up workspace. Quality check.

**Weeks 13-14: Presentation**
- Activity: Present finished project to the class. Explain: what went right, what went wrong, what you'd do differently.

### Unit 10: Metalwork Awareness (Weeks 15-20)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Metal samples, file, hacksaw for thin metal, magnets
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding metals — identification, properties, and basic working — is foundation for the metalworking track in Intermediate and Advanced bands

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify common metals by appearance, weight, and magnet test (iron/steel are magnetic)
- Understand basic metal properties (hardness, malleability, conductivity, corrosion resistance)
- Use a file and hacksaw on thin metal safely
- Understand the concept of forging (heating metal to make it workable)
- Know what a blacksmith does and why metalworking matters

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Metal Identification**
- Activity: Test metal samples. Iron: heavy, magnetic, rusts. Copper: reddish, not magnetic, conducts well. Aluminum: light, not magnetic, doesn't rust. Lead: very heavy, soft. "Knowing your metals matters for repair, construction, and trade."

**Lesson 3-4: Working Metal Cold**
- Activity: File metal edges smooth. Cut thin metal with a hacksaw. "Metal is harder than wood — it takes more patience and sharper tools."

**Lesson 5-6: The Forge Concept**
- Activity: Visit the community forge if available, or discuss: "When you heat steel to orange-hot, it becomes soft enough to bend, shape, and hammer. This is how tools, nails, hinges, and blades are made."
- Key Concept: "A blacksmith is one of the most valuable people in any community."

### Unit 11: Textile Arts — Basic Weaving (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Frame loom (sticks and nails), yarn/string/natural fibers, shuttle (flat stick)
**Survival Relevance**: Textile production creates essential goods — cloth, blankets, bags, mats. Weaving is a trade skill with high community value.

#### Learning Objectives
- Build and warp a simple frame loom
- Weave using tabby (plain) weave
- Create patterns using color changes
- Complete a woven piece (belt, placemat, or small blanket section)
- Understand that all cloth is made by some form of weaving or knitting

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Loom Setup**
- Activity: Build a frame loom from sticks and nails (or notches). String warp threads (vertical, even tension). "The warp holds the fabric. It must be tight and evenly spaced."

**Lesson 3-4: Basic Weaving**
- Activity: Pass weft thread (horizontal) over-under through warp threads using a shuttle. Push each row tight. "Over, under, over, under. Next row: under, over, under, over."

**Lesson 5-6: Pattern and Color**
- Activity: Change colors to create stripes. Change over-under patterns to create textures. "Every textile design in history comes from variations of this basic technique."

**Lesson 7-8: Complete Project**
- Activity: Finish weaving. Remove from loom. Tie off warp ends. "You made cloth. Humans have been doing this for at least 10,000 years."

### Unit 12: Electrical Circuit Building (Weeks 29-36)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Batteries, wire, bulbs, switches, LED lights, buzzer
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding circuits is essential for maintaining community electrical systems, radio equipment, and building useful devices

#### Learning Objectives
- Build series and parallel circuits
- Understand the difference (series: one path, if one bulb dies all die; parallel: multiple paths, independent)
- Add switches and multiple loads (bulbs, buzzers)
- Build a useful circuit device (flashlight, alarm, or simple light system)
- Read a simple circuit diagram

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Series Circuits**
- Activity: Connect battery → bulb 1 → bulb 2 → battery. "All electricity flows through one path. Remove one bulb and the circuit breaks — both go out."

**Lesson 3-4: Parallel Circuits**
- Activity: Connect battery → wire splits to bulb 1 and bulb 2 → wires rejoin → battery. "Each bulb has its own path. Remove one — the other stays on."

**Lesson 5-6: Circuit Diagrams**
- Activity: Learn symbols for battery, wire, bulb, switch. Draw circuit diagrams. Build circuits from diagrams.

**Lesson 7-8: Build a Device**
- Activity: Design and build a useful circuit device. Flashlight (battery + switch + LED in a tube). Simple alarm (battery + switch + buzzer). Night light. Present to class.

#### Assessment (Building Band Trades Overall)
- Can safely use 5+ hand tools
- Can tie all 8 essential knots
- Can sew a repair and a simple item
- Can measure, cut, and join wood
- Can build and sustain a fire safely
- Completed a full construction project
- Can build series and parallel circuits
- Can weave on a frame loom
