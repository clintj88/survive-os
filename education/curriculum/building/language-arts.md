# Building Band (Grades 3-5, Ages 8-11) — Language Arts

## Overview
Building Band language arts transforms competent readers into independent learners and effective communicators. Students move from reading simple stories to researching topics across reference materials, from writing paragraphs to composing multi-page essays and technical documents. A community journalism thread runs throughout — students write for real audiences. By Grade 5, students can read and comprehend survival manuals, write persuasive proposals, and speak confidently to groups.

---

## Grade 3: Reading to Learn, Writing to Communicate

### Unit 1: Chapter Books and Reading Stamina (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Prerequisites**: Foundation Band literacy (fluent reading at 70-90 WPM, paragraph writing)
**Materials**: Chapter books from community library, student journals
**Survival Relevance**: Extended reading stamina is essential for learning from technical manuals, reference books, and knowledge base entries

#### Learning Objectives
- Read a chapter book independently (80-120 pages)
- Maintain comprehension across multiple reading sessions
- Summarize chapters in 2-3 sentences
- Identify main characters, setting, problem, and resolution
- Read 90-110 words per minute with comprehension

#### Teacher Notes
The transition from picture books to chapter books is a major milestone. Some children will fly through books; others will struggle with the sustained attention. Key strategies: daily independent reading time (minimum 20 minutes), reading partners, and choice — let children pick books that interest them. Never force a specific book on a reluctant reader. The goal is building the HABIT of reading, not passing a specific text.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Choosing a Book**
- Activity: Book walk — look at cover, read the back or first page, flip through. "Does this interest you? Is it the right difficulty?" (Five-finger test: read a page, hold up a finger for each unknown word. 0-1 = too easy, 2-3 = just right, 4-5 = too hard.)
- Practice: Each child selects a chapter book

**Lesson 3-6: Independent Reading with Accountability**
- Activity: Daily structure: 20 min independent reading → 5 min reading journal (write 2 sentences about what happened) → 5 min partner share
- Teacher conferences: Meet with 3-4 students individually each day to check comprehension

**Lesson 7-8: Book Discussion Groups**
- Activity: Groups of 3-4 who read the same book discuss: What happened? What surprised you? What would you have done? What do you predict next?
- Key Concepts: Good readers think about what they read and talk about it with others

**Lesson 9-10: Book Reports**
- Activity: Each child presents their book to the class: title, author, summary, favorite part, would they recommend it? 2-minute presentation.
- First public speaking experience at this level

#### Assessment
- Completed a chapter book independently
- Reading journal shows comprehension (summaries are accurate)
- Participated in book discussion
- Delivered a 2-minute book presentation

### Unit 2: Paragraph Writing (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Writing supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Clear paragraph writing is the building block of all functional community writing — reports, proposals, records, instructions

#### Learning Objectives
- Write a topic sentence that introduces the paragraph's main idea
- Write 3-5 supporting sentences with details
- Write a closing sentence
- Write paragraphs in three modes: narrative (story), informational (facts), and opinion (argument)
- Use proper capitalization, punctuation, and spelling

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Topic Sentences**
- Activity: "A topic sentence is a promise. It tells the reader what the paragraph will be about." Read paragraphs, identify topic sentences. Write topic sentences for given topics.
- Practice: Write 5 topic sentences on different subjects

**Lesson 3-4: Supporting Details**
- Activity: "After the promise, you keep it. Each sentence adds a detail that supports the topic." Model: Topic sentence about the community garden → detail about what grows → detail about who tends it → detail about harvest.
- Practice: Add 3 supporting sentences to each topic sentence from Lesson 1-2

**Lesson 5-6: Closing Sentences**
- Activity: "End by wrapping up. Restate the main idea in different words, or tell why it matters."
- Practice: Complete full paragraphs with topic, support, and closing

**Lesson 7-8: Three Types of Paragraphs**
- Activity: Same topic, three ways. Topic: "Our Garden." Narrative: Tell the story of planting day. Informational: Describe what's in the garden. Opinion: Argue why the garden matters.
- Practice: Write one paragraph of each type

**Lesson 9-10: Editing and Publishing**
- Activity: Peer editing in pairs. Read partner's paragraph. Is there a topic sentence? Do the details support it? Is there a closing? Check spelling and punctuation. Revise. "Publish" by posting on the class or community board.

#### Assessment
- Can write a complete paragraph with topic sentence, 3+ supporting details, and closing
- Can write narrative, informational, and opinion paragraphs
- Mechanics: correct capitalization, end punctuation, most words spelled correctly

### Unit 3: Cursive Handwriting (Weeks 11-30, parallel with other units)
**Duration**: 20 weeks (15 minutes daily)
**Materials**: Writing supplies, lined paper if available
**Survival Relevance**: Cursive is faster than printing for note-taking and record-keeping. Historical documents are in cursive — reading them requires knowing the script.

#### Learning Objectives
- Write all 26 lowercase cursive letters
- Write all 26 uppercase cursive letters
- Write words and sentences in legible cursive
- Transition to using cursive for daily writing

#### Lessons
- Teach 2-3 letters per week following standard cursive progression
- Daily 15-minute practice sessions
- Transition to cursive for journal writing by second semester

### Unit 4: Research from Reference Materials (Weeks 21-30)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Community library, knowledge base printouts, any available reference books
**Survival Relevance**: The ability to find information in reference materials is how a community accesses its stored knowledge

#### Learning Objectives
- Use a table of contents and index to find information
- Take simple notes from a reference source (key facts, not copying sentences)
- Write a short report (3+ paragraphs) based on research
- Cite where information came from ("According to..." or "I learned from...")

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Finding Information**
- Activity: Given a question ("How do you purify water?"), use the knowledge base or reference books to find the answer. Practice using table of contents and index.

**Lesson 3-4: Note-Taking**
- Activity: "Don't copy the book. Read it, close it, write what you remember in your own words." Practice with short passages.

**Lesson 5-8: Research Report**
- Activity: Choose a survival-relevant topic. Find 2+ sources. Take notes. Write a 3-paragraph report: introduction, main information, conclusion.

**Lesson 9-10: Sharing Research**
- Activity: Present research findings to the class. "I researched ___ because ___. I learned ___."

#### Assessment
- Can locate information using table of contents and index
- Takes notes in own words (not copying)
- Completed a 3-paragraph research report with cited sources

### Unit 5: Poetry and Journal Writing (Weeks 31-40)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Writing supplies, nature access for observation
**Survival Relevance**: Poetry develops precise word choice and emotional expression. Journal writing creates daily records and develops writing fluency.

#### Learning Objectives
- Write poems using various forms (acrostic, haiku, free verse, rhyming)
- Maintain a daily journal with substantive entries
- Use descriptive language (adjectives, adverbs, sensory details)
- Understand that poetry is compressed expression — every word counts

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Acrostic and List Poems**
- Activity: Write your name vertically. Each letter starts a line about you. List poems: "Things I hear at dawn..." 10 items. Simple structures, powerful expression.

**Lesson 3-4: Haiku and Observation**
- Activity: Nature walk. Observe carefully. Write haiku (5-7-5 syllables). "Haiku forces you to say something true in very few words."

**Lesson 5-6: Rhyming Poetry**
- Activity: Learn rhyme schemes (ABAB, AABB). Write simple rhyming poems. "Rhyme makes things memorable — that's why we use it for rules and songs."

**Lesson 7-8: Free Verse**
- Activity: No rules about rhyme or rhythm — just express something true. "Free verse is about saying exactly what you mean in the most powerful way."

**Lesson 9-10: Poetry Performance**
- Activity: Each child selects their best poem and performs it for the class. Poetry slam format.

#### Assessment
- Wrote poems in 4+ forms
- Maintained journal with daily entries for 8+ weeks
- Uses descriptive, sensory language
- Performed a poem for an audience

## Grade 4: Research, Persuasion, and Technical Writing

### Unit 6: Multi-Source Research Reports (Weeks 1-12)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Reference materials, writing supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Complex decision-making requires gathering and synthesizing information from multiple sources

#### Learning Objectives
- Research a topic using 3+ sources
- Organize information with an outline before writing
- Write a 5+ paragraph research report
- Include a simple bibliography
- Distinguish between reliable and unreliable sources

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Choosing a Topic and Finding Sources**
- Activity: Select a survival-relevant research topic. Find at least 3 sources with different information.

**Lesson 3-4: Outlining**
- Activity: "Before you write, organize. What are your main points? What details support each point?" Create an outline with Roman numerals.

**Lesson 5-8: Drafting**
- Activity: Introduction (why this topic matters), body paragraphs (one per main point), conclusion (what we should do with this information). Write from outline.

**Lesson 9-10: Revision and Editing**
- Activity: Self-edit, peer edit, teacher conference. Revise for content, then edit for mechanics.

**Lesson 11-12: Final Copy and Presentation**
- Activity: Write final clean copy. Present key findings to the class.

### Unit 7: Persuasive Writing and Debate (Weeks 13-22)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Writing supplies
**Survival Relevance**: The ability to argue a position with evidence is essential for governance, trade negotiation, and community decision-making

#### Learning Objectives
- Write a persuasive piece with a clear position, 3+ reasons with evidence, and a call to action
- Anticipate and address counterarguments
- Deliver a persuasive speech (3 minutes)
- Engage in structured debate (present position, respond to opposition)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Makes an Argument?**
- Activity: Analyze a persuasive text. Identify: What do they want? What reasons do they give? Is it convincing? Why or why not?

**Lesson 3-4: Building an Argument**
- Activity: Choose a position on a community-relevant topic. Build the case: Position → Reason 1 + evidence → Reason 2 + evidence → Reason 3 + evidence → Call to action.

**Lesson 5-6: Counterarguments**
- Activity: "A strong argument acknowledges the other side. 'Some people think ___, but ___ because ___.' This shows you've thought about it carefully."

**Lesson 7-8: Persuasive Speeches**
- Activity: Each child delivers a 3-minute persuasive speech. Class evaluates: Were they convincing? Why?

**Lesson 9-10: Class Debate**
- Activity: Structured debate on a community issue. Two teams. Opening statements, rebuttals, closing statements. Class votes on which side argued better (not which side they agree with).

### Unit 8: Note-Taking and Technical Writing (Weeks 23-34)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Writing supplies, reference materials, community procedures to document
**Survival Relevance**: Technical writing (instructions, procedures, records) is the most practically important writing skill in a survival community

#### Learning Objectives
- Take organized notes from a spoken presentation or demonstration
- Write clear step-by-step instructions that someone else can follow
- Write a formal letter (header, greeting, body, closing, signature)
- Write a procedure document for a community task

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-3: Note-Taking from Presentations**
- Activity: Teacher or community member gives a 10-minute presentation. Students take notes. Compare notes — what did you capture? What did you miss?
- Key Concepts: Use abbreviations, bullet points, main ideas not every word

**Lesson 4-6: Writing Instructions**
- Activity: Write instructions for a task you know well (making a fire, planting seeds, purifying water). Give to a partner to follow EXACTLY. Did they succeed? If not, your instructions weren't clear enough. Revise.

**Lesson 7-8: Formal Letters**
- Activity: Write a formal letter to a community leader. Practice format: date, recipient, greeting, body (purpose, details, request), closing, signature.

**Lesson 9-12: Procedure Documentation**
- Activity: Shadow a community member doing a task. Document the procedure step-by-step. Review with the community member for accuracy. Final copy added to community procedures manual.
- This is REAL technical writing for real community use.

## Grade 5: Advanced Composition and Public Communication

### Unit 10: The Five-Paragraph Essay (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Writing supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Structured essay writing is the foundation of all complex written communication — proposals, reports, analyses

#### Learning Objectives
- Write a five-paragraph essay (introduction with thesis, 3 body paragraphs, conclusion)
- Develop a thesis statement that takes a clear position
- Use transitions between paragraphs
- Write essays in multiple modes: analytical, persuasive, comparative

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Essay Architecture**
- Activity: Read and dissect model essays. Identify: introduction (hook + thesis), body paragraphs (topic sentence + evidence + explanation), conclusion (restate thesis + broader significance).

**Lesson 3-4: Thesis Statements**
- Activity: "A thesis is the one sentence your whole essay is about. It makes a claim that needs to be supported." Practice writing thesis statements for given topics.

**Lesson 5-6: Body Paragraphs with Evidence**
- Activity: Each body paragraph: topic sentence → evidence (fact, example, or quote) → explanation of how evidence supports the thesis. Practice with one paragraph.

**Lesson 7-8: Transitions and Flow**
- Activity: Learn transition words (however, furthermore, in contrast, similarly, therefore). Practice connecting paragraphs so the essay flows logically.

**Lesson 9-10: Complete Essay**
- Activity: Write a complete five-paragraph essay. Full process: brainstorm → outline → draft → revise → edit → final copy.

### Unit 11: Literary Analysis and Creative Writing (Weeks 11-22)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Community library books, writing supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Analytical thinking applied to texts transfers to analyzing situations, decisions, and proposals. Creative writing develops innovation and communication skills.

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze a story for theme, character development, and author's purpose
- Write a literary analysis paragraph (claim about the text + textual evidence)
- Write an original short story with developed characters, conflict, and resolution
- Give and receive constructive feedback on writing

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Literary Analysis**
- Activity: Read a story together. Discuss: What is the theme (the big idea about life)? How do characters change? Why did the author write this?
- Write: Analysis paragraph with claim + evidence from text

**Lesson 5-8: Short Story Writing**
- Activity: Write an original short story (3-5 pages). Include: setting, characters with personality, a conflict, rising tension, climax, resolution. Workshop: share drafts, give feedback, revise.

**Lesson 9-12: Writing Workshop**
- Activity: Peer writing groups. Read each other's work. Give specific, constructive feedback: "I liked how you ___. I was confused when ___. I think you could improve ___ by ___."

### Unit 12: Debate, Public Speaking, and Community Journalism (Weeks 23-36)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Writing supplies, community bulletin board
**Survival Relevance**: Public speaking and debate are governance skills. Journalism creates community transparency and shared information.

#### Learning Objectives
- Deliver a 5-minute prepared speech on a topic of their choice
- Participate in formal debate with structured arguments
- Write news articles following who-what-when-where-why-how format
- Edit and revise others' writing constructively
- Produce a class newsletter or community bulletin

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Public Speaking**
- Activity: Each child prepares and delivers a 5-minute speech. Topics: something they're expert in, something they believe, something they've researched. Practice: eye contact, clear voice, organized structure.

**Lesson 5-8: Formal Debate**
- Activity: Lincoln-Douglas format debates on community-relevant topics. Research, prepare arguments, present, rebut. Class evaluates argument quality.

**Lesson 9-12: Community Journalism**
- Activity: Write news articles about community events, decisions, and achievements. Follow journalism format: headline, lead paragraph (the most important facts), body (details), quotes from community members.

**Lesson 13-14: Publish**
- Activity: Compile articles into a class newsletter. Post on community board. Distribute copies.
- This is real journalism for a real community. Take it seriously.

### Unit 13: Reading Survival and Technical Documents (Weeks 37-40)
**Duration**: 4 weeks
**Materials**: Survival manuals, knowledge base entries, technical documents
**Survival Relevance**: Directly applicable — students learn to read and follow the documents that keep the community functioning

#### Learning Objectives
- Read and comprehend technical documents (instructions, manuals, procedures)
- Follow multi-step written procedures accurately
- Identify key information in dense text (scanning, skimming, close reading)
- Read 120-140 words per minute with full comprehension

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Reading Technical Text**
- Activity: Read a knowledge base entry on a practical topic (water purification, first aid, garden planning). Identify key information. Follow the instructions.

**Lesson 3-4: Scanning and Skimming**
- Activity: "You don't always read every word. Sometimes you need to FIND specific information fast." Practice scanning tables of contents, indexes, and dense text for target information.

#### Assessment (Grade 5 Overall)
- Can write a five-paragraph essay with thesis, evidence, and transitions
- Can deliver a 5-minute speech confidently
- Can participate in structured debate
- Can write a news article in proper format
- Can read and follow technical documents independently
- Reading fluency: 120-140 WPM with comprehension
