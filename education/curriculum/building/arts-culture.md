# Building Band (Grades 3-5, Ages 8-11) — Arts & Culture

## Overview
Arts are not a luxury — they are a survival skill. Arts preserve culture, maintain morale, create trade goods, and build the shared identity that holds communities together. Students progress from skill-building through collaboration to cultural leadership and documentation.

## Grade 3: Skill Building

### Unit 1: Advanced Drawing (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Paper, pencils, charcoal, erasers (or bread), still-life objects
**Survival Relevance**: Detailed drawing is essential for botanical illustration, technical diagrams, map-making, medical documentation, and recording observations when cameras are unavailable

#### Learning Objectives
- Draw objects from observation with recognizable accuracy
- Understand and apply basic perspective (near things are larger, far things are smaller)
- Use shading to show three-dimensional form
- Create a detailed drawing of a natural object (plant, animal, tool)

#### Teacher Notes
Drawing is thinking made visible. It trains careful observation and attention to detail. These skills transfer directly to scientific observation, technical documentation, and communication. Do not emphasize "talent" — emphasize practice and observation.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Seeing Like an Artist**
- Activity: Place a simple object (cup, tool, vegetable) on the table. "Don't draw what you THINK it looks like — draw what you actually SEE." Compare drawing from memory vs drawing from observation.
- Key Concepts: Observation is a skill, drawing is recording what you see
- Practice: Draw the same object three times, each time looking more carefully

**Lesson 3-4: Perspective Basics**
- Activity: Stand at one end of a long path. "Does the path really get smaller, or does it just look that way?" Draw the path showing how parallel lines appear to converge in the distance.
- Key Concepts: Things farther away appear smaller, overlapping shows depth, objects lower on the page appear closer
- Practice: Draw a scene showing near and far objects with correct size relationships

**Lesson 5-6: Light and Shadow**
- Activity: Place an object near a single light source (window or lamp). Observe how light creates highlights and shadows. Practice shading with pencil — light touch for highlights, heavy for shadows.
- Key Concepts: Shading makes flat drawings look three-dimensional, observe where light falls
- Practice: Draw a still life of 3 objects with realistic shading

#### Assessment
- Complete a detailed observational drawing that is recognizable as the subject
- Demonstrate perspective in at least one drawing
- Use shading to show form in at least one drawing

### Unit 2: Community Songbook Project (Weeks 7-14)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Paper for songbook, writing tools, any available instruments
**Survival Relevance**: Songs preserve knowledge, maintain morale, create shared identity, and transmit cultural memory across generations — they are one of the oldest and most resilient forms of knowledge preservation

#### Learning Objectives
- Learn 15+ community songs (work songs, gathering songs, celebration songs, lullabies)
- Understand why cultures preserve songs (memory, morale, identity, instruction)
- Notate songs in a simple system (lyrics with rhythm marks)
- Contribute to a permanent community songbook

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Songs We Know**
- Activity: Gather all the songs the class knows. Songs from before, songs learned since, songs from different backgrounds. Sing them together. "Why do we remember these?"
- Key Concepts: Music is memorable — you can remember song lyrics from years ago when you forget spoken words
- Practice: Teach one song you know to someone who doesn't know it

**Lesson 3-4: Songs That Teach**
- Activity: Learn or compose work songs (songs that teach a procedure), counting songs, alphabet songs, plant identification songs. "How can we turn important knowledge into music?"
- Key Concepts: Songs are a technology for preserving information, cultures worldwide use music to teach
- Practice: Create a song that teaches something useful (first aid steps, plant identification, compass directions)

**Lesson 5-6: Collecting Songs**
- Activity: Interview community elders and members. "Do you know any songs? Lullabies? Work songs? Songs from your childhood?" Record lyrics and learn melodies.
- Key Concepts: Oral traditions are fragile — if we don't write them down, they may be lost
- Practice: Each student collects at least 2 songs from community members

**Lesson 7-8: The Songbook**
- Activity: Compile all collected and composed songs into a community songbook. Organize by type (work, celebration, lullaby, instructional, spiritual). Include illustrations.
- Key Concepts: Creating a lasting cultural artifact, organizing information for others
- Practice: Copy final versions neatly with illustrations. Present the songbook to the community.

#### Assessment
- Sing at least 10 songs from memory
- Contribute at least 2 songs to the songbook (collected or composed)
- Explain why preserving songs matters for community survival

### Unit 3: Pottery with Local Clay (Weeks 15-22)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Clay (found locally or purchased), water, tools for shaping (sticks, wire, shells), fire pit or kiln for firing
**Survival Relevance**: Pottery creates essential items — bowls, cups, storage jars, water vessels, cooking pots. It is one of the oldest and most useful crafts, and clay is available nearly everywhere.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify and prepare local clay for use
- Build pottery using pinch, coil, and slab techniques
- Understand drying and firing processes
- Create at least one functional item (bowl, cup, or storage vessel)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Finding and Preparing Clay**
- Activity: Where is clay found? (Stream banks, exposed soil, digging below topsoil.) Collect clay, remove debris, add water, knead until smooth.
- Key Concepts: Clay is abundant, preparation matters for quality, different clays have different properties
- Practice: Prepare a ball of usable clay from raw material

**Lesson 3-4: Pinch Pots and Coil Building**
- Activity: Pinch pot — start with a ball, push thumb into center, pinch walls evenly. Coil building — roll ropes of clay, stack and smooth them into a vessel shape.
- Key Concepts: Two ancient techniques available to anyone, wall thickness must be even for successful firing
- Practice: Create one pinch pot and one coil-built vessel

**Lesson 5-6: Slab Building and Decoration**
- Activity: Roll clay flat, cut shapes, join with slip (liquid clay). Build a box, plate, or tile. Decorate with stamps, carving, or textures pressed into the surface.
- Key Concepts: Slab building creates flat items (plates, tiles), decoration adds beauty and can mark ownership
- Practice: Create a decorated tile or plate using slab technique

**Lesson 7-8: Drying and Firing**
- Activity: Slow-dry pieces over several days (rushed drying causes cracking). Fire in a pit fire or kiln — discuss temperatures needed and what happens to clay when fired.
- Key Concepts: Firing makes clay permanent, temperature and time matter, pieces can break during firing
- Practice: Fire all pieces. Evaluate results — what worked? What broke? Why?

#### Assessment
- Create at least one functional fired pottery item (holds water, serves food)
- Explain the full process from finding clay to finished piece
- Identify what went wrong with any failed pieces and how to improve

### Unit 4: Dramatic Storytelling (Weeks 23-28)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Simple costumes or props from available materials, performance space
**Survival Relevance**: Storytelling builds communication skills, preserves community narrative, and provides entertainment and morale. Performance skills develop confidence, public speaking, and collaboration.

#### Learning Objectives
- Tell a story with vocal variety (volume, pace, tone, character voices)
- Use body language and gesture to enhance storytelling
- Collaborate to perform a group story
- Adapt a community story or historical event into a performance

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Voice as an Instrument**
- Activity: Tell the same simple story three ways — whispered, shouted, and with dramatic variation. Which holds attention? Practice character voices — how does a nervous person sound? A confident one? An old person? A child?
- Key Concepts: Vocal variety keeps the audience engaged, voice conveys emotion and character
- Practice: Each student tells a 2-minute story using at least 3 different voices

**Lesson 3-4: Body Tells the Story**
- Activity: Tell a story without words — pantomime. The audience guesses what happened. Then combine voice and gesture.
- Key Concepts: Body language communicates as much as words, gesture and movement support the story
- Practice: Perform a scene using both voice and full-body expression

**Lesson 5-6: Group Performance**
- Activity: In groups of 4-5, adapt a community story, historical event, or survival lesson into a short performance. Assign roles: narrator, characters, sound effects.
- Key Concepts: Collaboration in performance, rehearsal improves quality, adapting stories for an audience
- Practice: Rehearse and perform for the class. Give and receive constructive feedback.

#### Assessment
- Perform a solo storytelling piece with vocal variety and gesture
- Participate in a group performance demonstrating collaboration
- Adapt a real story into a performable version

## Grade 4: Expanding Skills

### Unit 5: Music Theory Basics (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Any available instruments (handmade or salvaged), voice, paper for notation
**Survival Relevance**: Music theory enables composition, teaching, and recreation of music without needing to have heard a piece before — it is the literacy of sound

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand rhythm (beat, tempo, time signatures: 4/4, 3/4)
- Understand pitch (high/low, steps, scales — at minimum the major scale)
- Read and write simple musical notation (staff or simplified system)
- Compose a simple melody with notation that someone else can play

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Rhythm**
- Activity: Clap a steady beat. Now vary it — some beats strong, some weak. Count in 4 (march feel) and in 3 (waltz feel). Add complexity — clap on beats 1 and 3, snap on 2 and 4.
- Key Concepts: Beat is the steady pulse, tempo is speed, time signature tells how beats are grouped
- Practice: Write rhythm patterns using a simple system (X for beat, - for rest). Trade and clap each other's patterns.

**Lesson 3-4: Pitch**
- Activity: Sing "do re mi fa sol la ti do." This is the major scale — the most common pattern of notes in music worldwide. Sing up and down. Sing intervals (skip from do to mi, do to sol).
- Key Concepts: Pitch is how high or low a sound is, scales organize pitches into patterns, the major scale is the foundation of most music
- Practice: Sing simple melodies using solfege (do-re-mi system)

**Lesson 5-6: Simple Notation**
- Activity: Introduce staff notation (5 lines, notes go up and down) OR a simplified number system (1=do, 2=re, etc.). Notate a familiar song. Can someone else play it from the notation?
- Key Concepts: Notation allows music to be preserved and shared without hearing it, it is the writing system of music
- Practice: Notate 3 familiar songs

**Lesson 7-8: Composition**
- Activity: Using the major scale and a simple rhythm, compose an 8-bar melody. Write it in notation. Teach it to a partner. Perform it.
- Key Concepts: Anyone can compose, composition is organized experimentation, constraints (scale, rhythm) help creativity
- Practice: Compose, notate, and perform an original melody

#### Assessment
- Clap a written rhythm pattern accurately
- Sing a major scale ascending and descending
- Write and perform an original melody that someone else can read and play

### Unit 6: Printmaking (Weeks 9-14)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Carving tools (or sharpened sticks), soft materials for blocks (potato, clay, soft wood, linoleum if available), ink or paint, paper or fabric
**Survival Relevance**: Printmaking produces multiple copies — essential for signs, labels, educational materials, currency, decorative items, and trade goods

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand relief printing (raised surface transfers ink to paper)
- Carve a simple print block
- Pull multiple consistent prints from one block
- Understand the historical importance of printing technology

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Stamp Printing**
- Activity: Cut a potato in half. Carve a simple design in relief (cut away what you DON'T want to print). Ink the surface and press onto paper. Make 10 copies.
- Key Concepts: The carved surface is a "plate" — it can produce many identical copies, the image is reversed
- Practice: Create 3 different stamp designs and print multiples

**Lesson 3-4: Larger Block Prints**
- Activity: Using a flat piece of soft wood or clay slab, carve a larger design (10x10 cm). Plan the design first — remember it prints in reverse. Ink and print.
- Key Concepts: Planning for reversal, even ink application, consistent pressure
- Practice: Create a print block and pull 5+ consistent prints

**Lesson 5-6: Printmaking Applications**
- Activity: Create something useful with printmaking — labels for storage containers, a sign for a community area, an illustration for the songbook, a decorative border for a document.
- Key Concepts: Art serves practical purposes, printmaking is a production technology, not just art
- Practice: Complete a practical printmaking project with at least 10 copies produced

#### Assessment
- Carve a print block with a recognizable design
- Pull 5 consistent prints from one block
- Complete one practical application of printmaking

### Unit 7: Community History Play (Weeks 15-22)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Simple costumes and props, performance space, writing materials for script
**Survival Relevance**: Community theater strengthens social bonds, preserves shared history, and develops public speaking, collaboration, and creative problem-solving skills

#### Learning Objectives
- Collaborate to write a script based on community history or experience
- Design simple costumes and sets from available materials
- Rehearse and perform a play for the community
- Understand theater as a tool for preserving and processing community experience

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Choosing the Story**
- Activity: As a class, brainstorm important community stories — founding events, challenges overcome, funny incidents, lessons learned. Vote on one to dramatize.
- Key Concepts: Deciding what stories to tell is a community act, every perspective matters
- Practice: Outline the story with a beginning, middle, and end

**Lesson 3-4: Writing the Script**
- Activity: Divide the story into scenes. Assign dialogue. Include a narrator to bridge gaps. Ensure multiple characters have meaningful roles.
- Key Concepts: Scripts organize performance, dialogue reveals character, staging tells the audience where to look
- Practice: Draft, read aloud, and revise the script

**Lesson 5-6: Design and Rehearsal**
- Activity: Design costumes from available materials (repurposed clothing, natural materials). Build simple set pieces. Block scenes (plan who stands where). Rehearse.
- Key Concepts: Resourcefulness in design, rehearsal builds confidence, taking direction and giving feedback
- Practice: Run full rehearsals with costumes and props

**Lesson 7-8: Performance**
- Activity: Perform the play for the community. Afterward, discuss — what did the audience respond to? What surprised them? What did we learn from performing our own story?
- Key Concepts: Performance is a gift to the community, shared stories build shared identity
- Practice: Post-performance reflection and documentation

#### Assessment
- Participate meaningfully in all phases (writing, design, rehearsal, performance)
- Demonstrate collaboration and constructive feedback skills
- Reflect on the experience — what worked, what didn't, what would you change?

### Unit 8: Textile Design and Natural Dyeing (Weeks 23-30)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Fabric scraps or handwoven cloth, natural dye sources (onion skins, berries, walnut hulls, marigolds, indigo if available), mordants (salt, vinegar, alum), string for tie-dye, stamps for block printing on fabric
**Survival Relevance**: Textile skills create clothing, blankets, bags, and decorative items. Natural dyeing uses locally available materials. These skills produce trade goods and maintain cultural expression through clothing and textiles.

#### Learning Objectives
- Extract dye from at least 3 natural sources
- Understand mordanting (fixing dye to fabric)
- Apply pattern techniques: tie-dye, resist, and block printing on fabric
- Create a finished dyed textile item

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Dye Extraction**
- Activity: Collect dye materials — onion skins (yellow/orange), berries (purple/blue), walnut hulls (brown), marigold flowers (yellow), red cabbage (blue/purple). Simmer in water to extract color.
- Key Concepts: Color is everywhere in nature, different materials yield different colors, concentration affects intensity
- Practice: Create a dye sample chart showing color from each source

**Lesson 3-4: Mordanting**
- Activity: Dye two pieces of fabric in the same color — one pre-soaked in salt/vinegar/alum mordant, one without. Wash both. The mordanted fabric holds color. The unmordanted fades.
- Key Concepts: Mordants fix dye to fiber, without mordant most natural dyes wash out, different mordants can shift colors
- Practice: Test at least 2 mordants with the same dye and compare results

**Lesson 5-6: Pattern Techniques**
- Activity: Tie-dye (wrap and bind fabric before dyeing to create patterns), resist dyeing (apply wax or paste to areas you want to stay undyed), block printing on fabric (use carved blocks from Unit 6 with fabric paint/dye).
- Key Concepts: Controlling where dye goes creates pattern, these techniques are used in cultures worldwide
- Practice: Create one item using each technique

**Lesson 7-8: Finished Textile Project**
- Activity: Design and create a finished textile item — a bag, headband, bandana, wall hanging, or book cover. Combine dyeing and pattern techniques.
- Key Concepts: Bringing skills together into a finished product, design choices, craftsmanship
- Practice: Complete the project and present to class. Explain techniques used and design choices.

#### Assessment
- Extract usable dye from at least 3 natural sources
- Explain what a mordant does and demonstrate its effect
- Complete a finished dyed textile item using at least one pattern technique

## Grade 5: Collaboration and Cultural Leadership

### Unit 9: Instrument Performance and Ensemble (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Any available instruments (handmade drums, flutes, string instruments, percussion), voices
**Survival Relevance**: Group music-making builds cooperation, timing, listening, and shared experience — the same skills needed for any coordinated community activity

#### Learning Objectives
- Play an instrument at a basic level (rhythm instruments, flute, string, or percussion)
- Play as part of a group — listening, matching tempo, taking turns
- Perform for the community
- Understand the role of music in community life

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Individual Skills**
- Activity: Each student chooses or is assigned an instrument based on availability. Learn to produce consistent sounds, keep a steady beat, play a simple melody or rhythm pattern.
- Key Concepts: Every instrument requires practice, consistent sound production comes before complexity
- Practice: Daily practice — 10 minutes minimum. Master 3 patterns or melodies.

**Lesson 3-4: Playing Together**
- Activity: Start with unison — everyone plays the same rhythm. Then add parts — one group keeps the beat, another plays a pattern, a third adds melody. Listen to each other.
- Key Concepts: Ensemble playing requires listening more than playing, tempo must be shared, parts must complement each other
- Practice: Play a 3-part arrangement of a familiar song

**Lesson 5-6: Arrangement and Rehearsal**
- Activity: Choose 3-5 songs for a community performance. Arrange them for the available instruments. Assign parts. Rehearse — focus on starting together, staying together, and ending together.
- Key Concepts: Arrangement is adapting music for the instruments you have, rehearsal turns individuals into a group
- Practice: Full rehearsals with performance-level focus

**Lesson 7-8: Community Performance**
- Activity: Perform at a community gathering. Include audience participation (clapping, singing along). Reflect afterward — what worked, what needed more practice?
- Key Concepts: Music serves the community, performance is sharing not showing off
- Practice: Post-performance reflection and plans for the next event

#### Assessment
- Play an instrument at a basic competent level (steady rhythm, recognizable melody)
- Participate in ensemble playing (stay in tempo, listen to others)
- Perform for the community demonstrating preparation and collaboration

### Unit 10: Visual Arts Portfolio (Weeks 9-16)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: All available art materials (drawing, painting, clay, printmaking, fabric)
**Survival Relevance**: A portfolio documents growth and demonstrates skill — it is evidence of capability, useful for trade negotiations, teaching credentials, and personal development tracking

#### Learning Objectives
- Create work across multiple media (drawing, painting, clay, printmaking, textile)
- Select and curate work into a portfolio
- Write artist statements explaining intent and technique
- Present work to an audience with confidence

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Mixed Media Exploration**
- Activity: Create one piece in each medium available. Experiment with combining media — drawing on dyed fabric, printing on clay, painting on found objects.
- Key Concepts: Different media suit different purposes, experimentation leads to discovery
- Practice: Create at least 4 pieces in different media

**Lesson 3-4: Focused Series**
- Activity: Choose one subject or theme. Create 3+ pieces exploring it in different media or from different angles. (Example: "Water" — a watercolor landscape, a clay water vessel, a printed water-themed pattern.)
- Key Concepts: A series deepens understanding, returning to a subject reveals new aspects
- Practice: Complete a 3-piece series

**Lesson 5-6: Portfolio Curation**
- Activity: Gather all work from Grade 3-5 arts. Select the strongest 8-10 pieces. Arrange them to show growth and range. Write a short statement for each explaining what you were trying to do and what you learned.
- Key Concepts: Curation is choosing what represents you best, reflection deepens learning
- Practice: Assemble and annotate the portfolio

**Lesson 7-8: Portfolio Exhibition**
- Activity: Display portfolios for the community. Each student stands by their work and discusses it with viewers. This is the visual arts equivalent of a performance.
- Key Concepts: Sharing work builds confidence, explaining your process helps others learn, art connects community members
- Practice: Exhibition day with guided viewing

#### Assessment
- Portfolio contains at least 8 pieces across 3+ media
- Each piece has a written artist statement
- Student can verbally discuss their work and artistic growth

### Unit 11: Creative Writing Anthology (Weeks 17-22)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Paper, writing tools, binding materials (needle, thread, or stapler)
**Survival Relevance**: Creative writing develops communication skills, preserves community voice, and creates a cultural record. An anthology demonstrates the community's literacy and creative capacity.

#### Learning Objectives
- Write in multiple forms (poetry, short fiction, personal narrative, descriptive essay)
- Edit and revise work for quality
- Collaborate to produce a class anthology
- Understand writing as cultural preservation

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Poetry**
- Activity: Write poems in multiple forms — haiku (5-7-5 syllables, nature focus), acrostic (first letters spell a word), free verse (no rules except quality), rhyming couplets. Read aloud and discuss.
- Key Concepts: Poetry distills language to its essence, constraints can spark creativity
- Practice: Write at least 3 poems, revise one for the anthology

**Lesson 3-4: Short Fiction and Narrative**
- Activity: Write a short story set in the community — past, present, or imagined future. OR write a personal narrative about a real experience. Focus on showing (sensory details) not telling.
- Key Concepts: Stories help us understand experience, detail makes writing vivid, revision improves everything
- Practice: Draft, peer-review, and revise one piece for the anthology

**Lesson 5-6: Anthology Production**
- Activity: Select each student's best 2-3 pieces. Edit together. Design a cover. Handwrite or print final copies. Bind into a book. Create multiple copies.
- Key Concepts: Publishing is an act of community, anthologies preserve voices, collaborative production
- Practice: Complete the anthology and present copies to the community library and leadership

#### Assessment
- Contribute at least 2 polished pieces to the anthology
- Demonstrate meaningful revision (compare draft to final version)
- Participate in collaborative anthology production

### Unit 12: Cultural Preservation Project (Weeks 23-30)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Paper, drawing supplies, recording tools if available, binding materials
**Survival Relevance**: This is the capstone — students take responsibility for preserving their community's cultural heritage. Traditions, songs, stories, recipes, and skills that are not documented will eventually be lost. This project prevents that.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify cultural knowledge at risk of being lost in the community
- Conduct interviews with community members to document traditions
- Create a permanent cultural record using multiple formats (written, drawn, performed)
- Present the preservation project to the community

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Needs Preserving?**
- Activity: Brainstorm — what does our community know that isn't written down? Recipes, songs, stories, skills, sayings, customs, celebrations, place names, plant knowledge, craft techniques. Prioritize by risk of loss (older community members hold knowledge that could be lost).
- Key Concepts: Culture is fragile, undocumented knowledge dies with its holders, preservation is an urgent responsibility
- Practice: Create a priority list of at-risk cultural knowledge

**Lesson 3-4: Interview Skills**
- Activity: Learn interview techniques — open-ended questions, active listening, follow-up questions, note-taking. Practice with each other before approaching community members.
- Key Concepts: Respectful interviewing, accurate recording, asking "can you show me?" not just "can you tell me?"
- Practice: Conduct practice interviews in pairs

**Lesson 5-6: Documentation**
- Activity: In teams, document assigned cultural items. Record recipes with exact quantities and procedures. Transcribe songs with lyrics and melody description. Draw craft techniques step by step. Write down stories word for word.
- Key Concepts: Accuracy matters in documentation, include context (who, when, where, why), multiple formats reinforce each other
- Practice: Each team completes documentation of at least 3 cultural items

**Lesson 7-8: The Cultural Record**
- Activity: Compile all documentation into a Community Cultural Record. Organize by type. Include illustrations. Create multiple copies. Present to community elders for review and correction. Deposit final copy with community leadership.
- Key Concepts: The cultural record is a living document — it should grow over time, this class has started something the next classes continue
- Practice: Community presentation and formal handover of the record

#### Assessment
- Conduct at least 2 interviews with community members and produce accurate documentation
- Contribute meaningfully to the Cultural Record (writing, illustration, organization)
- Present work to the community with confidence and respect for the source material
- Explain why cultural preservation is important for community survival
