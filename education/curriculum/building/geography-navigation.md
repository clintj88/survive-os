# Building Band (Grades 3-5, Ages 8-11) — Geography & Navigation

## Overview
Geography and navigation are survival essentials. Students progress from reading simple maps to navigating by compass and stars. Every skill taught is immediately applicable — finding their way in the field, understanding terrain, planning routes, and reading the land and sky for directional cues.

## Grade 3: Maps and Our World

### Unit 1: Maps and Map Symbols (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Paper, pencils, colored pencils, ruler, examples of simple maps
**Survival Relevance**: The ability to read and create maps is essential for patrols, resource gathering, trade routes, and emergency communication of locations

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that a map is a picture of a place viewed from above
- Identify common map symbols (water, roads, buildings, trees, hills)
- Create a simple map with a key/legend
- Read a basic map to locate features

#### Teacher Notes
Begin with the classroom. Map-making is one of the oldest human skills — cave paintings at Lascaux may include landscape features. Make this tactile and immediate. Walk the community and draw what you see from above.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Bird's-Eye View**
- Activity: Stand on an elevated spot and look down at the schoolroom or community area. Draw what you see. Compare to looking at it from ground level.
- Key Concepts: Maps show the world from above, scale (things look smaller from far away)
- Practice: Draw a map of your desk, then the classroom

**Lesson 3-4: Map Symbols and Keys**
- Activity: Introduce standard symbols — blue for water, green for vegetation, brown for elevation, squares for buildings. Create a class symbol set.
- Key Concepts: Symbols represent real things, a key/legend explains the symbols
- Practice: Create a map of the schoolroom using symbols and a key

**Lesson 5-6: Reading Someone Else's Map**
- Activity: Teacher creates a map of a nearby area with hidden "treasures." Students use the map to find them.
- Key Concepts: Maps are communication tools — someone else made this for YOU to use
- Practice: Trade maps with a partner. Can they navigate using your map?

#### Assessment
- Create a map with at least 8 symbols and a complete key
- Successfully navigate using a map created by someone else

### Unit 2: Cardinal Directions (Weeks 7-12)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Sticks, shadows, chalk or string
**Survival Relevance**: Knowing north, south, east, and west is the foundation of all navigation — without it, you are lost

#### Learning Objectives
- Name and identify the four cardinal directions
- Determine north using the sun and shadows
- Give and follow directions using cardinal directions
- Understand that the sun rises in the east and sets in the west

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: North, South, East, West**
- Activity: Go outside at midday. Plant a stick in the ground. The shadow points roughly north (in the Northern Hemisphere) or south (in the Southern Hemisphere). Mark the directions with stones.
- Key Concepts: The four cardinal directions, the sun moves east to west across the sky
- Practice: "Stand facing north. Turn to face east. Point south."

**Lesson 3-4: Directions on a Map**
- Activity: Orient a map using the shadow-stick method. "North is at the top of most maps." Walk the community with a map, keeping it oriented correctly.
- Key Concepts: Maps have a north arrow, orienting a map to the real world
- Practice: Give directions to a partner using only cardinal directions — "Walk north 20 paces, turn east, walk 10 paces"

**Lesson 5-6: Direction Scavenger Hunt**
- Activity: Teacher gives clue cards using cardinal directions. Students navigate from point to point.
- Key Concepts: Combining distance and direction for navigation
- Practice: Create a direction-based scavenger hunt for another group

#### Assessment
- Determine north using the sun/shadow method
- Successfully follow a 5-step set of cardinal-direction instructions
- Orient a map correctly to match the real world

### Unit 3: Local Area Mapping (Weeks 13-18)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Paper, measuring tape or rope, pencils, clipboards
**Survival Relevance**: Mapping your own area is critical for defense, resource management, and coordination during emergencies

#### Learning Objectives
- Measure distances by pacing (counting steps)
- Create a rough-scale map of the community and surroundings
- Identify and label key features (water sources, gardens, buildings, paths, boundaries)
- Understand why knowing your area matters for survival

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Measuring by Pacing**
- Activity: Measure a known distance (10 meters). Count your steps. Calculate your pace length. Now measure the schoolroom, the path to the garden, the distance to the water source.
- Key Concepts: Pacing as a measurement tool, consistency in stride
- Practice: Pace three distances and record them

**Lesson 3-4: Community Mapping Project**
- Activity: In groups, students pace out and map different sections of the community. Teacher assembles sections into a full community map.
- Key Concepts: Teamwork in mapping, combining data from different sources
- Practice: Each group presents their section and explains what they included and why

**Lesson 5-6: What's Important on a Map?**
- Activity: Discuss — if someone new arrived, what would they need to know about our area? Water sources, safe areas, danger zones, food gardens, medical area, gathering places. Add these to the community map.
- Key Concepts: Maps serve specific purposes, different maps show different things
- Practice: Create a "newcomer map" with the most essential information

#### Assessment
- Accurately pace a 50-meter distance (within 10% error)
- Contribute a section to the community map with at least 6 labeled features
- Explain why mapping the community is important for survival

### Unit 4: Landforms Vocabulary (Weeks 19-24)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Sand or clay for modeling, pictures of landforms, paper for drawing
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding terrain affects route planning, water sourcing, defense positioning, and agriculture placement

#### Learning Objectives
- Name and describe 10+ landforms (hill, valley, river, plain, mountain, plateau, cliff, ridge, canyon, island)
- Explain how landforms affect where people live and how they travel
- Identify landforms in the local area
- Build a 3D model of a landscape with labeled landforms

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Water Landforms**
- Activity: Use sand and water to demonstrate rivers, streams, lakes, ponds, deltas, and floodplains. Pour water on a sand pile and watch it create channels.
- Key Concepts: Water shapes the land, rivers flow downhill, valleys form along rivers
- Practice: Draw and label 5 water landforms

**Lesson 3-4: Land Landforms**
- Activity: Build hills, mountains, plateaus, plains, cliffs, and ridges from clay or sand. Compare sizes and shapes.
- Key Concepts: Mountains are tall and steep, plateaus are flat on top, plains are wide and flat, ridges are long and narrow
- Practice: Draw and label 5 land landforms

**Lesson 5-6: Our Landforms**
- Activity: Walk the area around the community. Identify every landform you can see. Which landforms help us (valleys channel water to us, hills give vantage points)? Which create challenges (cliffs block travel, floodplains flood)?
- Key Concepts: Landforms affect human settlement and survival decisions
- Practice: Add landform labels to the community map

#### Assessment
- Name and describe 10 landforms from memory
- Identify at least 3 landforms visible from the community
- Explain how one local landform affects community life

## Grade 4: Compass and Map Skills

### Unit 5: Compass Use (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Magnetic compass (at least one per group), maps from Grade 3 work
**Survival Relevance**: A compass works when GPS doesn't, when the sky is overcast, and when landmarks are unfamiliar — it is the most reliable navigation tool available

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand that a compass needle points to magnetic north
- Read compass bearings in degrees (0-360)
- Take a bearing to a visible landmark
- Walk a straight line on a compass bearing
- Understand magnetic declination (difference between true north and magnetic north)

#### Teacher Notes
If compasses are scarce, demonstrate and rotate. Even one compass per class is sufficient. You can also magnetize a needle by stroking it on a magnet, then floating it on water in a leaf — this is a survival compass.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How a Compass Works**
- Activity: Let students handle a compass. "Which way does the needle always point?" Walk in circles — the needle stays still. Face different directions — the needle always points the same way.
- Key Concepts: Earth is a giant magnet, the compass needle aligns with Earth's magnetic field
- Practice: Stand at a central point. What compass direction is the school? The garden? The water source?

**Lesson 3-4: Reading Bearings**
- Activity: Introduce the 360-degree circle. North = 0/360, East = 90, South = 180, West = 270. Take bearings to visible landmarks.
- Key Concepts: Bearings give exact directions, not just "north" or "east" but 47 degrees or 215 degrees
- Practice: Take bearings to 5 landmarks and record them

**Lesson 5-6: Walking a Bearing**
- Activity: "Walk on bearing 90 for 50 paces, then bearing 180 for 30 paces, then bearing 270 for 50 paces, then bearing 0 for 30 paces." You end up back where you started (a rectangle).
- Key Concepts: Combining bearing and distance for precise movement
- Practice: Create a compass course for another group

**Lesson 7-8: Magnetic Declination**
- Activity: Explain that magnetic north and true north are not the same place. Show how much the difference is for your location. Practice adjusting bearings.
- Key Concepts: Declination varies by location and changes slowly over time, maps usually show true north
- Practice: Take a bearing from a map and adjust it for declination before walking

#### Assessment
- Read a compass bearing within 5 degrees accuracy
- Navigate a 4-point compass course and arrive within 5 paces of the target
- Explain magnetic declination in their own words

### Unit 6: Scale, Distance, and Topographic Maps (Weeks 9-16)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Maps (hand-drawn or salvaged), ruler, string, pencils
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding scale lets you estimate travel time and distances — critical for patrols, supply runs, and emergency planning

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand and use map scale (1 cm = 100 m, etc.)
- Measure distances on a map and convert to real-world distances
- Read contour lines on a topographic map (hills are circles, valleys are V-shapes)
- Plan a route considering distance AND elevation

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Is Scale?**
- Activity: Draw the classroom at full size (impossible). Draw it on paper (much smaller). "How much smaller?" Introduce the concept of scale — everything is shrunk by the same amount.
- Key Concepts: Scale ratio, scale bar, consistency
- Practice: Create a 1:100 scale map of the schoolroom (1 cm = 1 m)

**Lesson 3-4: Measuring Map Distances**
- Activity: Using the community map, measure the distance between two points with a ruler. Convert using the scale. Then pace the actual distance. How close was the estimate?
- Key Concepts: Map distance vs real distance, the importance of accurate scale
- Practice: Measure 5 map distances and convert to real distances

**Lesson 5-6: Contour Lines**
- Activity: Stack layers of cardboard cut into decreasing sizes. Look from above — the edges are contour lines. Close lines = steep, far apart = gentle slope. A circle = hill, V pointing uphill = valley.
- Key Concepts: Contour lines connect points of equal elevation, interval between lines is consistent
- Practice: Read a simple topographic map — identify the highest point, the steepest slope, a valley, a ridge

**Lesson 7-8: Route Planning**
- Activity: Given a topographic map, plan a route from A to B. The shortest distance might go over a steep hill — is it faster to go around? Estimate travel time considering terrain.
- Key Concepts: Terrain affects travel time, route planning considers elevation not just distance
- Practice: Plan two routes between the same points — shortest vs easiest. Present reasoning.

#### Assessment
- Accurately convert 3 map distances to real distances using scale
- Read contour lines to identify hills, valleys, and slopes on a topographic map
- Plan a route with justified reasoning for the chosen path

### Unit 7: Climate Zones, Continents, and Oceans (Weeks 17-22)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: World map (drawn or salvaged), globe if available
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding climate and global geography informs trade planning, crop selection, and understanding of weather patterns

#### Learning Objectives
- Name and locate the 7 continents and 5 major oceans
- Understand the concept of climate zones (tropical, temperate, polar, arid)
- Explain why climate varies by latitude and proximity to water
- Identify the climate zone of the community's location

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The World Map**
- Activity: Draw a world map together on the largest paper available. Label continents and oceans. Discuss: people live on every continent except Antarctica.
- Key Concepts: The world is very large, most of it is water, humans live in extremely varied environments
- Practice: Quiz — name the continents and oceans from memory

**Lesson 3-4: Climate Zones**
- Activity: Why is it hot at the equator and cold at the poles? Demonstrate with a ball and a light source — direct light hits the middle, angled light hits the top and bottom.
- Key Concepts: Latitude determines how directly sunlight hits, oceans moderate temperature, elevation makes things colder
- Practice: On the world map, shade climate zones in different colors

**Lesson 5-6: Our Climate**
- Activity: What climate zone are we in? What does that mean for our growing season, water availability, temperature range, and weather patterns? How does our climate compare to where the community members came from?
- Key Concepts: Climate determines what you can grow, how you build, and how you live
- Practice: Create a "climate profile" of the community's location

#### Assessment
- Label continents and oceans on a blank map
- Explain why the equator is hotter than the poles
- Describe the community's climate zone and what it means for survival

## Grade 5: Advanced Navigation

### Unit 8: Dead Reckoning (Weeks 1-6)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Compass, notebook, pencil, measuring tools
**Survival Relevance**: Dead reckoning lets you track your position when you can't see landmarks — in fog, forest, or at night

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand dead reckoning as tracking position by direction and distance from a known point
- Maintain a navigation log (bearing, distance, time)
- Plot a course on paper and follow it in the field
- Correct for drift and error accumulation

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Concept**
- Activity: Close your eyes. Walk 20 paces on bearing 45. Open your eyes. Where are you? Now do it again while counting paces carefully and holding the bearing. Closer to the target?
- Key Concepts: Dead reckoning = last known position + direction + distance = estimated current position
- Practice: Walk a 3-leg course with eyes on the compass only. How close to the target?

**Lesson 3-4: The Navigation Log**
- Activity: Walk a planned route through the community, recording bearing, paces, and time at each leg. At the end, give only the log to a partner — can they retrace your route?
- Key Concepts: A navigation log is a record that someone else can follow, accuracy matters
- Practice: Create a navigation log for a route and have it tested

**Lesson 5-6: Error and Correction**
- Activity: Walk a bearing for 200 paces. How far off the target are you? Discuss: small errors add up over distance. What causes errors? (Inconsistent pace, poor bearing reading, obstacles that force detours.)
- Key Concepts: Dead reckoning accumulates error, periodic position fixes correct it (find a known landmark and reset)
- Practice: Walk a long course with planned correction points

#### Assessment
- Navigate a 4-leg course by dead reckoning and arrive within 15 paces of the target
- Maintain a complete navigation log that another person can follow
- Explain how error accumulates and how to correct for it

### Unit 9: Star Navigation (Weeks 7-14)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Clear night sky, star charts (hand-drawn), sticks for sighting
**Survival Relevance**: Stars work when you have no compass, no map, and no landmarks — they are the ultimate backup navigation system

#### Learning Objectives
- Find Polaris (North Star) using the Big Dipper (Northern Hemisphere)
- Find south using the Southern Cross (Southern Hemisphere) — teach both methods regardless of location
- Identify 5+ navigation-relevant constellations
- Determine approximate direction using stars
- Understand that stars shift position through the night and through the seasons

#### Teacher Notes
This unit requires clear night sessions — schedule at least 3 evening observation periods. Pair with a campfire and storytelling to make it memorable. Ancient navigators from every culture used the stars — this connects to history.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Finding North by Polaris**
- Activity (evening): Locate the Big Dipper. Follow the two "pointer stars" at the end of the cup upward — they point to Polaris. Polaris is almost exactly over the North Pole and barely moves all night.
- Key Concepts: Polaris indicates true north, all other stars appear to rotate around it
- Practice: On three different nights, find Polaris and confirm direction with a compass

**Lesson 3-4: Finding South by the Southern Cross**
- Activity: Locate the Southern Cross. Extend the long axis 4.5 times its length — that point is roughly south. Also use the pointer stars (Alpha and Beta Centauri) to confirm.
- Key Concepts: The Southern Hemisphere has no pole star — the Southern Cross method is an estimate
- Practice: Diagram the method and practice on star charts even if in the Northern Hemisphere

**Lesson 5-6: Navigation Constellations**
- Activity: Learn Orion (visible from both hemispheres — belt stars rise due east, set due west), Cassiopeia (backup to find Polaris if Big Dipper is obscured), and seasonal constellations.
- Key Concepts: Stars move across the sky east to west, different stars visible in different seasons
- Practice: Identify 5 constellations and explain their navigation value

**Lesson 7-8: Practical Night Navigation**
- Activity (evening): Navigate a short course at night using only stars. Start from a known point. Walk toward Polaris (north) for 100 paces. Turn to face Orion's belt as it rises (east). Walk 50 paces. Where are you?
- Key Concepts: Combining star directions with pacing for night navigation
- Practice: Navigate a 3-point night course using only the sky

#### Assessment
- Find north (or south) using stars within 15 degrees of accuracy
- Identify 5 navigation-relevant constellations and explain their use
- Successfully navigate a short night course using only celestial cues

### Unit 10: Map and Compass Together (Weeks 15-20)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Compass, topographic maps, protractor or straight edge
**Survival Relevance**: Combining map and compass is the core field navigation skill — it lets you travel through unfamiliar terrain confidently

#### Learning Objectives
- Orient a map using a compass
- Plot a bearing from a map and follow it in the field
- Take a field bearing and plot it on a map (back-bearing to determine position)
- Triangulate position using two or more bearings to known landmarks

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Orienting the Map**
- Activity: Place the compass on the map. Rotate the map until the compass needle aligns with the map's north arrow. Now everything on the map matches the real world.
- Key Concepts: An oriented map lets you point at a feature on the map and look up to see it in real life
- Practice: Orient a map and verify by pointing at 3 known landmarks

**Lesson 3-4: Map to Field**
- Activity: On the map, draw a line from your position to a destination. Measure the bearing with a protractor. Adjust for declination. Set your compass and walk.
- Key Concepts: Planning a route on paper then executing it in the real world
- Practice: Plot and walk 3 different bearings from the map

**Lesson 5-6: Triangulation**
- Activity: You can see two known landmarks (a hilltop and a water tower, for example). Take a compass bearing to each. Plot those bearings on the map from each landmark. Where the lines cross is your position.
- Key Concepts: Triangulation uses known points to determine unknown position, three bearings are more accurate than two
- Practice: Determine your position by triangulation from 3 different locations

#### Assessment
- Orient a map correctly using a compass
- Navigate to a point using a bearing taken from a map
- Determine position using triangulation within 50 meters accuracy

### Unit 11: Route Planning and Orienteering (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Maps, compass, flag markers or stakes for course
**Survival Relevance**: Route planning combines all navigation skills into the practical ability to plan and execute a journey through terrain

#### Learning Objectives
- Plan a multi-point route considering terrain, distance, and time
- Navigate an orienteering course (point-to-point using map and compass)
- Estimate travel time based on distance and terrain
- Make navigation decisions in the field (when to adjust a planned route)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Planning a Route**
- Activity: Given a map with a start and end point, plan the best route. Consider: shortest path? Easiest terrain? Water sources along the way? Visible landmarks for navigation checks?
- Key Concepts: Route planning balances distance, difficulty, safety, and navigation reliability
- Practice: Plan 3 different routes between the same two points. Justify each choice.

**Lesson 3-4: Orienteering Basics**
- Activity: Set up a 5-point orienteering course. Each control point has a code. Students navigate point to point using map and compass. Record codes to prove they found each point.
- Key Concepts: Orienteering combines all navigation skills — map reading, compass, terrain awareness, route choice
- Practice: Complete the course. Record time. Discuss what worked and what was difficult.

**Lesson 5-6: Time and Terrain Estimation**
- Activity: On flat ground, how long does it take to walk 1 kilometer? Uphill? Through brush? Carrying a load? Build a personal time estimation chart.
- Key Concepts: Naismith's Rule (1 hour per 5 km on flat, add 1 hour per 600m of climb), terrain slows you down
- Practice: Estimate travel time for a planned route, then walk it and compare

**Lesson 7-8: Orienteering Challenge**
- Activity: Full orienteering course — 8+ points over varied terrain. Students navigate independently or in pairs. Includes route choice decisions, terrain features, and at least one point requiring triangulation to locate.
- Key Concepts: Synthesis of all navigation skills in a timed challenge
- Practice: Debrief — what strategies worked best? Where did navigation errors happen?

#### Assessment
- Plan a route with written justification for the chosen path
- Complete an orienteering course finding at least 6 of 8 points
- Estimate travel time for a 3 km route within 20% accuracy

### Unit 12: Latitude, Longitude, and Grid Systems (Weeks 29-34)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: World map or globe, grid paper, maps with grid references
**Survival Relevance**: Grid references let you communicate exact locations to others via radio — critical for coordination, rescue, and resource marking

#### Learning Objectives
- Understand latitude (north-south) and longitude (east-west)
- Read and give a 4-digit and 6-digit grid reference
- Use a grid reference to locate a point on a map
- Communicate a location using grid references via radio practice

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Latitude and Longitude**
- Activity: On a ball, draw horizontal lines (latitude — like rungs of a ladder) and vertical lines (longitude — like segments of an orange). Any point on the ball can be described by two numbers.
- Key Concepts: Latitude measures how far north or south of the equator, longitude measures how far east or west of the Prime Meridian
- Practice: Find the approximate latitude and longitude of the community's location

**Lesson 3-4: Grid References**
- Activity: Overlay a numbered grid on the community map. Practice giving 4-digit references (identifies a grid square) and 6-digit references (identifies a point within a square).
- Key Concepts: Grid references are read right then up ("along the corridor, then up the stairs"), 6-digit is more precise than 4-digit
- Practice: Give and receive grid references for 10 locations on the map

**Lesson 5-6: Radio Position Reports**
- Activity: Practice scenario — a patrol finds a resource cache. They need to report its location via radio. Give the grid reference, describe the terrain, and provide a landmark reference. The class plots the position on the map.
- Key Concepts: Clear position reporting saves time and lives, standardized format prevents confusion
- Practice: Each student gives a radio position report and the class plots it

#### Assessment
- Read and give accurate 6-digit grid references
- Explain the latitude/longitude system in their own words
- Successfully communicate a position via simulated radio report
