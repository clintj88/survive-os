# Building Band (Grades 3-5, Ages 8-11) — Introduction to Agriculture

## Overview
Agriculture is the most critical practical skill in a survival community. This curriculum gives students hands-on experience growing food, managing soil, composting, and — by Grade 5 — managing their own garden plot and caring for livestock. Every lesson happens outside whenever possible. Students who complete this band understand the basics of feeding a community.

---

## Grade 3: Seeds, Soil, and Gardens

### Unit 1: Seed Germination Experiments (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Prerequisites**: Foundation science — plant parts, life cycle, basic observation
**Materials**: Seeds (bean, corn, squash, whatever is available), containers, soil, water, dark/light conditions
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding germination conditions determines planting success. Failed germination means lost food.

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the conditions seeds need to germinate (water, warmth, sometimes light)
- Conduct a controlled germination experiment (change one variable, keep others constant)
- Calculate germination rates (seeds planted vs. seeds sprouted)
- Identify seed parts (seed coat, embryo, stored food/cotyledon)

#### Teacher Notes
This unit connects directly to the scientific method taught in the science curriculum. Use it as applied science — students form hypotheses, test them, and draw conclusions. Seeds are cheap and available. If germination experiments fail, that's still learning — "Why didn't they sprout?" is a legitimate scientific question.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Inside a Seed**
- Activity: Soak beans overnight. Open them carefully. "See the tiny plant inside? That's the embryo. The thick part is stored food (cotyledon). The hard outside is the seed coat — it protects the embryo until conditions are right."

**Lesson 3-4: Germination Experiment Setup**
- Activity: Plant 10 seeds in each of 4 conditions: 1) Water + light + warmth (control). 2) No water + light + warmth. 3) Water + dark + warmth. 4) Water + light + cold. Predict which will germinate best. Record predictions.

**Lesson 5-6: Observation and Data Collection**
- Activity: Check daily for 2 weeks. Record: how many sprouted, how tall, leaf development. Graph results.

**Lesson 7-8: Analysis**
- Activity: "Which condition worked best? Why? What does this tell us about when and where to plant?" Write a lab report with hypothesis, procedure, data, and conclusion.
- Survival connection: "This is why we plant in spring, not winter. This is why we water. This is why we don't plant in deep shade."

#### Assessment
- Can label seed parts
- Conducted a controlled experiment with one variable
- Can calculate germination rate
- Can explain optimal germination conditions

### Unit 2: Soil Types and Testing (Weeks 9-16)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Soil samples from 3+ locations, jars, water, pH test strips (if available), vinegar, baking soda
**Survival Relevance**: Soil determines what you can grow. Testing soil before planting prevents crop failure.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify soil types (sandy, clay, loamy, silty) by feel and jar test
- Understand how soil type affects drainage, nutrient retention, and workability
- Perform basic soil pH testing (acid vs. alkaline)
- Recommend soil amendments based on test results
- Map soil types around the community

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Feel Test**
- Activity: Wet soil from 3+ locations. Sandy: gritty, won't hold shape. Clay: smooth, sticky, holds shape. Loam: crumbly, holds shape briefly then falls apart. "Loam is ideal for most crops."

**Lesson 3-4: Jar Test**
- Activity: Soil in jar + water. Shake. Let settle 24 hours. Bottom = sand. Middle = silt. Top = clay. Floating = organic matter. Measure proportions. "This tells you exactly what your soil is made of."

**Lesson 5-6: pH Testing**
- Activity: If pH strips available, test soil water. If not, use vinegar (fizzes in alkaline soil) and baking soda solution (fizzes in acidic soil). "Most vegetables like slightly acidic to neutral soil (pH 6-7)."

**Lesson 7-8: Soil Improvement**
- Activity: "Sandy soil: add compost for water retention. Clay soil: add sand and compost for drainage. Acidic soil: add wood ash. Low nutrients: add compost, manure, or bone meal."
- Project: Test and map soil types around the community garden. Recommend amendments.

### Unit 3: Garden Planning (Weeks 17-24)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Graph paper, seed packets or community seed stores, garden area
**Survival Relevance**: A well-planned garden produces more food with less effort. Poor planning wastes seeds, space, and labor.

#### Learning Objectives
- Design a garden layout on paper before planting
- Consider sun exposure, water access, spacing, and companion plants
- Understand growing seasons and planting calendars
- Calculate seed quantities needed for a given area
- Plant according to the plan

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Site Assessment**
- Activity: Visit the garden area. "Where does the sun hit? Where's the water source? Which areas are sheltered from wind? Where does water pool?" Map the site.

**Lesson 3-4: Design on Paper**
- Activity: Using graph paper, design a garden layout. Each square = 1 foot (or 30 cm). Place crops considering: sun needs, water needs, height (tall plants don't shade short ones), root depth.
- Math connection: Area calculations, spacing multiplication

**Lesson 5-6: Planting Calendar**
- Activity: "Different crops plant at different times. Cool-weather crops (lettuce, peas, radishes) plant early. Warm-weather crops (tomatoes, beans, squash) plant after last frost." Create a community planting calendar.

**Lesson 7-8: Plant!**
- Activity: Follow the plan. Measure spacing. Plant seeds at correct depth. Water. Label rows. Begin the observation journal.

### Unit 4: Composting (Weeks 25-30)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Compost bin or pile area, kitchen scraps, yard waste, pitchfork/shovel
**Survival Relevance**: Compost turns waste into soil fertility. Without chemical fertilizers, compost is the primary soil amendment.

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain what composting is (decomposition of organic matter into soil)
- Build and maintain a compost pile with correct brown-to-green ratio
- Monitor compost temperature, moisture, and turning schedule
- Explain the role of decomposers (bacteria, fungi, worms, insects)
- Use finished compost in the garden

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Is Composting?**
- Activity: "Nature composts everything — fallen leaves, dead animals, old trees. We speed up the process by creating the right conditions."
- Ingredients: Greens (nitrogen — food scraps, fresh grass, manure) + Browns (carbon — dried leaves, straw, cardboard, wood chips) + Water + Air

**Lesson 3-4: Building a Compost Pile**
- Activity: Layer browns and greens (2:1 ratio). Water until damp like a wrung-out sponge. "In a few days, it will get hot in the middle — that's decomposition happening."

**Lesson 5-6: Maintaining and Using**
- Activity: Turn every 1-2 weeks. Monitor moisture. "When it looks like dark, crumbly soil and smells earthy, it's ready." Apply to garden beds.

### Unit 5: Beneficial Insects vs. Pests (Weeks 31-38)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Magnifying glass, insect identification charts, garden access
**Survival Relevance**: Knowing which insects help and which harm crops enables targeted pest management without chemicals

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify 5+ beneficial insects (pollinators, predators of pests)
- Identify 5+ common crop pests
- Understand biological pest control (using beneficial insects to control pests)
- Practice integrated pest management (IPM) principles
- Build a simple insect habitat to attract beneficials

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: The Good Bugs**
- Activity: Garden walk with magnifying glass. Find and identify: bees (pollinators), ladybugs (eat aphids), praying mantis (eat many pests), spiders (catch flying pests), earthworms (aerate soil).
- Key Concept: "These are your garden's allies. Never kill them."

**Lesson 3-4: The Bad Bugs**
- Activity: Find and identify: aphids (suck plant juices), caterpillars (eat leaves), slugs (eat seedlings), whiteflies, beetle larvae.
- Key Concept: "Not all insects are pests. Learn to identify before you act."

**Lesson 5-6: Biological Control**
- Activity: "Instead of poisons, use nature. Ladybugs eat aphids. Birds eat caterpillars. Toads eat slugs. Attract these predators to your garden."
- Build: Simple insect hotel from stacked sticks, hollow reeds, and pine cones

**Lesson 7-8: IPM Principles**
- Activity: "IPM means: 1) Prevent (healthy plants resist pests). 2) Monitor (check plants regularly). 3) Identify (is it actually a problem?). 4) Act (start with least harmful: hand-pick, spray water, introduce predators, use botanical sprays only as last resort)."

## Grade 4: Propagation, Rotation, Irrigation, and Preservation

### Unit 6: Plant Propagation (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Plants for cuttings, sharp knife, rooting medium, seeds for saving
**Survival Relevance**: Propagation multiplies plants without buying seeds — essential when seed supply is limited

#### Learning Objectives
- Propagate plants from seeds, stem cuttings, root division, and layering
- Understand when each method is appropriate
- Start seeds indoors for transplanting
- Maintain a plant nursery area

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: From Seeds**
- Activity: Start seeds indoors in small containers 4-6 weeks before transplant date. Track germination. Thin seedlings. Harden off before transplanting outside.

**Lesson 3-4: Stem Cuttings**
- Activity: Cut a 6-inch piece from a healthy plant (herbs work well — mint, rosemary, basil). Remove lower leaves. Place in water or damp soil. Watch roots develop over 2-3 weeks.

**Lesson 5-6: Root Division**
- Activity: Dig up a mature clumping plant. Separate into sections, each with roots and shoots. Replant. "One plant becomes three — instant multiplication."

**Lesson 7-8: Layering**
- Activity: Bend a low branch to the ground. Pin it down. Cover with soil. Wait for roots. Cut from parent plant. "The plant roots itself while still connected to the mother."

### Unit 7: Crop Rotation (Weeks 9-16)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Garden plan, crop family charts
**Survival Relevance**: Rotation prevents soil depletion and disease buildup. Communities that plant the same crop in the same spot lose productivity within 2-3 years.

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain why crops must be rotated (nutrient depletion, disease buildup, pest cycles)
- Group crops into rotation families (legumes, brassicas, nightshades, root vegetables, cucurbits)
- Design a 4-year rotation plan for the community garden
- Understand that legumes fix nitrogen (plant them before heavy feeders)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Why Rotate?**
- Activity: "Corn is a heavy nitrogen feeder. Plant corn in the same spot for 3 years and the nitrogen is gone — yields drop. But beans ADD nitrogen to soil. Plant beans, then corn in that spot, and corn thrives."

**Lesson 3-4: Crop Families**
- Activity: Group community crops into families. Crops in the same family share pests and diseases — never plant them in the same spot consecutively.

**Lesson 5-6: 4-Year Plan**
- Activity: Design a rotation: Year 1: Legumes (beans, peas — fix nitrogen). Year 2: Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli — use nitrogen). Year 3: Nightshades/Cucurbits (tomatoes, squash). Year 4: Root vegetables (carrots, potatoes).

**Lesson 7-8: Implementation**
- Activity: Apply the rotation plan to the actual community garden. Present to the agriculture team.

### Unit 8: Watering and Irrigation (Weeks 17-22)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Watering cans, hose or buckets, drip irrigation materials if available
**Survival Relevance**: Water management determines crop survival. Too little = death. Too much = disease. Efficient irrigation conserves the community's most precious resource.

#### Learning Objectives
- Determine water needs for different crops
- Understand deep watering vs. shallow watering (deep encourages strong roots)
- Build a simple drip irrigation system from available materials
- Schedule watering for optimal plant health (morning, at base of plant, not on leaves)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: How Much Water?**
- Activity: "Most vegetables need 1 inch of water per week. But sandy soil needs more (drains fast) and clay soil needs less (holds water). Feel the soil — if it's dry 2 inches down, water."

**Lesson 3-4: Watering Technique**
- Activity: "Water at the base of the plant, not the leaves. Wet leaves encourage disease. Water in the morning so plants dry during the day. Water deeply but less often — this encourages deep roots."

**Lesson 5-6: DIY Drip Irrigation**
- Activity: Build a simple system from punctured containers, hoses, or bottles buried next to plants. "Drip irrigation delivers water directly to roots with minimal waste."

### Unit 9: Harvest and Food Preservation (Weeks 23-32)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Harvested produce, drying racks, salt, vinegar, jars
**Survival Relevance**: Knowing when to harvest and how to preserve food extends the food supply through seasons when nothing grows

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify harvest timing indicators for 10+ crops (color, size, firmness, days from planting)
- Demonstrate 3+ food preservation methods (drying, salting, fermentation)
- Understand the science behind preservation (removing moisture, acid/salt environments kill bacteria)
- Process and store preserved food safely

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: When to Harvest**
- Activity: "Pick tomatoes when fully red. Pull carrots when tops are 1/2 inch diameter. Cut lettuce before it bolts. Harvest beans when pods are plump but before they dry." Learn indicators for community crops.

**Lesson 3-4: Drying**
- Activity: Dry herbs by hanging upside down in bundles. Sun-dry fruit slices on racks. "Removing moisture prevents bacterial growth. Dried food lasts months to years."

**Lesson 5-6: Salting and Pickling**
- Activity: Salt-cure vegetables. Pickle cucumbers in vinegar brine. "Salt and acid create environments where bacteria can't grow."

**Lesson 7-8: Fermentation**
- Activity: Make sauerkraut (shredded cabbage + salt, packed tight, ferments 2-4 weeks). "Fermentation uses beneficial bacteria to preserve food AND adds nutrients."
- Connection to science: This is a controlled chemical reaction.

**Lesson 9-10: Storage**
- Activity: "Dried food: cool, dark, dry, sealed. Pickled food: sealed jars in cool area. Fermented food: cool and sealed after initial fermentation." Practice proper storage techniques.

## Grade 5: Livestock, Advanced Gardening, and Plot Management

### Unit 10: Chicken Care (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Chicken coop access, feed, water containers, egg collection supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Chickens are the most accessible livestock — they produce eggs, meat, and pest control. Every community member should know basic chicken care.

#### Learning Objectives
- Feed and water chickens daily with appropriate amounts
- Collect, clean, and store eggs properly
- Clean and maintain the coop (weekly bedding change, monthly deep clean)
- Recognize signs of illness in chickens
- Understand the egg-laying cycle and factors that affect production

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Daily Care Routine**
- Activity: Morning: feed, water, open coop for free-range (if applicable). Evening: collect eggs, check food/water, close coop for predator protection. Students rotate daily care responsibilities.

**Lesson 3-4: Egg Collection and Storage**
- Activity: Collect eggs gently. Check for cracks. Clean gently if dirty (don't wash — washing removes protective bloom). Store pointed end down. Use oldest first.

**Lesson 5-6: Coop Maintenance**
- Activity: Weekly: add fresh bedding. Monthly: full clean — remove all bedding, scrub surfaces, add fresh material. "Clean coops prevent disease and parasites."

**Lesson 7-8: Health Monitoring**
- Activity: Signs of healthy chicken: bright eyes, active, eating, smooth feathers. Signs of illness: lethargy, ruffled feathers, not eating, discharge, limping. "Report illness immediately — diseases can spread through the whole flock."

**Lesson 9-10: Understanding Production**
- Activity: Track egg production daily. Graph it. "Production drops in winter (less light), during molting, when stressed, and when nutrition is poor. More light and good feed = more eggs."

### Unit 11: Companion Planting and Seed Saving (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Garden area, seed collection supplies (paper bags, envelopes, dry area)
**Survival Relevance**: Companion planting increases yields without additional inputs. Seed saving ensures year-over-year food security.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify 10+ beneficial companion plant combinations
- Identify antagonistic plant combinations (plants that harm each other)
- Select, harvest, clean, dry, and store seeds from 5+ crop varieties
- Understand open-pollinated vs. hybrid seeds (only open-pollinated breed true)
- Plan next year's garden including companion planting

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Companion Planting**
- Activity: Classic combinations — "Three Sisters" (corn + beans + squash: corn provides bean pole, beans fix nitrogen, squash shades soil). Tomato + basil (basil repels pests). Marigolds around garden border (repel many pests).

**Lesson 3-4: What NOT to Plant Together**
- Activity: "Tomatoes and fennel suppress each other. Beans and onions inhibit each other. These compete or produce chemicals that harm neighbors."

**Lesson 5-6: Seed Saving Basics**
- Activity: Let some of the best plants go to seed. "Select seeds from your healthiest, most productive plants — this is selective breeding in action."

**Lesson 7-8: Seed Processing**
- Activity: Harvest seeds. Wet seeds (tomato, cucumber): ferment in water 2-3 days, rinse, dry. Dry seeds (beans, lettuce, herbs): let dry on plant, collect, clean chaff. Store in paper envelopes in a cool, dry place.

**Lesson 9-10: Seed Library**
- Activity: Label all saved seeds with variety, date, and source plant notes. Add to community seed library. "A seed library is a living food bank."

### Unit 12: Personal Plot Management (Weeks 21-40)
**Duration**: 20 weeks
**Materials**: Individual garden plots (4×4 feet minimum), all gardening supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Managing your own food production from planning through harvest is the capstone agricultural skill

#### Learning Objectives
- Design, plant, maintain, and harvest a personal garden plot
- Apply crop rotation, companion planting, composting, and pest management
- Keep a garden journal with planting dates, watering, observations, and harvest records
- Calculate yield and identify areas for improvement
- Present results and recommendations to the class

#### Lessons
**Weeks 21-24: Planning and Planting**
- Activity: Each student designs their 4×4 plot. Choose crops, plan layout with companion planting, prepare soil with compost, plant according to calendar.

**Weeks 25-32: Maintenance**
- Activity: Daily watering, weekly weeding, pest monitoring, journal entries. Teacher checks plots weekly. "Your plants depend on you. Neglect kills."

**Weeks 33-36: Harvest**
- Activity: Harvest crops at peak ripeness. Weigh and record yields. Save seeds from best plants.

**Weeks 37-40: Analysis and Presentation**
- Activity: Calculate total yield. Compare to plan. "What worked? What failed? Why? What would you do differently?" Present a one-page report with data and recommendations.

#### Assessment (Building Band Agriculture Overall)
- Can germinate seeds under controlled conditions
- Can test and amend soil
- Can design a garden layout with rotation and companion planting
- Can preserve food by 3+ methods
- Can care for chickens (daily and weekly routines)
- Can save seeds from 5+ varieties
- Successfully managed a personal garden plot from planning through harvest
