# Building Band (Grades 3-5, Ages 8-11) — Science

## Overview
Building Band science transitions students from nature observation to systematic investigation. Students learn the scientific method formally, conduct controlled experiments, and begin understanding the physical and chemical principles that govern the world. Every unit includes hands-on experiments using available materials — science is done, not just learned.

---

## Grade 3: Life Science, Earth Science, and Physical Science Foundations

### Unit 1: Plant and Animal Adaptations (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Prerequisites**: Foundation science — basic plant/animal knowledge, observation skills
**Materials**: Local plants and animals for observation, nature journals
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding adaptations helps with agriculture (selecting hardy crops), animal management, and ecological awareness

#### Learning Objectives
- Define adaptation as a feature that helps an organism survive in its environment
- Identify 5+ plant adaptations (thorns, deep roots, waxy leaves, seed dispersal methods)
- Identify 5+ animal adaptations (camouflage, hibernation, migration, thick fur, sharp claws)
- Explain how the same need (avoiding predators, finding food) is solved differently by different species

#### Teacher Notes
Adaptations are visible evidence of evolution — organisms that fit their environment survive. At this age, focus on the observable: "Why does this cactus have thick stems? Why does this rabbit change color in winter?" Let students discover patterns before introducing vocabulary.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Is an Adaptation?**
- Activity: Compare a fish and a bird. "Fish have gills — adapted for water. Birds have wings — adapted for air. Each is perfectly suited to its environment." Examine local plants — why do some have thorns? Deep roots? Waxy leaves?
- Key Concepts: Adaptation = feature that helps survive. Environment shapes organisms.

**Lesson 3-4: Plant Adaptations**
- Activity: Collect and compare 5+ local plants. Identify adaptations. "This plant has thick leaves to store water. This one has broad leaves to catch light. This one has thorns to stop animals eating it."
- Practice: Draw and label adaptations in nature journal

**Lesson 5-6: Animal Adaptations**
- Activity: Study community and local animals. Chickens: beaks for pecking grain. Dogs: keen sense of smell. Rabbits: large ears for hearing predators, fast legs for escaping.
- Hands-on: Camouflage experiment — hide colored paper pieces in grass. Which colors are found last? That's camouflage.

**Lesson 7-8: Comparing Solutions**
- Activity: "Both rabbits and turtles need to avoid predators. Rabbits run fast. Turtles have shells. Different solution, same problem." Create a comparison chart of how different animals solve the same survival challenge.

#### Assessment
- Can define adaptation and give 5 examples (plant and animal)
- Can explain how a specific adaptation helps survival
- Can compare how different organisms solve the same survival challenge

### Unit 2: Life Cycles (Weeks 9-16)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Caterpillars/larvae (if available), seeds, magnifying glass
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding life cycles is essential for agriculture (when to plant, harvest, save seeds), animal husbandry (breeding cycles), and pest management

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe complete and incomplete metamorphosis
- Diagram the life cycle of a plant (seed → sprout → mature plant → flower → seed)
- Diagram the life cycle of at least 2 animals (insect, amphibian, bird, or mammal)
- Understand that life cycles repeat — this is how species continue

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Plant Life Cycles (Review and Extend)**
- Activity: From Foundation science, students know basic plant life cycle. Now add detail: pollination (how does pollen get from one flower to another?), seed dispersal (wind, water, animal, explosion).
- Experiment: Plant seeds from different dispersal types. Which germinates fastest?

**Lesson 3-4: Insect Metamorphosis**
- Activity: If available, raise caterpillars through metamorphosis (or observe in nature). Egg → larva → pupa → adult. "The caterpillar completely reconstructs itself inside the chrysalis."
- Compare: Incomplete metamorphosis (grasshopper: egg → nymph → adult — looks like a small adult).

**Lesson 5-6: Bird and Mammal Life Cycles**
- Activity: Observe community chickens through their cycle: egg → chick → hen/rooster. Compare to mammals: gestation → birth → nursing → juvenile → adult.
- Key Concepts: Different animals develop differently, but all reproduce to continue their species.

**Lesson 7-8: The Cycle Continues**
- Activity: Draw complete life cycles for 3 different organisms. Identify the stage where the organism reproduces. "If this stage is disrupted, the whole cycle breaks."
- Survival connection: "This is why we save seeds and protect breeding animals."

### Unit 3: Magnets and Compass (Weeks 17-22)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Magnets (or magnetized needles), compass, iron filings or small nails, water bowl
**Survival Relevance**: Compass navigation is a critical survival skill. Understanding magnetism leads to understanding generators and motors.

#### Learning Objectives
- Demonstrate that magnets attract certain metals (iron, steel) and have two poles
- Demonstrate that like poles repel and opposite poles attract
- Explain that Earth is a giant magnet
- Build a simple compass from a magnetized needle floating in water
- Use a compass to determine cardinal directions

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Magnets Explore**
- Activity: Test magnets against various objects. What sticks? Metal door hinge: yes. Wooden table: no. Aluminum can: no. Iron nail: yes. "Magnets attract iron and steel, not all metals."

**Lesson 3-4: Poles and Forces**
- Activity: Bring two magnets together. "Feel that pull? Now flip one around. Feel that push? North pushes north away. North pulls south toward it. Opposites attract."

**Lesson 5-6: Earth's Magnetism and DIY Compass**
- Activity: Magnetize a needle by stroking it with a magnet in one direction 50 times. Float on a small leaf in still water. It points north. "Earth itself is magnetic. The needle follows Earth's magnetic field."
- Practice: Compare DIY compass to real compass. Use compass to find north, south, east, west.

### Unit 4: States of Matter and Changes (Weeks 23-28)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Water, heat source, containers, ice (if available), candle
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding state changes is foundation for water purification (boiling, distillation), food preservation, and metalworking

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify three states of matter (solid, liquid, gas) and their properties
- Explain what causes state changes (adding or removing heat energy)
- Demonstrate melting, freezing, evaporation, condensation, and boiling
- Understand that matter is not created or destroyed when it changes state

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Three States**
- Activity: Ice (solid), water (liquid), steam (gas). "Same substance, three forms. What's different? Solids hold their shape. Liquids flow. Gases spread out to fill any space."

**Lesson 3-4: Changing States**
- Activity: Heat ice → water → steam. Cool steam (cold plate over boiling pot) → water → freeze overnight. Track temperature changes through the process.
- Key Concepts: Heat makes particles move faster (solid → liquid → gas). Cooling slows them down.

**Lesson 5-6: Survival Applications**
- Activity: "Boiling water (changing liquid to gas and back) is how we purify water — germs die at boiling temperature, and distillation leaves impurities behind." "Melting metal (solid to liquid) is how we make tools."

### Unit 5: Soil Science (Weeks 29-34)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Soil samples from 3+ locations, jars, water, magnifying glass, plant pots
**Survival Relevance**: Soil is the foundation of all agriculture. Understanding soil composition determines crop success.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify soil components: sand, silt, clay, organic matter (humus)
- Perform a jar test to determine soil composition
- Understand how soil type affects water drainage and plant growth
- Explain how composting creates healthy soil
- Test soil from community garden areas

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What's in Soil?**
- Activity: Examine soil under magnifying glass. Find sand (gritty, visible grains), silt (smooth, fine), clay (sticky when wet, hard when dry), organic matter (decomposed plants, dark color).

**Lesson 3-4: Jar Test**
- Activity: Put soil in a jar with water, shake vigorously, let settle for 24 hours. Sand settles first (bottom), then silt, then clay (top). Organic matter floats. Measure layers. "This tells you what your soil is made of."

**Lesson 5-6: Soil and Plants**
- Activity: Plant same seeds in pure sand, pure clay, and garden soil. Water equally. Observe over 3 weeks. "Garden soil (mixture) grows best because it holds water but also drains."
- Connection to agriculture module: Composting adds organic matter, which improves any soil type.

### Unit 6: Weather Patterns (Weeks 35-40)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Thermometer, rain gauge, wind vane, barometer (if available), weather journal
**Survival Relevance**: Weather prediction is critical for agriculture planning, travel decisions, and disaster preparedness

#### Learning Objectives
- Record and analyze daily weather data over 6+ weeks
- Identify weather patterns (what conditions precede rain, cold fronts, heat waves)
- Understand the relationship between temperature, pressure, and weather
- Make simple weather predictions based on observations

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Weather Station**
- Activity: Set up or improve the class weather station. Record daily: temperature, cloud type, wind direction and speed estimate, precipitation. Chart on a large wall graph.

**Lesson 3-4: Weather Patterns**
- Activity: After 3 weeks of data, look for patterns. "What happened to the temperature before it rained? What cloud type preceded storms? What direction did the wind come from before cold weather?"

**Lesson 5-6: Prediction**
- Activity: Based on observations and patterns, make daily weather predictions. Check the next day. "Why were we right/wrong?" Discuss how farmers have predicted weather for thousands of years.

## Grade 4: Electricity, Human Body, Geology, and Ecosystems

### Unit 7: Electricity Basics (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Wire, batteries, small bulbs, switches, conductors/insulators samples
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding electricity is essential for maintaining community power systems, building circuits for radio/communication, and safely working with electrical infrastructure

#### Learning Objectives
- Build a complete simple circuit (battery + wire + bulb)
- Identify conductors (metals) and insulators (rubber, wood, plastic)
- Understand that electricity flows in a loop (circuit must be complete)
- Build a switch that opens and closes a circuit
- Understand basic electrical safety

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: What Is Electricity?**
- Activity: "Electricity is the flow of tiny particles called electrons through a conductor, like water flowing through a pipe." Demonstrate with a battery, wire, and bulb.

**Lesson 3-4: Building Circuits**
- Activity: Each student or pair builds a working circuit. Battery → wire → bulb → wire → battery. If any connection breaks, the light goes out. "Electricity needs a complete path."

**Lesson 5-6: Conductors and Insulators**
- Activity: Insert various materials into the circuit. Metal paperclip → light works (conductor). Wooden stick → light fails (insulator). Test 10+ materials. Chart results.

**Lesson 7-8: Switches**
- Activity: Build a switch from two metal strips that can be pressed together or separated. Insert into circuit. "A switch controls the flow — like a gate in a water channel."

**Lesson 9-10: Electrical Safety**
- Activity: "Electricity can kill. NEVER touch exposed wires. NEVER put anything into an electrical outlet. Water conducts electricity — never touch electrical equipment with wet hands."
- Connection: Visit community electrical systems with a knowledgeable adult.

### Unit 8: Human Body Systems (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Anatomical diagrams/drawings, stethoscope (if available)
**Survival Relevance**: Foundation for medical apprenticeship track. Understanding body systems enables first aid, health decisions, and communicating with medics.

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe the function of 6 body systems: skeletal, muscular, digestive, respiratory, circulatory, nervous
- Explain how systems work together
- Identify major organs and their functions
- Understand basic disease: what happens when a system doesn't work properly

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Skeletal and Muscular Systems**
- Activity: Feel your own bones. "206 bones form a framework. Muscles attach to bones and contract to make movement. Without bones: a blob. Without muscles: a statue."

**Lesson 3-4: Digestive System**
- Activity: Trace food from mouth through the digestive system. Teeth chew → esophagus → stomach (acid breaks food down) → intestines (nutrients absorbed) → waste eliminated. "Your body is a food processing machine."

**Lesson 5-6: Respiratory and Circulatory**
- Activity: "Breathe in — lungs fill with air. Oxygen crosses into your blood. Heart pumps blood through arteries to every cell. Blood picks up waste, returns through veins, waste breathed out as CO2."
- Demonstration: Listen to heartbeat with stethoscope. Count breaths per minute before and after exercise.

**Lesson 7-8: Nervous System**
- Activity: Reflex test (knee tap, catching a dropped ruler). "Your brain controls everything. Nerves carry signals like wires carry electricity. Some signals are automatic (reflexes) — they're faster because they don't go all the way to the brain."

**Lesson 9-10: Systems Working Together**
- Activity: "When you run: muscles contract (muscular), bones provide structure (skeletal), lungs breathe faster (respiratory), heart pumps harder (circulatory), brain coordinates it all (nervous), and you need food energy (digestive)."

### Unit 9: Rocks, Minerals, and Erosion (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Rock samples, streak plate, nail for hardness testing, vinegar
**Survival Relevance**: Identifying useful rocks and minerals for construction, tool-making, and understanding terrain

#### Learning Objectives
- Classify rocks into three types: igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic
- Test rocks for hardness, streak color, and acid reaction
- Understand the rock cycle (how rocks transform over time)
- Identify erosion and weathering and their effects on landscape
- Identify locally useful rocks and minerals

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Rock Types**
- Activity: Examine rock samples. Igneous: formed from cooled lava (often crystals or glassy). Sedimentary: formed in layers from compressed particles (often fossil-bearing). Metamorphic: changed by heat and pressure (often banded or folded).

**Lesson 3-4: Rock Testing**
- Activity: Test rocks — scratch with nail (hardness), streak on rough surface (streak color), drop vinegar (fizzes = limestone/calcite). "These tests help identify minerals without fancy equipment."

**Lesson 5-6: The Rock Cycle**
- Activity: Draw the rock cycle. Igneous → weathered to sediment → compressed to sedimentary → heated/pressured to metamorphic → melted to magma → cools to igneous. "This cycle takes millions of years."

**Lesson 7-8: Erosion and Weathering**
- Activity: Visit a stream bank — observe erosion. Find a cracked rock — freeze-thaw weathering. "Water, wind, ice, and roots break rocks apart over time. This creates soil."

### Unit 10: Food Webs and Ecosystems (Weeks 29-36)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Nature access, string/yarn for food web activity
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding ecosystems guides hunting/fishing sustainability, pest management, and environmental stewardship

#### Learning Objectives
- Build a food web showing energy flow in a local ecosystem
- Explain what happens when one species is removed from the web
- Distinguish producers, consumers (herbivores, carnivores, omnivores), and decomposers
- Understand that ecosystems are balanced — disruption causes cascading effects

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Energy Flow**
- Activity: "All energy starts with the sun. Plants capture it. Herbivores eat plants. Carnivores eat herbivores. Decomposers recycle everything." Build a food chain with string.

**Lesson 3-4: Food Webs**
- Activity: "Real ecosystems aren't chains — they're webs. A rabbit eats many plants. Many animals eat rabbits." Build a class food web with yarn connecting species. Each student holds one species card.

**Lesson 5-6: Removing a Species**
- Activity: Remove one species from the web. "What happens?" Pull the yarn. Everyone connected to that species feels the tug. "Removing one species affects many others."

**Lesson 7-8: Our Ecosystem**
- Activity: Map the local ecosystem. Identify producers, consumers, decomposers. "We are part of this web. Our farming, hunting, and waste all affect it."

### Unit 11: Scientific Method (Weeks 37-40)
**Duration**: 4 weeks
**Materials**: Experiment supplies (varies)
**Survival Relevance**: The scientific method is the most powerful problem-solving tool humans have ever developed. It works for any question.

#### Learning Objectives
- State the steps of the scientific method (question → hypothesis → experiment → data → conclusion)
- Design and conduct a simple controlled experiment
- Identify variables (what you change, what you measure, what you keep the same)
- Draw conclusions based on data

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1: Question and Hypothesis**
- Activity: "What makes plants grow faster — morning sun or afternoon sun?" Hypothesis: "I think ___ because ___."

**Lesson 2: Experiment Design**
- Activity: "What do we change? (Position.) What do we keep the same? (Water, soil, plant type.) What do we measure? (Height.)"

**Lesson 3-4: Conduct and Conclude**
- Activity: Run the experiment for 2 weeks. Record data. Graph results. "What does the data show? Does it support our hypothesis?"

## Grade 5: Chemistry, Simple Machines, Astronomy, and Energy

### Unit 12: Introduction to Chemistry (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Baking soda, vinegar, salt, water, iron nails, various household/natural substances
**Survival Relevance**: Chemistry underlies food preservation, water purification, soap making, medicine, and materials science

#### Learning Objectives
- Distinguish mixtures from solutions from chemical reactions
- Identify signs of a chemical reaction (gas production, temperature change, color change, precipitate)
- Conduct 5+ chemical reactions using available materials
- Understand that chemical reactions create new substances with different properties

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Mixtures**
- Activity: Mix sand and salt. "Is this a new substance? No — we can separate them (dissolve salt in water, filter out sand, evaporate water to recover salt)." Mixture = substances together but not chemically changed.

**Lesson 3-4: Solutions**
- Activity: Dissolve salt in water. "The salt disappears — but is it gone? No. Taste it. Evaporate and the salt returns." Solution = one substance dissolved in another.

**Lesson 5-6: Chemical Reactions**
- Activity: Baking soda + vinegar = gas bubbles (CO2), new substance (sodium acetate). Iron nail in water = rust over days (iron + oxygen = iron oxide). "A chemical reaction creates something NEW."

**Lesson 7-8: Fermentation**
- Activity: Mix yeast, warm water, and sugar. Observe gas production. "Yeast is eating sugar and producing CO2 gas and alcohol. This is fermentation — it's how bread rises and how alcohol is made."
- Connection: Food preservation through fermentation (sauerkraut, yogurt, pickles).

**Lesson 9-10: Applied Chemistry**
- Activity: "Chemistry is how we make soap (fat + ash water = soap), preserve food (salt draws out water), purify water (boiling kills germs, filtration removes particles), and will eventually make medicines."

### Unit 13: Simple Machines in Depth (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Levers, pulleys, inclined planes, wheels, screws — from community workshop
**Survival Relevance**: Every tool and construction technique uses simple machines. Understanding mechanical advantage means doing more work with less effort.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify and explain all 6 simple machines (lever, inclined plane, wedge, screw, wheel/axle, pulley)
- Calculate simple mechanical advantage (effort × distance = load × distance)
- Build compound machines (combinations of simple machines)
- Design and build a device to solve a real community problem

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: The Six Simple Machines**
- Activity: For each machine, demonstrate, identify in the community, and measure mechanical advantage.
- Lever: pry bar, wheelbarrow, scissors. "A longer lever arm means less effort."
- Inclined plane: ramp, road up a hill. "Going up a longer ramp is easier than climbing straight up."
- Wedge: axe, knife, doorstop. "A wedge converts downward force into sideways splitting force."
- Screw: bolt, drill bit, jar lid. "A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder."
- Wheel and axle: wheelbarrow, crank, doorknob. "The wheel multiplies force."
- Pulley: well bucket, clothesline, flagpole. "A pulley changes the direction of force. Multiple pulleys multiply force."

**Lesson 5-6: Mechanical Advantage**
- Activity: Measure! "How much weight can you lift with your hands? Now use a lever. Now use a pulley. How much more?" Simple calculation: if a lever arm is 3× longer, you need 1/3 the effort.

**Lesson 7-8: Compound Machines**
- Activity: "A bicycle is wheel/axle + lever + pulley (chain). A wheelbarrow is lever + wheel/axle." Identify compound machines in the community.

**Lesson 9-10: Design Challenge**
- Activity: Identify a real community problem that simple machines could solve. Design and build a solution. Present to the class and community.

### Unit 14: Earth's Place in the Solar System (Weeks 21-28)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Balls of different sizes (for scale model), flashlight, globe (or ball)
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding the solar system explains seasons, day/night, lunar cycles (planting calendars), tides, and celestial navigation

#### Learning Objectives
- Name and order the 8 planets
- Explain why we have day/night (Earth's rotation), seasons (Earth's tilt), and moon phases
- Build a scale model of the solar system
- Understand how celestial patterns are used for navigation and agriculture

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Scale Model**
- Activity: Build a scale model of the solar system. Even the smallest accurate model stretches across a large field. "Space is mostly empty. The distances are almost unimaginably large."

**Lesson 3-4: Day, Night, and Seasons**
- Activity: Use a globe and flashlight. Rotate the globe — half is lit, half is dark (day/night). Tilt the globe — when the northern hemisphere tilts toward the light, it gets more direct sunlight (summer). Tilt away = winter.

**Lesson 5-6: Moon Phases**
- Activity: Track the moon nightly for a month. Draw its shape each night. "The moon doesn't change shape — we see different amounts of the lit side as it orbits Earth."
- Connection: "Many planting calendars use moon phases. This is traditional knowledge."

**Lesson 7-8: Navigation by Sky**
- Activity: "Before compasses and GPS, people navigated by stars. The North Star (Polaris) is always due north. The Southern Cross points south." Night observation if possible.
- Connection: Links to Geography/Navigation curriculum.

### Unit 15: Water Cycle and Watersheds (Weeks 29-34)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Containers, heat source, soil trays, water
**Survival Relevance**: Water is the most critical survival resource. Understanding the water cycle and watersheds determines water strategy.

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain the complete water cycle including groundwater
- Define watershed and identify the local watershed
- Understand groundwater, water table, springs, and wells
- Explain how human activity affects water quality

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Complete Water Cycle**
- Activity: Expand Foundation water cycle to include: infiltration (water soaking into ground), groundwater flow, transpiration (plants releasing water vapor), and runoff.

**Lesson 3-4: Groundwater**
- Activity: Pour water on different soil types in a tray. "Where does the water go? It soaks in — this is how groundwater forms. A well taps into this underground water."

**Lesson 5-6: Our Watershed**
- Activity: "A watershed is the area where all water drains to the same place." Map the local watershed. Where does rain go? What streams flow where? Where does our water ultimately end up?
- "What we do upstream affects everyone downstream."

### Unit 16: Energy Types and Transformations (Weeks 35-40)
**Duration**: 6 weeks
**Materials**: Springs, balls, candle, solar panel (if available), magnifying glass
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding energy types and transformations is the foundation for power generation, heating, cooling, and efficient resource use

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify 5 forms of energy: kinetic (motion), potential (stored), thermal (heat), light, sound
- Demonstrate energy transformations (potential → kinetic, chemical → thermal, etc.)
- Understand that energy is conserved — it changes form but is never created or destroyed
- Connect energy concepts to community energy systems

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Energy Types**
- Activity: Identify each type in the environment. "A ball at the top of a hill has potential energy. Rolling, it has kinetic energy. A fire converts chemical energy to thermal and light energy."

**Lesson 3-4: Transformations**
- Activity: Drop a ball (potential → kinetic → sound when it hits). Rub hands together (kinetic → thermal). Burn wood (chemical → thermal + light). "Energy always changes from one form to another."

**Lesson 5-6: Community Energy**
- Activity: Map energy flow in the community. "Where does our energy come from? Wood? Solar panels? Water? How is it transformed into useful work?"
- Connection: "Understanding energy is key to building better systems."

#### Assessment (Grade 5 Overall)
- Can conduct a controlled experiment using the scientific method
- Can identify chemical reactions and their signs
- Can identify, explain, and calculate mechanical advantage for all 6 simple machines
- Can explain day/night, seasons, and moon phases
- Can describe the water cycle including groundwater
- Can identify energy forms and transformations
- Completed a design challenge using simple machines
