# Advanced Band (Grades 9-12, Ages 14-18) — Leadership

## Overview
Leadership education prepares students for community governance and project management. The curriculum progresses from self-management through group dynamics and organizational leadership to a capstone practicum leading a real community project.

## Grade 9: Self-Management

### Unit 1: Personal Leadership (Weeks 1-18)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: Journals, planning templates, project materials
**Survival Relevance**: Before you can lead others, you must lead yourself. Self-management skills (goal setting, time management, emotional regulation) are the foundation of all leadership.

#### Learning Objectives
- Set and pursue meaningful goals using structured methods (SMART goals)
- Manage time and priorities effectively
- Demonstrate emotional self-regulation under stress
- Work effectively as a team member before leading teams

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Goal Setting**
- Activity: SMART goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound. Set academic, personal, and community goals. Create action plans with milestones. Track progress weekly.
- Practice: Set and pursue 3 SMART goals over the semester with weekly tracking

**Lesson 5-8: Time and Priority Management**
- Activity: Eisenhower matrix: Urgent/Important (do now), Not Urgent/Important (schedule), Urgent/Not Important (delegate), Not Urgent/Not Important (eliminate). Apply to daily and weekly planning.
- Practice: Use priority management for 4 weeks and reflect on effectiveness

**Lesson 9-12: Emotional Intelligence**
- Activity: Self-awareness (recognizing your emotions and their triggers), self-regulation (managing emotions constructively), empathy (understanding others' emotions), social skills (building relationships, resolving conflicts).
- Practice: Keep an emotional intelligence journal for 4 weeks

**Lesson 13-18: Teamwork**
- Activity: Complete a significant team project (community improvement, curriculum contribution, infrastructure project). Practice: role negotiation, task distribution, conflict resolution, mutual accountability, project completion.
- Practice: Complete a team project and reflect on team dynamics

#### Assessment
- Achieve at least 2 of 3 SMART goals with documented progress
- Demonstrate effective time and priority management
- Complete a team project with documented reflection on teamwork

### Unit 2: Basic Project Management (Weeks 19-34)
**Duration**: 16 weeks
**Materials**: Planning templates, project resources
**Survival Relevance**: Every community improvement requires project management — defined scope, resources, timeline, and accountability

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Project Planning**
- Activity: Define scope (what are we doing and NOT doing?), identify deliverables, create work breakdown structure (big tasks → smaller tasks), estimate resources and time, identify risks.
- Practice: Plan a real community project with complete documentation

**Lesson 5-8: Project Execution**
- Activity: Task assignment, progress tracking, status reporting, change management (when the plan needs to change), problem-solving (when things go wrong).
- Practice: Execute the planned project with weekly status reports

**Lesson 9-12: Project Completion**
- Activity: Quality verification, stakeholder acceptance, documentation, lessons learned. "What went well? What went poorly? What would we do differently?"
- Practice: Complete the project and deliver lessons learned report

**Lesson 13-16: Evaluation**
- Activity: Did we achieve what we set out to do? On time? Within resources? What was the community impact? Self-evaluation and peer evaluation.
- Practice: Complete project evaluation and present to class

#### Assessment
- Plan, execute, and complete a real community project
- Maintain project documentation throughout
- Deliver a lessons learned report with actionable insights

## Grade 10: Group Dynamics and Decision-Making

### Unit 3: Group Dynamics and Conflict Resolution (Weeks 1-18)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: Discussion space, simulation materials, case studies
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding group dynamics enables effective meetings, prevents destructive conflict, and builds the social cohesion communities need to survive

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze group dynamics (forming, storming, norming, performing)
- Facilitate productive meetings and discussions
- Resolve conflicts using principled negotiation and mediation
- Build consensus without suppressing legitimate dissent

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Group Development**
- Activity: Tuckman's stages: Forming (polite, cautious), Storming (conflict, power struggles), Norming (establishing rules, building trust), Performing (productive teamwork). Identify what stage your groups are in and what helps them progress.
- Practice: Analyze 5 group situations and identify developmental stages

**Lesson 5-8: Meeting Facilitation**
- Activity: Agenda design (clear objectives, time allocation, decision method for each item). Facilitation skills (keeping discussion on track, ensuring all voices heard, managing dominant personalities, summarizing and confirming decisions). Practice facilitating real class and community meetings.
- Practice: Facilitate at least 3 meetings with post-meeting evaluation

**Lesson 9-12: Conflict Resolution**
- Activity: Conflict styles (competing, accommodating, avoiding, compromising, collaborating — each has appropriate uses). Principled negotiation (separate people from problems, focus on interests not positions, generate options, use objective criteria). Mediation skills (neutral third party helps disputants find their own solution).
- Practice: Mediate 3 real or simulated conflicts

**Lesson 13-18: Consensus Building**
- Activity: Consensus doesn't mean unanimity — it means everyone can live with the decision. Techniques: gradients of agreement (1=enthusiastic to 5=can't live with it), fist-to-five voting, modified consensus (discuss until no one objects, vs discuss until everyone agrees). When to use consensus and when majority rule is more appropriate.
- Practice: Build consensus on a real community issue with documented process

#### Assessment
- Analyze group dynamics using appropriate frameworks
- Facilitate productive meetings
- Resolve conflicts using principled methods
- Build consensus on a real issue

### Unit 4: Decision-Making and Communication (Weeks 19-34)
**Duration**: 16 weeks
**Materials**: Case studies, communication training materials
**Survival Relevance**: Good decisions under pressure and clear communication save lives, prevent waste, and build community trust

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-6: Decision-Making Frameworks**
- Activity: Rational decision-making (define problem → identify options → evaluate → decide → implement → evaluate). Decision-making under uncertainty (expected value, worst-case analysis, reversibility). Group decision-making (when to decide alone, when to consult, when to delegate, when to seek consensus).
- Practice: Apply frameworks to 10 real or simulated community decisions

**Lesson 7-12: Communication Skills**
- Activity: Active listening (paraphrase, ask clarifying questions, reflect emotions). Giving and receiving feedback (specific, timely, behavioral, constructive). Difficult conversations (addressing performance, delivering bad news, managing expectations). Cross-cultural communication.
- Practice: Practice communication skills in structured exercises and real interactions

**Lesson 13-16: Crisis Communication**
- Activity: Communication during emergencies: clear, concise, calm. Briefing format (situation, action taken, resources needed, next steps). Debriefing format (what happened, what went well, what to improve). Community communication during uncertainty (honest, transparent, timely).
- Practice: Lead communication during simulated crisis scenarios

#### Assessment
- Apply decision-making frameworks to community challenges
- Demonstrate advanced communication skills
- Lead communication during a simulated crisis

## Grade 11: Organizational Leadership

### Unit 5: Teaching, Mentoring, and Strategic Planning (Weeks 1-18)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: Teaching materials, planning frameworks, community access
**Survival Relevance**: Teaching ensures knowledge transfer. Mentoring develops future leaders. Strategic planning prevents communities from drifting toward crisis.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-6: Teaching and Mentoring**
- Activity: Learning theory: how people learn (see, hear, do, teach). Lesson design for different audiences. Mentoring relationships (not just teaching skills — developing whole people). Each student mentors a younger student for the semester.
- Practice: Mentor a younger student and document the relationship and outcomes

**Lesson 7-12: Crisis Management**
- Activity: Crisis types (natural disaster, security threat, epidemic, economic shock, internal conflict). Crisis leadership: assess, decide, communicate, act, adapt. After-action review. Building organizational resilience.
- Practice: Lead response to 3 simulated crises with after-action reviews

**Lesson 13-18: Strategic Planning**
- Activity: Strategic planning for the community: vision (where do we want to be in 10 years?), mission (what do we do?), environmental scan (opportunities and threats), strategic goals, action plans, accountability. Facilitate a real strategic planning session with community input.
- Practice: Facilitate a strategic planning session and produce a strategic plan

#### Assessment
- Mentor a younger student with documented outcomes
- Lead crisis response with after-action reviews
- Facilitate strategic planning and produce a community strategic plan

---

### Unit 6: Organizational Design (Weeks 19-34)
**Duration**: 16 weeks
**Materials**: Organizational design materials, community governance documents
**Survival Relevance**: How a community is organized determines its effectiveness, fairness, and resilience

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-6: Organizational Structures**
- Activity: Hierarchical (clear chain of command, fast decisions, bottleneck risk), flat (distributed authority, slower decisions, more input), matrix (dual reporting, flexible, complex), network (loosely connected, highly adaptive, coordination challenges). Which structure suits which community needs?
- Practice: Analyze the community's organizational structure and recommend improvements

**Lesson 7-12: Governance Design**
- Activity: Decision rights (who decides what?), accountability (who is responsible for outcomes?), transparency (who gets what information?), succession (how are leaders replaced?), checks and balances (how is power limited?). Cross-reference with History curriculum governance analysis.
- Practice: Design or improve a community governance element

**Lesson 13-16: Change Management**
- Activity: Why change fails (lack of urgency, poor communication, insufficient support, no plan). How to lead change (create urgency, build coalition, communicate vision, empower action, generate quick wins, sustain momentum, anchor in culture).
- Practice: Lead a real organizational change in a community team or committee

#### Assessment
- Analyze and improve organizational structures
- Design governance elements with accountability and transparency
- Lead an organizational change process

## Grade 12: Leadership Practicum

### Unit 7: Capstone — Lead a Community Project (Full Year)
**Duration**: Full academic year
**Materials**: Project-dependent
**Survival Relevance**: This is the final test of leadership — can you lead a significant community project from planning through execution to completion?

#### Structure
Each student leads a significant project:
- **Infrastructure Project**: Plan, organize, and lead construction of a community facility
- **Community Program**: Design and launch a new community program (education, health, cultural)
- **Economic Initiative**: Establish a new production, trade, or service operation
- **Governance Improvement**: Research, propose, and implement a governance reform
- **Student's Proposal**: Any significant project approved by teacher and community leadership

#### Requirements
- Project planning (scope, resources, timeline, risk assessment)
- Team recruitment and management
- Stakeholder communication and buy-in
- Execution with documented decision-making
- Problem-solving when things go wrong (they will)
- Project completion and evaluation
- Community presentation of results
- Mentoring of at least 2 younger students throughout
- Leadership journal reflecting on decisions, challenges, and growth

#### Assessment
- Project completed meeting its stated objectives
- Team members report positive leadership experience
- Community leadership evaluates the project's value
- Leadership journal demonstrates reflective growth
- Younger students mentored report learning and development
