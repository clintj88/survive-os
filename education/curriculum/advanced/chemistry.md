# Advanced Band (Grades 9-12, Ages 14-18) — Chemistry

## Overview
Chemistry provides the molecular understanding behind water treatment, food preservation, medicine, materials science, and energy production. The curriculum progresses from atomic theory through reactions and organic chemistry to applied chemistry directly serving community needs.

## Grade 9: Foundations

### Unit 1: Atomic Structure and the Periodic Table (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Periodic table, molecular model kits (or clay/toothpick models), element samples when available
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding elements and their properties enables identification of useful materials, understanding of toxicity, and foundational knowledge for all applied chemistry

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe atomic structure (protons, neutrons, electrons, shells)
- Read and interpret the periodic table (groups, periods, properties)
- Predict element properties from periodic table position
- Identify common elements and their survival applications

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-3: Atomic Structure**
- Activity: Build atom models — nucleus (protons + neutrons), electron shells. Atomic number = protons = identity. Mass number = protons + neutrons. Isotopes have different neutron counts. Ions have gained or lost electrons.
- Practice: Model 10 atoms and describe their structure

**Lesson 4-7: The Periodic Table**
- Activity: Groups (columns) share properties: Group 1 (reactive metals), Group 17 (halogens — disinfectants), Group 18 (noble gases — inert). Periods (rows) show increasing size. Metals (left), nonmetals (right), metalloids (border).
- Practice: Predict properties of 20 elements from their periodic table position

**Lesson 8-10: Survival-Relevant Elements**
- Activity: Iron (tools, construction), copper (electrical, plumbing), carbon (organic chemistry, fuel, filtration), chlorine (water disinfection), iodine (wound care, thyroid), sodium/potassium (nutrition), calcium (bones, mortar), nitrogen/phosphorus/potassium (fertilizer).
- Practice: Create element profiles for 15 survival-critical elements

#### Assessment
- Describe atomic structure for any element
- Predict properties from periodic table position
- Identify and describe uses of survival-critical elements

### Unit 2: Chemical Bonding and States of Matter (Weeks 11-20)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Molecular models, lab supplies for state-change demonstrations
**Survival Relevance**: Bonding determines material properties — why steel is strong, why salt dissolves, why oil and water don't mix

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain ionic, covalent, and metallic bonding
- Predict compound properties from bond type
- Describe states of matter at the molecular level
- Apply bonding knowledge to material selection and use

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Bonding Types**
- Activity: Ionic (electron transfer — NaCl: Na gives electron to Cl, opposites attract, forms crystal). Covalent (electron sharing — H₂O: oxygen shares electrons with two hydrogens). Metallic (electron sea — metals share electrons freely, conducts electricity).
- Practice: Predict bond type and properties for 20 compounds

**Lesson 5-7: Compound Properties**
- Activity: Ionic compounds: high melting point, dissolve in water, conduct electricity when dissolved. Covalent: lower melting point, many don't dissolve in water, don't conduct. Metallic: conduct heat and electricity, malleable, ductile.
- Practice: Given a compound's properties, predict bond type and vice versa

**Lesson 8-10: States of Matter**
- Activity: Solid (fixed shape and volume, molecules vibrate in place), liquid (fixed volume, takes container shape, molecules slide past each other), gas (fills container, molecules move freely). Phase changes: melting, boiling, condensation, freezing, sublimation. Energy determines state.
- Practice: Explain phase changes at the molecular level for 5 substances

#### Assessment
- Explain three bonding types with examples
- Predict compound properties from bonding
- Describe states of matter at the molecular level

### Unit 3: Mixtures and Solutions (Weeks 21-30)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Lab supplies for dissolution, filtration, and distillation experiments
**Survival Relevance**: Water purification, medicine preparation, food processing, and material extraction all involve mixtures and solutions

#### Learning Objectives
- Distinguish mixtures from compounds
- Understand solution chemistry (solute, solvent, concentration, saturation)
- Perform separation techniques (filtration, distillation, evaporation, chromatography)
- Apply solution chemistry to water treatment and medicine preparation

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-3: Solutions**
- Activity: Dissolve salt in water — it disappears but the water tastes salty. The salt is the solute, water is the solvent. Concentration = amount of solute / amount of solution. Saturation = maximum solute that can dissolve at a given temperature.
- Practice: Prepare solutions of specific concentrations and test saturation limits

**Lesson 4-6: Separation Techniques**
- Activity: Filtration (separate solids from liquids — sand from water). Distillation (separate liquids by boiling point — purify water). Evaporation (recover dissolved solids — extract salt from seawater). Chromatography (separate dissolved components — identify plant compounds).
- Practice: Perform all four separation techniques on prepared mixtures

**Lesson 7-10: Applied Solutions**
- Activity: Water treatment: dissolving chlorine tablets (concentration matters — too little doesn't disinfect, too much is toxic). Oral rehydration solution (precise salt and sugar concentrations). Antiseptic preparation. Fertilizer mixing.
- Practice: Prepare 5 community-relevant solutions at correct concentrations

#### Assessment
- Prepare solutions of specified concentrations
- Perform separation techniques correctly
- Apply solution chemistry to community water treatment and medicine preparation

## Grade 10: Chemical Reactions and Organic Chemistry

### Unit 4: Chemical Reactions and Stoichiometry (Weeks 1-14)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Lab supplies for reaction demonstrations, safety equipment
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding reactions enables soap making, food preservation, rust prevention, water treatment, and fire management

#### Learning Objectives
- Balance chemical equations
- Classify reaction types (synthesis, decomposition, single replacement, double replacement, combustion)
- Calculate quantities using stoichiometry (moles, molar mass, mole ratios)
- Apply reaction chemistry to practical problems

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Reaction Types and Balancing**
- Activity: Demonstrate each reaction type. Synthesis: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (hydrogen burns). Decomposition: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ (hydrogen peroxide decomposes). Single replacement: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu. Double replacement: NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O. Combustion: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O.
- Practice: Balance 30 equations and classify by type

**Lesson 5-8: Stoichiometry**
- Activity: Moles = mass / molar mass. Mole ratios from balanced equations predict quantities. "How many grams of NaOH do we need to neutralize 100g of HCl?" Convert to moles → use ratio → convert back.
- Practice: Solve 20 stoichiometry problems with practical applications

**Lesson 9-12: Acids, Bases, and pH**
- Activity: Acids donate H⁺ (vinegar, citric acid, stomach acid). Bases accept H⁺ (baking soda, lye, soap). pH scale: 0-14, 7 is neutral, below 7 is acid, above 7 is base. Neutralization: acid + base → salt + water.
- Practice: Test pH of 15 common substances, perform neutralization reactions

**Lesson 13-14: Applied Reactions**
- Activity: Soap making (fat + lye → soap + glycerin). Rust prevention (understanding oxidation). Fermentation chemistry (sugar → alcohol + CO₂). Water treatment chemistry (chlorination, pH adjustment).
- Practice: Make soap, demonstrate rust prevention, and explain water treatment chemistry

#### Assessment
- Balance equations and classify reactions
- Solve stoichiometry problems with practical applications
- Explain pH and perform acid-base reactions
- Apply reaction chemistry to soap making or water treatment

### Unit 5: Organic Chemistry and Electrochemistry (Weeks 15-28)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Organic chemistry models, electrochemistry demonstration supplies
**Survival Relevance**: Organic chemistry explains food, fuel, medicine, and materials. Electrochemistry explains batteries, corrosion, and metal extraction.

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify functional groups in organic molecules
- Explain organic reactions relevant to daily life (combustion, fermentation, polymerization)
- Understand electrochemical cells (batteries, corrosion, electrolysis)
- Apply organic and electrochemistry to community needs

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Organic Chemistry Basics**
- Activity: Carbon forms 4 bonds — the basis of organic chemistry. Hydrocarbons (fuel), alcohols (solvents, fuel, antiseptic), carboxylic acids (vinegar), esters (flavors, fragrances), amines and amides (proteins). Functional groups determine properties.
- Practice: Identify functional groups in 20 common organic molecules

**Lesson 5-8: Organic Reactions in Daily Life**
- Activity: Combustion (burning fuel: hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy). Fermentation (sugar → ethanol + CO₂, or sugar → lactic acid). Saponification (soap making). Polymerization (how plastics form). Decomposition (organic matter → compost).
- Practice: Explain the chemistry behind 5 daily community processes

**Lesson 9-12: Electrochemistry**
- Activity: Build a voltaic cell (battery) from a lemon, copper, and zinc (or similar). Explain: oxidation (electron loss) at the anode, reduction (electron gain) at the cathode. Corrosion is an electrochemical process — rust is iron oxidation. Electrolysis reverses the process (using electricity to drive a reaction).
- Practice: Build electrochemical cells, demonstrate corrosion, describe battery chemistry

**Lesson 13-14: Applied Electrochemistry**
- Activity: Battery maintenance and testing. Corrosion prevention for metal tools and structures. Electroplating concept. Metal extraction via electrolysis (aluminum from ore, for example).
- Practice: Maintain community batteries and implement corrosion prevention on metal tools

#### Assessment
- Identify organic functional groups and predict properties
- Explain the chemistry behind community processes
- Build and explain electrochemical cells
- Apply corrosion prevention and battery maintenance

## Grade 11: Applied Chemistry

### Unit 6: Practical Chemistry for Community Needs (Full Year)
**Duration**: Full year
**Materials**: Lab supplies, raw materials for production, safety equipment
**Survival Relevance**: This is the most directly applicable year — every unit produces something the community needs

#### Topics (each 4-6 weeks)

**Soap Making**: Fat + lye → soap + glycerin. Calculate lye amounts for different fats (saponification values). Hot process vs cold process. Additives (herbs, scent, abrasive). Quality testing.

**Water Treatment Chemistry**: Chlorination (dosing calculations), pH adjustment, flocculation (alum or moringa seeds), activated carbon filtration chemistry, water testing (hardness, dissolved solids, contaminants). Design a complete water treatment protocol.

**Food Preservation Chemistry**: Fermentation (lacto-fermentation pH requirements, alcohol fermentation), pickling (acetic acid concentration for safety), smoking (antimicrobial compounds in smoke), salt curing (osmotic pressure draws water from bacteria), sugar preservation (jams, syrups).

**Fuel Production**: Biodiesel basics (transesterification of vegetable oil), ethanol distillation (fermentation + distillation), charcoal production (pyrolysis), biogas (anaerobic digestion). Energy content comparison.

**Dye Chemistry**: Mordanting theory (metal salt bonding), natural dye extraction, color fastness testing, pH indicators from plants (red cabbage juice changes color with pH — science AND practical testing tool).

**Adhesive and Sealant Chemistry**: Hide glue (collagen extraction), plant-based adhesives (pine resin, birch bark tar), waterproofing (beeswax, tallow, pine pitch).

**Metal Extraction and Treatment**: Smelting concept (reducing metal ore with carbon), heat treatment of steel (hardening, tempering — crystal structure changes), patina and surface protection.

#### Assessment
- Produce at least 3 useful community products using chemistry
- Explain the chemistry behind each product
- Design a production protocol with quality control measures
- Maintain a lab notebook documenting all work

## Grade 12: Pharmacology, Toxicology, and Analytical Chemistry

### Unit 7: Pharmacology and Toxicology (Weeks 1-16)
**Duration**: 16 weeks
**Materials**: Reference materials, medicinal plants, chemistry supplies for extraction
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding how medicines and poisons work at the molecular level enables safer community medical practice

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-6: Pharmacology**
- Activity: Drug classes and mechanisms: analgesics (pain — aspirin blocks prostaglandins), antibiotics (target bacterial processes humans don't have), antipyretics (reduce fever), antihistamines (block allergic response), cardiovascular drugs, gastrointestinal drugs. For each: mechanism, indications, contraindications, dosing principles.
- Practice: Create a community drug reference for all available medications

**Lesson 7-10: Toxicology**
- Activity: Common poisons and their mechanisms: heavy metals (lead, mercury — enzyme disruption), plant toxins (alkaloids, glycosides), animal venoms, foodborne toxins (botulinum, aflatoxin). Dose-response relationships: "the dose makes the poison." Antidotes and supportive care.
- Practice: Create a toxicology reference for community-relevant poisons with first aid protocols

**Lesson 11-16: Pharmaceutical Chemistry**
- Activity: Extraction of active compounds from plants (tinctures, decoctions, poultices). Concentration calculations. Shelf life and storage. Quality control (consistency, contamination prevention). Documentation requirements.
- Practice: Prepare herbal medicines with proper documentation and quality control

#### Assessment
- Explain drug mechanisms for major drug classes
- Identify common poisons and describe first aid
- Prepare herbal medicines with quality control documentation

### Unit 8: Analytical Chemistry and Materials Science (Weeks 17-34)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: Testing supplies, samples, reference standards
**Survival Relevance**: Testing water, soil, food, and materials ensures community safety and optimizes resource use

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-6: Water and Soil Testing**
- Activity: Test water for: pH, hardness, chlorine residual, turbidity, dissolved solids, coliform bacteria (if test kits available). Test soil for: pH, nitrogen/phosphorus/potassium, organic matter, texture. Interpret results and recommend actions.
- Practice: Complete testing protocols for community water and soil with written reports

**Lesson 7-10: Food Testing**
- Activity: Test food for: pH (preservation safety), moisture content (storage suitability), rancidity (fat degradation), contamination indicators. Develop quality control protocols for preserved foods.
- Practice: Establish food testing protocols for community preservation operations

**Lesson 11-14: Materials Science**
- Activity: Material properties and selection: tensile strength, hardness, corrosion resistance, thermal properties, electrical conductivity. How material structure determines properties. Material selection for community engineering projects.
- Practice: Test and compare materials for specific applications

**Lesson 15-18: Chemistry Capstone**
- Activity: Design and implement a chemistry-based solution for a community need: water treatment system, soap production line, food testing laboratory, pharmaceutical preparation protocol, or materials testing program. Complete documentation including protocols, safety procedures, and quality control.
- Practice: Present capstone project to community leadership with complete documentation

#### Assessment
- Perform analytical testing on water, soil, and food
- Recommend actions based on test results
- Complete a chemistry capstone project serving a community need
