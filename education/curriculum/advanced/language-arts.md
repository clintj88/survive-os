# Advanced Band (Grades 9-12, Ages 14-18) — Language Arts

## Overview
Advanced language arts prepares students for leadership — writing governance documents, producing technical manuals, leading public discourse, teaching others, and creating works of lasting value. By graduation, students should be capable writers, speakers, and critical thinkers whose communication skills serve the community.

## Grade 9: World Literature and Analytical Writing

### Unit 1: World Literature Survey (Weeks 1-12)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Available literary texts from diverse traditions
**Survival Relevance**: Literature from different cultures provides insight into universal human challenges — governance, justice, survival, love, loss, meaning — that directly inform community life

#### Learning Objectives
- Read and analyze literature from at least 5 cultural traditions
- Identify universal themes across culturally diverse works
- Write analytical essays with sophisticated thesis and evidence
- Engage in Socratic discussion demonstrating critical thinking

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-3: Ancient and Classical Literature**
- Texts: Selections from the Epic of Gilgamesh (Mesopotamia), The Odyssey (Greece), The Mahabharata (India), The Tale of Genji (Japan), Sundiata (West Africa), or comparable available texts
- Analysis: How do these works address mortality, leadership, duty, and the meaning of civilization?
- Practice: Write a comparative analysis connecting themes in two texts from different traditions

**Lesson 4-6: Poetry Across Cultures**
- Texts: Selections from Rumi (Persian), Li Bai/Du Fu (Chinese), Basho (Japanese haiku), Neruda (Chilean), Dickinson (American), oral poetry traditions (African, Indigenous)
- Analysis: How does poetic form vary across cultures? What makes poetry powerful regardless of form?
- Practice: Write poetry in 3 different traditions and analyze the craft choices

**Lesson 7-9: Drama and Narrative**
- Texts: Available plays or dramatic works from diverse traditions — Greek tragedy, Shakespeare, African folk drama, one-act plays
- Analysis: How does drama reveal character through action and dialogue? What makes conflict compelling?
- Practice: Write a critical analysis of a dramatic work focusing on how conflict drives character revelation

**Lesson 10-12: Literature and Philosophy**
- Texts: Works that blend literature and philosophical inquiry — available selections from Plato's dialogues, Voltaire, Dostoevsky, Chinua Achebe, or comparable texts
- Analysis: How does literature explore philosophical questions? When is fiction a better vehicle for ideas than argument?
- Practice: Write a 5-page analytical essay connecting a literary work to a philosophical question relevant to the community

#### Assessment
- Read and analyze works from 5+ cultural traditions
- Write 3 analytical essays with thesis, evidence, and analysis
- Participate in Socratic discussion demonstrating engagement with ideas

### Unit 2: Rhetoric and Persuasion (Weeks 13-22)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Speech texts, writing materials, debate space
**Survival Relevance**: Rhetoric is the art of effective public communication — essential for governance, diplomacy, teaching, negotiation, and leadership

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify and use rhetorical strategies (ethos, pathos, logos)
- Analyze great speeches for rhetorical technique
- Write and deliver persuasive speeches
- Engage in formal debate with evidence and logic

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-3: Rhetorical Analysis**
- Activity: Read speeches from diverse leaders. Identify: ethos (credibility/character), pathos (emotional appeal), logos (logical argument). Analyze how each speech builds its case.
- Practice: Write rhetorical analyses of 3 speeches identifying specific techniques

**Lesson 4-6: Speech Writing and Delivery**
- Activity: Write speeches on community issues using deliberate rhetorical strategies. Practice delivery: pacing, emphasis, eye contact, gestures, vocal variety. Record or observe, review, improve.
- Practice: Write and deliver 3 speeches, each emphasizing a different rhetorical strategy

**Lesson 7-8: Formal Debate**
- Activity: Structured debate format: opening statements (2 min), rebuttals (2 min), cross-examination (3 min), closing (1 min). Students debate community-relevant propositions. Judges score on argument quality, evidence, and rebuttal.
- Practice: Participate in 3 formal debates, arguing both sides of at least one issue

**Lesson 9-10: Public Speaking Portfolio**
- Activity: Compile a portfolio of speeches, debate performances, and rhetorical analyses. Deliver a capstone 10-minute speech on a topic of community significance.
- Practice: Deliver capstone speech and defend in Q&A

#### Assessment
- Analyze rhetoric in speeches from diverse traditions
- Deliver persuasive speeches with deliberate rhetorical strategies
- Participate in formal debates demonstrating evidence-based argumentation

## Grade 10: Research, Technical Writing, and Journalism

### Unit 3: Research Methodology (Weeks 1-10)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Available reference materials, community data, writing tools
**Survival Relevance**: Rigorous research methodology enables evidence-based decision-making for community governance, health, agriculture, and infrastructure

#### Learning Objectives
- Design a research study (question, hypothesis, method, data collection, analysis)
- Evaluate sources critically (reliability, bias, relevance, currency)
- Write a research paper with proper methodology section
- Present research findings to a non-specialist audience

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Research Design**
- Activity: Identify a community-relevant research question. Form a hypothesis. Design a study: what data will you collect? How? From whom? How will you analyze it? What are the limitations?
- Practice: Complete a research proposal with all elements

**Lesson 5-8: Data Collection and Analysis**
- Activity: Execute the study. Collect data through observation, measurement, interview, or records review. Analyze using appropriate methods (statistical if quantitative, thematic if qualitative). Draw conclusions.
- Practice: Collect and analyze data, noting limitations and unexpected findings

**Lesson 9-10: Research Paper and Presentation**
- Activity: Write a formal research paper: abstract, introduction, methods, results, discussion, conclusion. Present findings in a 15-minute presentation to class and community members.
- Practice: Submit paper and deliver presentation

#### Assessment
- Complete a research paper with proper methodology
- Present findings clearly to a non-specialist audience

### Unit 4: Technical Writing (Weeks 11-22)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Community systems/processes to document, writing and illustration tools
**Survival Relevance**: Technical documents preserve operational knowledge — without them, when the person who knows how to fix the water pump leaves, that knowledge is lost

#### Learning Objectives
- Write clear procedural documentation (step-by-step instructions)
- Create technical manuals (multiple procedures + troubleshooting + maintenance schedules)
- Draft formal reports (investigation findings, status reports, proposals)
- Test documentation by having non-experts follow it

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Procedural Writing**
- Activity: Document 5 community processes: water purification, radio check-in, medical supply inventory, garden irrigation, or other essential procedures. Each must be followable by someone who has never done the task.
- Practice: Write procedures, test on non-experts, revise based on testing

**Lesson 5-8: Technical Manual**
- Activity: Create a comprehensive technical manual for one community system (water system, radio system, medical supplies, agricultural operation). Include: overview, components, operating procedures, maintenance schedule, troubleshooting guide, safety warnings.
- Practice: Complete a manual and submit to the relevant community team for review

**Lesson 9-12: Formal Reports**
- Activity: Write 3 types of reports: investigation report (analyze a community problem), status report (current state of a project or system), proposal (recommend a community improvement with justification, cost, and implementation plan).
- Practice: Complete one of each report type on real community topics

#### Assessment
- Procedural documents tested and confirmed usable by non-experts
- Technical manual reviewed and accepted by the relevant community team
- Formal reports demonstrating clear analysis and recommendation

### Unit 5: Journalism and Community Communication (Weeks 23-34)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Writing and illustration tools, distribution method (bulletin board, handwritten copies, printed if possible)
**Survival Relevance**: Community journalism keeps everyone informed, holds leadership accountable, and provides a forum for ideas and concerns

#### Learning Objectives
- Write news reports (factual, balanced, sourced)
- Write opinion pieces (clearly labeled, well-argued)
- Edit others' writing for clarity and accuracy
- Produce a community publication on a regular schedule

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: News Reporting**
- Activity: Cover community events and decisions. Use journalistic structure: lead (most important info first), body (details, quotes, context), conclusion. Verify facts with multiple sources. Present all sides of controversial issues.
- Practice: Write 5 news articles on community topics

**Lesson 5-8: Opinion and Editorial**
- Activity: Write opinion pieces on community issues — clearly labeled as opinion, well-argued with evidence, acknowledging counterarguments. Write editorials representing the publication's position.
- Practice: Write 3 opinion pieces and 1 editorial

**Lesson 9-12: Publication Production**
- Activity: Produce a community newsletter/bulletin on a regular schedule. Manage: content planning, writing assignments, editing, layout, illustration, production, distribution. Build a team from the class.
- Practice: Produce at least 4 editions of a community publication

#### Assessment
- Produce professional-quality news articles and opinion pieces
- Edit others' work effectively
- Produce a community publication on schedule

## Grade 11: Advanced Composition, Document Drafting, and Teaching

### Unit 6: Advanced Composition and Literary Criticism (Weeks 1-12)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Extensive reading materials, writing tools
**Survival Relevance**: Advanced composition skills enable governance document drafting, diplomatic communication, and the creation of educational materials

#### Learning Objectives
- Write extended compositions integrating multiple sources, perspectives, and analytical frameworks
- Apply literary criticism approaches (historical, feminist, postcolonial, structuralist) as analytical tools
- Create curriculum materials for younger students
- Produce creative writing of publishable quality

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Extended Analytical Writing**
- Activity: Write a 10-page analytical paper on a substantive topic. Integrate 5+ sources, address counterarguments, develop a nuanced thesis that acknowledges complexity.
- Practice: Draft, workshop, revise, submit

**Lesson 5-8: Literary Criticism as Tools**
- Activity: Apply different critical lenses to the same text. How does a historical reading differ from a feminist reading? A postcolonial reading? Each reveals different aspects. "These are not ideologies — they are analytical tools."
- Practice: Write comparative analyses using 3 different critical approaches

**Lesson 9-12: Teaching Writing**
- Activity: Develop a writing curriculum unit for Building Band students. Teach it. Assess student learning. Revise based on experience.
- Practice: Plan, deliver, and evaluate 5 writing lessons for younger students

#### Assessment
- Extended analytical paper demonstrating sophisticated argumentation
- Comparative critical analyses using multiple frameworks
- Writing curriculum delivered to younger students with documented outcomes

### Unit 7: Document Drafting and Creative Writing (Weeks 13-24)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Community document templates, creative writing materials
**Survival Relevance**: Community governance requires documents — charters, agreements, codes, procedures. Creative writing sustains cultural life and provides meaning.

#### Learning Objectives
- Draft governance documents (charters, agreements, codes of conduct, bylaws)
- Write legal-style documents (contracts, dispute resolution procedures, trade agreements)
- Produce creative writing of literary quality (fiction, poetry, personal essay, drama)
- Teach writing to others

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Governance Documents**
- Activity: Draft or revise real community documents: community charter sections, code of conduct, committee bylaws, decision-making procedures. Use clear, precise, unambiguous language.
- Practice: Draft 3 governance documents reviewed by community leadership

**Lesson 5-8: Legal Writing**
- Activity: Draft trade agreements, dispute resolution procedures, property use agreements. Each document must be clear enough that parties without legal training understand their rights and obligations.
- Practice: Draft 2 legal documents tested for clarity with community members

**Lesson 9-12: Creative Writing Advanced**
- Activity: Extended creative writing project — a short story collection, poetry chapbook, personal essay collection, one-act play, or beginning of a novel. Workshop process: draft, share, receive feedback, revise, polish.
- Practice: Complete a creative writing project of at least 5,000 words

#### Assessment
- Governance documents accepted by community leadership
- Legal documents tested for clarity and comprehensiveness
- Creative writing project demonstrating literary craft

## Grade 12: Capstone

### Unit 8: Capstone Written Work (Full Year)
**Duration**: Full academic year
**Materials**: All available resources
**Survival Relevance**: The capstone produces a significant work of lasting value to the community

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify a writing project of community significance
- Plan, research, draft, revise, and complete a major work
- Present and defend the work publicly
- Archive the work in community records

#### Project Options
- **Community History**: A comprehensive written history of the community from founding to present
- **Technical Manual**: A complete operational manual for a community system
- **Curriculum Contribution**: New curriculum materials for a subject area
- **Legal Code**: A draft community legal code or governance framework
- **Creative Work**: A novel, play, or extended creative work of literary quality
- **Student's Proposal**: Any significant writing project approved by the teacher

#### Process
- Weeks 1-4: Project proposal, research plan, approval
- Weeks 5-16: Research and first draft
- Weeks 17-24: Revision, peer review, second draft
- Weeks 25-30: Final revision, editing, formatting
- Weeks 31-34: Public presentation and defense, archiving

#### Assessment
- Capstone work of at least 10,000 words (or equivalent for dramatic/poetic works)
- Public presentation and defense demonstrating deep understanding
- Work archived in community records
- Demonstrated growth across the 4-year advanced language arts program
