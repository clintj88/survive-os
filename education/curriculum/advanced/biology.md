# Advanced Band (Grades 9-12, Ages 14-18) — Biology

## Overview
Advanced biology prepares students for medical, agricultural, and environmental specializations. The curriculum progresses from cellular and molecular biology through human physiology, ecology, and field biology, with every unit connected to practical community applications.

## Grade 9: Cell Biology, Genetics, and Evolution

### Unit 1: Cell Biology Advanced (Weeks 1-8)
**Duration**: 8 weeks
**Materials**: Microscope (if available), prepared slides, cell diagrams, molecular model materials
**Survival Relevance**: Cellular processes underlie all medicine, agriculture, and food science. Understanding them enables informed decision-making about health, disease, and food production.

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe cellular organelles and their functions in detail
- Explain cellular respiration and photosynthesis at the molecular level
- Understand cell division (mitosis and meiosis) and their significance
- Connect cellular processes to observable phenomena (growth, healing, disease, food production)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-2: Organelle Functions**
- Activity: Each organelle is a specialized system: nucleus (DNA storage, cell control), mitochondria (energy production via cellular respiration), ribosomes (protein synthesis), ER (protein processing and transport), Golgi (packaging and shipping), lysosomes (waste processing), chloroplasts (photosynthesis in plants).
- Practice: Diagram a cell with all organelles labeled and function described

**Lesson 3-4: Cellular Respiration**
- Activity: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP (energy). Glycolysis → Krebs cycle → electron transport chain. This is how every cell in your body extracts energy from food. "When you eat a potato, this equation is what happens next."
- Practice: Trace the energy pathway from food to cellular work

**Lesson 5-6: Photosynthesis**
- Activity: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. The reverse of respiration. Light reactions (in thylakoids) → Calvin cycle (in stroma). "Every calorie of food you eat was captured from sunlight by this process."
- Practice: Diagram photosynthesis and explain the relationship to respiration

**Lesson 7-8: Cell Division**
- Activity: Mitosis: one cell → two identical cells (growth, repair). Meiosis: one cell → four cells with half the chromosomes (sex cells). Errors in division → mutations → cancer, genetic disorders, or occasionally beneficial adaptations.
- Practice: Compare mitosis and meiosis, explain their roles and what happens when they go wrong

#### Assessment
- Explain cellular organelle functions and their interconnections
- Describe cellular respiration and photosynthesis at the molecular level
- Compare mitosis and meiosis and explain their biological significance

### Unit 2: Genetics and Heredity (Weeks 9-18)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Punnett square materials, case study resources
**Survival Relevance**: Genetics governs livestock breeding, crop improvement, disease inheritance, and forensic identification

#### Learning Objectives
- Solve complex genetics problems (dihybrid crosses, incomplete dominance, codominance, sex-linked traits)
- Understand DNA structure and function
- Explain gene expression (DNA → RNA → protein)
- Apply genetics to agricultural breeding and medical scenarios

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-3: Advanced Mendelian Genetics**
- Activity: Dihybrid crosses (two traits simultaneously), incomplete dominance (blending), codominance (both expressed), sex-linked traits (on X chromosome — explains why some conditions are more common in males).
- Practice: Solve 20 advanced genetics problems

**Lesson 4-6: DNA and Gene Expression**
- Activity: DNA structure: double helix, base pairs (A-T, G-C), genes are segments of DNA that code for proteins. Central dogma: DNA → (transcription) → mRNA → (translation) → protein. Proteins do all the work.
- Practice: Diagram DNA structure, trace the path from gene to protein

**Lesson 7-8: Mutations and Genetic Variation**
- Activity: Mutations are changes in DNA sequence. Most are neutral, some are harmful, a few are beneficial. Sources: copying errors, UV radiation, chemicals. Connection to evolution and disease.
- Practice: Analyze 10 mutation scenarios and predict effects

**Lesson 9-10: Applied Genetics**
- Activity: Livestock breeding: selecting for desired traits, avoiding inbreeding (pedigree analysis). Crop improvement: hybridization, selection, isolation. Medical genetics: inheritance of conditions, genetic counseling principles.
- Practice: Design a breeding program for a community livestock species with genetic justification

#### Assessment
- Solve complex genetics problems accurately
- Explain gene expression from DNA to protein
- Apply genetics to agricultural and medical scenarios

### Unit 3: Evolution and Ecology (Weeks 19-28)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Fossil record images, field observation equipment, ecological data
**Survival Relevance**: Evolutionary thinking explains antibiotic resistance, pest adaptation, crop domestication, and species interactions that affect community resources

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain natural selection with molecular and population-level evidence
- Analyze population genetics (gene pools, Hardy-Weinberg, genetic drift, gene flow)
- Conduct ecological field research
- Apply evolutionary and ecological principles to community resource management

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-3: Mechanisms of Evolution**
- Activity: Natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, sexual selection — five mechanisms of evolution. Population genetics: allele frequencies change over time. Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: the conditions under which evolution does NOT occur (and why those conditions are never fully met).
- Practice: Calculate allele frequencies and predict evolutionary outcomes

**Lesson 4-6: Evidence and Applications**
- Activity: Evidence: fossil record, comparative anatomy, molecular biology (DNA sequences), biogeography, observed evolution (antibiotic resistance, pesticide resistance, Darwin's finches). Applications: predicting resistance evolution, understanding disease emergence, managing biodiversity.
- Practice: Present evidence-based arguments for evolution from 5 different lines of evidence

**Lesson 7-10: Environmental Science**
- Activity: Ecosystem services (clean water, pollination, decomposition, climate regulation), biodiversity and stability, conservation biology, environmental impact assessment. Conduct a field study of a local ecosystem.
- Practice: Complete an environmental impact assessment for a proposed community project

#### Assessment
- Explain evolutionary mechanisms with evidence
- Analyze population genetics problems
- Complete an environmental field study with management recommendations

## Grade 10: Human Biology and Microbiology

### Unit 4: Human Anatomy and Physiology — Medical Track (Weeks 1-18)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: Anatomical models/diagrams, stethoscope, blood pressure cuff, clinical reference materials
**Survival Relevance**: This is pre-medical training — the depth of knowledge expected of a paramedic or community health worker

#### Learning Objectives
- Describe all body systems at medical-track depth
- Correlate structure with function for each system
- Identify pathological conditions (what goes wrong and why)
- Perform basic clinical assessments

#### Lessons (condensed — each system receives 2-3 weeks)
- **Cardiovascular**: Heart anatomy, ECG rhythm recognition (if equipment available), blood pressure physiology, cardiovascular diseases, shock physiology
- **Respiratory**: Lung anatomy, gas exchange physiology, respiratory failure signs, airway management
- **Neurological**: Brain regions and functions, peripheral nervous system, neurological assessment (pupil response, motor/sensory testing, consciousness scales)
- **Musculoskeletal**: Bone and joint anatomy, fracture classification, common injuries, rehabilitation principles
- **Gastrointestinal**: Digestive physiology, common pathology (appendicitis, obstruction, ulcer), nutrition absorption
- **Renal/Urinary**: Kidney function, fluid balance, electrolytes, dehydration management
- **Endocrine**: Hormone regulation, diabetes management, thyroid conditions
- **Reproductive**: Detailed reproductive physiology, pregnancy, labor, common complications
- **Immune**: Immune response in depth, autoimmune conditions, allergic reactions, immunodeficiency

#### Assessment
- Comprehensive anatomy and physiology examination
- Clinical assessment skills demonstration
- Pathology identification for common conditions

### Unit 5: Microbiology and Epidemiology (Weeks 19-28)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Microscope, culturing supplies (if available), epidemiological case studies
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding pathogens and disease spread is essential for community health management and epidemic prevention

#### Learning Objectives
- Classify microorganisms by type and pathogenicity
- Understand antimicrobial mechanisms (antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals)
- Conduct epidemiological investigation (outbreak source identification)
- Design community disease surveillance systems

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Pathogenic Microbiology**
- Activity: Study major pathogen groups: bacteria (classification, gram staining concept, antibiotic targets), viruses (structure, replication, why antibiotics don't work), fungi (common infections), parasites (protozoans, helminths). For each: transmission, disease mechanism, treatment.
- Practice: Create pathogen profiles for 20 common disease-causing organisms

**Lesson 5-7: Antimicrobial Science**
- Activity: How antibiotics work (targeting cell wall, protein synthesis, DNA replication). Resistance mechanisms (mutation, horizontal gene transfer). Antiviral strategies. Antifungal mechanisms. "Why you must complete a full course of antibiotics."
- Practice: Explain antimicrobial mechanisms and resistance for major drug classes

**Lesson 8-10: Epidemiology**
- Activity: Case studies: outbreak investigation methodology. Identify the pathogen, trace the transmission chain, find the source, implement containment. Apply to historical epidemics and simulated community scenarios.
- Practice: Conduct a simulated outbreak investigation and present findings

#### Assessment
- Classify pathogens and explain their disease mechanisms
- Explain antimicrobial mechanisms and resistance
- Conduct an epidemiological investigation with source identification

## Grade 11: Botany, Zoology, and Applied Biology

### Unit 6: Botany (Weeks 1-18)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: Plant specimens, field guides, microscope, garden access, medicinal plant references
**Survival Relevance**: Plant knowledge is directly applicable to food production, medicine, construction materials, and environmental management

#### Learning Objectives
- Identify 50+ plant species with uses (food, medicine, material, toxic)
- Understand plant physiology (photosynthesis, transpiration, nutrient uptake, reproduction)
- Apply botanical knowledge to agricultural improvement
- Evaluate medicinal plants critically (evidence-based assessment)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-6: Plant Identification and Taxonomy**
- Activity: Systematic field study — identify plants by family characteristics. Learn to use dichotomous keys independently. Document 50+ species with detailed descriptions, illustrations, habitats, and uses.
- Practice: Field identification portfolio of 50+ species

**Lesson 7-10: Plant Physiology**
- Activity: Photosynthesis efficiency factors (light, CO₂, water, temperature). Transpiration and water movement. Nutrient uptake and soil interactions. Reproductive strategies (pollination, seed dispersal). Growth hormones (auxins, gibberellins).
- Practice: Design experiments testing factors affecting plant growth

**Lesson 11-14: Agricultural Botany**
- Activity: Crop physiology — what makes plants produce more? Light interception, water use efficiency, nutrient management, pest and disease resistance. Selective breeding strategies based on plant genetics.
- Practice: Design a crop improvement program using botanical knowledge

**Lesson 15-18: Medicinal Botany**
- Activity: Evidence-based assessment of medicinal plants: pharmacologically active compounds, traditional uses vs clinical evidence, dosing, preparation, contraindications, toxicology. Create a community pharmacopoeia (medicinal plant reference).
- Practice: Complete a pharmacopoeia with evidence ratings for 20+ medicinal plants

#### Assessment
- Identify 50+ plant species with uses
- Explain plant physiology and its agricultural applications
- Create an evidence-based medicinal plant reference

### Unit 7: Zoology and Marine/Freshwater Biology (Weeks 19-34)
**Duration**: 16 weeks
**Materials**: Field observation equipment, specimens, aquatic sampling equipment
**Survival Relevance**: Animal behavior knowledge improves livestock management, pest control, hunting, and conservation. Aquatic biology supports fisheries and water management.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-6: Animal Behavior and Livestock Science**
- Activity: Behavioral ecology: foraging strategies, social structures, predator-prey dynamics, communication. Applied to livestock: stress indicators, welfare assessment, behavioral enrichment, handling techniques based on species-specific behavior.
- Practice: Conduct behavioral observations of community livestock and wild animals

**Lesson 7-10: Wildlife Management**
- Activity: Population assessment methods (mark-recapture, transect surveys, track counts). Sustainable harvest calculations. Habitat management. Conflict mitigation (protecting crops from wildlife, livestock from predators).
- Practice: Conduct a wildlife population assessment and recommend management strategies

**Lesson 11-16: Aquatic Biology**
- Activity: Freshwater ecology: macroinvertebrates as water quality indicators, fish biology, aquatic plant identification. Applied aquaculture: species selection, water chemistry, feeding, breeding, disease management.
- Practice: Assess aquatic habitat quality and design an aquaculture improvement plan

#### Assessment
- Conduct animal behavioral observations with analysis
- Complete a wildlife population assessment
- Assess aquatic habitat quality and design an aquaculture plan

## Grade 12: Biotechnology and Field Biology Capstone

### Unit 8: Biotechnology Concepts and Conservation (Weeks 1-12)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Reference materials, field equipment, community biological resources
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding biotechnology principles enables advanced agriculture and medicine. Conservation biology protects the resource base.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Biotechnology Principles**
- Activity: Selective breeding (applied genetics), fermentation science (microbial biotechnology), biological pest control, bioremediation (using organisms to clean contamination), composting science. These are low-tech biotechnologies available now.
- Practice: Design a biotechnology application for a community need

**Lesson 5-8: Conservation Biology**
- Activity: Biodiversity assessment methods, endangered species management, habitat restoration, invasive species management, genetic diversity and population viability. Apply to the community's ecosystem.
- Practice: Conduct a biodiversity assessment and create a conservation plan

**Lesson 9-12: Applied Biology Synthesis**
- Activity: Integrate all biological knowledge into community applications: agricultural improvement plan, health management protocols, environmental management strategy, resource sustainability assessment.
- Practice: Present an integrated biology-based community improvement plan

---

### Unit 9: Field Biology Capstone (Weeks 13-34)
**Duration**: 22 weeks
**Materials**: Field equipment, reference materials, documentation supplies
**Survival Relevance**: The capstone produces a comprehensive biological survey of the community's environment — a reference document for future agricultural, medical, and environmental decisions

#### Capstone Project: Local Ecosystem Survey
Comprehensive biological survey including:
- Flora inventory (all plant species with identification, location, uses)
- Fauna inventory (all observed animal species with behavior notes)
- Aquatic biology assessment (water quality, species, productivity)
- Soil biology assessment (microbiome health, earthworm counts, organic matter)
- Ecosystem map showing habitats, corridors, and critical areas
- Threat assessment (invasive species, habitat degradation, overuse)
- Management recommendations with scientific justification
- Seasonal calendar of biological events (migration, flowering, fruiting, breeding)

#### Assessment
- Comprehensive field biology survey documented and archived
- Species inventories with identification confidence levels
- Management recommendations based on scientific evidence
- Public presentation to community with practical recommendations
