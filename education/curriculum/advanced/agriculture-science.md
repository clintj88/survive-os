# Advanced Band (Grades 9-12, Ages 14-18) — Agriculture Science

## Overview
Advanced agriculture science prepares students to manage community food production systems. The curriculum progresses from soil and plant science through livestock management and farm design to the capstone: designing and managing a production system that feeds the community.

## Grade 9: Soil Science and Plant Physiology

### Unit 1: Soil Science and Chemistry (Weeks 1-14)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Soil testing equipment, lab supplies, garden access, composting area
**Survival Relevance**: Soil health determines food production capacity. Advanced soil science enables maintaining and improving fertility for generations.

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze soil chemistry (CEC, pH buffering, nutrient availability curves)
- Understand soil biology (microbiome, mycorrhizal networks, nutrient cycling organisms)
- Design comprehensive soil management programs
- Apply soil science to maximize crop production

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Advanced Soil Chemistry**
- Activity: Cation exchange capacity (CEC) — soil's ability to hold and release nutrients. pH buffering — why some soils resist pH changes. Nutrient availability curves — most nutrients are most available at pH 6.0-7.0. Micronutrient chemistry (iron, manganese, zinc, boron, molybdenum).
- Practice: Analyze soil samples for CEC, pH, and nutrient levels. Interpret results and recommend amendments.

**Lesson 5-8: Soil Biology**
- Activity: The soil microbiome: bacteria (decomposition, nitrogen fixation), fungi (mycorrhizal networks extending root reach by 100×), protozoa (bacterial grazers releasing nutrients), nematodes, arthropods, earthworms. "One teaspoon of healthy soil contains more organisms than people on Earth."
- Practice: Assess soil biological health using indicator organisms (earthworm count, fungal hyphal density)

**Lesson 9-12: Soil Management Programs**
- Activity: Design a 5-year soil building plan: composting systems (hot composting, vermicomposting, sheet composting), cover cropping strategy, crop rotation for soil health, reduced tillage, organic matter targets, monitoring schedule.
- Practice: Implement a soil building practice and monitor results

**Lesson 13-14: Applied Soil Science**
- Activity: Soil mapping of community agricultural areas: type, condition, limitations, potential, recommended management for each area. This becomes the community's soil management reference.
- Practice: Complete a soil map with management recommendations for all agricultural areas

#### Assessment
- Analyze and interpret soil chemistry data
- Assess soil biological health
- Design a multi-year soil building program
- Create a soil management map for community agricultural areas

### Unit 2: Plant Physiology and Genetics (Weeks 15-28)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Plants, microscope, seed collection, greenhouse or grow space
**Survival Relevance**: Understanding plant physiology enables optimizing growth conditions, diagnosing problems, and improving varieties through informed selection

#### Learning Objectives
- Explain photosynthesis, respiration, and transpiration at the physiological level
- Understand plant hormone systems and growth regulation
- Apply genetics to crop improvement (selection, hybridization, seed saving)
- Design plant breeding programs for community crops

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Plant Physiology**
- Activity: Photosynthesis efficiency factors (light intensity, CO₂ concentration, temperature, water status). Transpiration and stomatal regulation. Nutrient uptake mechanisms (active transport, ion exchange). Photoperiodism (day length affecting flowering).
- Practice: Design experiments testing physiological factors and measure effects on plant growth

**Lesson 5-8: Plant Hormones and Growth**
- Activity: Auxins (stem elongation, phototropism), gibberellins (seed germination, stem elongation), cytokinins (cell division, delay of senescence), abscisic acid (drought response, dormancy), ethylene (fruit ripening, stress response).
- Practice: Demonstrate hormone effects (ethylene ripening experiment, auxin bending experiment)

**Lesson 9-12: Crop Genetics and Breeding**
- Activity: Open-pollinated vs hybrid varieties. Inbreeding depression. Heterosis (hybrid vigor). Isolation distances for seed saving. Selection methods: mass selection, family selection, progeny testing. Trait heritability.
- Practice: Design a 5-generation breeding program for a community crop

**Lesson 13-14: Seed Science**
- Activity: Seed viability testing (germination rates), seed storage conditions (cool, dry, dark), seed longevity by species, seed sovereignty (importance of controlling your own seed supply).
- Practice: Establish a community seed bank with viability testing protocols

#### Assessment
- Explain plant physiological processes at the mechanism level
- Demonstrate understanding of plant hormones through experiments
- Design a crop breeding program with genetic justification
- Establish seed saving and storage protocols

## Grade 10: Agroecology and Livestock

### Unit 3: Agroecology and Pest Management (Weeks 1-14)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Garden access, pest identification guides, biological control resources
**Survival Relevance**: Sustainable pest management protects crops without depleting ecological resources. Agroecology designs production systems that work with nature rather than against it.

#### Learning Objectives
- Design agroecological production systems (polyculture, agroforestry, silvopasture)
- Implement advanced integrated pest management
- Monitor ecosystem health indicators in agricultural settings
- Balance production with environmental sustainability

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Agroecological Design**
- Activity: Polyculture design (multiple crops interplanted for mutual benefit), agroforestry (trees + crops), silvopasture (trees + livestock grazing), forest gardening (multi-layer food forest). Compare yields per unit area with monoculture.
- Practice: Design an agroecological system for a community field

**Lesson 5-8: Advanced IPM**
- Activity: Pest ecology (life cycles, population dynamics, natural enemies). Biological control programs (releasing beneficial insects, managing habitat for natural enemies). Cultural controls (resistant varieties, trap crops, timing). Monitoring protocols and economic thresholds (how much damage justifies intervention?).
- Practice: Implement a monitoring program and make evidence-based management decisions

**Lesson 9-14: Ecosystem Indicators and Sustainability**
- Activity: Monitor: soil organism diversity, pollinator populations, predator-prey ratios, water quality from agricultural runoff, soil organic matter trends. These indicators show whether the farming system is sustainable or degrading its resource base.
- Practice: Establish a monitoring program and assess sustainability trends

#### Assessment
- Design an agroecological production system
- Implement IPM with monitoring and evidence-based decisions
- Assess sustainability using ecosystem health indicators

### Unit 4: Livestock Nutrition and Breeding (Weeks 15-28)
**Duration**: 14 weeks
**Materials**: Livestock access, feed analysis materials, breeding records
**Survival Relevance**: Livestock are critical community assets — proper nutrition and breeding management maximizes their contribution

#### Learning Objectives
- Formulate balanced rations for different species and production stages
- Design breeding programs to improve livestock quality
- Manage reproductive health (heat detection, breeding management, birth assistance)
- Calculate feed conversion ratios and production efficiency

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Animal Nutrition Science**
- Activity: Nutrient requirements by species and stage (maintenance, growth, lactation, gestation). Energy (calories), protein (amino acids), minerals, vitamins. Feed analysis: crude protein, fiber, energy content. Ration balancing: meet requirements from available feeds.
- Practice: Formulate balanced rations for 5 different livestock scenarios

**Lesson 5-8: Breeding Programs**
- Activity: Genetic selection objectives (milk production, meat quality, egg production, disease resistance, temperament). Inbreeding avoidance (pedigree tracking, introducing new genetics). Crossbreeding for hybrid vigor. Selection intensity and generation interval.
- Practice: Design a 10-generation breeding program for community livestock

**Lesson 9-12: Reproductive Management**
- Activity: Heat detection (behavioral signs by species), breeding timing, gestation management, birthing assistance (normal vs complicated), neonatal care, castration, weaning management.
- Practice: Manage reproductive events under supervision

**Lesson 13-14: Production Economics**
- Activity: Feed conversion ratio (how much feed produces how much product). Production cost per unit (eggs, milk, meat). Break-even analysis. "Is this livestock operation producing enough value to justify the feed, labor, and housing investment?"
- Practice: Calculate production economics for community livestock operations

#### Assessment
- Formulate balanced rations for multiple species
- Design a breeding program with genetic goals
- Manage reproductive events safely
- Calculate production economics

### Unit 5: Aquaculture (Weeks 29-38)
**Duration**: 10 weeks
**Materials**: Pond or tank access, water testing supplies, fish or aquaculture species, feed
**Survival Relevance**: Fish are the most feed-efficient protein source — better conversion ratio than any land animal

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Aquaculture Systems**
- Activity: Pond culture, tank culture, raceway culture. Species selection by climate: tilapia (warm), trout (cold), catfish (warm), carp (adaptable). System design: water source, pond construction, aeration, drainage.
- Practice: Design an aquaculture system appropriate for the community

**Lesson 5-8: Fish Management**
- Activity: Stocking density, feeding programs, water quality monitoring (dissolved oxygen, ammonia, pH, temperature), disease recognition, harvest methods, processing.
- Practice: Manage an aquaculture system with daily monitoring for 4 weeks

**Lesson 9-10: Integrated Systems**
- Activity: Aquaponics (fish + plants — fish waste fertilizes plants, plants filter water). Rice-fish culture. Pond-garden integration. These systems produce more food per unit area than either component alone.
- Practice: Design an integrated aquaculture-agriculture system

#### Assessment
- Design an aquaculture system with species justification
- Manage water quality and fish health
- Design an integrated system maximizing production per area

## Grade 11: Farm Systems Design

### Unit 6: Permaculture and Farm Systems (Weeks 1-18)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: Design materials, community agricultural land, reference materials
**Survival Relevance**: Permaculture designs self-sustaining food systems that require less labor and fewer inputs over time

#### Learning Objectives
- Apply permaculture design principles to farm planning
- Design complete farm systems integrating crops, livestock, water, and energy
- Plan food processing and preservation infrastructure
- Create agricultural economic projections

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-6: Permaculture Design**
- Activity: Ethics (earth care, people care, fair share). Principles (observe and interact, catch and store energy, obtain a yield, self-regulate, use renewables, produce no waste, design from patterns to details, integrate rather than segregate, use small and slow solutions, use and value diversity, use edges, creatively use change).
- Practice: Apply all principles to a community farm design

**Lesson 7-12: Integrated Farm Design**
- Activity: Complete farm design integrating: annual crops (garden, field crops), perennial crops (fruit trees, berry bushes, nut trees), livestock (poultry, ruminants, bees), water management (irrigation, drainage, aquaculture), energy (fuel wood, biogas, solar), structures (greenhouse, barn, storage), and processing (kitchen, preservation facility).
- Practice: Create a comprehensive farm design with all elements integrated

**Lesson 13-16: Food Processing Infrastructure**
- Activity: Design processing facilities: drying racks and dehydrator, smoke house, fermentation area, grain storage, root cellar, canning kitchen (if equipment available). Calculate throughput capacity — can the processing handle peak harvest volume?
- Practice: Design and build at least one processing facility

**Lesson 17-18: Agricultural Economics**
- Activity: Complete farm economic analysis: production projections by crop and livestock, labor requirements by month, input costs, surplus for trade, capital investment priorities, 5-year financial projection.
- Practice: Present agricultural economic plan to community leadership

#### Assessment
- Design a complete permaculture-based farm system
- Integrate all farm elements with resource flow analysis
- Create agricultural economic projections
- Present farm plan for community review

### Unit 7: Water Management and Irrigation Engineering (Weeks 19-30)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Surveying equipment, pipe/tubing, pump access, water measurement tools
**Survival Relevance**: Water management is the single most critical agricultural infrastructure — adequate water means food, inadequate water means famine

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Watershed Management**
- Activity: Watershed analysis, rainwater harvesting calculations (roof area × rainfall × efficiency = capture volume), swales and berms for water retention, erosion control, groundwater recharge.
- Practice: Design a watershed management plan for community agricultural areas

**Lesson 5-8: Irrigation Engineering**
- Activity: System design: gravity-fed, drip, sprinkler. Hydraulic calculations: flow rate, pressure, pipe sizing. Pump selection (if powered). Water use efficiency optimization. Scheduling based on crop needs and soil moisture.
- Practice: Design, build, and commission an irrigation system for a community garden section

**Lesson 9-12: Water Storage and Treatment**
- Activity: Pond construction, cistern design, dam/reservoir assessment. Water quality management for irrigation. Greywater recycling for agriculture. Drought contingency planning.
- Practice: Design a complete water management system including storage and drought contingency

#### Assessment
- Design watershed management for agricultural areas
- Engineer irrigation systems with hydraulic calculations
- Plan water storage and drought contingency

## Grade 12: Agricultural Capstone

### Unit 8: Capstone — Design, Implement, and Manage a Production System (Full Year)
**Duration**: Full academic year
**Materials**: Community agricultural resources, all available inputs and tools
**Survival Relevance**: This is the culminating test — can the student actually produce food for the community?

#### Structure
Each student or pair takes complete responsibility for a significant production unit:
- A one-acre diversified garden plot
- A livestock operation (poultry flock, rabbit colony, or goat management)
- An aquaculture system
- A food processing and preservation operation
- An orchard/perennial system establishment

#### Requirements
- Design the system with written justification for all decisions
- Implement from planning through establishment
- Manage daily operations for the growing season
- Maintain records (inputs, outputs, labor, observations, financial)
- Harvest, process, and preserve products
- Calculate production efficiency and economic viability
- Write a comprehensive season report with lessons learned
- Present results and recommendations to community agricultural leadership

#### Teaching Component
- Develop and deliver 5 agricultural lessons to Building or Intermediate band students
- Mentor younger students in agricultural skills
- Contribute to the community agricultural knowledge base

#### Assessment
- Production system produces usable food for the community
- Complete records demonstrating management competence
- Season report with analysis and recommendations
- Teaching delivered to younger students
- Community agricultural leadership evaluation
