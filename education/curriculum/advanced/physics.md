# Advanced Band (Grades 9-12, Ages 14-18) — Physics

## Overview
Physics explains how the physical world works — forces, energy, electricity, and waves. Every unit connects to engineering applications: building structures, generating power, designing communication systems, and managing water resources. The capstone is designing and building a functional device.

## Grade 9: Mechanics

### Unit 1: Forces and Motion (Weeks 1-18)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: Spring scales, ramps, carts/balls, stopwatch, measuring tools, weights
**Survival Relevance**: Mechanics governs construction, transportation, tool design, water systems, and every physical interaction in daily community life

#### Learning Objectives
- State and apply Newton's three laws of motion
- Calculate force, mass, acceleration, velocity, and displacement
- Analyze forces in real-world scenarios (friction, gravity, tension, normal force)
- Apply work and energy concepts to practical problems (machines, lifting, moving loads)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Kinematics**
- Activity: Measure motion: displacement (how far), velocity (how fast and in what direction), acceleration (how quickly velocity changes). Equations of motion: v = v₀ + at, d = v₀t + ½at², v² = v₀² + 2ad. Application: "How long does it take a dropped rock to fall 20 meters?"
- Practice: Solve 20 kinematics problems with real-world contexts

**Lesson 5-8: Newton's Laws**
- Activity: 1st Law: objects at rest stay at rest (inertia — why loads shift in a cart). 2nd Law: F = ma (force = mass × acceleration — the fundamental equation). 3rd Law: every action has an equal opposite reaction (why a rifle recoils, why rockets work).
- Practice: Apply Newton's laws to 20 scenarios and solve for unknown quantities

**Lesson 9-12: Forces in Action**
- Activity: Gravity (F = mg, g ≈ 9.8 m/s²). Friction (static vs kinetic, coefficient of friction). Tension (rope/cable forces). Normal force (support force from surfaces). Free body diagrams — draw ALL forces acting on an object.
- Practice: Draw free body diagrams and calculate net force for 15 scenarios

**Lesson 13-16: Work, Energy, and Power**
- Activity: Work = Force × Distance (W = Fd). Kinetic energy = ½mv². Potential energy = mgh. Power = Work/Time. Conservation of energy: energy is neither created nor destroyed. Application: "How much work to lift 500 kg of water 10 meters?"
- Practice: Calculate work, energy, and power for community-relevant problems

**Lesson 17-18: Simple Machines**
- Activity: The six simple machines: lever, pulley, wheel-and-axle, inclined plane, wedge, screw. Mechanical advantage = output force / input force. "A pulley lets you lift 100 kg with only 50 kg of force — but you pull twice the distance."
- Practice: Calculate mechanical advantage and design simple machine solutions for community tasks

#### Assessment
- Apply Newton's laws and solve force problems
- Calculate work, energy, and power
- Design simple machine solutions with calculated mechanical advantage

### Unit 2: Momentum, Pressure, and Structural Analysis (Weeks 19-34)
**Duration**: 16 weeks
**Materials**: Collision demonstration materials, pressure measurement tools, structural testing materials
**Survival Relevance**: Momentum analysis prevents accidents, pressure calculations design water systems, and structural analysis ensures buildings don't collapse

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Momentum and Collisions**
- Activity: Momentum = mass × velocity (p = mv). Conservation of momentum: total momentum before = total momentum after. Application: vehicle collisions, falling objects, recoil. Impulse = force × time (why padding reduces injury force).
- Practice: Solve momentum problems, design impact-reducing solutions

**Lesson 5-8: Pressure and Fluids**
- Activity: Pressure = Force/Area (P = F/A). Fluid pressure increases with depth (P = ρgh). Pascal's principle (hydraulics). Bernoulli's principle (airflow and lift). Application: water pressure calculations for plumbing, dam design, hydraulic presses.
- Practice: Calculate pressures in community water systems and hydraulic applications

**Lesson 9-12: Structural Physics**
- Activity: Stress = Force/Area (will this beam break?). Strain = deformation/original length. Young's modulus (material stiffness). Analyze beams, columns, and connections for structural adequacy. Center of mass and stability.
- Practice: Analyze community structures for safety and design improvements

**Lesson 13-16: Applied Mechanics Project**
- Activity: Design and analyze a mechanical solution for a community need: lifting system (crane, pulley array), transport system (cart, ramp), water delivery system (pump, siphon, hydraulic ram), or structural improvement.
- Practice: Complete design with full calculations and present

#### Assessment
- Calculate momentum and analyze collisions
- Design pressure-based systems with calculations
- Analyze structural elements for safety

## Grade 10: Electricity and Magnetism

### Unit 3: Circuits and Electromagnetic Theory (Weeks 1-18)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: Wire, batteries, motors, generators, magnets, multimeter, components
**Survival Relevance**: Electricity powers lighting, communication, medical equipment, and computing. Understanding electromagnetic theory enables building and maintaining power systems.

#### Learning Objectives
- Analyze complex circuits (series-parallel combinations, Kirchhoff's laws)
- Understand magnetism and electromagnetic induction
- Design and build generators and motors
- Explain electromagnetic wave propagation (radio theory foundation)

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Advanced Circuit Analysis**
- Activity: Kirchhoff's voltage law (voltages around a loop sum to zero) and current law (currents at a node sum to zero). Analyze series-parallel combinations. Calculate power: P = IV = I²R = V²/R. Application: design wiring for a building.
- Practice: Analyze 15 complex circuits and design 3 wiring layouts

**Lesson 5-8: Magnetism and Induction**
- Activity: Moving charges create magnetic fields (electromagnetism). Changing magnetic fields create electric current (electromagnetic induction — Faraday's law). Generators convert motion to electricity. Motors convert electricity to motion. Transformers change voltage levels.
- Practice: Build a simple generator and motor, measure outputs

**Lesson 9-12: Power Systems**
- Activity: AC vs DC. Transformers (stepping voltage up for transmission, down for use). Safety: grounding, circuit breakers, insulation. Solar panel theory (photovoltaic effect). Battery chemistry and management. Inverters (DC to AC conversion).
- Practice: Design a power system for a community building with calculations

**Lesson 13-16: Electromagnetic Waves**
- Activity: Accelerating charges produce electromagnetic waves. Wave properties: frequency, wavelength, speed (c = fλ). The electromagnetic spectrum. Antenna theory: antenna length relates to wavelength. Propagation: line-of-sight, ground wave, sky wave.
- Practice: Calculate antenna dimensions, analyze propagation for different frequencies

**Lesson 17-18: Radio Wave Theory**
- Activity: Advanced radio: modulation (AM/FM/digital), bandwidth, signal-to-noise ratio, receiver sensitivity. Link budget: transmitter power - path loss + antenna gains = received signal strength.
- Practice: Calculate link budgets for community radio links

#### Assessment
- Analyze complex circuits using Kirchhoff's laws
- Build and explain generators, motors, and transformers
- Design power systems and radio links with calculations

## Grade 11: Thermodynamics, Fluids, and Waves

### Unit 4: Thermodynamics (Weeks 1-12)
**Duration**: 12 weeks
**Materials**: Thermometers, heat sources, insulation materials, engine diagrams
**Survival Relevance**: Thermodynamics governs heating, cooling, cooking, food preservation, engines, and building insulation design

#### Learning Objectives
- Apply the laws of thermodynamics
- Calculate heat transfer (conduction, convection, radiation)
- Understand heat engines and refrigeration cycles
- Design thermal management systems for community buildings

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-4: Laws of Thermodynamics**
- Activity: 0th: thermal equilibrium. 1st: energy is conserved (Q = ΔU + W). 2nd: entropy increases — heat flows from hot to cold, never spontaneously the reverse. 3rd: absolute zero is unattainable. Application: why insulation works, why engines waste heat.
- Practice: Apply thermodynamic laws to 15 practical scenarios

**Lesson 5-8: Heat Transfer**
- Activity: Conduction (through solid material — metal pot handle gets hot). Convection (through fluid motion — hot air rises). Radiation (through electromagnetic waves — feeling warmth from a fire). R-value (insulation effectiveness). Design insulation systems.
- Practice: Calculate heat loss through walls and design insulation improvements

**Lesson 9-12: Engines and Refrigeration**
- Activity: Heat engines convert thermal energy to mechanical work (steam engines, internal combustion). Carnot efficiency = 1 - T_cold/T_hot (no engine can be 100% efficient). Refrigeration: reverse heat engine, moves heat from cold to hot (requires work input).
- Practice: Calculate engine efficiency, analyze community heating/cooling needs

#### Assessment
- Apply thermodynamic laws to practical problems
- Calculate heat transfer and design thermal management
- Analyze engine efficiency and thermal system design

### Unit 5: Fluid Mechanics and Wave Physics (Weeks 13-28)
**Duration**: 16 weeks
**Materials**: Containers, tubing, pumps (if available), string instruments or tuning forks, optical equipment
**Survival Relevance**: Fluid mechanics designs water systems. Wave physics explains sound (communication) and light (optics, solar energy).

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-6: Fluid Mechanics**
- Activity: Hydrostatics: pressure in fluids, buoyancy (Archimedes' principle), hydraulic systems. Hydrodynamics: flow rate, pipe sizing (continuity equation: A₁v₁ = A₂v₂), Bernoulli's equation, pump design. Application: community water systems, hydraulic power, dam/reservoir design.
- Practice: Design water delivery systems with full calculations

**Lesson 7-10: Sound**
- Activity: Sound as longitudinal waves. Properties: frequency (pitch), amplitude (volume), wavelength. Speed of sound in air (~343 m/s). Resonance, harmonics, acoustics. Application: building design for acoustic quality, signal audibility calculations.
- Practice: Calculate sound properties, design acoustics for a community meeting space

**Lesson 11-14: Optics**
- Activity: Light as electromagnetic waves. Reflection (mirrors), refraction (lenses), diffraction. Lens equation: 1/f = 1/d₀ + 1/dᵢ. Application: magnification (microscope/telescope principles), solar concentration (focusing sunlight for heat), lighting design.
- Practice: Calculate lens properties, design a solar concentrator

**Lesson 15-16: Applied Wave Physics**
- Activity: Telecommunications physics: signal attenuation, multipath interference, antenna directivity. Solar energy physics: solar spectrum, panel efficiency factors, tracking systems.
- Practice: Analyze a communication link or solar installation using wave physics

#### Assessment
- Design water systems using fluid mechanics calculations
- Apply acoustics and optics to community design problems
- Analyze telecommunications and solar systems using wave physics

## Grade 12: Applied Physics Capstone

### Unit 6: Structural Engineering and Renewable Energy (Weeks 1-16)
**Duration**: 16 weeks
**Materials**: Building materials, solar panels, wind measurement equipment, structural testing
**Survival Relevance**: Structural engineering ensures buildings are safe. Renewable energy provides power without fuel dependence.

#### Lessons
**Lesson 1-8: Structural Engineering Physics**
- Activity: Load analysis (dead loads, live loads, environmental loads). Beam analysis (bending moments, shear forces). Column analysis (buckling). Foundation design (soil bearing capacity). Apply to real community structures.
- Practice: Analyze community buildings for structural adequacy

**Lesson 9-16: Renewable Energy Engineering**
- Activity: Solar: panel orientation, tilt angle, shading analysis, system sizing. Wind: wind power equation P = ½ρAv³, turbine design parameters, site assessment. Micro-hydro: available head, flow rate, turbine selection, penstock design.
- Practice: Design a complete renewable energy system for a community building

### Unit 7: Applied Physics Capstone (Weeks 17-34)
**Duration**: 18 weeks
**Materials**: All available construction and electrical materials
**Survival Relevance**: Students design and build a functional device that serves the community

#### Capstone Project
Design and build a significant functional device:
- **Generator**: Hand-crank, bicycle-powered, wind, or water-driven generator producing usable electrical output
- **Water pump**: Manual, wind, or solar-powered pump for well or irrigation
- **Radio transceiver**: Functional radio from available components
- **Solar water heater**: Thermosiphon system for community use
- **Hydraulic ram pump**: No-power water pump using water hammer effect

**Requirements:**
- Physics analysis with calculations justifying the design
- Technical drawings
- Build and test with measured performance
- Performance documentation (power output, efficiency, reliability)
- Maintenance manual for community use

#### Assessment
- Design with complete physics calculations
- Functional device that performs as designed
- Performance documentation with measured results
- Maintenance manual for long-term community use
