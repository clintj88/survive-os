# Student Portfolio System

## Overview
Every student in the SURVIVE OS education system maintains a portfolio throughout their education. The portfolio documents growth, projects, practical achievements, and community contributions. It serves as the student's transcript, qualification record, and evidence of competence.

**Principle**: The portfolio answers the question "What can this person DO?" — not "What grades did they receive?"

---

## Portfolio Structure

### Section 1: Personal Profile
- Student name, age, band/grade
- Learning goals for the current year
- Strengths and areas for growth (self-identified, updated annually)
- Community roles and contributions

### Section 2: Academic Work Samples
For each subject area, include:
- **Best work**: 2-3 examples demonstrating highest competence
- **Growth evidence**: An early piece and a recent piece showing improvement
- **Reflections**: Student's own analysis of their learning (what they learned, what was difficult, what they want to learn next)

Minimum samples per subject per year:
- Language Arts: 3 writing samples, 1 reading response
- Mathematics: 3 problem sets showing different skill areas
- Science: 2 lab reports or observation journals
- History: 2 analytical pieces (comparison, source analysis, governance proposal)
- Other subjects: 1-2 representative samples

### Section 3: Practical Skills Documentation
For each practical skill demonstrated:
- Skill name and description
- Date demonstrated
- Evaluator name and signature/mark
- Performance level (mastery / approaching / beginning)
- Notes or evidence (photograph, product, evaluator comments)

Organized by category:
- Survival skills (fire, water, shelter, navigation)
- Agricultural skills
- Trade skills (woodworking, metalwork, electrical, etc.)
- Medical/first aid skills
- Communication skills
- Leadership/social skills

### Section 4: Community Contribution Record
Document all community service and contribution:
- Projects completed for the community
- Teaching/mentoring of younger students
- Community roles served
- Special assignments or responsibilities
- Community member testimonials (optional)

### Section 5: Mastery Checklist
A running checklist of mastery criteria for current band transition:
- Each criterion listed
- Date mastered (blank until demonstrated)
- Evidence location in portfolio
- Evaluator initials

### Section 6: Reflection and Goals
Annual reflections:
- What am I most proud of this year?
- What was most challenging?
- What do I want to learn next year?
- How am I contributing to the community?
- What kind of work do I want to do as an adult?

## Portfolio Management

### Physical Format
Portfolios are physical binders or folders. In a post-collapse environment, assume paper-based unless digital alternatives are available. Organize with dividers by section. Use durable materials — these portfolios should last a lifetime.

### Digital Backup
If SURVIVE OS computers are available:
- Photograph or scan key portfolio entries
- Store in the education module database
- Back up per the community data backup schedule

### Student Ownership
- Students own their portfolios
- Students select which work samples to include (with teacher guidance)
- Students write their own reflections
- Students present their portfolios at band transitions
- Younger students (K-2) receive help from teachers in organizing

### Teacher Role
- Guide students in selecting representative work
- Ensure practical skills are properly documented with evaluator signatures
- Facilitate annual reflection conversations
- Review portfolios quarterly for completeness
- Support students who struggle with self-reflection

---

## Portfolio Reviews

### Quarterly Check-In
- Teacher reviews portfolio for completeness
- Student adds recent work and updates mastery checklist
- Brief conversation about progress and goals
- Takes 15-20 minutes per student

### Annual Portfolio Presentation (All Bands)
- Student presents their portfolio to teacher and at least one other evaluator
- Highlights growth, achievements, and community contributions
- Discusses goals for the next year
- Takes 20-30 minutes per student
- Foundation Band: presented to teacher and parent/guardian
- Building Band: presented to teacher and one community member
- Intermediate Band: presented to teacher panel (2-3 teachers)
- Advanced Band: presented to teacher panel and community leader

### Band Transition Portfolio Review
- Formal review of entire portfolio against mastery criteria
- Student presents evidence for each mastery criterion
- Review panel: 2+ teachers and 1+ community practitioner
- Panel verifies mastery, identifies gaps, recommends advancement or additional work
- Takes 30-60 minutes per student
- Documented with panel signatures

## Rubric Templates

### Writing Assessment Rubric
| Criteria | Mastery | Approaching | Beginning |
|----------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Content** | Clear thesis supported by relevant evidence; addresses all aspects of prompt | Thesis present but evidence is thin or partially relevant | No clear thesis; evidence missing or irrelevant |
| **Organization** | Logical structure with clear introduction, body, conclusion; smooth transitions | Structure present but transitions weak or organization unclear | No clear structure; ideas presented randomly |
| **Evidence/Support** | Multiple pieces of specific evidence; properly cited if applicable | Some evidence but insufficient or not properly connected to claims | Little or no evidence to support claims |
| **Writing Quality** | Clear, varied sentences; appropriate vocabulary; minimal errors | Generally clear but some awkward sentences or vocabulary issues | Frequent errors that interfere with understanding |

### Practical Skill Rubric
| Criteria | Mastery | Approaching | Beginning |
|----------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Technique** | Correct technique performed consistently without prompting | Correct technique but requires occasional reminders | Technique incorrect or requires constant guidance |
| **Safety** | All safety procedures followed automatically | Safety procedures followed with occasional reminders | Safety procedures forgotten or ignored |
| **Efficiency** | Task completed in reasonable time with minimal wasted effort | Task completed but with unnecessary steps or time | Task incomplete or extremely slow |
| **Quality** | Product/result meets standard for community use | Product/result is functional but below community standard | Product/result is not functional |

### Oral Presentation Rubric
| Criteria | Mastery | Approaching | Beginning |
|----------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Content** | Thorough, accurate, well-organized; demonstrates deep understanding | Mostly accurate with adequate organization; understanding present | Inaccurate, disorganized, or demonstrates little understanding |
| **Delivery** | Clear voice, eye contact, appropriate pace; engages audience | Generally clear but some issues with pace, volume, or engagement | Difficult to hear or understand; reads from notes without engagement |
| **Evidence** | Specific examples and evidence support all claims | Some evidence but not consistently connected to claims | Little or no evidence provided |
| **Response to Questions** | Answers questions thoroughly with additional insight | Answers most questions adequately | Unable to answer questions or gives irrelevant responses |

### Historical Analysis Rubric
| Criteria | Mastery | Approaching | Beginning |
|----------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Multiple Perspectives** | Presents event from 2+ perspectives with equal depth and fairness | Attempts multiple perspectives but one dominates or is superficial | Single perspective only |
| **Evidence** | Uses specific historical evidence to support analysis | Some evidence but insufficient or poorly connected | No specific historical evidence |
| **Analysis** | Identifies patterns, causes, and consequences with sophistication | Identifies some patterns but analysis is surface-level | Descriptive only; no analysis |
| **Application** | Draws specific, actionable lessons for the community | Draws general lessons but lacks specificity | No connection to community application |

### Community Contribution Rubric
| Criteria | Mastery | Approaching | Beginning |
|----------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Initiative** | Identifies needs and acts without being asked | Completes assigned tasks reliably | Requires frequent reminders to complete tasks |
| **Quality** | Work meets community standards for the task | Work is adequate but below community standards | Work must be redone by others |
| **Collaboration** | Works effectively with community members of all ages | Works with peers but struggles with other age groups | Prefers to work alone; difficulty collaborating |
| **Reflection** | Articulates what was learned and how to improve | Can describe what was done but limited reflection | Cannot articulate learning from the experience |

## Special Considerations

### Students with Learning Differences
- Portfolio format may be adapted (oral recordings instead of written reflections, photographs instead of drawings)
- Mastery criteria remain the same — the path to demonstrating mastery may differ
- Additional support documented in portfolio
- Strengths highlighted alongside areas of challenge

### Transfer Students
- Students arriving from outside the community receive an intake assessment
- Existing skills documented in a new portfolio
- Placement determined by demonstrated competence, not age or previous grade
- Gaps in skills receive targeted instruction

### Portfolio as Community Record
- Completed portfolios are archived in the community records
- They serve as a skills inventory for the community (who can do what)
- They document the education system's effectiveness over time
- Former students' portfolios can be referenced for mentoring and teaching assignments
